# PMS-AXIOM: PART VII - SEX

## 10. SEX-Overlay: Consent Validity Under Asymmetry, Drift Markers, and Boundary Failure Modes

Part VII introduces the SEX overlay as the PMS add-on layer for consent-adjacent validity questions under asymmetry, temporality, publicness, body-related cost topology, and priced distance. The cases in this part remain grounded in PMS core, but they require an additional lens because their central closure-demands concern when consent-talk remains structurally coherent, when it becomes under-specified, and when it risks being used as a stabilizing label over conditions that the frame cannot actually carry.

The purpose of this part is not to define sexual normality, evaluate persons, infer motives, classify identities, provide advice, adjudicate guilt, or supply legal, clinical, therapeutic, or enforcement heuristics. It is to show what must remain visible before consent-related claims can be responsibly handled: frame (□), salient differences (Δ), asymmetry (Ω), temporal accumulation (Θ), residue and non-events (Λ), practical stop-capability (Χ), integration capacity (Σ), and binding discipline (Ψ).

The SEX overlay is therefore a guardrail-first, non-mixing disciplinary lens. It does not redefine PMS operators. It does not add a new base grammar. It supplies reduced signatures, drift markers, viability corridors, cost-topology handles, publicness/media extensions, and validity-boundary language that make SEX-specific risks visible: covert Ψ demand, pseudo-symmetry, Ω escalation, Λ-as-steering, Α fixation, Χ loss, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, publicness sedimentation, and boundary confusion.

PMS remains the canonical operator grammar. SEX only sharpens the reading where the case-specific issue is not merely agreement, desire, trust, intimacy, or relationship structure, but consent-validity under load: the question whether the configuration can actually carry the consent description without suppressing asymmetry, hiding temporal effects, pricing refusal, externalizing cost, or turning absence into steering.

### 10.1 Case Set and Role in the Paper

The cases addressed in Part VII are:

Together, these cases show why consent cannot be reduced to a single utterance, a purely internal state, a retrospective narrative, or a formal parity claim. They are not grouped here because PMS-SEX decides whether consent is valid or invalid in a concrete case. They are grouped here because each case contains a validity-pressure: a consent description seeks closure while the surrounding configuration may remain under-specified, asymmetric, temporally loaded, or boundary-confused.

The cases cluster into three SEX-relevant families:

- **Consent under asymmetry** shows why agreement and asymmetry must both be kept visible without allowing either to settle the case by itself.
- **Consent laundering** shows how consent language can be used to stabilize or launder a configuration whose frame, cost topology, stop-capability, or binding discipline has not been reconstructed.
- **Boundary confusion** shows how consent-adjacent material can move between intimacy, privacy, publicness, institutional procedure, accountability discourse, or tribunal-like frames without a stable validity grammar.

Part VII therefore demonstrates a central AXIOM discipline: consent analysis must be structurally sharp but non-verdictive. PMS-SEX can specify what a validity gate would require; it must not become a person label, moral ranking, adultness verdict, sexual classification, legal conclusion, clinical inference, or enforcement tool.

### 10.2 Output Contract for Part VII

The admissible output in Part VII is:

- **Validity gate:** a structured, non-prescriptive artefact specifying what must be true for a consent description to remain structurally coherent inside a given frame, and which patterns mark drift-preparedness or invalidity risk.

A validity gate may include:

- **Frame declaration:** what the scene is, what counts, what is excluded, and whether publicity, media, institutionalization, or record effects extend the frame.
- **Difference and pressure map:** salient boundaries, access points, pace, stakes, roles, escalation paths, and any impulse pressure toward closure.
- **Asymmetry profile:** access, exposure, dependency, obligation, authority, leverage, exit capacity, and publicness gradients, separated rather than moralized.
- **Temporal realism:** repetition, accumulation, after-effects, irreversibility windows, review points, withdrawal conditions, and exit realism.
- **Residue / non-event handling:** pauses, refusals, silence, withdrawal, missing safeguards, absent review, or non-response, without automatically treating absence as proof.
- **Distance / stop-capability check:** whether stopping, slowing, refusing, revising, or moving to meta-position is practically available or priced.
- **Integration and binding discipline:** whether contradictions and boundary mismatches can be coordinated without coercive simplification, and whether commitments are explicit, frame-supported, and revisable where required.
- **SEX drift markers:** overlay handles such as covert Ψ demand, pseudo-symmetry, Ω escalation, Λ-as-steering, Α fixation, Χ loss, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, or boundary confusion.
- **Explicit non-claims:** no person-labeling, no motive inference, no moral ranking, no adultness verdict, no diagnosis, no legal/forensic conclusion, no clinical conclusion, no enforcement guidance, and no PMS Base validation.

The SEX overlay may identify drift markers, but drift marker is not drift finding. A case may show that consent language is structurally vulnerable to laundering, steering, boundary confusion, or asymmetric cost externalization without asserting that any concrete person, relationship, institution, or practice has committed that drift.

MIP is active in this part only as an artefact-facing guardrail layer, not as a person-evaluation layer. Because consent analyses are high-risk for misuse, publicness, shaming, ranking, coercive leverage, and dignity-language overreach, MIP must document the analysis artefact’s publicity level, misuse corridors, responsibility ceilings, IA-box limits, and D0-protective boundary conditions where relevant. MIP does not score persons, assign adultness, activate D as a case finding, or produce guilt, maturity, legal, clinical, or enforcement conclusions. AHP may harden the analysis artefact where precision, excerptability, publicness, attack surfaces, or claim-boundary risks require it. AHP does not rescore, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or create new findings.

### 10.4 Cases

#### 10.4.1 Consent Under Asymmetry

##### Why this case is here

`Consent Under Asymmetry` is included because it concentrates one of AXIOM’s most difficult closure-demands: the demand to decide whether agreement counts as meaningful consent when agreement, refusal, pressure, dependency, vulnerability, or unequal capacity may be structurally entangled. Consent seems to invite a clean yes/no closure. Either consent was given or it was not. Either refusal was possible or it was not. Either pressure matters or it does not. Yet this case shows why that closure can become too quick.

The difficulty is not merely that consent can be ambiguous. The deeper difficulty is that agreement, refusal, and contestation may belong to different structural layers. A person may formally say yes while the surrounding frame makes refusal costly. A person may be vulnerable without losing agency. A person may be dependent without being incapacitated. A person may later contest a scene without thereby proving that the original agreement was invalid. Conversely, the absence of later contestation does not prove meaningful consent. The case therefore belongs in AXIOM because it asks for a map of the closure-demand rather than a verdict machine.

The case is a conceptual projection, not a concrete scene. That has strong consequences. PMS can structurally render the field of consent under asymmetry, but it cannot assign scene-specific responsibility, action, maturity, self-binding, or validity without additional evidence. PMS-SEX can make sexuality-specific calibration requirements visible if the case is later concretized as sexual praxis, but it cannot sexualize the abstract case by itself. MIP can clarify why maturity-in-practice language must remain non-scoring here, but it cannot evaluate persons. AHP can harden the analysis artefact against over-transmission, but it cannot turn a working note into evidence.

This is why the case is mapped rather than solved. It shows where closure is structurally tempting, where closure may be possible only after additional reconstruction, and where non-capture must remain visible.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement begins with the consent field itself. The case activates Δ (Difference), because it requires distinctions between agreement and meaningful consent, refusal and silence, pressure and coercion, dependency and incapacity, vulnerability and lack of agency, unequal capacity and ordinary difference, and later contestation and proof of invalidity. It activates ∇ (Impulse), because the field contains directional tensions: the movement toward agreement, refusal, compliance, withdrawal, pressure-response, clarification, or contestation. It requires □ (Frame), because consent becomes legible only inside a frame that defines what counts as consent, refusal, pressure, capacity, vulnerability, dependency, or contestation.

Λ (Non-event / residue) remains conditional. It would matter if a later concrete scene showed meaningful silence, absent opportunity to refuse, missing confirmation, delayed withdrawal, or an omitted safeguard inside an expectation structure. But absence of concrete evidence is not itself a PMS non-event. Α (Attractor) is also conditional. It would matter if pressure, dependency, compliance, refusal difficulty, or frame-control stabilized into a recurring pattern. The abstract case does not establish recurrence.

Ω (Asymmetry) is the central operator. The case explicitly concerns unequal power, exposure, dependency, vulnerability, capacity, obligation, role authority, or exit access. Yet Ω must be calibrated rather than moralized. Asymmetry is not automatically coercion, abuse, invalidity, guilt, or blame. It is a structural imbalance that requires reconstruction: what kind of asymmetry, in which direction, under which frame, and with which consequences?

Θ (Temporality) is structurally necessary but under-specified. Consent may be given, withheld, delayed, revised, withdrawn, pressured, repeated, or contested over time. A moment of agreement may not exhaust the consent trajectory. Dependency may accumulate; exit may become more costly; publicness may sediment; later contestation may reframe an earlier scene. But no chronology is supplied, so Θ cannot become a concrete finding.

Φ (Recontextualization) is active because the same surface agreement may be read as meaningful consent, compliance, pressure-response, dependency-preserving action, constrained refusal, ambiguity, or later-contested enactment. This is one of the case’s main structural tensions. Yet Φ must not become an absorption device. Resistance, disagreement, rupture, non-fit, or rival framing must not automatically be redescribed as recontextualization inside PMS.

Χ (Distance) is the main guard. It keeps the analysis from fusing into verdict, diagnosis, legal conclusion, sexualization, public accusation, person-ranking, or maturity judgment. Χ does not mean empty neutrality. It means reflective restraint: the analysis may discriminate sharply, but it may not close more than the case material supports.

Σ (Integration) remains conditional. The case does not show that agreement, refusal, pressure, dependency, vulnerability, capacity, frame, temporality, and asymmetry have been integrated into a coherent enactment. The analyst may produce a coherent structural reading, but that coherence is not proof that the scene itself is integrated. Ψ (Self-binding) is likewise conditional for the scene. Agreement alone is not self-binding. Abstract consent language is not self-binding. A self-binding consent structure would require integrated, temporally extended, reflectively held commitment; the case does not supply that.

The derived structures follow from this. Awareness_A is weak: the analysis can sustain distinctions, but no participant awareness is established. Coherence_C is weak: the conceptual frame is coherent, but no scene-level trajectory or structured absence is shown. Responsibility_R is relevant but not attributable: asymmetry may generate responsibility questions, but no role-holder responsibility can be assigned. Action_E is under-specified: no integrated realization of directedness across time is supplied. Dignity_in_Practice_D is active only as a praxeological application constraint: the analysis must not shame, rank, diagnose, or fix persons under asymmetry. SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only: no selfhood, identity, autobiographical continuity, or stable self-binding is derived from the abstract case.

PMS drift-patterns remain risks, not findings. AD_A>>E is relevant as a possible risk where awareness or reflective comprehension grows while effective action is suppressed or delayed, but no such distribution is shown. AD_Sigma_low is relevant where recontextualization outruns integration, producing unstable coherence or responsibility, but the case does not establish such drift. Under-specification is therefore the responsible result, not a low score.

PMS-SEX is added only as an overlay because consent under asymmetry may, if later specified as sexual praxis, require visibility for body remainder, publicness, exit cost, sexual cost topology, asymmetry-switch drift, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, and adult sexual reciprocity. PMS-SEX does not decide that this case is sexual. It asks what would have to be reconstructed if it were. In that conditional sense, sexual cost topology is the central overlay handle: which bodily, temporal, exposure-related, reputational, binding-related, exit-related, publicness-related, and responsibility-related costs attach where, and which burdens may be externalized under parity or autonomy language? Here, that topology is not mapped as a finding. It remains a required later calibration interface.

The active PMS-SEX reduced signatures are the frame signature, recontextualization signature, distance signature, and Dignity-in-Practice application guard. Weak signatures include sexual cost topology, temporality, exit realism, suspended self-binding, integration, self-binding, and adult sexual reciprocity. Not applicable signatures include deviance and high-risk practice structures, because the case material does not describe such scenes. PMS-SEX drift handles such as asymmetry-switch drift, parity-to-exit, and responsibility-to-authority remain risk lenses only.

MIP adds no score. The case-specific MIP result is non-assignment: no A-score, no M-score, no IA verdict, no D-profile, no adultness verdict, no maturity ranking, and no person finding. This is not an omission. It follows from the abstract status of the case. Role-position, knowledge, power-to-act, predictability, alternatives, serious integration attempts, transparency, justification, time-boundedness, reversibility, and safe conditions for D-module activation are not supplied.

AHP adds second-order hardening of the MIP output. Its case-specific result is that the analysis artefact is precise as a working note but vulnerable to authority-misreading. High precision does not mean evidentiary strength. Formal completeness does not make the analysis transmissible as a decision basis. AHP therefore protects the article against a specific misuse: treating structured non-closure as if it were a hidden verdict.

##### What the analysis shows

The central structural movement can be stated as:

Χ (Distance) governing Φ (Recontextualization) of a Θ (Temporality)-under-specified Ω (Asymmetry) inside a □ (Frame) structured by ∇ (Impulse) and Δ (Difference), while Λ (Non-event / residue), Α (Attractor), Σ (Integration), and Ψ (Self-binding) remain conditional.

This formula does not summarize a finding about actual consent. It summarizes the shape of the closure-demand. PMS Core shows that agreement under asymmetry cannot be read without distinguishing frame, pressure, dependency, vulnerability, temporality, and recontextualization. PMS-SEX shows that, in a later sexual-praxis specification, cost topology and publicness would have to be reconstructed before stronger claims could be made. MIP shows that maturity and responsibility language cannot be scored from the current material. AHP shows that the analysis itself must not be transmitted as authority.

The case permits structural closure only at the level of mapping. It is possible to say that Ω (Asymmetry) is central, that Θ (Temporality) is needed but under-specified, that Φ (Recontextualization) is active, that Χ (Distance) is mandatory, and that Σ (Integration) and Ψ (Self-binding) cannot be cheaply asserted. It is not possible to say whether consent was valid, invalid, coerced, absent, authentic, inauthentic, mature, immature, legally operative, clinically impaired, or morally blameworthy.

Λ-residue remains visible in two ways. First, the abstract case leaves missing scene data: no actors, no chronology, no words, no evidence, no exit channel, no publicness, no body or cost topology. Second, a later concrete scene may contain meaningful absences: silence, missing refusal opportunities, absent safeguards, delayed withdrawal, or unspoken expectations. The analysis must not collapse these two. Missing data for the analyst is not automatically a PMS non-event in the scene.

Rival readings remain open. A legal framework may ask about coercion, undue influence, validity, capacity, or evidence. A phenomenological account may ask about lived experience, fear, hesitation, desire, or felt agency. A relational autonomy account may ask how dependency and agency co-constitute each other. A care-ethical reading may ask what responsibility under vulnerability requires. A political or domination analysis may ask how social structures shape consent conditions. These rival readings are not defects in the PMS analysis; they preserve dimensions that a structural operator grammar may only partially render. PMS can map the operator field, but it does not exhaust these rival framings.

The case is weakened if PMS collapses distinctions, if PMS-SEX sexualizes the abstract material, if MIP scoring appears despite absent scene data, or if AHP precision is mistaken for evidence. The reading would require revision if a concrete scene showed no relevant asymmetry, no meaningful consent issue, a different primary frame, or a rival framework with stronger discriminative force.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk in this case is double closure. One closure says that agreement settles consent. The other says that asymmetry settles non-consent. The case rejects both closures. Agreement matters but does not settle the case. Asymmetry matters but does not settle the case.

A second distinctive risk is sexual overreach. PMS-SEX is relevant only because a consent-under-asymmetry case may later require sexual-praxis calibration. It does not make the present abstract case sexual. Sexual cost topology, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, asymmetry-switch drift, and adult sexual reciprocity remain overlay handles, not PMS operators and not findings.

A third risk is maturity overreach. MIP is active only as a working-note layer. Non-assignment is the result: no score, no D-profile, no IA verdict, no adultness or maturity finding. This non-assignment is not a low score.

A fourth risk is artefact authority. AHP shows that a careful, formal analysis can become misleading if transmitted as if it were a decision-ready assessment. In this case, the analysis must remain a map of what would have to be known before closure, not closure itself.

##### Example 1 — Formal agreement in a dependency frame

This worked example illustrates the PMS Core problem before any add-on is allowed to carry extra weight. It is not introduced as evidence for the case; it functions as a bounded demonstration of how formal agreement, dependency, asymmetry, and later contestation must be held apart.

A junior researcher is asked by the director of a small institute to present preliminary findings at a public event. The request is framed as an opportunity: the director says it would be “good exposure” and adds that “of course, it is your choice.” The researcher says yes. No threat is made. No explicit penalty is mentioned. The director does not say that funding, recommendation letters, future contracts, or access to data depend on acceptance. Still, the researcher is on a short contract, depends on the director’s recommendation, and knows that declining visible requests has previously made others seem “not committed.” Later, when the presentation receives criticism, the researcher says the agreement did not feel meaningfully free.

This example demonstrates why the case cannot be closed by the presence of agreement alone. The “yes” is structurally relevant, but it is not sufficient by itself to settle whether consent was meaningfully given. At the same time, the dependency structure does not automatically prove coercion, invalidity, exploitation, or blame. The point is not that the director acted wrongly, nor that the researcher lacked agency. The point is that consent under asymmetry requires more discrimination than the surface contrast between yes and no.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes agreement from meaningful consent, opportunity from pressure, dependency from incapacity, and criticism after the fact from proof that the original agreement was invalid. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as directional tension: the director’s request, the researcher’s wish to remain in good standing, the pull of career opportunity, and the possible impulse to avoid refusal. **□ (Frame)** is the institutional research frame: contracts, recommendations, reputation, visibility, and implicit expectations shape what the agreement means. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** remains conditional. If the institute had an expected refusal channel and none was offered, or if silence around consequences carried meaning, Λ could become relevant. But absence of an explicit threat is not automatically a PMS non-event. **Α (Attractor)** is also conditional: prior cases of people being treated as “not committed” may suggest a possible attractor, but the vignette does not give enough evidence to establish a stabilized pattern.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is active because the relation includes dependency, role authority, and possible future costs. Yet Ω must be calibrated. The asymmetry may be real without being coercive in a legal sense. **Θ (Temporality)** is relevant because the agreement cannot be read only at the instant of saying yes: prior institute culture, contract duration, future recommendations, public criticism, and later contestation all matter. But the example still does not provide enough chronology for a full temporal trajectory. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is active because the same event can be recontextualized: as opportunity, institutional expectation, pressure-response, professional development, or contested consent. **Χ (Distance)** is required to prevent the analysis from becoming accusation or exoneration. **Σ (Integration)** remains conditional: we do not yet know whether the request, dependency, refusal options, and later criticism were integrated into a coherent institutional practice. **Ψ (Self-binding)** cannot be inferred from the researcher’s yes. Saying yes to the presentation is not automatically a self-binding commitment to absorb all future consequences.

No add-on hook is necessary for PMS-SEX here unless the example is later moved into a domain involving sexual praxis, bodily exposure, sexual cost topology, or public intimacy. As written, it is deliberately non-sexual. This helps the article show that consent under asymmetry is structurally broader than sexual consent while still preparing the reader for why PMS-SEX may later become relevant in domain-specific contexts.

MIP status remains working-note only. The vignette does not warrant an A-score, M-score, IA verdict, D-profile, adultness judgment, or maturity ranking. The IA-box would ask whether the asymmetry was transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible: did the researcher know the real stakes, was the request justified by a legitimate institutional purpose, was refusal reviewable, and were exit or repair options available? But these questions are not yet answered. The AHP relevance is indirect: a formal write-up of this vignette could easily sound more decisive than the evidence allows, so any analysis artefact would need to mark what is known, inferred, and unknown.

The forbidden claims are clear. This example does not prove coercion. It does not prove valid consent. It does not diagnose dependency, fear, ambition, immaturity, or manipulation. It does not make a legal or HR finding. It does not rank the director or researcher. It does not show that asymmetry always invalidates agreement. It also does not show that formal agreement is enough.

The non-capture note is important. The researcher’s lived experience may not be captured by the structural map. Legal standards may treat the case differently. Institutional ethics may require additional inquiry. A labor-rights frame, a relational autonomy frame, or an organizational culture analysis may preserve aspects that PMS only marks as requiring calibration.

The article takeaway is that PMS Core makes the consent field more legible without closing it. The example shows why Δ, Ω, Θ, Φ, and Χ carry major weight, and why Σ and Ψ must remain conditional. The analysis becomes stronger by refusing the two easy closures: “they said yes, so consent is settled,” and “there was dependency, so consent is impossible.”

##### Example 2 — Conditional sexual cost topology without a sexual finding

This worked example illustrates why PMS-SEX is used as a conditional overlay rather than as a sexualizing interpretation. It demonstrates how sexual cost topology could become relevant in a later concrete scene while remaining unavailable as a finding in the present abstract case.

Two adults are in a private relationship. One suggests making a private intimate exchange more visible within a small social circle, not by sharing explicit material, but by allowing others to know that the relationship has a non-standard dynamic. The other agrees at the time. Both describe the decision as mutual. Later, one partner says that the agreement carried different consequences for each of them: one could treat the disclosure as playful and reversible, while the other faced reputational exposure, family pressure, and a much higher cost of withdrawal. The original discussion did not name those different costs.

This example is intentionally bounded. It does not describe explicit sexual content, does not provide arousal-oriented detail, and does not instruct anyone how to manage such a situation. It is included only to show why PMS-SEX may be relevant as an overlay when consent under asymmetry becomes entangled with intimacy, publicness, role visibility, and unequal consequence distribution. The example does not turn the article’s abstract case into a sexual case; rather, it demonstrates what would have to be reconstructed if a concrete sexual-praxis scene were supplied.

The PMS Core hooks remain primary. **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes mutual agreement from equal cost, private meaning from public readability, and disclosure from consent to all future consequences. **∇ (Impulse)** appears in the pull toward visibility, recognition, play, intimacy, or shared identity, as well as the later pull toward withdrawal or contestation. **□ (Frame)** is the relationship frame, but it begins to extend toward a publicness frame once others become aware. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may become relevant if certain expected safeguards were absent: no explicit discussion of reputational cost, no later review point, no withdrawal pathway, no clarification of what “mutual” covers. But these absences must be established as expectations within the frame; they cannot simply be assumed. **Α (Attractor)** would require recurrence: repeated disclosures, repeated pressure, or a stabilized role-script. The vignette gives a possible pattern but not enough to assert one.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is active because consequences may distribute asymmetrically. One role-position may bear higher reputational cost, family cost, exit cost, or publicness cost. **Θ (Temporality)** is active because the effects of disclosure may sediment over time: what seemed reversible in private may become difficult to undo once others know. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is central: the same agreement may be recontextualized from “mutual playful disclosure” to “unequal public exposure” or “unmapped cost topology.” **Χ (Distance)** is necessary to avoid accusation, moral panic, or dismissal. **Σ (Integration)** remains conditional: the question is whether internal meaning, outer legibility, publicness, and cost distribution were integrated. **Ψ (Self-binding)** cannot be inferred from the initial agreement. Consent to one disclosure does not automatically bind a person to all later public interpretations.

The PMS-SEX hooks are the reason for the example. The overlay does not add new PMS operators. It sharpens the question of **sexual cost topology**: which costs attach where, which are reversible, which sediment under Θ, which are publicness-related, which are reputational, which are exit-related, and which can be externalized through mutuality language. The example also illustrates **inner / outer / public layer separation**. Internally, the disclosure may have been framed as trust, play, intimacy, or mutual recognition. Externally, it may become readable as a marked relationship form. Publicly, even a small social circle may create reputational sedimentation. These layers overlap but must not be collapsed.

The overlay also shows why **parity-to-exit** is a risk lens, not a finding. If one partner says “we both agreed, so no one owes anything,” parity language could potentially function as an exit from responsibility under unequal cost topology. But the example does not establish that this happened. The phrase “mutual” may be sincere, partially accurate, or insufficiently calibrated. Similarly, **responsibility-to-authority** is only a risk: if one partner later says, “because I was protecting you, I get to decide how this is interpreted,” responsibility may drift toward frame control. But that is not stated in the vignette. **Asymmetry-switch drift** also remains a risk only: it would require unmarked, repeatable asymmetry activation without Σ/Ψ re-binding.

MIP status remains non-scoring. No A-band or M-band should be assigned. The example does not show what either person knew, could predict, could refuse, could revise, or could realistically exit. The IA-box would ask whether the publicness asymmetry was transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. Were the reputational costs named? Was the disclosure bounded? Was later withdrawal possible? Was there a review point? These are necessary questions, not answered findings. D-module activation remains inappropriate unless the scene is safely bounded and D-language would clarify rather than shame.

AHP status is relevant because the example is high-risk for over-reading. A polished analysis could easily sound like it is judging the relationship. AHP would require a warning: this example is an analysis of structural conditions, not evidence of wrongdoing, immaturity, coercion, or dignity violation.

Forbidden claims must be stated plainly. The example does not say the disclosure was wrong. It does not deny that consent may have been real. It does not say that non-standard intimacy is immature, pathological, deviant, or undignified. It does not classify identities, orientations, or practices. It does not make a legal or clinical claim. It does not treat publicness as public punishment license. It does not turn sexual cost topology into a score.

The non-capture note is substantial. The partners’ lived meanings may exceed the structural map. A queer-theoretical, relational, legal, cultural, or phenomenological account might interpret the disclosure differently. Internal framing may be real even where it is not total. Publicness may matter without being the origin of the scene. The overlay should not erase erotic meaning, relational trust, cultural context, or agency.

The article takeaway is that PMS-SEX is useful precisely when it remains conditional. It does not decide the scene. It shows what must be visible before consent under sexualized asymmetry can be responsibly interpreted: not only agreement, but cost topology, exit realism, publicness, temporal sedimentation, frame control, and non-externalization of unequal burdens.

##### Example 3 — When a careful analysis artefact becomes too authoritative

This worked example illustrates the AHP layer by shifting attention from the underlying consent problem to the analysis artefact itself. It shows why a careful, formally complete working note can still require protection against over-transmission and authority-misreading.

A research group prepares a structured internal note about a disputed consent situation. The note is careful: it says no legal conclusion is reached, no diagnosis is made, no A-score or M-score is assigned, and the D-module is not activated. It repeatedly states that the material is under-specified. It marks the output as a working note. Nevertheless, because the note is long, formally organized, and written in technical language, a manager later forwards it as if it were an expert assessment. The receiving committee reads the structure as authority. Even without explicit scores, the presence of headings such as asymmetry, responsibility, dignity protection, and drift risks creates the impression that the situation has already been evaluated.

This example demonstrates why the AHP layer matters. It is not about the original consent case. It is about the analysis artefact itself. Even a well-guarded MIP working note can become risky when its form is mistaken for decision-readiness. Formal precision can create authority-pressure. A structured document can be misused even when its content says “do not use this as a verdict.”

The PMS Core hooks are indirect but important. **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes analysis precision from evidentiary strength, working note from decision basis, structural risk from finding, and under-specification from low score. **∇ (Impulse)** appears in the institutional pull toward closure: the committee wants a usable assessment, the manager wants a document that organizes uncertainty, and the technical form seems to move the case toward decision. **□ (Frame)** is the transmission frame: the same note means one thing inside an internal analytic workspace and something else when forwarded to a committee. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may become relevant if the note lacks a prominent non-transmission banner or if review conditions are expected but absent. **Α (Attractor)** could become relevant if such notes are repeatedly reused as quasi-verdicts. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is active because document authority, expertise, managerial power, and affected-party vulnerability may be unevenly distributed. **Θ (Temporality)** matters because the note’s effects may sediment: once forwarded, it may influence records, reputations, or decisions. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is central because the artefact is recontextualized from working note to institutional evidence. **Χ (Distance)** is the needed restraint: pause before transmission, preserve context, and prevent the document from becoming a closure machine. **Σ (Integration)** would require integration of evidence status, use boundary, review process, and institutional decision rules. **Ψ (Self-binding)** would require the institution to bind itself to the note’s limits rather than merely cite them.

PMS-SEX may be relevant only if the underlying case involved sexual praxis, but this example does not require that. Its main add-on relevance is negative: if PMS-SEX terms such as sexual cost topology, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, or adult sexual reciprocity appeared in the note, they would need especially strong boundary language. Such terms are rhetorically powerful. A committee could misread them as findings even when marked as risk lenses. The overlay’s guardrails therefore reinforce the AHP concern: technical handles must not become accusation, identity classification, or maturity verdict.

MIP status is the center of the example. The note should remain a working note. It should not assign A-score or M-score. It should not activate D-profile. It should not issue IA verdict. It should not become adultness judgment, responsibility finding, disciplinary recommendation, HR assessment, legal advice, clinical interpretation, or public communication. If transmitted at all, it would require explicit review conditions, scope explanation, and domain separation.

AHP status is fully active in this example, but only as analysis-artifact hardening. AHP would identify attack points: working-note status may be lost in reuse; formal precision may be mistaken for evidence; unknown may be mistaken for known absent; technical risk handles may be over-read as findings; D-language may invite dignity misreadings; and the document’s completeness may create institutional authority-pressure. The hardening actions would include a top-level non-transmission banner, a distinction between analysis precision and evidentiary strength, explicit “risk lens not finding” notices, staged evidence requirements before any IA or MIP assessment, and a rule that the document cannot be used as a decision basis without further review.

The forbidden claims are the lesson. The example does not prove that structured analysis is harmful. It does not say institutions should avoid formal notes. It does not say that careful models are manipulative. It does not diagnose managers or committees. It does not infer bad faith. It does not rank the analyst, manager, or committee. It does not make any legal claim about process. It shows only that an analysis artefact has its own transmission risks.

The non-capture note is also important. Institutional practice may have legal requirements, procedural duties, risk-management obligations, or safeguarding responsibilities that PMS, MIP, and AHP do not replace. A committee may need to act under uncertainty. AHP does not prohibit action; it prevents a working note from being misrepresented as stronger than it is. Other frameworks may govern what must happen next.

The article takeaway is that precision is not innocence. A formally precise analysis can still become dangerous if transmitted outside its scope. The AHP layer therefore protects the case, the affected parties, and the model itself: it keeps structural analysis from becoming hidden adjudication, and it keeps under-specification visible rather than allowing the document’s form to close what its content leaves open.

##### Case takeaway

`Consent Under Asymmetry` shows that AXIOM can map a powerful closure-demand without becoming a closure machine. The case does not ask whether agreement is valid or invalid in a particular scene. It asks what has to remain visible before such a question can be responsibly answered.

PMS Core shows that consent under asymmetry is structurally organized by distinctions, impulses, frames, possible absences, possible patterns, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, conditional integration, and conditional self-binding. PMS-SEX shows that a later sexual-praxis specification would require cost topology, publicness, body remainder, exit realism, and non-externalization of unequal burdens. MIP shows that maturity language must remain non-scoring and non-verdictive here. AHP shows that the analysis artefact itself must be protected from becoming authority.

The case should be used in the chapter as a disciplined example of non-closure. It preserves the central tension between surface agreement and suspicion closure. It shows why risk is not finding, why coherence is not proof, why non-assignment is not low score, and why non-capture is not failure. The remaining Λ (Non-event / residue) appears as the missing scene itself: no actors, chronology, evidence, role-position, publicness, exit channel, or cost topology is supplied. #### 10.4.2 Consent Laundering

##### Why this case is here

Consent Laundering is included because it exposes a particularly sharp closure-demand: the demand that an apparent act of consent should close inquiry into the conditions that made that consent meaningful. The case concerns situations in which a consent-sign is later used to legitimate, sanitize, or obscure the conditions under which it was obtained, framed, or invoked. Its structural difficulty lies in the fact that consent is both an act-like marker and a frame-dependent relation. A statement, checkbox, signature, gesture, continued participation, or agreement may function as a consent-sign, but that sign does not automatically carry all the conditions that make it intelligible.

The case therefore belongs in AXIOM as a map of closure pressure rather than as a solved dispute. It asks how an apparent consent-act can become a later warrant: the matter is settled, the frame is clean, further questions are unnecessary, and any challenge appears to reopen what has already been agreed. This is the distinctive closure-demand. It is not simply “was there consent?” but “what happens when consent is used to close the question of its own frame?”

The case is marked as a conceptual projection rather than a concrete scene. No actors, institutions, body-consequence records, publicness records, legal records, role-specific sequences, or documented consent events are supplied. This matters because responsibility, action, asymmetry, and self-binding can be structurally rendered, but they must not become scene-specific findings without additional evidence. The case can show how a closure structure would work; it cannot say that it occurred.

This case also matters because it resists two opposing shortcuts. One shortcut says: consent occurred, therefore the matter is closed. The other says: consent is later invoked, therefore laundering occurred. Both are too crude. Ordinary consent continuity remains possible. A later invocation can be legitimate, bounded, transparent, and continuous with the original frame. Conversely, the presence of a consent-sign does not by itself settle scope, alternatives, disclosure, revisability, exposure, publicness, cost distribution, or later use. The case is mapped because these distinctions must remain visible.

##### Structural reading

At PMS Core level, the case begins with **Δ (Difference)**: the difference between apparent consent and the conditions under which that consent was obtained, framed, or later invoked. This is the root distinction. Consent as sign, consent as framed meaning, consent as later warrant, legal validity, moral legitimacy, and subjective experience must not be collapsed. The case does not begin by deciding whether consent is valid or invalid. It begins by refusing to treat the consent-sign and its conditions as identical.

From this difference, **∇ (Impulse)** appears as a structural pull toward closure. Apparent consent can orient the scene toward legitimation: “consent was given,” therefore the frame appears settled. This is not a motive claim. The analysis does not infer manipulation, bad faith, desire to obscure, or psychological intention. It identifies only a directional use: the consent-sign can be made to do closure-work.

**□ (Frame)** is central because consent becomes meaningful only inside a frame. The frame defines what counts as consent, what scope the consent has, what alternatives existed, which conditions mattered, whether refusal or revision were available, and how later invocation may remain continuous with the original scene. Consent Laundering becomes visible when the consent-sign is moved from a source frame into a target frame that changes its force. The source frame is the setting in which the consent was obtained or made meaningful. The target frame is the later setting in which consent is invoked as legitimacy.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is active but calibration-sensitive. Consent Laundering may involve missing disclosure, missing alternatives, missing refusal-space, missing revisability, missing scope limitation, missing acknowledgement of constraints, or missing continuity between original consent and later invocation. These absences matter only where the frame made them expected. Λ (Non-event / residue) is not “anything absent.” It is structured absence inside a frame. Silence, missing evidence, rupture, refusal, disagreement, or non-fit must not automatically become PMS-legible residue.

**Α (Attractor)** remains weak. The case names a phenomenon-class, so repetition is possible, but no repeated pattern is supplied. Α (Attractor) would become stronger only if a concrete scene or institutional pattern showed repeated consent-framing, routine later invocation, role-script stabilization, or path dependence. The concept is intelligible without proving a stabilized pattern.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is conditionally relevant but not established. Consent laundering often becomes structurally serious when one position controls framing, evidence, interpretation, later invocation, publicness, or exit conditions, while another bears exposure, dependency, reputational sedimentation, or cost. But the present case does not specify who holds power, who bears cost, who can refuse, who can revise, who can invoke consent later, or who can contest the frame. Ω (Asymmetry) remains a calibration question, not a finding.

**Θ (Temporality)** is active. The structure explicitly spans time: consent is obtained or framed at one point and invoked later at another. This temporal displacement is not incidental. A consent-sign may travel from an original frame into a later legitimating context. The later invocation may preserve the original scope, in which case the laundering reading weakens, or it may recontextualize the earlier sign so that relevant conditions disappear.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is the central operator of the case. Consent Laundering occurs as a candidate structure where apparent consent is placed into a later or wider frame that legitimates, sanitizes, or obscures the conditions of its own production. Yet Φ (Recontextualization) must be calibrated carefully. Not every later interpretation is laundering. Recontextualization can clarify scope, preserve continuity, or make the original frame more explicit. It becomes laundering-relevant only where the new frame erases or sanitizes conditions that remain structurally material.

**Χ (Distance)** is the main application constraint. It prevents the analysis from fusing with verdict, accusation, legal conclusion, sexual diagnosis, maturity judgment, or moral closure. It also prevents apparent consent from automatically closing inquiry. Χ (Distance) does not deny that laundering can occur; it prevents the analysis from claiming it too quickly. In this case, distance is not neutral indifference. It is the condition that allows the consent-sign and its conditions to remain separately readable.

**Σ (Integration)** is weak and withheld as a completed structure. A coherent story may say: consent occurred, therefore the later invocation is legitimate. But such coherence may be pseudo-integration if it excludes scope, alternatives, missing conditions, asymmetry, publicness, or temporal change. Genuine Σ (Integration) would require holding apparent consent, frame, absences, asymmetry, temporality, later invocation, and rival readings together without smoothing over residue. The case does not show that.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is not active as a case finding. The material supplies no self-binding subject, stable commitment, role obligation, institution, or responsibility-as-self-commitment. Ψ (Self-binding) remains relevant only as application discipline: the analysis itself must remain bound by its own limits. It must not use structural elegance to become authority.

The main PMS movement may therefore be rendered as follows: a consent-sign is distinguished from its conditions; it is framed; possible residue is preserved; recurrence and asymmetry remain conditional; temporality makes later invocation structurally important; recontextualization becomes the central risk; distance prevents verdict; integration and self-binding are withheld unless a concrete scene supplies the missing conditions.

The PMS-SEX overlay sharpens the case only conditionally. It does not sexualize the Core case merely because consent is mentioned. It asks what would become visible if Consent Laundering were instantiated in sexual praxis: internal framing, outer legibility, publicness, body remainder, sexual cost topology, exit realism, parity-to-exit, asymmetry-switch drift, and responsibility-to-authority. These are overlay handles, not PMS operators. In the present case they remain mostly weak, risk-only, or not applicable because no concrete sexual scene is supplied.

The most relevant PMS-SEX signatures are the non-event signature, the recontextualization signature, the distance signature, and the sexual cost topology signature as a future reconstruction requirement. The non-event signature tracks missing disclosure, missing revisability, missing explicit cost topology, or missing asymmetry marking where a sexual scene would make these expectations relevant. The recontextualization signature tracks how apparent consent can be moved into a later legitimating frame. The distance signature prevents sexual overlay language from becoming accusation. The sexual cost topology signature asks, only if a sexual scene exists, which bodily, exposure-related, reputational, binding-related, exit-related, publicness-related, and responsibility-related costs attach where.

The PMS-SEX drift risks remain risks, not findings. Φ (Recontextualization) narrative inflation is the strongest risk: a later consent narrative may become too smooth while conditions disappear. Pseudo-symmetry is a risk where consent-language produces a parity surface that hides unequal costs. Parity-to-exit is a risk where “it was mutual” or “there was consent” functions to exit further responsibility under unequal cost distribution. Asymmetry-switch drift is a risk where parity is claimed for autonomy or access while asymmetry is activated for protection, exposure, cost, or responsibility. Responsibility-to-authority is not shown in this case.

MIP is relevant only as a downstream non-scoring result. The case cannot support an A-score because there is no concrete enactment, role-context-time window, or actor-level awareness evidence. It cannot support an M-score because there is no role-holder, knowledge profile, power-to-act profile, predictability profile, access-to-alternatives profile, or serious integration-attempt record. The IA-box cannot return a verdict because asymmetry cannot be tested for transparency, justification, time-boundedness, or reversibility. The D-module remains off because dignity language without concrete protective context would create more risk than clarity.

This MIP non-result is not a failure. It is a case-specific result. Non-assignment is not a low score. Working-note status is not a verdict. No person finding follows. MIP blocks a common misuse of structural analysis: once the PMS and PMS-SEX readings become coherent, one might want to translate coherence into responsibility, maturity, adultness, or dignity language. MIP refuses that move here.

AHP adds only analysis-artifact hardening. It does not rescore, does not activate D, does not change the IA result, and does not upgrade the case into a transmission-ready assessment. Its case-specific contribution is to identify risks in the analysis text: formal completeness could make a working note look more final than it is; “not scored” could be misread as low maturity; high precision could be misread as case certainty; evidence thresholds could be consolidated more visibly; rival readings could be linked more directly to falsifiers; dignity language remains vocabulary-sensitive; and export context remains a publicness risk. These are hardening points for the artefact, not new claims about the case.

##### What the analysis shows

The analysis shows that Consent Laundering is structurally strongest where a consent-sign is detached from the frame that made it meaningful and then invoked later as closure. The central movement is not “consent is false,” but “consent becomes a closure-token when its acquisition conditions, scope, alternatives, absences, or later recontextualization are no longer examinable.”

PMS Core makes visible the structural chain: **Δ (Difference)** between consent-sign and consent-conditions; **□ (Frame)** as the condition of consent’s meaning; **Λ (Non-event / residue)** as possible missing disclosure, revisability, scope, or acknowledgement; **Θ (Temporality)** as the movement from original consent to later invocation; **Φ (Recontextualization)** as the relocation of consent into a legitimating frame; and **Χ (Distance)** as the restraint that prevents the analysis from becoming a verdict. **Σ (Integration)** and **Ψ (Self-binding)** remain withheld unless stronger scene evidence is supplied.

PMS-SEX adds conditional visibility for sexual-praxis cases, but does not add a finding. It shows that if the case is concretely sexual, analysis would need to reconstruct internal framing, outer legibility, publicness, body remainder, sexual cost topology, exit realism, parity-to-exit, and asymmetry-switch risk. The overlay’s value is that it prevents “mutuality” or “consent” language from automatically settling cost distribution. Its boundary is equally important: it must not become sexual diagnosis, identity taxonomy, maturity verdict, or person ranking.

MIP adds the refusal of assignment. The case is too abstract for A-score, M-score, IA classification, or D-module activation. This refusal is a methodological result: the case can be mapped, but not scored. AHP adds the hardening result: the analysis is precise, but its precision must not be mistaken for case certainty.

The closure possible here is therefore limited. The article can close on the structural form of the problem: consent can become laundering when later invocation detaches the consent-sign from the conditions that make it meaningful. It cannot close on whether any actual consent was laundered. The remaining Λ (Non-event / residue) is not a defect of the analysis. It is the object the analysis must keep visible.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk in this case is that the phrase “Consent Laundering” can itself become a laundering device. It can make suspicion look settled. It can make structural possibility sound like moral finding. It can make later consent invocation appear guilty by default. For that reason, the case requires especially strong Χ (Distance): consent later invoked is not automatically laundering; missing conditions are not automatically structured absence; asymmetry possibility is not coercion; coherence is not proof.

A second misuse risk arises from the PMS-SEX overlay. Because consent is a central sexual-praxis term, the overlay can easily be overread. In this case, PMS-SEX must not sexualize a non-sexual scene, must not treat sexual cost topology as present without evidence, must not treat parity-to-exit as a person label, and must not treat adult sexual reciprocity as an adultness verdict. It can sharpen future questions only where a concrete sexual scene supplies the needed data.

A third misuse risk arises from MIP and AHP. MIP non-scoring must not be read as low maturity or low responsibility. AHP precision must not be read as case certainty. The downstream layers harden the analysis and preserve non-assignment; they do not intensify the case into a verdict.

The main weakening condition is straightforward: if a concrete case shows transparent continuity between original consent-frame and later invocation, with no relevant obscured condition, no frame shift, no meaningful absence, no unmarked asymmetry, and real reversibility, the laundering reading weakens or fails. Ordinary consent continuity must remain a live rival reading.

Other rival readings also remain available. A formal-procedural reading may treat consent as valid because explicit procedural requirements were satisfied. A legal reading may decide validity, scope, waiver, coercion, or evidentiary sufficiency through standards external to PMS. An ethical autonomy reading may focus on informed consent, exploitation, manipulation, or respect. A discourse-theoretic reading may focus on recognition, contestability, uptake, and mutual justification. A phenomenological or trauma-informed reading may preserve lived experience, embodiment, fear, hesitation, shame, delayed recognition, or survival strategies that PMS does not claim to capture. Feminist, queer, cultural, or institutional readings may also preserve dimensions of power, sexuality, publicness, and normativity that remain only partially visible in the PMS / PMS-SEX rendering. These rival readings are not defects in the case analysis; they mark the non-capture zones that the case must keep open.

##### Example 1 — The consent checkbox as later closure

This example illustrates the basic source-frame / target-frame structure without activating the sexual overlay and without turning the vignette into a legal or institutional finding.

A person joins an online research platform. Before participating, they click a consent checkbox. The form says that their responses may be used “for research and quality improvement.” Months later, a dispute arises because excerpts from participant responses are used in a public-facing promotional report. The platform points to the original checkbox and says that consent was given. The participant argues that they understood the form as permission for internal research use, not public promotional use.

This example is bounded. It does not claim that the platform acted unlawfully, that the participant was deceived, that the consent was invalid, or that the later report was impermissible. It shows only how a consent-sign can travel from one frame into another. The checkbox is not structurally identical with the full consent situation. It belongs to a source frame: platform participation, research, and quality improvement. The later report belongs to a target frame: public-facing representation and institutional legitimation. The question is not simply whether the box was checked. The question is whether the later invocation of that checked box preserves the original frame and scope or recontextualizes the consent-sign so that the conditions of consent disappear.

What the example demonstrates is the difference between ordinary consent continuity and possible consent laundering. If the original form clearly included public promotional use, if the participant had a meaningful way to understand that scope, and if the later report remained within the stated frame, the laundering reading weakens. If, however, the later invocation treats the checkbox as a general legitimacy-token while suppressing ambiguity about scope, publicness, and later use, then the structure becomes PMS-legible as a candidate laundering configuration.

The PMS Core hooks are clear. **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes the checked box from the conditions under which it was checked. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as the structural pull toward closure: the institution can say, “consent was given.” **□ (Frame)** is central because the consent-sign is meaningful only inside a frame. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may become relevant if the original frame omitted public-use scope, withdrawal conditions, or later notification where the frame would reasonably require them. **Α (Attractor)** remains weak unless the platform repeatedly uses broad consent language in this way. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is conditional: there may be asymmetry in expertise, drafting power, information access, or public exposure, but this must be shown rather than presumed. **Θ (Temporality)** is active because the original checkbox is later invoked. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is the central operator: the consent-sign may be moved from internal research participation to public legitimation. **Χ (Distance)** must remain active in the analysis: the example does not decide legal validity or moral blame. **Σ (Integration)** is not automatically present; a coherent institutional account may still fail to integrate scope, publicness, and participant understanding. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is not established unless a concrete role or institution is shown to be self-bound by obligations across time.

The PMS-SEX overlay is not relevant here except as a boundary reminder. The example is not sexual, so body remainder, sexual cost topology, parity-to-exit, adult sexual reciprocity, and asymmetry-switch drift should not be activated. This is important because it shows that PMS-SEX must not sexualize every consent problem. Consent is structurally relevant across many domains, but the SEX overlay applies only where a concrete sexual-praxis instantiation supplies the needed scene features.

MIP remains a working-note layer only. No A-score or M-score can be assigned from the vignette as written. One would need concrete evidence: what the form said, what was reasonably knowable, what alternatives existed, what publicness consequences followed, who controlled the wording, and whether correction or withdrawal was available. The IA-box could become relevant if a concrete asymmetry were documented, but it cannot return a verdict merely from the fact that a platform drafted a form. AHP would flag the main artifact risk: a polished structural reading could make the case appear decided before the scope evidence is actually examined.

The forbidden claims are therefore explicit. The example does not prove invalid consent, coercion, deception, legal liability, bad faith, institutional wrongdoing, participant harm, or PMS Base validity. It also does not show that every broad consent form is laundering. It demonstrates only how later invocation can become structurally suspect when the consent-sign is detached from scope conditions.

The non-capture note is equally important. Legal doctrine may decide the matter differently. Research ethics, data governance, institutional policy, participant expectation, and public communication norms may each preserve dimensions that PMS does not settle. A procedural reading might find the checkbox sufficient. A discourse-theoretic reading might focus on communicative transparency and contestability. PMS maps the closure structure; it does not exhaust the case.

**Article takeaway:** This example shows the core architecture of Consent Laundering without overclaiming: the structurally decisive issue is not the mere presence of consent, but the later use of a consent-sign across frame, scope, publicness, and temporal displacement.

##### Example 2 — “You agreed to this” inside a changing relationship frame

This example illustrates the temporal and integrative difficulty of Consent Laundering: an earlier agreement may remain relevant while still failing to settle what the changed frame now requires.

Two adults agree to keep a private arrangement informal and non-binding. At the time, both describe the arrangement as flexible. One person later invokes the earlier agreement whenever the other raises discomfort about how the arrangement is being interpreted. The response is: “You agreed to this from the beginning.” The statement may be true in a narrow sense. There was an agreement. But over time, the situation has accumulated new expectations, repeated patterns, and consequences that were not explicitly discussed at the start.

This example must remain carefully bounded. It does not say that either person is manipulative, immature, coercive, dishonest, or legally culpable. It does not say that the original agreement was invalid. It does not say that discomfort retroactively cancels consent. It shows a structural problem: an earlier consent-sign is used as a later frame-control device. The question is whether the phrase “you agreed” preserves the original scope or prevents the conditions of the arrangement from being reread as the situation changes.

The example demonstrates why Consent Laundering is not reducible to a single moment. The original agreement belongs to one temporal layer. The later invocation belongs to another. Between them, patterns may stabilize, expectations may shift, and costs may accumulate. If the later invocation remains open to review—“yes, we agreed, but let us check whether the frame still holds”—then laundering is less likely. If the later invocation blocks review—“agreement existed, therefore discomfort is irrelevant”—then the consent-sign begins to function as closure against its own conditions.

The PMS Core hooks show the structure. **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes original agreement from later invocation. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as the pressure to stabilize or close the dispute. **□ (Frame)** is the relationship frame or arrangement frame: informal, flexible, non-binding. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may become relevant if expected review, clarification, exit, or scope discussion does not occur. The absence matters only if the frame made such review expected; it cannot be assumed. **Α (Attractor)** is possible if the phrase “you agreed” becomes a repeated script. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is conditional: one person may have more emotional leverage, social power, interpretive control, or capacity to exit, but this must be reconstructed. **Θ (Temporality)** is active because the arrangement changes over time. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is central because the original agreement is recontextualized as later closure. **Χ (Distance)** is required from the analyst: the example must not become a tribunal. **Σ (Integration)** is the central unresolved question. A mature structural integration would hold together the original agreement, later discomfort, changed conditions, and possible review. A pseudo-integration would say only, “There was agreement; therefore the matter is coherent.” **Ψ (Self-binding)** remains conditional unless the relationship frame includes stable self-binding obligations across time.

The PMS-SEX overlay may be relevant only if the arrangement is explicitly sexual. If it is sexual, PMS-SEX asks additional questions without making findings. What is the internal framing: play, intimacy, experimentation, casualness, non-binding autonomy, or something else? What is the outer legibility: does the enacted form remain flexible, or has it become structurally one-sided? Is there publicness, reputational risk, documentation, or exposure? Is there a sexual cost topology: body, exit, publicness, reputational sedimentation, responsibility externalization? Could parity-to-exit appear in the phrase “we agreed, so no one owes anything further”? These are overlay hooks, not conclusions. If the arrangement is not sexual, these PMS-SEX hooks should remain inactive.

MIP status remains non-scored. The example contains more narrative than the abstract case, but still not enough for A-score or M-score. One would need to know what each person understood, what was predictable, what alternatives existed, whether review was realistically possible, whether either person had greater power-to-act, and whether serious integration attempts occurred. The IA-box could be used as a checklist: was any asymmetry transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible? But the example does not provide enough information for a verdict. D should remain off unless a concrete protective purpose, safe communication space, and role-bound context exist.

AHP would identify a likely attack point: the phrase “you agreed to this” is easy to overinterpret. It could be a legitimate reminder of a shared frame. It could also be a closure move. The analysis must not decide based on the phrase alone. AHP would harden the example by requiring evidence-to-field thresholds: what was agreed, what changed, what was reviewable, who could exit, what costs accumulated, and what rival interpretation remains plausible.

Forbidden claims must be named. The example does not show abuse, coercion, pathology, sexual immaturity, bad faith, or invalid consent. It does not show that discomfort always defeats prior agreement. It does not authorize public accusation. It does not rank either person. It does not convert possible asymmetry into guilt.

The non-capture note matters because relationship meaning can exceed formal structure. Lived trust, attachment, fear, care, desire, ambivalence, shame, and biography may matter in a concrete case. PMS can map frame and temporality, but it does not exhaust relational experience. A phenomenological or therapeutic account might preserve dimensions PMS deliberately leaves aside. A legal account might also decide differently depending on facts and standards.

**Article takeaway:** This example shows why temporality and integration are decisive: the problem is not that an earlier agreement exists, but that the earlier agreement may be invoked later to prevent the changed frame from becoming readable.

##### Example 3 — A guarded sexual-praxis example: mutuality language and unequal cost topology

This example shows why the PMS-SEX overlay is useful only as a guarded conditional layer: it can make sexual cost topology and parity-to-exit risks visible without turning mutuality language into a verdict.

Two adults enter a private sexual arrangement that both describe as casual and mutually chosen. The vignette does not describe sexual acts and does not require such description. The relevant structure is this: when one person later expresses concern about reputational exposure, emotional cost, or difficulty exiting the arrangement, the other responds that the arrangement was mutual and non-binding from the start. The phrase “it was mutual” is not false by default. Mutuality may be real. But the concern is whether mutuality language is being used to close inquiry into costs that are not equally distributed.

This example is high-risk, so it must remain abstract and guardrail-only. It does not claim that either person exploited the other. It does not claim invalid consent. It does not claim sexual harm, coercion, trauma, pathology, deviance, immaturity, adultness failure, or moral guilt. It does not classify anyone’s sexuality, identity, orientation, or character. It illustrates how PMS-SEX can add visibility when a consent-laundering structure is concretely sexual: internal framing, outer legibility, publicness, exit realism, and sexual cost topology become relevant.

The bounded vignette demonstrates a specific overlay question. “It was mutual” can function in at least two ways. It may coordinate reciprocity: both persons acknowledge the frame, the risks, the exit options, and the costs. #### Boundary Confusion

##### Why this case is here

Boundary Confusion belongs in AXIOM because it names a recurring closure demand that is easy to recognize but difficult to settle responsibly: where does one person’s role, responsibility, space, decision, authority, access, or obligation end, and where does another’s begin? The case is not difficult because boundaries never exist. It is difficult because the meaning of a boundary usually depends on a frame. The same gesture can appear as help, intrusion, care, control, shared responsibility, overreach, flexibility, or violation depending on which role-space and expectation structure makes it readable.

This case therefore tests whether PMS can map a strong demand for closure without becoming a closure machine. The relevant tension is not simply “there is a boundary” versus “there is no boundary.” The tension lies in the way multiple possible boundary types can become compressed into a single label: role boundary, responsibility boundary, spatial boundary, privacy boundary, access boundary, decision-authority boundary, institutional mandate, or relational expectation. A case titled Boundary Confusion can therefore become too smooth if it is treated as one problem. The analysis must keep open which boundary is actually at stake.

The case is a conceptual projection rather than a concrete scene. This matters directly. Because no actors, episode sequence, institutional setting, legal frame, clinical frame, sexual scene, publicness condition, or evidence base is supplied, the analysis can render structural possibilities but cannot convert them into scene-specific findings. Responsibility, action, asymmetry, repeated crossing, integration, and self-binding may be structurally described as possible operator paths, but they must not become participant-level conclusions without additional evidence.

The case belongs especially well in a chapter section because it demonstrates AXIOM’s cartographic orientation. It shows how PMS Core can identify the primary structural movement, how an add-on can remain useful even when it mostly refuses activation, how MIP can preserve non-scoring as a case-specific result, and how AHP can harden the analysis artefact without making the case more certain. The case is mapped rather than solved because the missing frame, missing scene, and missing calibration are not defects to hide. They are part of the structure being made visible.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core reading begins with Δ (Difference). Boundary Confusion first becomes legible because a distinction is at stake: one role is not another role, one responsibility is not another responsibility, one space is not another space, one decision is not another decision. Yet the analysis cannot stop at the mere existence of difference. The case does not say which difference is primary. It may concern role, responsibility, privacy, authority, access, decision-making, obligation, or a conflation among several of these.

The next decisive operator is □ (Frame). The conservative PMS formula for this case is therefore □ (Frame) ∘ Δ (Difference): boundary confusion is minimally a framed distinction problem. A boundary becomes meaningful only within a frame that says what kind of distinction matters, who is authorized to act, what counts as crossing, what counts as refusal, and what counts as repair. This is the case’s central structural movement. The frame might be relational, institutional, familial, legal, ethical, procedural, spatial, or decision-authority-based. Without specifying the frame, PMS cannot determine whether a boundary was crossed, negotiated, shared, misunderstood, or simply not yet defined.

∇ (Impulse) remains weak. Boundary confusion often involves directional pressure: someone pushes, enters, edits, decides, resists, yields, clarifies, refuses, withdraws, repairs, takes over, or contests. The analysis may say that boundary confusion can generate directed tension; it may not infer motive, aggression, manipulation, avoidance, or refusal from the case title alone.

Λ (Non-event / residue) is weak but important. Boundary confusion often depends on something expected but absent: missing clarification, missing refusal, missing permission, missing repair, missing restraint, missing acknowledgment, missing decision, or missing non-interference. The completed YAML analysis preserves Λ-residue as possible but not established. This is important because unresolved absence is one of the places where overclaim most easily enters. Α would become stronger only if structurally similar episodes showed stabilization: repeated takeover, repeated responsibility dumping, repeated intrusion, repeated ambiguity, repeated refusal to clarify, or repeated collapse of role boundaries. Ω (Asymmetry) is possible but under-calibrated. ##### Example 1 — Shared Help, Unclear Decision

A small team is preparing a public report. One member, Mara, is formally responsible for the final wording. Another member, Jonas, has more technical expertise and has helped shape the analysis throughout the project. During the final week, Jonas begins editing the report directly, rewriting several passages and rearranging the order of recommendations. Mara does not immediately object, because some of the edits improve accuracy. But after two days, she notices that the document no longer reflects the decision structure she had agreed to manage. Jonas says he was “only helping” and that the report is “shared work anyway.” Mara says the distinction between help and taking over has become unclear.

This example demonstrates the basic PMS Core structure of Boundary Confusion without turning it into a verdict. The relevant distinction is not whether Jonas is a good or bad collaborator, or whether Mara is too protective of her role. The first structural question is simpler: which boundary is at stake? The example contains several possible boundaries at once: role boundary, decision-authority boundary, authorship boundary, responsibility boundary, and perhaps institutional mandate. PMS begins with Δ (Difference), because a distinction between Mara’s responsibility and Jonas’s contribution has become salient. But Δ alone does not settle the matter. The distinction becomes meaningful only within □ (Frame): project governance, authorship norms, team collaboration, accuracy responsibility, deadline pressure, or final accountability. This is why the minimal PMS formula remains □ ∘ Δ.

The case also shows why later operators cannot be used cheaply. There is possible ∇ (Impulse), because Jonas acts into the document and Mara experiences pressure around her role, but the example does not yet establish motive, aggression, evasion, or manipulation. There may be Λ (Non-event / residue) if an expected clarification never occurs: perhaps nobody said who had final edit authority, or Mara did not explicitly mark the boundary when Jonas began editing. Yet this absence remains ambiguous. It could be a failure of communication, a normal collaborative gap, an institutional omission, or a reasonable assumption under deadline pressure. It should not automatically be translated into drift or maturity failure.

Α (Attractor) and Θ (Temporality) would become stronger only if the pattern repeated: if Jonas repeatedly takes over final decisions in similar projects, or if the team repeatedly leaves formal responsibility unclear until conflict emerges. In this bounded vignette, there is only a short sequence. Ω (Asymmetry) is also possible but not established. Jonas may have technical authority; Mara may have formal responsibility; the institution may make Mara accountable for final output; Jonas may bear epistemic burden for accuracy. The direction of asymmetry is therefore not obvious. One role may carry expertise; another may carry accountability. Treating either person as simply dominant or weak would overread the scene.

Φ (Recontextualization) is visible as a rival-frame possibility. Jonas frames his action as help; Mara frames it as role displacement. The analysis must not decide too quickly which frame is correct. Χ (Distance) is required from the analyst: pause before assigning fault, preserve reversibility, and ask what frame the team actually uses. Σ (Integration) remains conditional: integration would require a concrete coordination of expertise and final responsibility, not a forced consensus that “both were partly right.” Ψ (Self-binding) is also conditional. If Mara formally owns final wording, there may be self-binding to the role; if Jonas is accountable for technical accuracy, he may have another kind of responsibility. But neither can be converted into a person-level maturity verdict from the vignette alone.

The PMS-SEX overlay is not activated. The words “boundary,” “space,” “taking over,” and “responsibility” may appear in many domains, but nothing here supplies sexual scene material, body remainder, sexual cost topology, public sexual legibility, adult sexual reciprocity, or sexuality-specific drift. The overlay would therefore serve only as a guardrail: do not sexualize the example by inference.

MIP remains under-specified. No A-score or M-score should be assigned. Mara’s awareness, Jonas’s awareness, and the team’s responsibility cannot be scored without knowing the explicit role mandate, previous agreements, real alternatives, time pressure, predictability, and repair options. The IA-box would also remain unassigned unless a concrete asymmetry is specified and tested for transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. AHP would treat the example as a useful analysis-artifact test: does the prose preserve non-score status, avoid person labels, and prevent “help” or “overreach” from becoming verdict terms?

Forbidden claims are therefore clear. The example does not show that Jonas violated Mara’s role. It does not show that Mara failed to communicate. It does not establish manipulation, incompetence, immaturity, legal responsibility, workplace misconduct, or psychological pattern. It does not validate PMS. It only shows how boundary confusion can become PMS-readable as a frame-dependent distinction problem.

The non-capture note is that in a real organization, the decisive framework might be a contract, editorial policy, workplace hierarchy, authorship norm, or project-management rule. PMS can map the structural ambiguity, but it cannot replace those domain standards.

The article takeaway is that boundary confusion often looks like a dispute over behavior, but the first analytic task is to identify the frame that makes the behavior count as help, overreach, shared work, or role displacement. Until that frame is calibrated, the mature PMS reading remains conservative.

##### Example 2 — The Open Door That Was Not a Standing Invitation

In a shared apartment, one resident, Eli, often leaves his door open while working. Another resident, Sam, sometimes steps in to ask short questions or continue household conversations. At first, Eli does not object. Over time, Sam begins entering more frequently, sometimes while Eli is on calls or preparing for deadlines. Eli finally says, “Leaving the door open does not mean I am always available.” Sam replies, “I thought the open door meant it was fine. You never said it was a problem.”

This vignette is deliberately ordinary. Its usefulness lies in showing how easily a boundary can be interpreted as obvious by one participant and ambiguous by another. The relevant boundary is partly spatial, partly temporal, partly attentional, and partly relational. The open door is not simply a physical fact. It becomes meaningful only in a frame: household friendliness, privacy, availability, interruption norms, shared space, or individual work time. PMS Core therefore begins again with Δ (Difference) and □ (Frame): a distinction between shared access and personal availability becomes contested within a frame that had not been explicitly stabilized.

The example demonstrates the importance of Λ (Non-event / residue). The conflict turns on non-events: Eli did not explicitly say, “Please knock before entering”; Sam did not explicitly ask, “Does an open door mean I can come in?” The absence of clarification becomes meaningful only after repeated entries accumulate. But PMS must not overread this absence. Eli’s silence is not necessarily consent, avoidance, passivity, or immaturity. Sam’s assumption is not necessarily entitlement, intrusion, or disregard. The non-event is structurally relevant, but it does not yet decide responsibility.

Α (Attractor) and Θ (Temporality) are stronger here than in a one-off case, because the pattern repeats. The repeated entries may begin to form an attractor: open door → entry → interruption → no explicit correction → continued assumption. Temporality matters because the meaning of the open door changes over time. A single entry might be harmless; repeated entries during calls or deadlines may create a different structure. Still, even here, Α and Θ are not verdicts. Repetition shows pattern potential, not blame by itself.

Ω (Asymmetry) is possible but must be calibrated. If Eli has less ability to refuse because he wants household peace, Sam may have an access advantage. If Sam reasonably relies on visible signals and Eli never clarifies them, Sam may lack relevant knowledge. If one resident owns the apartment or controls shared resources, asymmetry may run differently. The example therefore blocks a cheap asymmetry reading. PMS must ask: who can define the access rule, who bears interruption cost, who can exit the interaction, who can repair, and who has realistic alternatives?

Φ (Recontextualization) appears when the same open door is recontextualized. For Sam, it was an availability signal. For Eli, it was ventilation, habit, or non-isolation, not a standing invitation. Recontextualization can clarify the conflict, but it can also become a way to erase one frame with another. The analysis should preserve both frames until the relevant household norm is negotiated or specified.

Χ (Distance) is central. A mature analysis must not rush to say “Sam violated privacy” or “Eli failed to communicate.” Either may become true in a fuller case, but not from the vignette alone. Σ (Integration) would require a new coordination: perhaps an open door means “visible but not interruptible,” or a knock rule is introduced, or deadline hours are marked. Ψ (Self-binding) would become relevant only if the residents bind themselves to a new shared rule over time. Without such binding, PMS cannot claim stable responsibility-as-self-commitment.

The PMS-SEX overlay remains non-activating. The example includes a room, entry, privacy, and personal space, but no sexual scene, bodily exposure, sexual role-position, sexual cost topology, public sexual legibility, or adult sexual reciprocity. This is precisely the kind of example where the overlay’s restraint matters. “Space” and “entry” are not sufficient to infer sexuality-specific structure. PMS-SEX would add only the guardrail that body remainder, publicness, and sexual cost topology must not be invented.

MIP status remains mostly non-scoring. One might be tempted to score Eli’s awareness or Sam’s responsibility, but that would be premature. Eli’s A-awareness of Sam’s interpretation is unclear. Sam’s A-awareness of Eli’s work boundary is unclear. M-responsibility cannot be assigned without knowledge, predictability, power to act, alternatives, and integration attempts. The IA-box could become useful if a concrete asymmetry appears: for example, if one resident has authority over the housing arrangement, or if repeated entry creates a real inability to protect work time. But as written, IA remains under-specified. AHP would flag a likely attack point: the example is readable enough to tempt verdicts. The article must therefore keep its language disciplined.

Forbidden claims include: Sam is intrusive; Eli is conflict-avoidant; an open door legally or morally means permission; repeated entry proves disrespect; silence proves consent; discomfort proves violation; household ambiguity proves immaturity. None of these follows from the vignette alone.

The non-capture note is that real household boundaries may be governed by culture, disability, work conditions, tenancy rules, trauma history, family norms, or material constraints. PMS can map the boundary ambiguity, but it cannot replace the lived or institutional context that determines what repair is appropriate.

The article takeaway is that boundary confusion often arises not from one dramatic crossing but from repeated low-level non-events. The open door becomes a boundary object because it carries more meaning than either participant explicitly gave it. PMS helps preserve that structure without deciding the case prematurely.

##### Example 3 — When Responsibility Becomes Frame Ownership

A university department asks a senior coordinator, Dr. Vale, to “support” a new lecturer, Rowan, during the first semester. The instruction is informal. Dr. Vale begins by answering questions and explaining procedures. Soon, however, she starts reviewing Rowan’s course emails before they are sent, advising students to copy her on requests, and making small changes to Rowan’s seminar schedule “to prevent problems.” Rowan feels that support has become supervision, but is unsure whether objecting would seem ungrateful or unprofessional. Dr. Vale says she is simply carrying responsibility for a new colleague’s success.

This example is more structurally loaded than the first two because it shows how boundary confusion can arise through responsibility language. The vignette is still bounded. It does not establish misconduct, harassment, incompetence, legal breach, or institutional liability. But it makes visible a recurring AXIOM problem: responsibility may clarify a frame, or it may become a route into another person’s role-space.

PMS Core begins with Δ (Difference): there is a distinction between support and supervision, advice and control, responsibility for one’s own teaching and responsibility for another’s integration, institutional care and role capture. The frame □ (Frame) is unstable because the department’s instruction is informal. “Support” is not defined. It could mean availability for questions, mentorship, oversight, quality assurance, pastoral care, risk management, or temporary delegated authority. Without frame clarification, each action can be read differently.

∇ (Impulse) appears as directional pressure: Dr. Vale moves from answering questions into reviewing communications and adjusting schedules; Rowan feels pressure around objection, gratitude, and professional standing. But PMS must not infer motive. Dr. Vale may be conscientious, controlling, anxious, institutionally pressured, or acting under unclear expectations. Rowan may be reasonably concerned, uncertain, conflict-avoidant, strategically silent, or still learning the frame. The vignette does not decide.

Λ (Non-event / residue) is significant. The missing event is formal clarification: what kind of support was authorized, what remains Rowan’s decision, whether students should copy Dr. Vale, and whether schedule changes require consent. This non-event structures the situation. It is not merely absence; it becomes meaningful because actions accumulate under the undefined support frame.

Α (Attractor) and Θ (Temporality) are plausible but still conditional. The shift from answering questions to reviewing emails and changing schedules may suggest pattern formation over time. If this continues, an attractor could form: support → preventive intervention → reduced autonomy → more dependency or oversight → further intervention. But the example remains a vignette, not a full timeline. Α and Θ are visible as risk conditions, not established drift findings.

Ω (Asymmetry) is central but must be handled carefully. Dr. Vale has seniority, institutional knowledge, and perhaps informal authority. Rowan may have less status, less security, and less ability to refuse. But Dr. Vale may also bear institutional responsibility if new lecturers are unsupported. The asymmetry is therefore not morally simple. It must be mapped by channels: status, access to students, control over procedures, accountability for course quality, reputational exposure, and capacity to object. PMS does not say asymmetry equals abuse. It says asymmetry must be named before responsibility can be understood.

Φ (Recontextualization) appears in the rival framing. Dr. Vale frames her actions as responsibility and protection; Rowan experiences them as supervision or role capture. The same practice is recontextualized across support, governance, care, control, and institutional risk. PMS can make this visible, but it must not decide by vocabulary. Calling an act “support” does not settle its outer structure. Calling it “control” does not prove bad intent.

Χ (Distance) is required to keep the analysis from becoming a tribunal. Σ (Integration) would become relevant if the department creates a clearer arrangement: what Dr. Vale may advise on, what Rowan decides, how students communicate, when review ends, and how disagreement is handled. Ψ (Self-binding) would become relevant only if roles bind themselves to clarified commitments over time: Dr. Vale to restraint and transparent support, Rowan to participation or refusal pathways, and the department to formal role definition.

This is the strongest of the three examples for showing where MIP may become relevant, while still refusing scores. MIP would ask about awareness, coherence, responsibility, and power-to-act. Did Dr. Vale know that support had become directive? Did Rowan have a real option to object? Did the department create ambiguity and then leave both parties to absorb it? Was the asymmetry transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible? These are maturity-in-practice questions. But no A-score or M-score should be assigned from the vignette alone. MIP would remain a working note unless more evidence is provided.

The IA-box is tempting here, because asymmetry is more visible. But even then, it should not become a verdict. Transparency is unclear: the support arrangement was informal. Justification is unclear: support may serve a legitimate good, but the scope is not defined. Time-boundedness is unclear: no end condition is specified. Reversibility is unclear: Rowan’s ability to object safely is unknown. Therefore the IA-box may identify questions but not produce a result.

PMS-SEX remains non-activating. The example contains role support, authority, responsibility, and possible control, but no sexual scene, no body remainder, no sexual publicness, no sexual cost topology, no adult sexual reciprocity, and no SEX drift finding. However, the PMS-SEX overlay is still useful as a negative guardrail. It reminds the article that `responsibility-to-authority` is a PMS-SEX drift handle only in sexual-praxis contexts where sexual cost topology, Ω/Θ distribution, and re-binding conditions are actually present. In this vignette, that handle is not activated. The article may speak in ordinary prose about responsibility language becoming frame ownership, but it must not import the PMS-SEX drift category as a sexuality-specific finding.

AHP status is especially relevant to how this example is written. It is vivid enough to invite judgment. The article must therefore harden the language: Dr. Vale is not labeled controlling; Rowan is not labeled passive; the department is not labeled negligent; the vignette is not treated as legal or HR evidence. AHP would likely flag any sentence that converts unclear support into established domination, or unclear discomfort into established harm.

Forbidden claims include: Dr. Vale abused authority; Rowan lacked maturity; the department violated policy; support is really control; seniority is inherently coercive; responsibility always becomes authority; Rowan’s discomfort proves boundary violation. These may be possible claims in a fuller case, but the vignette does not supply the evidence.

The non-capture note is that a real case might require institutional governance, employment law, mentoring policy, union rules, professional ethics, or safeguarding procedures. PMS and MIP can map the structural ambiguity, but cannot replace those frameworks.

The article takeaway is that boundary confusion becomes most difficult when responsibility language is itself the vehicle of crossing. A role can enter another role-space in the name of care, prevention, or support. PMS makes that structure visible, MIP asks what mature handling would require, and the add-on/AHP layers prevent the example from becoming a verdict before the frame is specified.

##### Case takeaway

Boundary Confusion shows that AXIOM can preserve a strong closure demand without satisfying it prematurely. The case’s central movement is not a verdict about boundary violation but a structural rendering of framed distinction: □ (Frame) ∘ Δ (Difference). The article should be used in the chapter as a case of disciplined calibration: identify the frame before deciding the boundary, identify the missing evidence before assigning responsibility, and preserve residue where the scene does not yet support closure.

The examples show how the same structural problem appears across collaboration, shared space, and institutional support. They are illustrative, not evidentiary. They demonstrate why help may become role displacement, why an open door may not be a standing invitation, and why responsibility language may become frame ownership. They also show why each case remains bounded: none proves fault, diagnosis, legal breach, maturity failure, or PMS validation.

The case-specific residue is the unclosed question of frame. Until the frame is specified, later structures remain conditional. Λ (Non-event / residue) remains visible where clarification is missing. Ω (Asymmetry) remains possible where role-power or exposure might be uneven. Θ (Temporality) remains possible where recurrence might accumulate. Σ (Integration) and Ψ (Self-binding) remain unavailable as findings until concrete coordination and durable commitment are shown.

Methodologically, Boundary Confusion is a useful chapter case because it demonstrates non-escalation as analytic strength. PMS Core maps; PMS-SEX refuses unwarranted activation; MIP refuses scoring; AHP hardens the artefact without upgrading the claim. The result is not a smaller analysis. It is a cleaner one: boundary confusion is made structurally visible without being turned into diagnosis, person-ranking, legal conclusion, sexualization, maturity verdict, or closure by authority.

### 10.5 Summary

Part VII establishes SEX as the PMS add-on layer for consent-adjacent validity questions under asymmetry, temporality, body-related cost topology, publicness, and priced distance. The cases in this part do not treat consent as a single utterance, a moral token, a proof object, a personality marker, or a legal finding. They treat consent-talk as a configuration claim: a description is structurally coherent only if the surrounding frame can actually carry agreement, refusal, boundary, cost, time, stop-capability, and re-binding.

The common result is not that PMS-SEX decides whether consent was valid or invalid in any concrete scene. The result is that PMS-SEX makes visible where consent-talk remains structurally under-supported: agreement may coexist with dependency; mutuality may conceal unequal cost; earlier agreement may be invoked to block review; boundary language may remain unframed; ambiguity may be stabilized as a script; and a precise analysis artefact may itself become too authoritative when transmitted.

SEX therefore does not replace PMS core. PMS remains the operator grammar. SEX adds overlay handles for consent-specific validity risks: sexual cost topology, body/publicness residue, covert Ψ demand, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, asymmetry-switch risk, Λ-as-steering, Χ loss, boundary confusion, publicness sedimentation, and consent laundering. These handles are not person labels, practice labels, identity labels, adultness verdicts, or enforcement categories.

#### 10.5.1 Baseline result of Part VII

The baseline established by Part VII is that consent-talk must not become closure by label.

Across the cases, PMS core makes the closure-demand visible, while SEX sharpens the validity discipline. Consent Under Asymmetry shows that agreement and asymmetry must both remain visible: “yes” does not settle the case, but asymmetry does not settle it either. Consent Laundering shows how a consent-sign may later be used as a closure-token, while also preserving ordinary consent continuity as a live rival reading. Boundary Confusion shows that the most conservative reading may be the strongest one: first identify the frame and the relevant distinction before importing sexuality-specific handles, MIP scoring, or stronger drift language.

The strongest shared discipline is:

**explicit □ + operative Χ + mapped Ω/Θ + tested Σ/Ψ**

The frame must say what the scene is and what consent or boundary language can mean there. Stop-capability must be practically available, not merely verbal. Asymmetry and temporality must be mapped without being moralized. Integration and self-binding must be shown through the configuration, not inferred from agreement, ritual, statement, apology, mutuality language, or formal precision.

#### 10.5.2 Cluster-level result

The Part VII cases form three SEX-facing clusters.

**Consent under asymmetry** shows the basic validity pressure. Formal agreement can matter, but it does not automatically prove meaningful consent. Dependency, vulnerability, role authority, publicness, reputation, bodily exposure, or unequal exit cost can matter, but they do not automatically prove invalidity, coercion, guilt, or blame. The case’s contribution is to block both premature closures: agreement-as-settlement and asymmetry-as-verdict.

**Consent laundering** shows the temporal and recontextualizing risk. A consent-sign can travel from its source frame into a later target frame, where it may be used to close inquiry into scope, conditions, alternatives, revisability, publicness, or cost topology. But later invocation of consent is not automatically laundering. The case preserves the difference between ordinary consent continuity and consent-as-closure-token.

**Boundary confusion** shows the restraint side of PMS-SEX. Not every boundary case is sexual. Not every responsibility, access, space, privacy, or role-confusion case activates sexual cost topology. Here, the overlay contributes partly by refusing improper activation. The result is not weaker analysis, but cleaner analysis: □ (Frame) and Δ (Difference) must be established before later operators, add-ons, MIP/AHP layers, or drift handles are allowed to carry weight.

#### 10.5.3 Cross-case dependency discipline

The recurring failure mode in Part VII is:

**consent / boundary token → validity closure**

This occurs when a word, sign, gesture, ritual, checkbox, agreement, public statement, mutuality claim, boundary label, or polished analysis artefact is asked to do the operator-work that the configuration itself has not carried. Once that shortcut appears, the dependency chain usually breaks: □ is replaced by a token, Χ is priced or assumed, Ω is denied or over-moralized, Θ is flattened into a moment, Λ is treated as irrelevant or as proof, Σ is simulated by narrative coherence, and Ψ is leaked or externalized as obligation.

In Part VII this shortcut appears in several forms:

- agreement becomes consent-validity;
- dependency becomes invalidity;
- mutuality becomes equal cost;
- earlier consent becomes permanent closure;
- discomfort becomes proof;
- absence of protest becomes proof;
- boundary ambiguity becomes blame;
- responsibility language becomes authority;
- publicness becomes legitimacy;
- careful analysis becomes decision-ready assessment.

Part VII blocks these shortcuts without emptying consent or boundaries of meaning. Consent can be real. Refusal can be real. Asymmetry can be decisive. Boundaries can be crossed. Publicness can matter. But none of these may be converted into a finding without the frame, sequence, cost topology, stop-capability, integration path, and evidence base needed to support it.

#### 10.5.4 What Part VII carries forward

Part VII carries forward the strongest guardrail lesson of AXIOM: the more socially and personally dangerous a vocabulary is, the more strictly its structural conditions must be held apart from verdicts. Consent, boundary, adultness, responsibility, dignity, asymmetry, publicness, and repair language can clarify configurations, but they can also shame, rank, expose, coerce, or launder authority.

SEX is justified because PMS core can map consent-adjacent closure-demands, but the overlay makes SEX-specific validity risks sharper: when agreement is asked to carry frame work, when mutuality hides unequal cost, when publicness changes the configuration, when stop-capability is priced, when consent-signs travel across time, and when boundary ambiguity is stabilized rather than resolved.

MIP is active only as an artefact-facing guardrail layer. It documents publicity level, misuse corridors, responsibility ceilings, IA-box limits, and D0-protective boundary conditions where relevant. It does not score persons, assign adultness, decide consent validity, activate D as a case finding, or produce guilt, maturity, legal, clinical, forensic, or enforcement conclusions. AHP may harden the analysis artefact against over-transmission, excerptability, publicness drift, and false authority. It does not rescore, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or create new findings.

Part VII closes with the SEX rule for AXIOM: where consent-talk appears, reconstruct the frame before reading the sign; where asymmetry appears, map cost before assigning meaning; where stop-capability is priced, do not treat language as enough; where publicness or temporality changes the scene, do not pretend the original frame remains unchanged; and where evidence is missing, preserve Λ rather than laundering uncertainty into consent, non-consent, guilt, maturity, diagnosis, or governance permission.
