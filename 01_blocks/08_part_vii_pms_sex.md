# PMS-AXIOM: PART VII - SEX

## 10. SEX-Overlay: Consent Validity Under Asymmetry, Drift Markers, and Boundary Failure Modes

Part VII introduces the SEX overlay as the PMS add-on layer for consent-adjacent validity questions under asymmetry, temporality, publicness, body-related cost topology, and priced distance. The cases in this part remain grounded in PMS core, but they require an additional lens because their central closure-demands concern when consent-talk remains structurally coherent, when it becomes under-specified, and when it risks being used as a stabilizing label over conditions that the frame cannot actually carry.

The purpose of this part is not to define sexual normality, evaluate persons, infer motives, classify identities, provide advice, adjudicate guilt, or supply legal, clinical, therapeutic, or enforcement heuristics. It is to show what must remain visible before consent-related claims can be responsibly handled: frame (□), salient differences (Δ), asymmetry (Ω), temporal accumulation (Θ), residue and non-events (Λ), practical stop-capability (Χ), integration capacity (Σ), and binding discipline (Ψ).

The SEX overlay is therefore a guardrail-first, non-mixing disciplinary lens. It does not redefine PMS operators. It does not add a new base grammar. It supplies reduced signatures, drift markers, viability corridors, cost-topology handles, publicness/media extensions, and validity-boundary language that make SEX-specific risks visible: covert Ψ demand, pseudo-symmetry, Ω escalation, Λ-as-steering, Α fixation, Χ loss, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, publicness sedimentation, and boundary confusion.

PMS remains the canonical operator grammar. SEX only sharpens the reading where the case-specific issue is not merely agreement, desire, trust, intimacy, or relationship structure, but consent-validity under load: the question whether the configuration can actually carry the consent description without suppressing asymmetry, hiding temporal effects, pricing refusal, externalizing cost, or turning absence into steering.

### 10.1 Case Set and Role in the Paper

The cases addressed in Part VII are:

46. Consent Under Asymmetry
47. Consent Laundering
48. Boundary Confusion

Together, these cases show why consent cannot be reduced to a single utterance, a purely internal state, a retrospective narrative, or a formal parity claim. They are not grouped here because PMS-SEX decides whether consent is valid or invalid in a concrete case. They are grouped here because each case contains a validity-pressure: a consent description seeks closure while the surrounding configuration may remain under-specified, asymmetric, temporally loaded, or boundary-confused.

The cases cluster into three SEX-relevant families:

- **Consent under asymmetry** shows why agreement and asymmetry must both be kept visible without allowing either to settle the case by itself.
- **Consent laundering** shows how consent language can be used to stabilize or launder a configuration whose frame, cost topology, stop-capability, or binding discipline has not been reconstructed.
- **Boundary confusion** shows how consent-adjacent material can move between intimacy, privacy, publicness, institutional procedure, accountability discourse, or tribunal-like frames without a stable validity grammar.

Part VII therefore demonstrates a central AXIOM discipline: consent analysis must be structurally sharp but non-verdictive. PMS-SEX can specify what a validity gate would require; it must not become a person label, moral ranking, adultness verdict, sexual classification, legal conclusion, clinical inference, or enforcement tool.

### 10.2 Output Contract for Part VII

The admissible output in Part VII is:

- **Validity gate:** a structured, non-prescriptive artefact specifying what must be true for a consent description to remain structurally coherent inside a given frame, and which patterns mark drift-preparedness or invalidity risk.

A validity gate may include:

- **Frame declaration:** what the scene is, what counts, what is excluded, and whether publicity, media, institutionalization, or record effects extend the frame.
- **Difference and pressure map:** salient boundaries, access points, pace, stakes, roles, escalation paths, and any impulse pressure toward closure.
- **Asymmetry profile:** access, exposure, dependency, obligation, authority, leverage, exit capacity, and publicness gradients, separated rather than moralized.
- **Temporal realism:** repetition, accumulation, after-effects, irreversibility windows, review points, withdrawal conditions, and exit realism.
- **Residue / non-event handling:** pauses, refusals, silence, withdrawal, missing safeguards, absent review, or non-response, without automatically treating absence as proof.
- **Distance / stop-capability check:** whether stopping, slowing, refusing, revising, or moving to meta-position is practically available or priced.
- **Integration and binding discipline:** whether contradictions and boundary mismatches can be coordinated without coercive simplification, and whether commitments are explicit, frame-supported, and revisable where required.
- **SEX drift markers:** overlay handles such as covert Ψ demand, pseudo-symmetry, Ω escalation, Λ-as-steering, Α fixation, Χ loss, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, or boundary confusion.
- **Explicit non-claims:** no person-labeling, no motive inference, no moral ranking, no adultness verdict, no diagnosis, no legal/forensic conclusion, no clinical conclusion, no enforcement guidance, and no PMS Base validation.

The SEX overlay may identify drift markers, but drift marker is not drift finding. A case may show that consent language is structurally vulnerable to laundering, steering, boundary confusion, or asymmetric cost externalization without asserting that any concrete person, relationship, institution, or practice has committed that drift.

MIP is active in this part only as an artefact-facing guardrail layer, not as a person-evaluation layer. Because consent analyses are high-risk for misuse, publicness, shaming, ranking, coercive leverage, and dignity-language overreach, MIP must document the analysis artefact’s publicity level, misuse corridors, responsibility ceilings, IA-box limits, and D₀-protective boundary conditions where relevant. MIP does not score persons, assign adultness, activate D as a case finding, or produce guilt, maturity, legal, clinical, or enforcement conclusions. AHP may harden the analysis artefact where precision, excerptability, publicness, attack surfaces, or claim-boundary risks require it. AHP does not rescore, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or create new findings.

### 10.4 Cases

#### 10.4.1 Consent Under Asymmetry

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because consent under asymmetry creates a strong closure demand. A scene may appear settled because someone agrees, does not refuse, continues, or later seems to accept the frame. At the same time, the same scene may be reopened by asymmetry, dependency, pressure-response, delayed contestation, withdrawal, exit cost, or after-scene interpretation. The case therefore tests whether the PMS grammar can preserve consent as a structured field without reducing it either to formal agreement or to automatic invalidation.

The case is mapped rather than solved. Its YAML basis treats the underlying material as an abstract consent-under-asymmetry configuration, not as a concrete incident record. That matters because the analysis can render responsibility, action, asymmetry, temporality, self-binding, sexual cost topology, and dignity-in-practice as structural questions, but it cannot turn them into scene-specific findings without additional evidence. There is no supplied chronology, role testimony, bodily fact, institutional setting, publicness record, after-scene conduct, or concrete evidence of real exit and reversibility.

For AXIOM, the case makes visible a central problem of closure by label. “Consent” can become a label that closes too quickly. So can “asymmetry,” “harm,” “autonomy,” “dignity,” “maturity,” or “risk.” The analytic task is not to discard those terms, but to test whether the configuration can carry the distinctions they require. The case belongs especially in a PMS-SEX context because sexuality adds praxeological pressure: cost may be embodied, reputational, temporal, relational, public, or after-scene; direct otherness may be preserved or absorbed; withdrawal and non-binding language may conceal or fail to conceal binding effects. The case therefore requires PMS Core, the PMS-SEX overlay, and a downstream MIP/AHP reading only where they add case-specific discipline.

##### Structural reading

The Core PMS movement begins with `Δ (Difference)`: agreement must be distinguished from meaningful consent; refusal from non-response; compliance from free orientation; vulnerability from ordinary exposure; and given consent from withheld, delayed, withdrawn, or contested consent. `Δ` is active because the case would collapse if these distinctions were folded into one consent label. It must nevertheless remain calibrated: the existence of distinctions does not itself prove that consent failed.

`∇ (Impulse)` marks the directional tension of the case. The field can move toward agreement, hesitation, pressure-response, withdrawal, contestation, reinterpretation, or closure. This operator is active, but it cannot infer hidden motive. It marks structural direction, not psychology.

`□ (Frame)` is decisive because the case is an abstract consent-under-asymmetry frame, not a legal, clinical, forensic, or moral verdict frame. Inside the frame are agreement, refusal, dependency, exposure, exit, temporality, reversibility, and role-position structure. Outside the frame are concrete findings about what happened, who intended what, who was harmed, who was responsible, or what a legal or clinical authority would decide.

`Λ (Non-event / residue)` remains conditional. Absences may matter: no refusal, no confirmation, no explicit review point, no documented exit, no after-scene repair. But the absence of refusal cannot be treated as consent, and the absence of documented exit cannot be treated as coercion. `Α (Attractor)` is also conditional. Repetition, dependency, or patterned pressure would matter if supplied, but no recurrence or stabilized scene pattern is evidenced.

The central active operators are `Ω (Asymmetry)`, `Θ (Temporality)`, `Φ (Recontextualization)`, and `Χ (Distance)`. `Ω` makes the case readable as consent under uneven cost, capacity, exposure, access, refusal, or exit. Yet asymmetry is not itself wrongdoing. `Θ` keeps consent from being frozen into one moment: agreement, withdrawal, later contestation, repair, and after-scene interpretation may all matter. Yet later contestation is not automatically decisive. `Φ` allows an earlier consent frame to be reread if later structure reveals hidden cost, dependency, or constrained exit. Yet recontextualization is not retrospective omnipotence. `Χ` prevents all these readings from becoming verdicts. It is the operator that keeps the analysis from becoming accusation, diagnosis, legal conclusion, or person ranking.

`Σ (Integration)` and `Ψ (Self-binding)` remain conditional. The YAMLs do not supply an integrated scene-level enactment, nor do they supply durable self-binding, repeated care structure, after-scene obligation, or binding expectation. The analysis may discuss where integration or self-binding would become relevant, but it cannot assert them as findings. This is especially important because the topic is serious. Seriousness does not make later operators available cheaply.

PMS-SEX sharpens what the Core reading leaves domain-general. It asks how consent under asymmetry changes when the field is sexual-praxeological: when cost may be bodily, reputational, temporal, public, relational, interpretive, or after-scene; when direct otherness must remain available as refusal, delay, correction, non-identity, and withdrawal; and when autonomy or parity language may hide unequal cost. The overlay’s reduced shorthand can be summarized as `Δ∇□ → Ω/Θ → Φ/Χ`, with `Λ/Α/Σ/Ψ` conditional. That shorthand is not a new dependency graph. It records the case-specific emphasis: distinctions and tensions inside a sexual-consent frame make asymmetry and temporality central, while recontextualization and distance remain the key safeguards.

The PMS-SEX reading also functions here as a guarded validity-gate reading. Consent-talk is not invalid merely because asymmetry is present, but neither may it close the configuration by label. Consent-language remains analytically usable only if the frame can carry boundary, asymmetry, temporality, stop-capability, integration, and binding discipline. This is a structural validity gate, not a legal validity finding.

The MIP result matters because maturity language is especially risky in this case. The completed MIP layer treats A/M banding as YAML-internal configuration analysis, not as empirical maturity distribution. A-score and M-score language belongs to the abstract analysis object and role-context conditions; it does not describe the personal maturity, guilt, dignity, sexuality, adultness, or character of role_A, role_B, or any person. The IA result remains `IA_risk`: asymmetry is named and bounded, but transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and real reversibility are not established at scene level. Dignity-in-Practice is activated only as a qualitative protection lens. D is generally default-off in MIP, but here it is activated because the configuration makes dignity-in-practice protection analytically relevant. That activation remains guardrail-bound and qualitative only.

AHP contributes only second-order hardening. The case-specific AHP result is that the MIP article is high-precision as an analysis artefact, but still vulnerable to being overread. Its Precision Heuristic rests on term definition, scope boundaries, conditions and thresholds, evidence linking, reversibility, and language hygiene. Those dimensions harden readability; they do not add evidence, rescore A or M, revise the IA-box, activate D independently, or upgrade the working note into a decision.

##### What the analysis shows

The central movement of the case is:

PMS Core distinguishes consent from consent-label, then tracks how asymmetry and temporality make the field recontextualizable, while distance prevents the reading from becoming verdictive. PMS-SEX then shows that in a sexual-praxeological field, consent cannot be assessed without attention to cost topology, direct otherness, withdrawal/binding, and after-scene dignity. MIP adds that the configuration is maturity-sensitive but not person-scoring. AHP adds that high analytic precision itself creates a misuse risk if readers confuse structured analysis with evidentiary completion.

The case therefore shows a bounded form of closure. It can close against certain invalid shortcuts: formal agreement alone is not enough; asymmetry alone is not enough; later contestation alone is not enough; analytic coherence alone is not enough. But it cannot close the concrete scene. Λ-residue remains visible in the missing chronology, missing role accounts, missing evidence of real alternatives, missing publicness data, missing after-scene conduct, and missing reversibility conditions. Non-capture also remains visible: legal, phenomenological, communicative-consent, relational-autonomy, clinical, ethical, and anti-capture readings could preserve dimensions that PMS/PMS-SEX/MIP do not decide.

The case-specific drift risks are therefore conditional. `AD_A>>E` marks the danger that analytic awareness outruns enacted capacity, but it is not a finding that any role knew more than they enacted. `AD_Sigma_low` marks the lack of scene-level integration, but it is not a low score or a failure. PMS-SEX drift risks such as cost-topology mismatch, pseudo-symmetry, parity-to-exit, access-entitlement drift, covert-Ψ expectation, withdrawal expectation disowned, responsibility-to-authority, and after-scene dignity failure remain risk lenses. They do not identify defects in persons.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk of this case is closure through seriousness. Because the subject is consent, sexuality, asymmetry, and dignity, readers may feel that the analysis should decide more than it can. The case must therefore keep several boundaries visible inside the section itself.

First, consent-talk is structurally examined, not legally decided. Second, sexual cost topology is an overlay handle, not proof of harm. Third, Dignity-in-Practice is protective and praxeological, not a dignity ranking. Fourth, MIP’s A/M language is configuration-bound and provisional, not a maturity or adultness verdict. Fifth, AHP’s high precision is not validation. Sixth, the examples below are constructed illustrations; they do not supply missing case evidence.

##### Example 1 — Formal Agreement and Real Exit

A bounded vignette can begin with a deliberately minimal scene. The vignette is constructed for analytical illustration and is not supplied case material. Role_A and role_B are not persons but analytic role positions. Role_A proposes an intimate or sexually charged situation inside a context where role_A has more control over timing, framing, or social interpretation. Role_B does not refuse. Role_B may even agree in ordinary language. Nothing in the vignette states that role_A used force, that role_B lacked capacity, that a legal threshold was crossed, or that any concrete harm occurred. The scene remains bounded to a structural question: what follows, analytically, when formal agreement is present but real exit, real refusal, and cost distribution are not yet specified?

The example demonstrates why the PMS Core analysis cannot stop at the surface distinction “agreement occurred.” Agreement matters, but it does not exhaust the field. Δ separates formal agreement from meaningful consent, refusal from non-response, and free orientation from pressure-response. ∇ marks the directional tension between agreement, hesitation, compliance, withdrawal, or later contestation. □ defines the frame as a consent-under-asymmetry problem rather than as a legal, clinical, forensic, or moral verdict. Ω becomes relevant because the case turns on whether role_A and role_B bear equal or unequal costs of continuing, refusing, pausing, or exiting. Θ becomes relevant because the meaning of agreement may change if later withdrawal, delay, or contestation becomes structurally salient.

The key point is that the example does not allow either closure. It does not permit the sentence “Role_B agreed, therefore the case is settled.” But it also does not permit the opposite sentence “There was asymmetry, therefore consent was invalid.” PMS Core requires the analysis to stay inside the difficulty. Φ allows the agreement to be recontextualized if later evidence shows pressure, dependency, cost externalization, or constrained exit. Χ prevents that recontextualization from becoming a retrospective verdict. Σ remains conditional because no integrated scene-level pattern is established. The PMS-SEX overlay becomes relevant because this is not merely a generic asymmetry example. It is a sexual-praxeological field in which cost topology may matter. Does role_B carry more exposure, interpretive burden, bodily vulnerability, reputational risk, or after-scene ambiguity? None of these costs may be asserted as findings in the vignette. They are questions the overlay makes visible. Sexual cost topology is therefore a calibration interface, not a score. It does not rank suffering, maturity, dignity, sex, identity, or personhood.

If the example were written so that role_B’s silence or agreement became an access entitlement, it would violate the PMS-SEX boundary. It preserves the other as real.

MIP enters only as a guarded maturity-in-practice lens. The example may ask whether the configuration-handling would require awareness, coherence, responsibility, and power-to-act. It may ask whether real alternatives were available, whether asymmetry was transparent, whether refusal was permitted, whether exit was realistic, and whether review or repair could occur. It may not assign a maturity score to role_A or role_B. It may not call role_A immature or role_B harmed. It may not infer guilt, pathology, dignity failure, or adultness. AHP and boundary discipline are relevant as transmission controls. The example should not be quoted as a decision-ready scenario. It is a worked illustration of why formal completeness is not evidentiary completeness. AHP would flag any wording that makes the example sound like a finding. The surrounding boundary discipline requires the example to remain non-diagnostic, non-legal, non-forensic, non-clinical, non-prescriptive, and non-person-ranking.

The forbidden claims are therefore clear. The example cannot claim that valid consent occurred. It cannot claim that invalid consent occurred. ##### Example 2 — Later Contestation and the Reversibility Problem

A second bounded vignette can focus on temporality. The vignette is constructed for analytical illustration and is not supplied case material. Role_A and role_B pass through an initially ambiguous consent field. At the time of the interaction, the field appears settled enough for the participants or observers to describe it as mutually acceptable. Later, role_B contests the meaning of the earlier scene. The example does not specify whether role_B’s later contestation is legally relevant, clinically significant, morally justified, or empirically accurate. It also does not specify whether role_A acted wrongly. The only bounded question is structural: how should the analysis handle a scene whose meaning is re-opened over time?

This example demonstrates why Θ is not an optional embellishment in the case. Consent is temporally structured. Agreement, refusal, withdrawal, revision, and contestation do not always occur in a single clean moment. A person may understand a cost only later. A role may reinterpret pressure only after the frame changes. A later contestation may reveal an earlier asymmetry. But a later contestation may also overread, misremember, or reframe an event in ways that require independent evaluation. PMS therefore cannot treat the later statement as automatically decisive. It also cannot ignore it.

Δ remains necessary because the example requires distinctions among initial agreement, later contestation, withdrawal, revision, regret, pressure-response, and reinterpretation. ∇ marks the directional movement from apparent closure toward reopened meaning. □ keeps the analysis inside a structural consent-under-asymmetry frame and prevents immediate migration into legal, clinical, or moral judgment. Λ may become conditionally relevant if the scene contains meaningful non-events: no explicit refusal, no review point, no pause, no opportunity for correction, no after-scene repair. But Λ cannot be used cheaply. The absence of a refusal does not prove consent, and the absence of a recorded review point does not prove coercion.

Ω remains central because later contestation often concerns asymmetry: who had more control over timing, interpretation, social cost, exit, or public framing? Yet Ω does not decide the case. It only keeps uneven role conditions visible. Θ carries the main structural load. It marks that the meaning of the scene may not be exhausted by the initial description. Φ then allows recontextualization. The earlier scene may be reread in light of later contestation, but rereading is not rewriting. Φ is not retrospective omnipotence.

Χ is the operator that keeps the example scientifically usable. It prevents two invalid closures. The first invalid closure says: “Role_B later contested the scene, therefore the earlier consent was not meaningful.” The second says: “The scene seemed acceptable at the time, therefore later contestation is irrelevant.” Χ requires distance from both. It preserves the capacity to stop, review, test rival readings, and avoid verdict.

Σ remains conditional. There is no integrated enactment unless the example supplies concrete chronology, communication, role knowledge, alternatives, and after-scene handling. Ψ remains conditional because later contestation may imply that some binding expectation, after-scene dignity condition, or self-binding failure matters, but the vignette does not establish that. Ψ cannot be inferred simply because the topic is sexual or emotionally serious.

The PMS-SEX overlay helps specify what later contestation might reveal without deciding whether it does reveal it. Sexual cost topology may become visible only after the scene: exposure cost, reputational risk, bodily boundary concern, emotional residue, publicness, or responsibility externalization. But these remain possible cost dimensions. The example must not assert them as facts unless the vignette supplies evidence. PMS-SEX also keeps withdrawal and binding from being moralized. Later withdrawal is neither automatic guilt for role_A nor automatic exculpation for role_B. It is a temporally structured event whose meaning depends on the concrete scene.

Direct otherness is again central. Later contestation may be one way the other reappears as real rather than as a category inside the original script. Yet direct otherness cannot become an entitlement to explanation, confession, or public exposure. The example should show that correction matters without making correction compulsory in a way that violates the other’s boundary.

MIP reads the example as a maturity-in-practice problem only at the level of configuration-handling. The relevant questions are: Was the possibility of later recontextualization anticipated? Were review, pause, clarification, refusal, or repair available? Could role_A or role_B realistically carry the consequences of ambiguity? Did the frame permit correction without punishment? These are maturity-sensitive questions. But the example must not assign A-score or M-score to persons. It must not infer that role_A failed maturity or that role_B’s later contestation proves harm. It may only show why the configuration would require careful assessment before stronger claims.

AHP and boundary discipline would flag the example if it turned later contestation into a conclusion. AHP’s attack-surface concern is especially relevant here: the more polished the example becomes, the more it may seem evidentiary. The surrounding boundary discipline therefore requires repeated boundary language. The example is a structural demonstration, not a reconstruction.

Forbidden claims include: “later contestation proves the earlier scene was invalid,” “initial agreement disproves later contestation,” “role_A is responsible because role_B later objected,” “role_B is unreliable because the objection came later,” “the situation is clinically explained,” or “the law would decide it one way.” All of these exceed the vignette.

The non-capture note should point to rival frameworks. A phenomenological reading might ask how role_B’s experience changed over time. A legal reading might ask whether later contestation affects evidentiary standards. A communicative-consent reading might ask whether there were adequate opportunities for clarification. PMS does not replace those analyses. It clarifies why temporality and recontextualization must not be erased.

The article takeaway is that later contestation is structurally meaningful but not automatically decisive. Θ makes time visible. Φ permits rereading. Χ prevents overclaim. PMS-SEX shows why after-scene cost and dignity may matter. MIP shows why review and reversibility are maturity-sensitive. AHP and boundary discipline show why the example must remain a bounded analytic tool rather than a verdict.

##### Example 3 — A High-Precision Working Note Is Not a Decision

A third example should shift from the consent scene to the analysis artefact itself. The vignette is constructed for analytical illustration and is not supplied case material. A research team has produced a layered article draft from PMS Core, PMS-SEX, MIP, and AHP. The draft is formally complete. It contains operator analysis, overlay cost topology, MIP configuration-handling, AHP attack points, hardening actions, non-capture notes, falsifiers, and misuse boundaries. A reader encounters the document and says: “This is so structured that it must be ready for decision use.” The example is bounded to that interpretive moment. It does not ask what happened in the underlying consent field. It asks how a high-precision analysis can itself become a misuse risk.

This example demonstrates the importance of AHP and QC. The previous examples show why the case cannot be closed by consent or later contestation. This example shows why it also cannot be closed by formal analysis. A complete YAML-derived article can create authority-pressure. It can appear more evidentiary than it is. It can make abstract role positions sound like actual people. It can make attack points sound like case findings. It can make Precision Heuristic sound like validation. AHP exists to prevent that second-order misuse.

PMS Core hooks remain present. Χ is the most important. Application restraint applies not only to the original case but also to the article that renders it. If the article loses Χ, it becomes a closure machine. Φ is also relevant because the article itself can recontextualize the case: once a conceptually abstract consent field is translated into fluent academic prose, readers may treat it as a concrete scenario. Θ appears because transmission over time changes the artefact’s risk. A working note used internally has one status; the same text quoted publicly or applied institutionally has another.

Σ and Ψ must remain guarded. A polished article may seem integrated, but textual integration is not scene integration. The existence of a coherent chapter section does not establish an integrated event. Likewise, the article may discuss self-binding, responsibility, and after-scene dignity, but it cannot produce Ψ as a concrete finding. This example therefore demonstrates a meta-level version of the PMS dependency discipline: later textual operators cannot be used cheaply merely because the article is well written.

The PMS-SEX hooks are also present. Sexual cost topology, direct otherness, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, and after-scene dignity are rhetorically strong terms. If extracted from their guardrails, they can be misused as accusation language. A reader might treat “cost-topology mismatch” as a finding of harm, or “direct otherness” as proof that access was wrongly demanded, or “after-scene dignity” as proof of dignity violation. The example should show that PMS-SEX terms must remain calibration lenses unless concrete evidence supports stronger use. They are not scene findings by themselves.

MIP hooks are central. The MIP layer may contain A-band and M-band language, IA-risk, and D-module activation as qualitative protection. These terms are particularly vulnerable to misuse. A reader may treat A-band as person maturity, M-band as guilt, IA-risk as legal or moral verdict, and D-language as dignity judgment. The example must show why MIP’s own guardrails matter: configuration-handling is not character; shared structural responsibility is not blame; IA-risk is assessability risk; D₀ remains untouched; D₁/D₂ refer only to role-context enactment where evidence supports them.

AHP is the main layer illustrated. AHP identifies attack surfaces in the analysis artefact: formal completeness mistaken for evidentiary completeness, working-note status weakened by formal presentation, red-zone exclusions misread as known absence, IA thresholds not operationally staged, M-score ceiling needing further evidence categories, D-language needing explicit boundaries, and PMS-SEX terms inviting over-reading. The example demonstrates why these attack points are not embarrassments. They are part of scientific cleanliness. A serious article names how it can be misread.

Boundary status is likewise relevant. The relevant check is not whether the article is rhetorically persuasive, but whether it remains within permitted claims. It must preserve that no diagnosis is made, no person ranking occurs, no legal or forensic conclusion is implied, no clinical claim is introduced, no add-on becomes an operator, no drift becomes a verdict, no PH becomes validation, and no working note becomes decision basis. In this example, the article must display its own non-transmission boundary before any reader could treat it as usable outside scholarly analysis.

Forbidden claims include: “The high PH score means the case is true,” “the article is complete, so the event is decidable,” “AHP confirms the MIP reading,” “QC proves PMS is valid,” “attack points show defects in role_A or role_B,” “hardening actions are practical interventions,” or “working-note coherence authorizes public transmission.” Each claim confuses artefact quality with case truth.

The non-capture note is especially important here. AHP does not replace peer review, empirical method, legal procedure, clinical expertise, ethical deliberation, or affected-party testimony. It is a second-order overlay for analysis quality. It can harden the article, but it cannot supply missing facts. A high-precision working note may be more responsible than a vague note, but it is still a working note.

The article takeaway is that precision must not become authority. A well-structured PMS/PMS-SEX/MIP/AHP article demonstrates analytic discipline only if it preserves its own limits. The stronger the architecture, the more explicit the misuse boundary must be. This example prepares the case takeaway by showing that the case’s central lesson is not simply about consent under asymmetry. It is also about the ethics of formal analysis under conditions of missing evidence.

##### Case takeaway

This case shows how AXIOM can map consent under asymmetry without turning the map into a decision. #### 10.4.2 Consent Laundering

##### Why this case is here

“Consent Laundering” belongs in this chapter because it isolates a structurally difficult closure problem: a consent-sign can appear to settle a scene while simultaneously obscuring the conditions that made the consent meaningful. The case does not ask whether consent matters. It assumes that consent is powerful. The more precise question is what happens when the later invocation of consent becomes cleaner, more portable, or more authoritative than the frame in which consent was originally given.

The case is therefore not about the absence of consent in a simple sense. It is about a possible transformation in the use of consent. A sign of permission, agreement, or participation may later be invoked as a warrant, a closure device, a procedural shield, or a narrative stabilizer. In some cases this later invocation may be ordinary and legitimate: it may preserve accountability, clarify scope, or document agreement. In other cases, however, the same later invocation may make relevant conditions less visible: missing disclosure, missing refusal-space, missing publicness limits, missing alternatives, missing reversibility, missing cost recognition, or missing continuity between the original frame and the later use.

This is why the case is mapped rather than solved. The completed analysis treats “Consent Laundering” as a conceptual-domain projection, not as a concrete scene. No actors, testimony, legal record, institutional file, chronology, bodily facts, affected-side account, or evidentiary packet are supplied. The analysis can therefore render a structural phenomenon-class, but it cannot decide whether laundering occurred in any concrete case. This distinction is not a caution added from outside; it is central to the case itself. Consent laundering is a structure in which signs can become too authoritative. The analysis would contradict itself if it allowed its own formal rendering to become a new closure-sign.

The closure demand is double. Consent can close inquiry too quickly: “permission was given, therefore the matter is settled.” But critique of consent laundering can also close inquiry too quickly: “consent was later invoked, therefore the invocation is suspect.” The case is important because both closures are structurally tempting. The PMS reading refuses both. It preserves the distinction between a consent-sign and the conditions of its meaningfulness, while also preserving the possibility that later reference to consent may be transparent, bounded, justified, and non-laundering.

The case becomes especially relevant where consent operates across time and across frames. A consent-sign may originate in one setting and later appear in another: a private agreement becomes a public reference; an internal permission becomes external publication; a signed form becomes indefinite authorization; a bounded act becomes a broader narrative warrant. The issue is not the mere fact of movement. The issue is whether the later frame preserves the source frame’s scope, conditions, costs, alternatives, and reversibility.

For AXIOM, the case makes visible how a formal sign can become a demand for philosophical closure. It also shows why PMS must remain disciplined: Δ (Difference), □ (Frame), Θ (Temporality), Φ (Recontextualization), and Χ (Distance) are central, but they cannot be pushed into Σ (Integration) or Ψ (Self-binding) without further evidence. The case therefore belongs where the chapter examines how structural clarity must resist becoming verdictive authority.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement begins with Δ (Difference). The crucial distinction is between apparent consent and the conditions under which consent became meaningful. This is not merely a distinction between “yes” and “no.” It is a distinction between consent as event, consent as sign, consent as framed meaning, consent as later warrant, consent as legal or procedural status, and consent as moral or relational significance. If these are collapsed, the case cannot be read properly. Consent laundering becomes thinkable only where the consent-sign can be separated analytically from its conditions of production, its scope, and its later invocation.

∇ (Impulse) appears as structural directedness, not psychological motive. The analysis does not infer bad faith, manipulation, desire, or intent. The directional tension is formal: once a consent-sign exists, it may orient a field toward legitimation, sanitation, closure, or evidentiary stability. The sign can be used to settle something. That tendency does not prove laundering, but it marks why consent has closure force.

□ (Frame) is one of the central operators. Consent does not count in the abstract. It counts inside a frame that defines what permission means, what conditions are relevant, what alternatives matter, how scope is bounded, how later use is permitted, and what kind of publicness is implied. In the completed case, no concrete legal, institutional, sexual, relational, procedural, or cultural frame is selected. This is why the analysis remains conceptual. It can say that frame continuity matters; it cannot say which real frame governed a real event.

Λ (Non-event / residue) is relevant but calibration-sensitive. The possible residue includes missing disclosure, missing refusal-space, missing publicness limitation, missing scope clarification, missing withdrawal recognition, missing reviewability, or missing continuity between the original consent frame and the later invocation. But Λ must not be inflated. Not every absence is a structured non-event. Some absences are simply missing evidence. Some may belong to rival frameworks. Some may mark PMS non-capture. Λ only becomes structurally active where the frame made the missing element expectable.

Α (Attractor) remains weak. A phenomenon named “Consent Laundering” suggests possible recurrence or pattern formation, but the completed case does not supply a repeated institutional routine, stable role script, or path-dependent practice. Α would become stronger only if a concrete case showed recurrent consent-framing or repeated later invocation under similar conditions. Without that, attractor language must remain cautious.

Ω (Asymmetry) is conditionally important but not established. Consent laundering often becomes sharper where one role controls documentation, later invocation, publicness, interpretation, institutional procedure, or narrative continuity while another role carries exposure, exit cost, bodily cost, reputational cost, refusal burden, or after-scene consequences. But no concrete asymmetry is supplied. Ω therefore marks a major calibration site, not a finding. It must not be read as coercion, invalidity, domination, blame, or legal defect.

Θ (Temporality) is active. Consent laundering requires temporal movement: consent is obtained or framed at one point and invoked at another. The later use may transform the earlier sign’s function. A sign that was bounded, private, internal, or contextual may later operate as public authorization, institutional evidence, reputational measure, or closure device. This temporal structure is not incidental. It is one of the reasons the case is philosophically difficult.

Φ (Recontextualization) is the central operator. The case turns on the movement of a consent-sign from its source frame into a later target frame. Recontextualization may be legitimate: it can clarify scope, preserve accountability, or make a prior agreement intelligible. But it can also sanitize or obscure. A prior consent-sign may be made to carry more authority than the original frame warranted. The analytical task is therefore not to condemn recontextualization, but to discriminate between transparent frame-continuity and laundering-relevant frame shift.

Χ (Distance) is essential as both operator and application discipline. The analysis must not allow the consent-sign to close the case, and it must not allow suspicion of laundering to close the case either. Χ suspends verdict. It keeps the reading reversible, non-tribunal, and non-personal. In this case, Distance is not neutrality as indifference; it is the methodological condition under which consent can be examined without being either automatically accepted as closure or automatically dissolved into suspicion.

Σ (Integration) is weak and withheld. A smooth story that “consent occurred” may look integrated while excluding the conditions, costs, absences, asymmetries, or later shifts that make the sign contestable. This is one of the case’s central dangers: coherence itself can become part of the laundering structure. Integration would require holding consent, frame, cost topology, temporality, publicness, possible asymmetry, and rival readings together without erasing friction. The completed case does not provide enough material to claim that.

Ψ (Self-binding) is not active as a case structure. No concrete agent, institution, stable commitment, identity trajectory, or self-binding obligation is supplied. Ψ remains relevant only as discipline on the analysis itself: the model must be self-bound by its own constraints. It must not use formal clarity to force closure.

The PMS-SEX overlay sharpens what PMS Core leaves general. It does not replace PMS operators. Its handles make visible the sexual-domain or sexualized stakes that may become relevant if the consent-sign is tied to embodiment, boundary, direct otherness, withdrawal, publicness, or after-scene dignity. The key overlay contribution is cost topology: consent may be formally visible while bodily, reputational, access-related, exit-related, publicness-related, or after-scene costs remain unstaged. Pseudo-symmetry is another major handle: the existence of consent can make a scene appear balanced even where actual exit, review, refusal, or correction capacities are uneven. These remain risks, not findings.

Parity-to-exit and responsibility-to-authority are especially important. Parity-to-exit asks whether the role that apparently consented also had meaningful access to exit, review, withdrawal, or correction. Responsibility-to-authority asks whether the role carrying cost is also the role holding authority over later invocation, documentation, or publicness. Neither handle assigns responsibility. Both identify what a concrete scene would need to make visible.

The MIP layer adds a maturity-in-practice result specific to the analytic artifact. The A-reading remains provisional and artifact-bound: the analysis shows strong distinction-awareness, but the absence of scene data prevents stronger calibration. M is capped for any later concrete role application because no actor-specific knowledge, control, predictability, alternatives, or serious integration attempt are supplied. The IA-box result is IA-risk, not inadult asymmetry. D is active only as qualitative dignity-protection: D₀ remains untouched, and D-language must prevent shaming, person-reduction, public dignity labelling, or maturity ranking. The D-Quick-Grid is not used because no bounded role, context, or time window is supplied.

AHP adds only a second-order hardening result. It identifies how the analysis itself could be misread: formal completeness as evidence, working-note status as decision-readiness, PMS-SEX terms as scene findings, D-language as person judgment, M-ceiling as responsibility assignment, or IA-risk as verdict. Its function is to harden transmission, not to rescore the case.

##### What the analysis shows

The case shows a structural movement from consent-sign to frame travel. Its core movement can be rendered as a restrained composition centered on Δ, □, Θ, Φ, and Χ: a consent-sign is distinguished from its conditions, situated in a frame, carried across time, recontextualized into a later frame, and held under reflective distance rather than closure. ∇, Λ, Α, Ω, and Σ remain weak or conditional; Ψ is not active as a case structure.

What PMS makes visible is the possibility that consent can remain formally legible while becoming structurally overburdened. The sign may be asked to carry scope, publicness, legitimacy, authority, memory, and closure that were not fully contained in the original frame. What PMS-SEX makes visible is that, in sexual or sexualized domains, this overburdening can involve cost topology, direct otherness, embodiment, withdrawal, publicness, and after-scene dignity. What MIP makes visible is that responsible handling requires non-assignment: no person score, no maturity verdict, no responsibility escalation, no dignity evaluation. What AHP makes visible is that the article’s own precision could become misleading if it is mistaken for empirical evidence.

The central closure is therefore not “there was no consent” or “consent was invalid.” The central closure risk is: “there was consent, therefore the conditions no longer need to be reconstructed.” Consent laundering names the structure in which that closure may become too clean. The Λ-residue remains visible wherever the consent-sign does not account for missing scope, missing alternatives, missing publicness limits, missing review, missing cost distribution, or missing frame continuity. But residue is not automatically proof. It is a site for inquiry.

The strongest weakening condition is transparent frame-continuity. If a concrete case shows clear scope, visible costs, real alternatives, justified later use, reviewability, reversibility, and continuity between source frame and invocation frame, the laundering reading must be downgraded or rejected. Ordinary bounded reference to consent remains an important rival reading. Legal, procedural, ethical, phenomenological, language-practice, institutional-audit, and anti-capture readings also remain open.

##### Case-specific boundary

This case has a distinctive misuse risk because both consent and critique of consent can function as closure devices. The article must therefore not allow either side to become automatic. A consent-sign must not be treated as self-sufficient proof that all conditions were transparent. But a later objection, rupture, discomfort, missing detail, or frame shift must also not be treated as automatic proof of laundering.

The second distinctive risk is that PMS-SEX vocabulary can sound more concrete than the evidence. Terms such as cost topology, direct otherness, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, publicness, and after-scene dignity are overlay handles. They identify what would matter in a bounded scene. They do not establish that bodily cost, exposure, humiliation, coercion, invalidity, or dignity harm occurred.

The third distinctive risk is formal over-precision. This case has a layered, well-structured analysis. That formal completeness can make it appear more evidentiary than it is. AHP’s main case-specific function is to prevent that misreading: high precision of the artifact does not mean high evidence for a concrete case.

#### Example 1 — The consent record that becomes a closing device

A small research group holds an internal workshop. During the session, participants are asked whether brief comments from the discussion may be used later in a methodological appendix. The request is framed as low-stakes: the appendix will be anonymized, used only for internal training, and circulated within the group. Several participants agree. One participant says yes after hearing that the use will be “only illustrative” and “not attached to any personal evaluation.”

Months later, a sentence from that workshop appears in a public-facing report. It is still anonymized, but the surrounding context makes the speaker’s role indirectly recognizable to some readers in the field. When the participant objects, the report editor points to the earlier consent record: permission was given, anonymization was preserved, and the sentence was not used as a named attribution. The objection is therefore treated as late discomfort rather than as a reason to reopen the frame.

The vignette is bounded. It does not establish wrongdoing, invalid consent, bad faith, legal defect, institutional misconduct, or personal immaturity. It only supplies a structural scene in which a prior consent-sign is later invoked under a frame that may no longer be identical to the one in which the consent became meaningful. The example is deliberately non-sexual so that the PMS Core mechanism can be seen before the PMS-SEX overlay is introduced. Its function is to demonstrate how consent can remain formally legible while its scope, publicness, and later use become contested.

What the example demonstrates is the difference between a consent-sign and the conditions of its meaningfulness. The participant’s “yes” is not erased. The example does not imply that the participant did not consent. The question is narrower: whether the later invocation of that consent preserves the original frame or silently moves the consent-sign into a wider frame. If the original workshop frame was internal, low-stakes, anonymized, and non-evaluative, while the later report frame is public-facing, reputationally legible, and field-visible, then the later invocation cannot simply inherit the full authority of the earlier consent without further scrutiny.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. Δ appears in the distinction between consent as recorded and consent as condition-bound. □ appears in the workshop frame: internal use, anonymization, illustrative purpose, limited circulation. Λ appears as possible structured absence: the absence of explicit notice about public circulation, indirect recognizability, or later reputational effects. Θ appears in the temporal gap between workshop consent and later report use. Φ is the central hook: the earlier consent-sign is recontextualized into a later publication frame. Χ is required from the analyst: the example must not become an accusation against the editor or a validation of the participant’s objection. Σ remains conditional because the scene may or may not be integrable once the original and later frames are compared. Ψ is not active unless the institution’s stable obligations, policies, and self-binding commitments are explicitly supplied.

The add-on hooks are limited. PMS-SEX is not activated by this vignette as a sexual-domain overlay, because no sexual or sexualized scene is present. That absence is analytically useful. It shows that Consent Laundering is not reducible to PMS-SEX; PMS-SEX sharpens certain cases but does not define the PMS Core pattern. However, the example does include publicness and dignity-adjacent concerns in a weak, non-sexual sense: indirect recognizability, reputational exposure, and the possibility that a person is reduced to a previously captured statement. These remain general PMS/MIP concerns, not PMS-SEX findings.

For this vignette, a MIP-style reading would remain guarded. No A-score or M-score should be assigned to any person. If the vignette were used in the article, it would support a qualitative discussion of role-position and reviewability: the editor may hold a frame-control role, the participant may occupy a cost-bearing or exposure-bearing role, and the institution may mediate publicness. But responsibility cannot be assigned without more information: what exactly was said at the workshop, what documentation existed, what review process was available, what alternatives the editor had, and whether the participant’s objection was processed through a reversible mechanism. IA status would remain a risk check rather than a verdict: transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility would have to be examined.

AHP-style relevance for the article lies in the risk that the example itself becomes too persuasive. A reader may treat the vignette as proof that the editor “laundered” consent. That would be too strong. The example should be used to clarify attack surfaces: formal consent records may be mistaken for full evidentiary completeness; a tidy narrative may conceal a frame shift; publicness may be under-specified; and later invocation may need a review clause. The example therefore demonstrates why the article’s own language must remain reversible and non-accusatory.

Forbidden claims are clear. The example must not claim that the participant’s consent was invalid. It must not claim that the editor acted deceptively. It must not claim legal liability, ethical guilt, psychological motive, institutional abuse, or dignity violation. It must not rank either role as mature or immature. It must not treat the participant’s later objection as automatically authoritative, nor the consent record as automatically dispositive.

The non-capture note is also important. A legal reading might say the permission language was broad enough. A discourse-pragmatic reading might focus on how “internal,” “illustrative,” or “anonymous” functioned in the workshop conversation. An institutional audit might ask whether report-publication procedures required renewed approval. A phenomenological reading might ask how the participant experienced recognizability. PMS can frame the structure, but it does not exhaust these rival readings.

The article takeaway is that consent-signs travel. When they travel, they may retain continuity, or they may be recontextualized beyond their original frame. The example prepares the reader to understand why Consent Laundering is not “consent was absent,” but “the later use of consent may obscure the conditions under which consent became meaningful.”

#### Example 2 — The private boundary that becomes a public permission-image

Two adults in a private relationship agree to a bounded form of intimacy under a shared understanding: the agreement concerns that evening, that setting, and that level of privacy. The vignette does not describe the act, the people, the relationship history, or the validity of consent. It only supplies a minimal structure: a private consent-sign exists inside a bounded interpersonal frame.

Later, one person refers to that prior consent in a semi-public social setting. The reference is not explicit enough to be a detailed disclosure, but it is clear enough to make the other person feel indirectly exposed. When discomfort is raised, the response is: “But you agreed to it.” The prior consent-sign is thus invoked as if it settled not only the original private boundary, but also the later social meaning of mentioning it.

This is a high-risk example, so it must remain abstract and guardrail-only. It is not a scene of sexual wrongdoing. It is not a claim about coercion, invalid consent, humiliation, violation, trauma, or abuse. It does not provide bodily details or role-specific blame. Its purpose is to demonstrate why PMS-SEX is relevant as an overlay when consent, privacy, embodiment, publicness, and after-scene dignity can become entangled.

The bounded vignette demonstrates a possible gap between permission within a private frame and later permission-image in a social frame. The earlier consent may have been real within its frame. The later question is whether that consent can be invoked as authorization for public or semi-public reference. The laundering risk is not that the original consent did not happen. It is that the later invocation may use the original consent-sign to close inquiry into publicness, exposure, privacy, and after-scene standing.

The PMS Core hooks begin with Δ: private consent within a bounded scene must be distinguished from later public or semi-public invocation. □ is the private interpersonal frame, which sets the scope of what the consent-sign means. Λ may appear as missing publicness clarification or missing later-use boundary, but only if the frame made such clarification expectable. Ω is possible but not established: one person may control disclosure, social framing, or reputational exposure, but the example does not prove a power imbalance. Θ is essential because the later invocation changes the temporal location of the consent-sign. Φ is central because the prior private consent is recontextualized as a later permission-image. Χ must be maintained so the article does not become a verdict about either person. Σ is not assumed; the private and public frames may or may not be integrable after review. Ψ is not active unless a stable commitment or role obligation is supplied.

The PMS-SEX hooks are stronger here than in Example 1. The overlay makes visible that sexual-domain consent may carry costs not captured by formal permission language alone. Cost topology includes privacy cost, exposure cost, reputational cost, bodily-boundary meaning, and after-scene dignity. Direct otherness is also relevant: the other person must not be reduced to the fact that they previously consented. The prior consent-sign does not exhaust their standing in the later social frame. Withdrawal or binding is relevant because the later statement “you agreed” may treat the earlier consent as continuing authorization beyond its bounded context. Publicness is central because the meaning of a private act can change when brought into a social field.

Yet the overlay must not overreach. PMS-SEX does not establish harm. It does not decide that the later reference was degrading. It does not determine whether the social setting was truly public, whether the reference was identifiable, whether the speaker intended exposure, or whether the original frame included later mention. These are scene facts that would need evidence. PMS-SEX only names why those facts matter.

For this vignette, a MIP-style reading remains protective and non-scoring. No person receives an A-score, M-score, dignity score, or maturity label. A MIP reading could ask whether the later-invocation role had knowledge of publicness, control over disclosure, predictability of exposure, alternatives to mention, and serious integration attempt after discomfort was raised. But without concrete evidence, M remains capped and not person-assignable. The IA-box would remain IA-risk unless transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility are established. D may be active only as dignity-protection: D₀ untouched, no D-score, no public D-reading of identifiable persons, no claim that someone “lacks dignity” or “damaged dignity.”

AHP-style hardening would flag language risk. This example can easily become morally charged. Terms such as “public permission-image,” “after-scene dignity,” and “exposure” are analytically useful but rhetorically strong. The article should therefore attach boundary language wherever this example appears: the vignette demonstrates a possible structure; it does not establish a case finding. AHP-style review would also flag publicness-context collapse: the private frame and later social frame must not be blurred.

Forbidden claims include: the original consent was invalid; the later mention was abuse; the speaker is manipulative; the other person is harmed as a matter of fact; a legal privacy violation occurred; the scene proves sexual misconduct; the discomfort automatically determines the ethical meaning; or the prior consent automatically authorizes later reference. All of those require more than the vignette supplies.

The non-capture note matters especially here. A phenomenological reading might focus on how the exposed person experienced the later reference. A legal reading might ask whether privacy, defamation, or consent-to-disclosure rules apply. A relationship ethics reading might ask what duties persist after intimacy. A language-practice reading might examine what was actually said and how identifiable it was. PMS-SEX can make these issues visible, but it cannot replace them.

The article takeaway is that sexual-domain consent does not automatically travel into later publicness. The example demonstrates how a consent-sign may remain formally true while becoming structurally unstable when invoked outside the frame that made it meaningful. It prepares the article’s later conclusion by showing why the central issue is not anti-consent suspicion, but frame-sensitive handling of consent under time, publicness, and dignity-protection constraints.

#### Example 3 — The signed form and the invisible cost topology

A small organization uses a standard consent form for participation in a media project. The form states that participants agree to the use of their image and comments in future educational materials. A participant signs it. The project later becomes more visible than expected: the materials are used at a conference, uploaded to a restricted professional platform, and cited in a grant report. The participant is not named, but their image, role, and context make them recognizable to some colleagues.

When the participant asks whether the material can be withdrawn from future use, the project lead replies that the signed form already granted permission. The form is treated as sufficient authorization for all later uses within the broad category of “educational materials.” The organization does not refuse conversation, but it has no clear review procedure, no staged publicness categories, and no explicit distinction between internal teaching, conference presentation, platform circulation, and grant reporting.

This example is more developed than the first two because it brings PMS Core, PMS-SEX-adjacent publicness, MIP, and AHP together. It is still bounded. It does not assert illegality, coercion, exploitation, clinical harm, or moral blame. It does not claim the form is invalid. It does not decide whether the participant should have read it differently or whether the organization acted wrongly. The vignette demonstrates how a formal consent instrument can become structurally overburdened when later uses multiply and cost topology remains under-specified.

The example demonstrates the difference between formal scope and lived or practical scope. The written form may be formally broad. It may even be procedurally valid. The PMS question is not whether broad wording exists. It is whether the later invocation of the signed form preserves transparency about publicness, recognizability, review, reversibility, and after-scene consequences. If “educational materials” covers radically different publicness levels, the consent-sign may be carrying more later justificatory weight than the original frame made visible.

The PMS Core hooks are distributed across the whole vignette. Δ distinguishes signing the form from understanding future publicness gradients. ∇ appears as the organization’s structural orientation toward efficient authorization and continued use, without implying bad motive. □ is the form-governed participation frame. Λ may appear in missing staged publicness categories, missing withdrawal procedure, or missing review points, if those are reasonably expected in the frame. Α may become more active than in the abstract case because organizational repetition and standard forms suggest possible routine, though the example still does not prove systemic pattern. Ω is possible where the organization controls documentation, circulation, and withdrawal procedure while the participant bears recognizability cost. Θ is active because the form’s meaning changes across later uses. Φ is central: the original consent-sign is placed into conference, platform, and grant-report frames. Χ is required so the example remains non-verdictive. Σ is conditional because the form, later uses, participant objection, and organizational review process may or may not be integrable. Ψ could become relevant only if the organization has explicit self-binding policies or commitments over time; otherwise it remains unassigned.

The PMS-SEX overlay may or may not be relevant depending on the content of the media project. If the image or comments concern sexualized, bodily, intimate, or boundary-sensitive material, PMS-SEX becomes relevant. If not, PMS-SEX should remain mostly inactive, with only general publicness and dignity-adjacent concerns carried by PMS/MIP. This conditionality is important. The example demonstrates how an overlay should be activated by domain features, not by rhetorical convenience.

Where PMS-SEX is relevant, the key hook is cost topology. A signed form may name permission while leaving costs unstaged: recognizability, exposure, professional reputation, bodily display, role-identification, platform persistence, future audience expansion, and withdrawal difficulty. Direct otherness matters because the participant must not be reduced to the form they signed. Publicness matters because a restricted platform, conference room, and grant report do not have the same social meaning. After-scene dignity matters only as a protective lens, never as a dignity finding.

For this vignette, a MIP-style check is especially useful. The analysis should not assign scores to the participant or project lead. Instead, it can identify what would be needed for a stronger maturity-in-practice reading. For M, the five factors would need concrete evidence: what the organization knew about future uses; how much control it had; whether recognizability was predictable; whether alternatives or withdrawal mechanisms were available; and whether it seriously integrated the participant’s later concern. Without that, M remains a ceiling condition rather than responsibility assignment. For IA, the case would require T/J/TB/R examination: Was the future-use scope transparent? Was the later use justified by a protected purpose? Was the consent time-bound or reviewable? Was withdrawal or correction possible?

D remains protective. If the participant becomes recognizable in professional circulation, D-P risk may be relevant as public dignity protection. But the example must not say that the participant’s dignity was harmed as a fact or that the organization degraded them. D₀ remains untouched. D₁ is not assessed. D₂ and D-P, if used, only guide anti-shaming, non-ranking, and reversibility-sensitive analysis.

AHP-style hardening would identify at least three attack surfaces. First, formal completeness may be mistaken for evidentiary completeness: a signed form can look decisive while later-use conditions remain under-described. Second, working-note status matters: if this vignette were used in the article, it must not become an implied accusation against real organizations. Third, minimum evidence thresholds should be staged before stronger claims: the example itself shows why AHP recommends evidence-status matrices, publicness categories, IA thresholds, and M-factor staging.

#### 10.4.3 Boundary Confusion

##### Why this case is here

*Boundary Confusion* is included because it shows a distinctive closure demand: once a boundary is named, the analysis is tempted to decide where the line is, who crossed it, who owns it, who may define it, who must repair it, and who is responsible for the confusion. The case is structurally difficult because “boundary” is not one thing. It may refer to a role distinction, responsibility allocation, decision threshold, spatial limit, privacy claim, legal right, institutional mandate, relational expectation, or authority structure. These different forms can overlap, but they cannot be treated as identical.

The completed analysis treats the case as a conceptual domain projection rather than a concrete scene. That matters. The case does not supply actors, chronology, evidence, institutional setting, affected-party perspective, or an enacted boundary event. The task is therefore not to decide whether a boundary was actually violated. The task is to show how PMS can render the structural movement of boundary confusion while refusing premature closure.

This makes the case useful for AXIOM because it tests whether the framework can remain precise under conceptual pressure. *Boundary Confusion* is highly legible for PMS: it immediately invites Δ (Difference), □ (Frame), possible Λ (Non-event / residue), possible Α (Attractor), possible Ω (Asymmetry), possible Θ (Temporality), possible Φ (Recontextualization), Χ (Distance), possible Σ (Integration), and possible Ψ (Self-binding). The danger is not that the model cannot read the case. The danger is that it can read it too smoothly.

The case also belongs here because the completed stack contains a PMS-SEX overlay, a MIP layer, and an AHP precision layer. Their function is not to intensify the verdict. It is to sharpen layer separation. PMS Core maps the boundary/frame structure. PMS-SEX checks whether a sexual-praxeological scene is actually available and stops at non-capture. MIP checks whether maturity-in-practice language can be used without scoring persons and refuses ordinary A/M assignment. AHP marks the analysis artifact’s own vulnerabilities, especially the risk that formal completeness is mistaken for evidentiary completeness.

The case is therefore mapped rather than solved. It is not an empirical case report, not a diagnostic reading, not a legal or forensic determination, and not a maturity or adultness verdict. Its value lies in showing how a layered formal analysis can name a structural problem while preserving the gap between readability and justified closure.

##### Structural reading

At the PMS Core level, the strongest entry point is preserving the gap between readability and justified closure.

##### Structural reading

At the PMS Core level, the strongest entry point is **Δ (Difference)**. *Boundary Confusion* becomes readable because it concerns distinctions: one role is not another role, one responsibility is not another responsibility, one space is not another space, one decision is not another decision. But Δ alone does not decide what kind of boundary is involved. A role boundary differs from a responsibility boundary; a responsibility boundary differs from a spatial boundary; a spatial boundary differs from a decision boundary; a decision boundary differs from authority, access, privacy, or obligation. The first calibration task is therefore to ask which difference is being treated as a boundary and whether multiple boundary types are being collapsed under one term.

The second central operator is **□ (Frame)**. A boundary becomes meaningful only inside a frame. A family frame, institutional frame, care frame, legal frame, privacy frame, authorship frame, medical frame, or decision-authority frame may make the same action legible in different ways. A crossing in one frame may appear as care in another, overreach in a third, mandate in a fourth, and violation in a fifth. The Core reading therefore treats the case not as “a boundary was crossed,” but as “a distinction becomes boundary-relevant under a frame.” This is the minimal Core movement: **□ ∘ Δ**.

**∇ (Impulse)** remains weak. Boundary confusion may involve pressure to cross, defend, clarify, contest, withdraw, repair, or reassign a boundary. But the completed case does not supply an enacted orientation. It gives no concrete movement, motive, urgency, refusal, takeover, or repair attempt. ∇ may mark possible directed tension, but it cannot become a psychological explanation.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is conditional. Boundary cases often depend on what did not happen: no clarification, no refusal, no permission, no repair, no acknowledgment, no decision, no restraint. But in this case no expected event is specified. Λ would require a frame-specific expectation. Without that, absence remains underdetermined. The analysis must not assume that every unclear boundary is caused by a missing clarification.

**Α (Attractor)** is a weak-to-moderate candidate. The case-type mentions boundaries being repeatedly crossed, and repetition can indicate pattern formation. Yet the YAML supplies no repeated episodes, no sequence, and no evidence of stabilization. Α therefore remains a possible calibration point, not a finding. The word “repeatedly” is not enough; PMS requires structurally comparable recurrence.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is possible but weak. Boundary confusion may involve uneven authority, exposure, vulnerability, access, responsibility, interpretive control, or burden of correction. One position may define the boundary while another carries its cost. But the direction and magnitude of asymmetry are not supplied. The analysis may ask Ω-questions, but it cannot assign asymmetry as a concrete fact.

**Θ (Temporality)** is likewise weak and conditional. Repeated crossing suggests duration, accumulation, anticipation, escalation, delay, or failed repair. But no chronology is given. There are no dates, order of events, intervals, after-effects, or repair attempts. Θ marks what would be needed for a stronger trajectory reading; it is not an observed trajectory.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is important because boundary confusion often arises through rival frames. The same act may be recontextualized as support, intrusion, shared responsibility, overreach, autonomy, abandonment, flexibility, or violation. Φ helps make those frame shifts visible. But it must not absorb every disagreement. A valid Φ-reading needs a source frame, a target frame, and a discriminative gain. If every refusal or rival reading is redescribed as recontextualization, PMS becomes a closure machine.

**Χ (Distance)** is active as application discipline. It prevents the reading from fusing into verdict. In this case, Χ is not merely one operator among others; it governs the responsible use of the whole analysis. The reading must remain reversible, non-totalizing, non-diagnostic, and non-person-ranking. Χ also prevents the PMS-SEX overlay from sexualizing the case and prevents MIP from turning formal maturity language into a person score.

**Σ (Integration)** remains weak. Boundary confusion may require integration of roles, frames, expectations, responsibilities, and refusals. But no integrated solution, failed integration, or concrete coordination is supplied. Σ would be overclaimed if it became forced coherence: smoothing over conflict, suppressing refusal, erasing separation, or treating PMS coherence as proof.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is weak and mostly analytic. A concrete boundary case might involve durable promises, role ownership, accountability, or responsibility-as-self-commitment. The completed case does not supply that. Ψ is therefore not a case-level structure. It matters mainly as application self-binding: the analysis itself must remain bound to distance, reversibility, and dignity-in-practice.

The PMS-SEX overlay sharpens the boundary of applicability. It uses the Core anchors **Δ**, **□**, and **Χ** as a scope-guard signature: **Δ/□/Χ scope-guard shorthand only**. This is not a new PMS dependency path. It means that the sexual overlay sees a boundary distinction, a frame, and the need for restraint, but no sexual-praxeological scene. There is no sexual access threshold, no sexual cost topology, no direct-otherness sexual encounter, no body-boundary or genital meaning, no withdrawal/binding profile, no after-scene dignity material, and no sexual drift chain. PMS-SEX therefore contributes a negative but important result: the case must not be sexualized by analogy.

The MIP layer adds a case-specific maturity discipline. Awareness and coherence are strongest at the documentation level: the analysis knows its own Core, SEX, and MIP boundaries. Responsibility attaches primarily to analysis conduct: preserving limits, keeping non-capture visible, and preventing score or maturity language from becoming a verdict. Power-to-act lies in wording, review, and transmission control. No ordinary A-score or M-score is assigned because there is no concrete enactment, role-holder, time window, predictability, access to alternatives, or serious integration attempt. D remains guardrail-only; the D-module is not activated as a full profile.

The AHP layer hardens the analysis artifact rather than the case. Its most important point is that formal completeness may be mistaken for evidentiary completeness. The analysis is precise as an artifact, but the underlying case remains abstract. AHP also marks transmission-status ambiguity, possible over-reading of red-zone false values, unstaged IA thresholds, implicit future M-score escalation thresholds, D-language risks, and PMS-SEX technical-term overreach. None of this changes the case reading. It only makes the risks of presentation explicit.

##### What the analysis shows

The central structural result is:

**Boundary Confusion = Core □ ∘ Δ under Χ discipline, with later operators weak, conditional, or analytic unless concrete scene evidence is supplied.**

This formula is intentionally modest. It says that the case is structurally readable as a framed distinction problem. It does not say that a boundary was violated, that a person failed, that a responsibility was established, or that a maturity verdict follows.

The distinctive misuse risk in this case is over-closure through formal elegance. Because *Boundary Confusion* maps easily onto PMS, the reading can appear stronger than the material warrants. The article must therefore preserve the difference between conceptual projection and scene finding.

#### Example 1 — The shared document that became a role boundary

A research team maintains a shared draft for a conference paper. One member, A, regularly rewrites sections written by another member, B. At first, this is interpreted as ordinary collaboration: the file is shared, the deadline is close, and everyone has permission to improve the argument. After several rounds, B says that the edits no longer feel like collaboration but like displacement. A replies that the document is collective, that no one “owns” a paragraph, and that the project requires decisive revision. The team lead has not specified whether contributors retain section authority, whether edits require prior discussion, or whether the final argument belongs to the group as a whole. No one has yet accused anyone of misconduct. No formal policy is invoked. The case remains a bounded academic collaboration vignette about role, authorship, and decision boundaries.

This example demonstrates why *Boundary Confusion* cannot be solved by the word “boundary” alone. At the PMS Core level, Δ is immediately visible: there is a distinction between contribution and control, editing and replacing, collaboration and takeover, shared authorship and local authorship. But Δ does not yet decide whether A crossed a boundary. That depends on □, the frame. In a collective drafting frame, A’s conduct may be ordinary revision. In a section-authorship frame, the same conduct may be overreach. In a deadline-management frame, it may be emergency coordination. In a mentorship frame, it may be guidance. In a peer-equality frame, it may become unilateral authority.

The example also shows why ∇ remains cautious. There is directed action: A edits, B objects, the draft changes. But the vignette does not establish motive. It does not show domination, insecurity, hostility, carelessness, rescue, or ambition. ∇ can mark directional pressure toward revision and control of the text, but it cannot become a psychological explanation. Λ may become relevant if an expected clarification is absent: perhaps the team lead should have defined edit authority; perhaps A should have asked before replacing B’s prose; perhaps B should have stated earlier what level of revision was acceptable. Yet the example does not automatically decide which expectation governs. Λ requires a frame-specific expectation, not merely disappointment.

Α and Θ are plausible but still bounded. The repeated edits suggest possible pattern formation, and the sequence across several rounds gives minimal temporality. Still, this is not enough to call the pattern stabilized drift. The example would require more detail: how many revisions, over what period, whether only B’s sections were rewritten, whether A responds differently to different collaborators, whether objections were heard, and whether the process changed after clarification. Ω is also possible but not settled. A may have more technical expertise, institutional standing, confidence, or access to the team lead; B may carry more exposure because their visible contribution is being erased. But asymmetry cannot be presumed simply because B feels displaced.

Φ is central because the dispute is partly a clash of frames. A recontextualizes the file as a shared product. B recontextualizes the same file as a field of contribution boundaries. The team lead may recontextualize it again as a deadline problem. PMS can make these frame shifts visible, but it must not treat every recontextualization as progress. If A uses “collective work” to erase local authorship, Φ may conceal rather than clarify. If B uses “my section” to block all integration, Φ may preserve a boundary that the project frame does not support. The point is not to decide but to show what must be discriminated.

Χ is the main guardrail. The analysis must not jump to “A violated B,” “B is territorial,” “the team lead failed,” or “the group is immature.” Σ remains conditional: the group may need to integrate contribution boundaries and collective authorship, but no integration has yet occurred. Ψ remains weak: durable commitments, authorship obligations, and responsibility-as-self-binding would require explicit agreement or repeated accountability over time.

The PMS-SEX overlay is not substantively relevant. The case contains no sexual scene, no sexual access threshold, no body-boundary or genital meaning, no sexual cost topology, and no after-scene sexual carryover. PMS-SEX would function only as a scope guard if someone tried to import sexuality or embodied intimacy into the word “boundary” by analogy. That would be an overreach. The add-on hook is therefore negative: the example confirms why the SEX overlay must not activate merely because boundary language is present.

MIP would also remain cautious. Awareness and coherence can be discussed at the documentation level: the team may become more aware of role boundaries, and a coherent process may need to be created. But no A-score or M-score should be assigned to A, B, or the team lead from this vignette. The case lacks enough information about knowability, alternatives, power, predictability, and serious integration attempts. AHP would flag an analysis risk if the example were written as though repeated editing already proved responsibility, domination, or immaturity. The precision task is to keep “possible pattern” separate from “finding.”

Forbidden claims are therefore clear. The example does not prove plagiarism, harassment, legal authorship violation, clinical control dynamics, narcissism, immaturity, exploitation, or moral fault. It does not rank A and B. It does not decide the correct policy. It does not validate PMS as sufficient for academic governance. It only demonstrates how a boundary case requires frame discipline before responsibility language can become justified.

The non-capture note is important. A publication ethics framework, institutional authorship policy, supervisor-student power analysis, or ordinary collaborative writing norm may be more decisive than PMS if concrete facts are added. PMS helps sort the structure; it does not replace those frameworks.

This example shows that *Boundary Confusion* begins with Δ and □, not with blame. It demonstrates how PMS can slow down closure: distinguish the boundary, name the frame, test recurrence and asymmetry, preserve rival readings, and refuse Σ or Ψ until the evidence warrants them.

#### Example 2 — The family appointment and the responsibility boundary

An adult daughter, A, accompanies her elderly father, B, to a medical appointment. B has asked A to help him remember details, but he has not clearly asked her to speak for him. During the appointment, the clinician asks B about medication changes. A answers before B does, explains the situation in detail, and asks the clinician to adjust the plan. B remains quiet. Later, B tells A that he felt “managed” rather than supported. A replies that she was only helping, that B often forgets important details, and that the appointment was too short for hesitation. No formal guardianship, incapacity ruling, emergency, or written proxy is present in the vignette. The scene is bounded as a family-care and appointment-support situation, not as a legal or clinical case analysis.

This example demonstrates why boundary confusion is structurally difficult when care, dependence, and authority overlap. PMS Core begins with Δ: there is a distinction between helping someone remember and speaking in their place; between support and substitution; between accompanying and deciding; between practical assistance and authority. But none of these distinctions is self-interpreting. The governing □ matters. In a care frame, A’s action may appear protective. In an autonomy frame, it may appear intrusive. In a medical-efficiency frame, it may appear pragmatic. In a legal-authority frame, it may be impermissible unless authorized. In a family-history frame, it may repeat an old pattern of overmanagement or rescue.

∇ is visible as directed pressure: A moves the conversation forward, answers quickly, and tries to secure a medication change. But PMS must not infer motive. The example does not establish domination, anxiety, contempt, competence, dependence, or pathology. A may be caring, worried, controlling, habituated, or simply responding to time pressure; PMS does not decide this. Λ may also be present. The missing event could be explicit authorization: B did not clearly say whether A may speak for him. It could also be missing pause: A did not wait to see whether B would answer. Or it could be missing clarification by the clinician: the clinician did not ask who should answer. Yet the example cannot decide which absence is decisive without a frame.

Ω is more salient here than in the shared-document example, but still not a verdict. There may be asymmetry in age, health, memory, family role, conversational speed, medical literacy, and institutional comfort. A may have more communicative power in the appointment. B may carry the dignity exposure of being spoken over. But asymmetry does not automatically mean wrongdoing. It creates a responsibility-sensitive field. The analysis must ask whether the asymmetry was named, bounded, and carried, or whether it silently shifted decision power.

Θ matters because the vignette hints at recurrence: A says B often forgets details. If this is a repeated pattern, an Attractor may form around A speaking and B yielding. But the example does not show a history. One appointment is not enough to establish a stabilized role script. If later material showed repeated substitution, B’s increasing silence, or A’s refusal to recalibrate after objection, Α and Θ would strengthen. If later material showed that B had repeatedly asked A to take over in appointments, the reading would change.

Φ is central because the same action can be recontextualized. A frames it as help under time pressure. B frames it as being managed. A medical frame might focus on accuracy; a dignity-in-practice frame might focus on whether B’s agency remained visible; a legal frame might focus on authority; a family-system frame might focus on long-term role drift. PMS can hold these rival frames without collapsing them. Χ requires the analysis to avoid verdict: A is not diagnosed as controlling, B is not ranked as dependent, and the clinician is not assigned legal fault from the vignette.

Σ remains conditional. A mature coordination might involve an explicit appointment protocol: B answers first; A adds details when invited; the clinician checks consent before accepting A’s answer. But this integrated solution is not yet present in the vignette. Ψ remains conditional as well. Durable responsibility-as-self-binding would require repeated commitments: A binding herself to support without substitution, B clarifying desired assistance, or the clinical frame establishing a stable protocol. The vignette suggests such needs; it does not establish them.

PMS-SEX is not activated. Although the scene involves bodies, vulnerability, and care, it is not a sexual-praxeological scene. There is no sexual access threshold, no sexual cost topology, no direct-otherness sexual encounter, no genital meaning, and no withdrawal/binding sexual residue. The PMS-SEX add-on, if mentioned, functions only as a guard against overextension: body-related or intimate care boundaries must not be sexualized by default.

Awareness may concern whether A understands the difference between helping and substituting. Coherence may concern whether the appointment role matches the family’s stated expectations. Responsibility may concern who had realistic power to clarify the speaking boundary. Power-to-act may be distributed among A, B, and the clinician. But the vignette does not warrant an A-score or M-score. It lacks sufficient evidence about B’s prior preferences, A’s knowledge, clinical constraints, urgency, alternatives, and predictability. D remains guardrail-only unless a full D-profile is explicitly warranted by concrete evidence.

The examples show the same methodological point in reusable forms. A shared document, a family appointment, and a personal-boundary conversation each make boundary confusion concrete without becoming evidence for the original case. They show why Δ and □ must be calibrated, why Ω and Θ cannot be assumed, why Φ must not absorb rival readings, why Σ and Ψ remain conditional, and why overlays must stop when their scene material is absent.

The remaining residue is the unresolved boundary itself: which boundary type, which frame, which expectation, which recurrence, which asymmetry, and which rival framework would govern a concrete case. The chapter should use *Boundary Confusion* as a model of restraint: PMS can make the structure visible, but the strongest result is knowing where the analysis must stop.

### 10.5 Summary

Part VII establishes SEX as the PMS add-on layer for consent-adjacent validity questions under asymmetry, temporality, body-related cost topology, publicness, and priced distance. The cases in this part do not treat consent as a single utterance, a moral token, a proof object, a personality marker, or a legal finding. They treat consent-talk as a configuration claim: a description is structurally coherent only if the surrounding frame can actually carry agreement, refusal, boundary, cost, time, stop-capability, and re-binding.

The common result is not that PMS-SEX decides whether consent was valid or invalid in any concrete scene. The result is that PMS-SEX makes visible where consent-talk remains structurally under-supported: agreement may coexist with dependency; mutuality may conceal unequal cost; earlier agreement may be invoked to block review; boundary language may remain unframed; ambiguity may be stabilized as a script; and a precise analysis artefact may itself become too authoritative when transmitted.

SEX therefore does not replace PMS core. PMS remains the operator grammar. SEX adds overlay handles for consent-specific validity risks: sexual cost topology, body/publicness residue, covert Ψ demand, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, asymmetry-switch risk, Λ-as-steering, Χ loss, boundary confusion, publicness sedimentation, and consent laundering. These handles are not person labels, practice labels, identity labels, adultness verdicts, or enforcement categories.

#### 10.5.1 Baseline result of Part VII

The baseline established by Part VII is that consent-talk must not become closure by label.

Across the cases, PMS core makes the closure-demand visible, while SEX sharpens the validity discipline. Consent Under Asymmetry shows that agreement and asymmetry must both remain visible: “yes” does not settle the case, but asymmetry does not settle it either. Consent Laundering shows how a consent-sign may later be used as a closure-token, while also preserving ordinary consent continuity as a live rival reading. Boundary Confusion shows that the most conservative reading may be the strongest one: first identify the frame and the relevant distinction before importing sexuality-specific handles, MIP scoring, or stronger drift language.

The strongest shared discipline is:

**explicit □ + operative Χ + mapped Ω/Θ + tested Σ/Ψ**

The frame must say what the scene is and what consent or boundary language can mean there. Stop-capability must be practically available, not merely verbal. Asymmetry and temporality must be mapped without being moralized. Integration and self-binding must be shown through the configuration, not inferred from agreement, ritual, statement, apology, mutuality language, or formal precision.

#### 10.5.2 Cluster-level result

The Part VII cases form three SEX-facing clusters.

**Consent under asymmetry** shows the basic validity pressure. Formal agreement can matter, but it does not automatically prove meaningful consent. Dependency, vulnerability, role authority, publicness, reputation, bodily exposure, or unequal exit cost can matter, but they do not automatically prove invalidity, coercion, guilt, or blame. The case’s contribution is to block both premature closures: agreement-as-settlement and asymmetry-as-verdict.

**Consent laundering** shows the temporal and recontextualizing risk. A consent-sign can travel from its source frame into a later target frame, where it may be used to close inquiry into scope, conditions, alternatives, revisability, publicness, or cost topology. But later invocation of consent is not automatically laundering. The case preserves the difference between ordinary consent continuity and consent-as-closure-token.

**Boundary confusion** shows the restraint side of PMS-SEX. Not every boundary case is sexual. Not every responsibility, access, space, privacy, or role-confusion case activates sexual cost topology. Here, the overlay contributes partly by refusing improper activation. The result is not weaker analysis, but cleaner analysis: □ (Frame) and Δ (Difference) must be established before later operators, add-ons, MIP/AHP layers, or drift handles are allowed to carry weight.

#### 10.5.3 Cross-case dependency discipline

The recurring failure mode in Part VII is:

**consent / boundary token → validity closure**

This occurs when a word, sign, gesture, ritual, checkbox, agreement, public statement, mutuality claim, boundary label, or polished analysis artefact is asked to do the operator-work that the configuration itself has not carried. Once that shortcut appears, the dependency chain usually breaks: □ is replaced by a token, Χ is priced or assumed, Ω is denied or over-moralized, Θ is flattened into a moment, Λ is treated as irrelevant or as proof, Σ is simulated by narrative coherence, and Ψ is leaked or externalized as obligation.

In Part VII this shortcut appears in several forms:

- agreement becomes consent-validity;
- dependency becomes invalidity;
- mutuality becomes equal cost;
- earlier consent becomes permanent closure;
- discomfort becomes proof;
- absence of protest becomes proof;
- boundary ambiguity becomes blame;
- responsibility language becomes authority;
- publicness becomes legitimacy;
- careful analysis becomes decision-ready assessment.

Part VII blocks these shortcuts without emptying consent or boundaries of meaning. Consent can be real. Refusal can be real. Asymmetry can be decisive. Boundaries can be crossed. Publicness can matter. But none of these may be converted into a finding without the frame, sequence, cost topology, stop-capability, integration path, and evidence base needed to support it.

#### 10.5.4 What Part VII carries forward

Part VII carries forward the strongest guardrail lesson of AXIOM: the more socially and personally dangerous a vocabulary is, the more strictly its structural conditions must be held apart from verdicts. Consent, boundary, adultness, responsibility, dignity, asymmetry, publicness, and repair language can clarify configurations, but they can also shame, rank, expose, coerce, or launder authority.

SEX is justified because PMS core can map consent-adjacent closure-demands, but the overlay makes SEX-specific validity risks sharper: when agreement is asked to carry frame work, when mutuality hides unequal cost, when publicness changes the configuration, when stop-capability is priced, when consent-signs travel across time, and when boundary ambiguity is stabilized rather than resolved.

MIP is active only as an artefact-facing guardrail layer. It documents publicity level, misuse corridors, responsibility ceilings, IA-box limits, and D₀-protective boundary conditions where relevant. It does not score persons, assign adultness, decide consent validity, activate D as a case finding, or produce guilt, maturity, legal, clinical, forensic, or enforcement conclusions. AHP may harden the analysis artefact against over-transmission, excerptability, publicness drift, and false authority. It does not rescore, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or create new findings.

Part VII closes with the SEX rule for AXIOM: where consent-talk appears, reconstruct the frame before reading the sign; where asymmetry appears, map cost before assigning meaning; where stop-capability is priced, do not treat language as enough; where publicness or temporality changes the scene, do not pretend the original frame remains unchanged; and where evidence is missing, preserve Λ rather than laundering uncertainty into consent, non-consent, guilt, maturity, diagnosis, or governance permission.
