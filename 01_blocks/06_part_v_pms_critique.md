# PMS-AXIOM: PART V - CRITIQUE

## 8. CRITIQUE-Overlay: Reach, Scale, and the Limits of Legitimate Critique

Part V introduces the CRITIQUE overlay as the PMS add-on layer for critique reach, scale transition, publicness, and the boundary between interruption, correction, exposure, and authority. The cases in this part remain grounded in PMS core, but they require an additional lens because their central closure-demands concern how far critique can travel, when it remains interruptible, when it becomes productive, when it stabilizes into follow-up, and when it drifts into judgment, exposure, silence, or legitimacy laundering.

The purpose of this part is not to arbitrate truth, assign blame, validate critique, suppress critique, or legitimate authority. It is to show how critique becomes structurally viable only under explicit frames (□), calibrated mismatch (Δ), preserved interruptibility (Χ), and disciplined integration (Σ). Where critique expands across scale — from local objection to institutional complaint, public exposure, or systemic framing — exposure, temporality, and audience effects reshape what the critique can legitimately claim.

The CRITIQUE overlay is therefore a non-mixing disciplinary lens. It does not redefine PMS operators. It does not add a new base grammar. It supplies reach levels, scale tags, publicness handles, drift catalogues, and boundary rules that make critique-specific risks visible: reaction, judgment, narrative repair, commentary drift, silence attractor, public pillory, integration bypass, reach laundering, and the conversion of critique language into sanction or legitimacy.

PMS remains the canonical operator grammar. CRITIQUE only sharpens the reading where the case-specific issue is not merely difference or disagreement, but critique under reach pressure: the demand that a mismatch should interrupt, correct, travel upward or outward, become public, bind follow-up, or justify a broader frame.

### 8.1 Case Set and Role in the Paper

The cases addressed in Part V are:

36. Critique Reach (Micro → Macro)
37. Interpretability vs. Reality
38. Epistemic Injustice
39. Legitimacy Without Authority
40. Privacy vs. Publicness

Together, these cases show how critique changes when its frame, audience, exposure field, temporal residue, and correction pathway change. They are not grouped here because CRITIQUE decides which critique is true, justified, or morally superior. They are grouped here because each case contains a reach problem: a mismatch seeks response, but the permissible scale of response depends on frame, distance, integration, publicness, and non-coercive follow-up.

The cases cluster into several CRITIQUE-relevant families:

- **Critique reach and scale transition** show how local objection, institutional complaint, public critique, and systemic critique differ structurally rather than merely in intensity.
- **Interpretability vs. reality** shows how critique of model outputs, explanations, or representations can overreach when interpretability is mistaken for access to reality.
- **Epistemic injustice** shows how critique may be necessary where local frames suppress recognition, but also why recognition language must not become verdict or person-ranking.
- **Legitimacy without authority** shows how critique can stabilize as a structural claim without becoming governance permission.
- **Privacy vs. publicness** shows how critique can become exposure, and why visibility is not automatically correction.

Part V therefore demonstrates a central AXIOM discipline: critique may be structurally necessary, but critique language does not by itself authorize scale escalation, public exposure, sanction, person evaluation, or institutional closure.

### 8.2 Output Contract for Part V

The admissible outputs in Part V are PMS + CRITIQUE artefacts. A case may yield:

- **Reach policy:** a structured, non-prescriptive artefact specifying how far critique may travel under given frame, mismatch, distance, integration, publicness, and binding conditions.
- **Disclosure / publicness rule:** a guardrail for when making critique public is structurally defensible and when publicness becomes exposure-as-substitute, authority laundering, or correction bypass.
- **Critique taxonomy:** a classification of critique forms, including irritation, reaction, minimal critique, productive critique, stable critique, public critique, systemic critique, refusal, and non-correction-oriented critique.
- **Structural reading:** a dependency-sensitive PMS rendering, with CRITIQUE reach levels, scale tags, and publicness handles used only as overlay constructs, not as PMS operators.
- **Transmission guardrail:** a boundary statement for cases where critique artefacts could be reused for ranking, exclusion, punishment, reputational closure, institutional decision-making, or legitimacy claims.

The CRITIQUE overlay may identify drift risks, but drift risk is not drift finding. A case may show that judgment drift, public pillory, silence attractor, narrative reset, integration bypass, or reach laundering is structurally plausible without asserting that any concrete person, institution, group, critic, or audience has committed that drift.

MIP is not a person-evaluation layer in this part. It becomes relevant as a downstream governance and transmission guardrail where critique artefacts are public, institutional, decision-facing, reputationally sensitive, or otherwise likely to acquire coercive force. In those contexts, MIP evaluates the analysis artefact and its use conditions, not persons, maturity, dignity, motives, beliefs, or legitimacy as such. AHP may harden the analysis artefact where precision, excerptability, attack surfaces, publicness, or claim-boundary risks require it. AHP does not rescore, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or create new case findings.

The general AXIOM discipline is not repeated in full inside every case. It remains operative throughout: AXIOM maps frames, closure-demands, Λ-residues, operator chains, add-on signatures, drift risks, output types, and optional governance hooks. It does not diagnose, evaluate persons, rank dignity, assign guilt, prescribe enforcement, decide which critique is true, claim final epistemic or political authority, or validate PMS Base.

The core discipline of Part V is that critique must not become covert authority. PMS can render why mismatch generates critique pressure. CRITIQUE can identify how critique changes across reach, scale, publicness, distance, integration, and binding. MIP and AHP can harden the analysis artefact against publicness, transmission, and misuse risks. But where critique lacks frame discipline, interruptibility, integration, correction path, or legitimate follow-up, AXIOM must preserve the boundary rather than laundering exposure, judgment, or scale escalation as legitimate critique.

### 8.4 Cases

#### 8.4.1 Critique Reach (Micro → Macro)

##### Why this case is here

This case is included because critique changes when its reach changes. A local objection, an institutional complaint, a public accusation, and a systemic critique may all be called “critique,” but they do not operate under the same structural conditions. The same critical claim can shift in meaning when it moves from a small interpersonal setting into a wider institutional, public, or systemic frame. Its addressee changes, its audience changes, its exposure field changes, its reversibility changes, and its possible correction path changes.

The closure demand is therefore not simply “what is critique?” but: **what happens to critique when it moves from micro to macro scale?** At the micro level, critique may be a framed interruption within a relationship, discussion, or local practice. At the macro level, critique may become institutional, public, archival, reputational, systemic, or symbolic. The case is difficult because macro reach can make something visible that a local frame suppresses, but it can also overgeneralize, flatten local specificity, or convert critique into public exposure without correction.

The case belongs here because AXIOM is not trying to solve critique metaphysically or determine when critique is ultimately true. It maps the structural transition. The completed analysis treats this as a conceptual projection rather than a concrete scene. No specific actor, utterance, institution, evidence sequence, injury, response, public circulation event, or repair attempt is supplied. As a consequence, responsibility, action, asymmetry, and self-binding can be structurally rendered, but they cannot become scene-specific findings without additional evidence.

PMS Core maps the main scale transition. PMS-CRITIQUE then sharpens what is specifically critique-related: the difference between minimal critique, productive critique, and stable critique; the role of publicness; and the drift risks that arise when critique expands in reach without integration or follow-up. MIP and AHP remain downstream and artefact-facing: they help preserve maturity-in-practice and analysis-hardening discipline, but they do not add first-order findings about critique, persons, institutions, or publics.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement can be rendered as:

**Δ (Difference) → ∇ (Impulse) → □ (Frame) → Λ? (Non-event / residue) → Α? (Attractor) → Ω (Asymmetry) → Θ (Temporality) → Φ (Recontextualization) → Χ (Distance) → Σ? (Integration) → Ψ? (Self-binding)**

The central movement is a **Φ-scale transition across □-frames under Ω and Θ, constrained by Χ and tested for Σ, with Ψ withheld unless concrete follow-up binding is supplied**.

The case begins with **Δ (Difference)**: micro critique and macro critique are not structurally identical. A local objection, an institutional complaint, a public critique, and a systemic diagnosis differ in frame, audience, exposure, temporality, and possible correction path. **∇ (Impulse)** is present as directed critical pressure: objection, interruption, challenge, exposure, clarification, or reframing. But it remains structural directedness, not a psychological claim about anger, resentment, courage, purity, or motive.

**□ (Frame)** is one of the central operators. The case depends on the difference between interpersonal/local frames and institutional/public/systemic frames. A local critique may be addressed to someone who can respond directly. An institutional critique may require procedure, documentation, review, or role-bound answerability. A public critique may create audience effects, reputational residue, and reduced reversibility. A systemic critique may reveal patterns that local frames cannot see, but it may also absorb local specificity into a broader narrative too quickly.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is relevant but not established as a finding. Critique often forms around absence: silence, non-response, missing uptake, delayed repair, omitted acknowledgment, or institutional failure to answer. But the case material does not supply a concrete absence. Λ therefore remains conditional. It would require a specified expectation: who expected what response, in which frame, and within what time window. The same applies to **Α (Attractor)**. Recurrent critique-handling scripts such as silence, deferral, outrage, procedural absorption, or public labeling may stabilize, but no recurrence is evidenced in the abstract case. Α remains a possible risk zone rather than an observed pattern.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is strongly active at the conceptual level. Moving critique outward changes exposure, vulnerability, response capacity, public pressure, institutional authority, and audience power. Micro critique may involve intimacy, hierarchy, dependency, or direct vulnerability. Macro critique may involve institutions, publics, platforms, archives, reputational stakes, or systemic invisibility. The analysis does not moralize Ω. Macro critique may challenge power, but it may also create new exposure. Micro critique may be local, but not necessarily safe or symmetrical.

**Θ (Temporality)** is equally central. Critique reach is temporal because effects accumulate. A local objection may be momentary; an institutional complaint may enter records; a public critique may leave residue; a systemic critique may invoke historical pattern and future reform. Θ does not imply progress or maturity. Longer duration and wider reach do not make critique more valid. They make its consequences and reversibility conditions structurally different.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is the main operator of the case. A local concern may become an institutional pattern; an institutional pattern may become public critique; a public critique may become systemic diagnosis; a systemic frame may return to reinterpret the original local scene. This Φ-move can illuminate hidden structure, but it can also overgeneralize, flatten the local case, moralize ambiguity, or replace evidence with abstraction. The calibration question is always: what does the new frame reveal, what does it preserve, and what does it erase?

**Χ (Distance)** is required because critique reach easily fuses with verdict. Distance keeps the analysis interruptible and reversible. It prevents macro critique from becoming authority merely by scale, and it prevents the analysis itself from becoming a tribunal. Χ is not neutrality, passivity, or suppression of critique. It is reflective restraint: the ability to keep critique from collapsing into sanction, public shaming, person-ranking, or closure by force of frame.

**Σ (Integration)** is active only as a test. A macro critique would need to integrate local specificity, institutional patterning, publicness, temporality, asymmetry, rival readings, and evidence limits. But the abstract case does not show that such integration occurs. Σ is therefore not a completed result. It is a demand placed on any stronger macro reading. PMS coherence alone is not evidence that Σ has succeeded.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** remains weak and conditional. Stable critique would require some actor, role, institution, or process to bind itself to follow-up, review, correction, or revision over time. No such self-binding is supplied. Public intensity, repetition, or moral seriousness are not Ψ. In this case, Ψ marks a possible condition of stable critique, not an achieved structure.

The derived PMS structures remain formula-derived rather than primitive. **Awareness_A** is active only as structural scale-awareness: the analysis can distinguish micro and macro frames and track their temporal extension, but this is not empirical participant awareness. **Coherence_C** is present as artefact coherence, yet remains risky because a smooth PMS reading can still under-discriminate between interpersonal feedback, public accusation, systemic critique, legal complaint, protest, or refusal. **Responsibility_R** remains conditional because it would require Ψ, Φ, Θ, and Ω in a concrete role-bound follow-up structure; no such role-holder is supplied. **Action_E** is under-specified because critique reach is mapped, but no corrective enactment is shown. **Dignity_in_Practice_D** functions as a praxeological constraint under asymmetry: it guards against humiliation, exposure-as-correction, person-typing, and dignity-language misuse, without becoming ontological dignity or a dignity score. **SELF_FIXPOINT** is not established; the case does not describe a self, an institutional self-relation, or durable self-binding practice.

PMS-CRITIQUE sharpens this core reading. The case reaches **CRITIQUE_MIN** at the conceptual level: there is mismatch, frame, and interruptibility. It only weakly projects toward **CRITIQUE_PROD**, because productive critique would require Φ and Σ to consolidate an actual corrective step. It does not establish **CRITIQUE_STABLE**, because no Ψ-level follow-up binding is shown. **Publicness P** is relevant as an overlay handle, not a PMS operator: it amplifies Ω, Θ, and possible Α, but it does not create truth, legitimacy, correction, or maturity.

The critique-specific drift risks are therefore visible but not findings. Judgment drift may occur if macro critique becomes evaluative labeling without correction. Narrative repair drift may occur if recontextualization becomes the endpoint. Commentary drift may occur if distance suspends correction indefinitely. Public pillory may occur if exposure replaces correction. Silence attractor may occur if non-response stabilizes into a default script. But no drift is assigned here; the case lacks the concrete sequence required.

The reading would weaken if a concrete case showed no meaningful frame shift between micro and macro critique, no change in asymmetry, no temporal extension, or no recontextualization problem. It would also weaken if a rival grammar — for example epistemological, political, rhetorical, legal-procedural, or anti-capture — explained the movement more precisely without the PMS/CRITIQUE apparatus. The reading would become under-discriminating if the same formula were made to fit all critique forms equally, and over-assimilative if resistance, refusal, disagreement, rupture, absence, or rival framing were automatically redescribed as drift or failed integration. Stronger claims would require concrete scene data: actors, utterances, publicness level, response sequence, missing uptake, recurrence, correction path, reversibility window, role capacities, and evidence for or against follow-up binding.

MIP adds only a case-specific downstream result: the analysis artefact is relatively strong in awareness and coherence because it keeps boundaries, non-capture, publicness, and score hygiene visible. But this does not become a maturity finding about critique itself. A provisional A-band belongs only to the analysis artefact. M-score is not assignable to any person or role-holder. IA remains risk-level only. Dignity-in-Practice is active only as qualitative protection against humiliation, exposure-as-correction, and dignity-language misuse. AHP hardens this by warning that formal completeness may be mistaken for evidentiary completeness.

##### What the analysis shows

The case shows that critique reach is not a linear increase in force, authority, or maturity. It is a structural transformation. Moving from micro to macro changes the frame, and changing the frame changes the critique.

The PMS Core result is that critique reach is best mapped as:

**Φ (Recontextualization) of critique across □ (Frame) under Ω (Asymmetry) and Θ (Temporality), constrained by Χ (Distance), tested for Σ (Integration), and withholding Ψ (Self-binding) unless concrete follow-up is supplied.**

This permits a bounded form of closure. The analysis can say that micro-to-macro critique involves frame shift, exposure shift, temporal extension, and integration demand. It can also say that later operators cannot be used cheaply: Σ is not guaranteed, Ψ is not established, drift is not finding, and publicness is not correction.

PMS-CRITIQUE adds the critique-specific result: the case reaches minimal critique only at the conceptual level. It does not prove productive or stable critique. Productive critique would require a corrective step. Stable critique would require non-coercive follow-up binding. Publicness may amplify exposure and residue, but it cannot substitute for correction or self-binding.

The analysis also shows a distinctive non-capture zone. Some critique may not aim at correction. It may function as protest, refusal, testimony, symbolic rupture, ethical interruption, or political exposure. PMS-CRITIQUE’s threshold ladder must not treat such cases as failed critique merely because they do not reach CRITIQUE_PROD or CRITIQUE_STABLE. Non-capture remains possible, and in some forms of critique it may be structurally decisive.

##### Case-specific boundary

This case specifically invites several overreadings.

First, **Φ (Recontextualization)** must not be read as automatic clarification. A macro frame may reveal hidden structure, but it may also erase local constraints. Systemic framing is neither automatic overreach nor automatic maturity.

Second, **Χ (Distance)** must not be used to neutralize critique. Distance prevents verdictive fusion; it does not require passivity, politeness, or delay where critique is structurally necessary.

Third, **Σ (Integration)** must not be treated as proof. A coherent PMS reading may still be under-discriminating. Integration has to preserve local scene, macro frame, rival readings, and non-capture.

Fourth, **Ψ (Self-binding)** must not be inferred from publicness, repetition, intensity, or moral seriousness. It requires concrete follow-up binding.

Fifth, publicness must not be mistaken for correction. Public exposure may sometimes be necessary, especially where local or institutional frames block response, but exposure does not by itself satisfy integration, reversibility, proportionality, or self-binding.

Finally, the examples below are illustrative, not evidentiary. They do not add new case material, prove empirical patterns, or establish drift findings. They make the structural analysis readable while preserving the case’s claim boundaries.

##### Example 1 — From local objection to institutional pattern

A small seminar group has developed a habit: whenever one participant raises methodological concerns, the group acknowledges the point briefly and then returns to the original discussion. In one session, the participant says, “I think this keeps happening: the concern is recognized, but it never changes how the work proceeds.” At the micro level, this can be heard as a local objection about conversational uptake. It concerns a specific room, a specific practice, and a small set of participants who can respond directly.

The structure changes if the same objection is moved into an institutional frame. Suppose the participant later writes to the department that the seminar’s review process repeatedly records objections without creating any pathway for revision. The critique is no longer only about one exchange. It now concerns a procedure: how concerns are received, documented, acted on, or allowed to disappear. If the case is then discussed publicly as an example of how academic settings absorb critique without changing practice, the reach changes again. The critique becomes public, more generalized, and more exposed to audience effects.

This example demonstrates why critique reach cannot be treated as simple enlargement. The local objection is not identical to the institutional critique, and the institutional critique is not identical to the public critique. Each frame changes the relevant expectations. In the seminar, the expected response may be conversational adjustment. In the department, the expected response may be procedural review. In public, the expected response may become reputational accountability, symbolic recognition, or wider reform. The same concern may remain traceable, but it is not structurally unchanged.

The PMS Core hooks are clear. **Δ** appears in the mismatch between acknowledgment and actual change. **∇** appears as the directed pressure to interrupt the pattern. **□** changes as the critique moves from seminar conversation to institutional procedure to public discussion. **Λ** may be relevant if the repeated “acknowledgment without follow-up” is treated as a structured non-event, but it would need evidence: how often did it occur, what response was expected, and under which frame? **Α** may become relevant if the pattern stabilizes into a recognizable routine: concerns are heard, logged, and then neutralized. But again, recurrence must be shown, not assumed.

The major operators become more visible at wider scale. **Ω** changes because the participant may be vulnerable in the seminar, the department may hold procedural authority, and a public discussion may expose both the institution and specific participants. **Θ** matters because the concern is not only a momentary interruption; it accumulates through repetition and delayed non-response. **Φ** is central because the objection is recontextualized from a conversational scene into an institutional pattern and then possibly into a public critique. **Χ** is required so the analysis does not immediately turn the seminar group into culprits, the participant into a mature critic, or the institution into a verdictive object. **Σ** remains conditional: the critique would become more integrated only if it coordinated the local scene, procedural frame, repetition evidence, possible institutional constraints, and rival accounts. **Ψ** is not established unless some role-holder binds themselves to follow-up, review, or correction.

PMS-CRITIQUE sharpens the example. At the local level, the objection may reach **CRITIQUE_MIN** if the mismatch is framed and interruptible. It becomes weakly oriented toward **CRITIQUE_PROD** only if the recontextualization produces an actual corrective pathway, such as a revised review protocol or a clear response mechanism. It does not reach **CRITIQUE_STABLE** unless there is non-coercive follow-up over time: review dates, ownership, revisability, and protection against retaliation or humiliation. Publicness **P** is relevant only if the matter circulates beyond the institution; then it amplifies exposure and residue, but it does not automatically make the critique more true or more corrective.

The MIP and AHP status remains guarded. No A-score, M-score, or D reading should be assigned to any participant. The example could support a future MIP analysis only if concrete roles, knowledge, power-to-act, alternatives, predictability, and follow-up conditions were supplied. AHP would warn that a polished description of “institutional absorption” can look more evidenced than it is. The phrase may be analytically useful, but it requires documentation before it becomes a finding.

The forbidden claims are therefore explicit. This example does not prove that the seminar group acted wrongly. It does not prove that the institution silenced critique. It does not diagnose anyone as defensive, avoidant, immature, or oppressive. It does not establish a legal or procedural breach. It does not validate PMS or PMS-CRITIQUE. It does not show that public critique is justified, only that publicness changes the structure.

The non-capture note is equally important. The participant’s objection may not aim at correction in the PMS-CRITIQUE sense. It may be a refusal to continue under a frame that repeatedly absorbs objections. It may be an ethical protest, a demand for recognition, or a marker of exhaustion. If so, failure to reach CRITIQUE_PROD or CRITIQUE_STABLE is not automatically a defect. The critique may preserve rupture because the available correction pathway is itself part of the problem.

The article takeaway is that critique reach changes the scene. A local objection can become an institutional critique only through recontextualization, and that recontextualization requires distance, evidence discipline, integration, and dignity-in-practice. Without those, macro reach may clarify a pattern, but it may also overgeneralize a local scene.

##### Example 2 — Public exposure is not correction

A member of a small organization notices that meeting summaries repeatedly omit dissenting comments. Initially, the concern is raised internally: “The minutes make it look as if there was agreement, but several objections were made.” This is a local critique about documentation. The relevant frame is narrow: meeting procedure, record accuracy, and the possibility of correction. A simple correction may be available: amend the minutes, add a dissent note, or clarify the record.

Now imagine that the concern is posted publicly: “This organization systematically erases dissent.” The critique may be understandable, especially if internal correction has repeatedly failed. But the public form is structurally different from the internal objection. It changes the audience, raises reputational stakes, increases temporal residue, and may expose people who cannot easily answer in the same medium. Publicness may be necessary in some cases, but it is not equivalent to correction. The act of making a pattern visible does not by itself integrate the local evidence, institutional procedure, role obligations, and possible response path.

This example demonstrates the difference between critique reach and critique completion. The public statement may reach more people, but reach is not the same as integration. Exposure may create pressure, but pressure is not the same as correction. A public audience may amplify a critique, but amplification is not proof. A wider frame may reveal a real pattern, but it may also compress distinctions among negligence, error, procedural habit, disagreement about documentation norms, or deliberate suppression.

PMS Core begins with **Δ**: there is a mismatch between actual dissent and the official record. **∇** appears as pressure to object. **□** first frames the issue as documentation accuracy. If objections have repeatedly disappeared from the minutes despite prior requests, **Λ** may become active as structured omission, but only if the expectation and recurrence are specified. **Α** may become relevant if omission stabilizes as a pattern. **Ω** appears because whoever controls the record has disproportionate power over institutional memory. **Θ** matters because minutes persist; they become the archive through which later readers understand what happened. **Φ** appears when the local documentation issue is recontextualized as an organizational or public critique. **Χ** is necessary to prevent the public statement from becoming a verdict before the pattern is evidenced. **Σ** would require coordinating the meeting record, prior objections, procedural standards, the organization’s response capacity, and the risks of public exposure. **Ψ** would require follow-up: who owns correction, what changes, and how future minutes are reviewed.

PMS-CRITIQUE makes the publicness problem especially visible. The internal objection may satisfy **CRITIQUE_MIN** if the mismatch is framed and interruptible. It may approach **CRITIQUE_PROD** if the record is corrected and the procedure is adjusted. But the public statement does not automatically become productive critique. It may widen the frame through **Φ**, but without **Σ** it can become a public label rather than correction. It reaches a broader audience, yet the correction path may become less clear.

Publicness **P** is not a PMS operator. It amplifies **Ω**, **Θ**, and **Α**. It may increase exposure, harden residue, and stabilize a public script: “this organization erases dissent.” That script may be warranted in a concrete case, but it is not warranted merely by the form of the statement. The PMS-CRITIQUE drift risks are therefore clear but not findings. **Judgment drift** is possible if the public label replaces a correction pathway. **Narrative repair drift** is possible if a broader explanation becomes the endpoint. **Public pillory** is possible if exposure becomes punishment. **Integration bypass** is possible if the critique moves from local evidence to public conclusion without preserving intermediate frames. None of these is active without more evidence.

The MIP status is guardrail-bound. No M-score can be assigned to the person posting publicly, the person keeping minutes, or the organization. A future MIP analysis would need to know who controlled the record, what they knew, what alternatives were available, how predictable the consequences were, whether prior correction was attempted, and whether public exposure was proportionate or avoidable. Dignity-in-Practice would be relevant because public critique can expose persons or roles. But D must not be used to suppress legitimate critique. The issue is not whether public criticism is impolite; the issue is whether exposure replaces correction, whether voice and reversibility remain possible, and whether persons are degraded rather than roles or procedures being challenged.

AHP would flag a likely analysis-artifact risk: a tidy phrase such as “public exposure is not correction” can be misread as a rule against public critique. That would be wrong. Some critique must become public because internal frames block correction, because affected parties lack safe response channels, or because the relevant pattern is invisible within the local frame. The point is therefore not to demote public critique. The point is to prevent publicness from being mistaken for completion. Public exposure may be necessary, but it does not by itself satisfy correction, integration, reversibility, proportionality, or self-binding.

The forbidden claims must remain visible. This example does not prove that the organization erases dissent. It does not prove that the public post is unjustified. It does not prove that internal handling was adequate. It does not diagnose anyone as manipulative, cowardly, punitive, or immature. It does not make a legal claim about defamation, liability, or due process. It does not produce a governance recommendation.

The non-capture note is important. A public statement may be intended not as correction but as testimony, warning, refusal, or solidarity with others who experienced the same omission. PMS-CRITIQUE can describe the correction threshold, but not all critique is correction-oriented. If the available institutional frame has repeatedly neutralized critique, public rupture may preserve something that procedural integration would erase. That possibility must remain open.

The article takeaway is that macro reach increases structural load. Publicness can make a critique visible, but it also changes asymmetry, temporality, exposure, and reversibility. The question is not whether public critique is allowed; the question is what its reach changes and what claims remain unwarranted without integration.

##### Example 3 — Systemic framing and the risk of erasing the local scene

A workplace team has a recurring conflict about task allocation. One member says that undesirable tasks keep being assigned to the same few people. In the immediate scene, the issue can be framed as a local coordination problem: who is assigning tasks, what criteria are used, whether the pattern is visible, and whether it can be corrected. A local response might involve reviewing recent assignments, clarifying responsibilities, and changing the distribution.

The critique becomes more complex when the member frames the pattern as part of a wider systemic problem: “This is how invisible labor gets reproduced.” That recontextualization may be important. It can connect a local pattern to broader structures of recognition, role expectation, gendered or status-based labor, institutional habit, or normalized unfairness. The systemic frame may reveal something the local frame cannot see. But it may also risk erasing the local scene if it moves too quickly. The people assigning tasks may have acted under constraints, incomplete information, rotating emergencies, or unclear procedures. The systemic frame may be illuminating, but it does not automatically settle the local case.

This example demonstrates why **Φ** is central and why **Σ** cannot be assumed. Recontextualization may be necessary for critique to reach its object. If the problem is genuinely systemic, staying local may domesticate the critique and protect the pattern. Yet if the systemic frame replaces the local scene entirely, it can over-assimilate the evidence. The challenge is to preserve both: the local sequence and the macro pattern, the immediate correction and the structural diagnosis, the role-specific responsibility and the wider context.

The PMS Core reading begins with **Δ**: there is a mismatch in task distribution. **∇** appears as pressure to object. **□** first frames the issue as team coordination, then as systemic reproduction of invisible labor. **Λ** may be relevant if previous concerns were ignored or if invisible labor is structured by non-recognition, but this must be shown. **Α** may be relevant if task allocation repeatedly stabilizes around the same people. **Ω** is central because task distribution may reflect unequal authority, status, visibility, or capacity to refuse. **Θ** matters because a single assignment may not show much, but recurrence over time can create burden and expectation. **Φ** recontextualizes the local pattern as systemic. **Χ** is required so the systemic frame does not become immediate verdict. **Σ** would require integrating assignment records, role constraints, repeated patterns, affected perspectives, possible systemic literature or institutional norms, and counter-readings. **Ψ** would require follow-up ownership: who changes the allocation process, how it is reviewed, and how future invisibility is prevented.

The dependency discipline matters because this example tempts shortcuts. One invalid shortcut would be **Δ → systemic verdict**: unequal task distribution is noticed, therefore the team is reproducing structural injustice. Another shortcut would be **local frame → dismissal**: because the issue can be explained locally, the systemic critique is overblown. Both shortcuts fail. PMS requires the analysis to hold the transition open. A local mismatch can be systemically meaningful, but only through calibrated recontextualization and integration.

PMS-CRITIQUE adds threshold clarity. The initial objection may reach **CRITIQUE_MIN** if the mismatch is framed and interruptible. It may become **CRITIQUE_PROD** if the team reviews the pattern and changes the allocation process while preserving the systemic insight. It does not become **CRITIQUE_STABLE** unless the team or institution binds itself to non-coercive follow-up: review cycles, transparent criteria, revisability, and protection against retaliation or humiliation.

The drift risks are again risks, not findings. **Narrative repair drift** is possible if systemic explanation becomes the endpoint and no allocation process changes. **Judgment drift** is possible if the team or assigner is labeled without a correction path. **Integration bypass** is possible if the critique moves from local pattern to systemic conclusion without preserving evidence. **Commitment evasion** is possible if the team agrees with the systemic language but never changes the practice. Yet none of these can be claimed from the vignette alone.

MIP would treat this as requiring careful role-context analysis. If a concrete case were supplied, MIP would ask who knew about the distribution, who had power to change it, how predictable the burden was, what alternatives existed, and whether serious integration attempts were made. Without such data, M-score cannot be assigned. Dignity-in-Practice would matter because both affected members and accused role-holders can be exposed. The aim would be to protect against degradation while not neutralizing legitimate critique.

AHP would warn that systemic language can create false precision. “Invisible labor” may be a powerful and appropriate concept, but in a concrete analysis it must be linked to the scene rather than used as an automatic conclusion. AHP would also warn against the opposite misuse: demanding perfect evidence before allowing any systemic framing can protect the very pattern being criticized. The hardening requirement is to state what evidence would strengthen the systemic reading, what would weaken it, and what rival readings remain possible.

The forbidden claims are clear. This example does not prove discrimination, exploitation, bad faith, legal wrongdoing, or moral failure. It does not diagnose the assigner or the affected worker. It does not rank the team’s maturity. It does not validate systemic theory or PMS. It does not prescribe a workplace policy. It only illustrates the structural problem of moving from a local distribution complaint to a systemic critique.

The non-capture note is decisive. Some systemic critique works by naming what local procedures cannot recognize. If the team demands that the critique remain local, it may erase the very structure at issue. Conversely, some local complexity may resist systemic capture. PMS must not decide in advance which is true. It must keep the recontextualization open, preserve the local scene, and require integration before stronger claims are made.

The article takeaway is that systemic framing is neither automatic overreach nor automatic maturity. It is a powerful Φ-move whose adequacy depends on Ω, Θ, Χ, and Σ. It can reveal structure, but it can also erase local constraints. The case’s discipline lies in refusing both premature closure and premature dismissal.

##### Case takeaway

This case shows that critique reach is a structural transformation, not a simple increase in scale. PMS Core maps the movement from difference and directed pressure through frame, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, possible integration, and possible self-binding. PMS-CRITIQUE sharpens the critique-specific thresholds: minimal critique is conceptually present, productive critique remains weak, and stable critique is not established.

The methodological use of the case is to keep scale transitions legible without turning them into verdicts. It helps the chapter show why macro critique may matter, why it may become risky, and why neither local nor systemic framing should be granted automatic priority. Publicness may reveal what local frames hide, but it may also intensify exposure and residue. Systemic framing may clarify patterns, but it may also erase local constraints. Integration remains a task, not a given.

The remaining Λ-residue is the unresolved relation between critique as correction and critique as refusal, testimony, rupture, or symbolic exposure. PMS-CRITIQUE makes correction thresholds visible, but it must not force all critique to satisfy them. The case should therefore be used as a bounded map of critique reach: it shows where structural closure becomes possible, where stronger claims would require evidence, and where non-capture must remain visible.

#### 8.4.2 Interpretability vs. Reality

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because it tests a closure-demand that appears whenever moral difficulty becomes interpretable: the demand that a coherent explanation should coincide with what remains real in practice. The case asks what happens when that coincidence does not occur. A situation may be rendered intelligible, given a frame, explained through a sequence of reasons, and made available to critique; yet the practical remainder may persist. The burden may still be carried, the deployment risk may remain, the loss may still be real, or the obligation may remain unresolved.

This makes the case structurally important for AXIOM because it asks the framework to map without absorbing. The case is not about whether interpretation is useless. It is also not about whether reality is metaphysically inaccessible. Its difficulty lies in the fact that interpretation can be valid and still insufficient. Explanation may clarify how a situation came to be, reduce false blame, or make a mismatch discussable. But that does not mean the practical condition has been repaired.

The completed analysis treats this as a conceptual projection rather than a concrete scene. No actor, role, affected party, institutional setting, action sequence, evidence record, public controversy, or implementation context is supplied. That matters. Operators such as responsibility, action, asymmetry, and self-binding can be rendered only as weak, conditional, or analytic structures unless a concrete scene later supplies the missing material. The case is mapped rather than solved because its central object is precisely the gap between intelligibility and practical resolution.

For AXIOM, the case is a useful stress test. A weaker reading would make one of two mistakes. It would either treat every remainder as merely incomplete integration, thereby making PMS into a closure machine, or it would abandon structure because reality exceeds interpretation. The completed reading avoids both. It lets PMS Core mark the framed difference and the absent coincidence; it lets PMS-CRITIQUE show that the case reaches only minimal critique, not correction; it lets MIP withhold scoring and decision-status; and it lets AHP harden the analysis artefact without turning precision into validation.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement begins with **Δ (Difference)**: the case distinguishes what can be interpreted or explained from what remains real in practice. This is not a metaphysical split between appearance and reality. It is a structural contrast inside a philosophical-praxeological frame. The relevant difference is not “truth versus illusion,” nor “maturity versus immaturity,” but “intelligibility versus practical remainder.”

That difference becomes meaningful only within **□ (Frame)**. The active frame is not empirical, legal, clinical, or implementation-oriented. It is a philosophical-praxeological frame that asks how morally difficult situations are handled when explanation does not dissolve the practical condition. Inside the frame are explanation, recontextualization, practical residue, critique-readiness, and the temptation of closure. Outside the frame are verdicts about persons, legal responsibility, clinical diagnosis, metaphysical proof, and system validation.

The central structural pressure is carried by **Λ (Non-event / residue)**. The case marks an absent or incomplete coincidence: explanation arrives, but practical resolution does not necessarily follow. The expected event would be something like: “the situation has been explained, therefore the practical remainder has been handled.” The case interrupts that expectation. Λ is therefore active, but it must be calibrated carefully. The residue is not automatically a PMS-internal defect, not automatically missing evidence, and not automatically a demand for stronger integration. Some of it may be genuine non-capture.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is also active. Interpretation and explanation place practical difficulty into a new frame. They can make the case more legible. They can reduce false accusation or show why a situation is structurally difficult. But Φ is also where the main risk appears: recontextualization may become a substitute for practical response. A complaint can be reframed as a process issue while the burden remains. A technical failure can be explained while deployment risk remains. A tragedy can be clarified while the loss remains. The case therefore requires a strong **Χ (Distance)**: reflective distance from the seductive smoothness of interpretation. Χ keeps the analysis from turning explanation into verdict, formula into capture, or critique-readiness into correction.

The remaining PMS operators are present only with restrictions. **∇ (Impulse)** is weak: the case implies a pressure toward understanding and closure, but no actor’s motive or urgency is supplied. **Α (Attractor)** is weak: one can imagine explanation stabilizing as a substitute for response, but no repeated pattern is evidenced. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is weak: moral difficulty often involves exposure, power, or obligation, but no concrete asymmetry is given. **Θ (Temporality)** is weak: “remains real” suggests persistence, but no actual sequence or trajectory is supplied. **Σ (Integration)** is deliberately weak because coherence is exactly what may mislead here. A clean analysis may still leave practical reality untouched. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is analytic-only: no case actor binds themselves to follow-up; only the analysis binds itself to restraint, reversibility, and non-capture.

The resulting PMS movement is best captured by the compressed formula:

**Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ Δ**

This formula is intentionally compressed. It does not activate the entire PMS chain as if a full praxis scene existed. It says: reflective distance over recontextualization of an absent or incomplete coincidence within a frame generated by the difference between interpretability and practical reality. That is a structural reading, not a proof of capture.

The derived structures follow the same restraint. **Awareness_A** is weakly present only as framed differentiation between explanation and practical remainder; it is not phenomenological awareness or a person’s insight. **Coherence_C** is present as artefact coherence but remains contested, because coherence is precisely what may fail to coincide with practical adequacy. **Responsibility_R** is not established, since no actor, obligation, asymmetry, temporal commitment, or self-binding is supplied. **Action_E** is under-specified, because no integrated enactment or corrective step occurs. **Dignity_in_Practice_D** functions only as application constraint: it protects against shaming, dignity-labelling, person-ranking, or tribunal closure, but it is not an ontological dignity claim. **SELF_FIXPOINT** remains analytic-only; it marks the analysis binding itself to restraint, not a self-model of any case actor.

The add-on layer, **PMS-CRITIQUE**, sharpens what PMS Core alone does not isolate: the critique threshold. The case reaches **CRITIQUE_MIN** in a conceptual sense because the mismatch is framed and held under Distance. The difference between explanation and practical remainder becomes visible; it does not collapse into mere reaction or irritation. But the case does not reach **CRITIQUE_PROD**, because productive critique would require a consolidated corrective step. Here, no repair, redistribution, operational decision, institutional change, or action plan is supplied. Nor does the case reach **CRITIQUE_STABLE**, because stable critique would require follow-up binding over time. No such owner, trajectory, or self-binding appears.

The CRITIQUE overlay therefore makes visible the difference between critique-readiness and correction. It also names case-specific drift risks. **Judgment drift** is a risk if the framed difference becomes a verdict. **Narrative repair drift** is a risk if explanation makes the remainder feel handled. **Commentary drift** is a risk if refined discussion substitutes for practical response. **Diffusion drift** is a weak risk because abstraction can make responsibility disappear unless a concrete scene is later supplied. **Integration bypass** is central: interpretation may appear coherent while bypassing practical consolidation. These are risks, not findings. The case gives no concrete drift pattern.

MIP adds only a case-specific downstream result: the analysis remains a working note. No A-score is assigned because there is no bounded enactment or role-context episode. No M-score is assigned because there is no actor or role-holder whose knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, or integration attempt can be assessed. The IA-box yields no concrete asymmetry classification; it marks only a conceptual risk that model legibility may become one-sided authority over practical remainder. The D-module remains off: D₀ is untouched, and no D₁/D₂ profile is created. Non-assignment is not a low score. It is the correct result of missing scene material.

AHP then hardens the MIP artefact. Its case-specific result is not a new case finding. It says the analysis artefact is precise but still vulnerable. The high Precision Heuristic confirms boundary clarity, scope control, reversibility, and language hygiene; it does not upgrade the case into empirical knowledge, practical resolution, IA classification, D-profile, or transmittable decision artefact. AHP identifies the main attack surfaces: cross-layer coherence may look like practical capture; IA non-classification may be overread as partial classification; D-language may be mistaken for a latent D-profile; key philosophical terms remain broad; rival-formalism comparison is preserved but not operationalized; and future concrete-scene requirements are not yet prioritized.

##### What the analysis shows

The case shows that interpretability can be structurally valuable without being practically sufficient. PMS Core maps the difference between explanation and practical remainder, the frame that makes that difference meaningful, the absent coincidence that produces Λ-residue, the recontextualizing work of interpretation, and the need for Distance. PMS-CRITIQUE shows that the case becomes critique-ready but not correction-complete. MIP shows that downstream scoring and decision use must be withheld. AHP shows that even a careful analysis artefact remains vulnerable to over-smoothness.

The main closure possible here is **bounded structural clarification**. The case can be clarified as a relation between Δ, □, Λ, Φ, and Χ. The closure not available is practical completion. The case cannot say that the remainder has been resolved, that responsibility has been assigned, that action has been integrated, that asymmetry has been concretely classified, or that a stable follow-up commitment exists.

The specific Λ-residue is the practical reality that remains after interpretation or explanation. That residue may take different forms in later examples: a workload cost, a deployment risk, a tragic loss, an unresolved obligation, an affected-side experience, or a consequence not changed by explanation. PMS can mark the residue. It cannot assume that marking it exhausts it.

Rival readings remain important. A hermeneutic reading may preserve the irreducibility of interpretation. A phenomenological reading may preserve lived experience. A pragmatic reading may ask whether explanation changes situated handling. A moral-remainder or tragic reading may preserve loss that no formal grammar dissolves. A discourse-ethical or testimony-centered reading may preserve affected-side voice. An anti-formalist or anti-capture reading may challenge the very move by which PMS marks non-capture inside its own vocabulary; this is not a failure of the rival reading, but one of the case’s central pressures. These rival readings are not defects in the PMS analysis. They name what PMS and the overlays may not fully capture.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk in this case is **closure-by-interpretability**. Because the case is about explanation becoming coherent, the analysis itself can reproduce the danger it maps. A clean PMS formula, a clear CRITIQUE threshold, a careful MIP working-note decision, and a high AHP precision result can together make the case feel more resolved than it is. That is the central boundary.

Here, **Σ (Integration)** must not be strengthened merely because the article is coherent. **Ψ (Self-binding)** must not be read as case-agent follow-up. **Ω (Asymmetry)** and **Θ (Temporality)** must not be upgraded from weak status merely because moral difficulty usually involves exposure and time. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** must not be treated as captured simply because PMS can name it. CRITIQUE drift risks must not be converted into findings. MIP non-assignment must not be read as low maturity. AHP hardening must not be read as validation.

The case would be weakened if the difference between explanation and practical remainder cannot be sustained, if the frame is too unstable, or if the supposed residue turns out to be only missing information. It would become under-discriminating if it cannot distinguish practical remainder from ordinary ambiguity, incomplete evidence, or rhetorical contrast. It would become over-assimilative if resistance, rupture, disagreement, rival framing, or unresolved absence were automatically redescribed as drift, failed integration, or calibration residue.

A stronger concrete analysis would require actor or role, affected side, time window, action or non-action, consequence, power/control, alternatives, review condition, publicness status, and rival-formalism comparison. Until then, the case remains a chapter-ready structural section, not a decision-ready artefact.

##### Example 1 — The Explained Complaint That Still Leaves a Cost

The first example shows the case structure in a small organizational setting without turning it into a tribunal or responsibility finding. A small team receives a complaint from one member that a recurring review process leaves them carrying extra work. The explanation arrives quickly and is not obviously false. The process was designed under time pressure. The review checklist grew gradually. Several people assumed that the extra coordination step was informal, not assigned. The person who now carries it happens to be the one with the most context, so the work keeps returning to them. Once this is explained, the situation becomes intelligible. The team can now say why the burden appeared, why no one originally intended it, and why the pattern persisted.

Yet the practical reality has not disappeared. The person still lost time. The recurring burden still exists. The explanation may reduce blame, clarify origin, or prevent a crude accusation, but it does not by itself redistribute the work, acknowledge the cost, or create a revised process. The complaint has become interpretable; the practical remainder remains.

This example demonstrates the central structure of the case: explanation can be valid as interpretation without being sufficient as practical response. The important point is not that the explanation is bad. On the contrary, it may be a good explanation. The problem is the closure-demand that tries to make explanation coincide with repair. The case shows why PMS must distinguish interpretive coherence from practical adequacy.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ (Difference)** appears as the difference between “the process is now explained” and “the burden remains real.” **□ (Frame)** is the team-process frame: within that frame, the issue is not metaphysical reality or personal character, but role allocation, workload, and process expectation. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** appears as the missing event: after explanation, one might expect the burden to be handled, but that handling has not occurred. **Φ (Recontextualization)** appears in the recontextualization of the complaint: it is reframed from “someone is being unfair” into “a process gradually produced an unassigned burden.” That recontextualization may be useful, but it is not repair. **Χ (Distance)** is required to prevent either side from collapsing the matter into verdict. The complaint should not become a person-accusation; the explanation should not become a closure device. **Σ (Integration)** remains conditional because no integrated redistribution has yet occurred. **Ψ (Self-binding)** remains absent unless someone explicitly binds themselves to a follow-up change.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay adds a sharper reading. The case reaches **CRITIQUE_MIN** if the mismatch is framed and held interruptibly: the complaint marks a real difference, the frame makes it relevant, and the team does not immediately retaliate, dismiss, or moralize. But the case does not yet reach **CRITIQUE_PROD**, because no corrective step has been consolidated. A meeting that explains the burden without changing the process risks **commentary drift** or **narrative repair drift**. The team may now have a better story about the problem while the practical burden remains. That is a risk, not a finding about the people involved.

MIP would remain limited unless more concrete material were supplied. If this were a real case, MIP would need role-context-time-window, affected-side perspective, authority to change the process, available alternatives, and review conditions before any responsibility or maturity analysis could be attempted. In the example as written, no A-score, M-score, IA classification, or D-profile is warranted. AHP would be relevant only as analysis-artifact hardening: it would ask whether the written analysis makes too much of the explanation, whether it preserves the practical cost, and whether it avoids turning the complaint or the explanation into a verdict.

The forbidden claims are important. This example does not show that anyone is malicious, immature, avoidant, irresponsible, or morally deficient. It does not establish a legal claim, a clinical pattern, or an empirical generalization about teams. It does not prove that explanation is useless. It also does not prove that every unresolved burden is a PMS Λ-residue. The vignette is only a bounded illustration of how explanation and practical remainder can diverge.

The non-capture note is that the affected person’s actual experience, the real cost of the work, and the practical adequacy of any future repair are not exhausted by the structural description. PMS can mark the gap between explanation and remaining burden, but the burden itself would still require concrete handling.

The article takeaway is that interpretation may be necessary for repair, but it is not identical with repair. A case can become clearer while remaining unresolved. The correct structural result is not to reject explanation, but to keep the absent practical follow-up visible.

##### Example 2 — The Interpretability Report That Does Not Settle Deployment Risk

The second example translates the same structure into a technical-governance register while preserving the non-implementation boundary. A technical review group examines a model output that behaved unexpectedly in a sensitive workflow. The team produces an interpretability report. The report identifies the main input features that contributed to the output, reconstructs the decision path, compares several nearby cases, and explains why the system behaved as it did under the configured objective. The report is careful, bounded, and useful. It shows that the output was not random. It also shows that the model followed patterns consistent with its training and deployment configuration.

After the report, the situation is more interpretable. But the deployment risk is not automatically resolved. The practical question remains: should the workflow continue unchanged, be paused, be redesigned, be monitored differently, or be removed from that setting? The interpretability report may support such decisions, but it is not itself the decision. It may explain the output without proving that the output is acceptable, safe, fair, lawful, clinically meaningful, or operationally legitimate.

This example demonstrates the case’s core warning in a domain where the temptation is especially strong: interpretability can be mistaken for reality-management. Once a technical explanation exists, there is pressure to treat the issue as handled. But a model’s legible internal path does not settle the practical consequences of using the model. The report can reduce opacity; it cannot by itself resolve the deployment frame.

The PMS Core hooks are again precise. **Δ (Difference)** is the difference between interpretability of the model output and the practical reality of deployment risk. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as pressure to move from “we understand why it happened” to “therefore the situation is under control.” **□ (Frame)** is the technical-governance frame: what counts as explanation, risk, admissible evidence, and decision relevance must be declared. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** appears as several missing events: no decision has yet been made; no affected-side review is supplied; no operational validation is complete; no future-risk assessment is established merely by explanation. **Α (Attractor)** would become relevant only if interpretability reports repeatedly stabilize as a substitute for governance review. In this vignette, that is a risk, not a finding. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is possible because some roles may authorize deployment while others bear downstream effects, but the example does not specify enough to assign a concrete asymmetry finding. **Θ (Temporality)** is possible because deployment risk unfolds over time, but no actual trajectory is supplied. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is active because the unexplained output is recontextualized as a legible model behaviour. **Χ (Distance)** is required to stop the report from becoming a verdict. **Σ (Integration)** remains weak unless the report is integrated with risk review, affected context, and operational decision criteria. **Ψ (Self-binding)** remains absent unless a responsible body binds itself to a review, redesign, monitoring, or withdrawal commitment.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay is particularly useful here. The interpretability report may bring the case to **CRITIQUE_MIN**: a mismatch is framed, made legible, and held open for review. But it does not reach **CRITIQUE_PROD** unless it leads to a coherent corrective step. It does not reach **CRITIQUE_STABLE** unless follow-up is owned over time without coercion or responsibility diffusion. The main drift risks are **integration bypass** and **commentary drift**. Integration bypass occurs if explanation appears to do the work that practical risk review should do. Commentary drift occurs if the report generates further discussion and documentation without changing the deployment conditions. These remain risks, not findings, unless a concrete workflow shows them recurring.

MIP status must stay guarded. A real deployment case might eventually require MIP because governance relevance would be present: roles, asymmetries, consequences, reversibility, and public or institutional stakes might matter. But in this example, as stated, MIP should not assign A-score or M-score. It can only say what would be required: role mapping, affected-side perspective, authority distribution, consequence horizon, alternatives, review windows, and reversibility conditions. AHP would be useful as a second-order hardening layer for the interpretability report itself. It would ask whether the report’s precision is being confused with practical adequacy, whether non-capture is explicit, whether rival risk interpretations remain open, and whether the transmission status is clear.

The forbidden claims are strict. The example does not claim that the system is unsafe or safe. It does not validate or invalidate the interpretability method. It does not make a legal, clinical, empirical, or implementation finding. It does not diagnose the review team as negligent or overconfident. It does not prove that technical interpretability is inadequate in general. It only shows that interpretability and practical deployment adequacy are distinct.

The non-capture note is that the real deployment effects, affected-side experience, institutional obligations, and future consequences are not contained in the interpretability report. They may be informed by it, but not replaced by it. A model can be explainable and still practically unresolved.

The article takeaway is that interpretability becomes dangerous when it is treated as closure. Its proper role is bounded: it can support critique-readiness and possible correction, but it does not itself prove that correction has occurred.

##### Example 3 — The Philosophical Explanation That Leaves the Tragedy Intact

The third example prepares the later discussion of non-capture and rival readings by showing how explanation can clarify tragedy without undoing it. A philosophical discussion examines a morally painful situation: two commitments conflict, and any available choice leaves someone harmed or something important unrepaired. The participants carefully analyse the structure. They distinguish the roles involved, identify the competing obligations, clarify the frame, and explain why no option fully satisfies all constraints. The discussion is rigorous. It avoids cheap blame. It prevents a simplistic verdict. It shows why the situation is difficult rather than merely confused.

The analysis succeeds at one level. It makes the situation interpretable. It explains why closure is unavailable. It may even protect the participants from false moralization by showing that the conflict is structurally tragic rather than caused by one person’s failure. But the tragedy remains. The person who loses support still loses support. The promise that cannot be kept is still broken. The burden that cannot be fairly distributed still lands somewhere. The explanation may be humane and clarifying, but it does not undo the practical loss.

This example demonstrates the deepest non-capture zone in the case. Sometimes the remainder is not missing information. It is not an unintegrated frame. It is not an immature refusal to accept complexity. It is the persistence of practical loss after explanation. A structural analysis can protect against false closure, but it cannot make the tragedy disappear.

The PMS Core hooks are substantial. **Δ (Difference)** is the difference between interpretive clarity and remaining loss. **□ (Frame)** is the moral-praxeological frame within which conflicting commitments are considered. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** is not merely a missing fact; it is the absent event of full repair or non-tragic closure. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as pressure to find a satisfying resolution, but it should remain weak unless a concrete actor or decision pressure is specified. **Ω (Asymmetry)** may be relevant if one party carries more cost or has less say, but it must not be asserted without a concrete scene. **Θ (Temporality)** may be relevant because commitments and losses unfold over time, but the vignette does not give enough sequence to ground a full trajectory. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is active because the situation is recontextualized from personal failure into structural tragedy. **Χ (Distance)** is essential because it prevents the analysis from turning tragedy into either blame or consolation. **Σ (Integration)** remains limited: one can integrate the explanation, but not the loss itself. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is absent unless some actor binds themselves to future care, repair, acknowledgment, or prevention.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay helps distinguish the levels. The discussion may achieve **CRITIQUE_MIN** because the mismatch between explanation and remaining loss is framed and held without coercive verdict. It may approach productive critique if the analysis leads to a concrete practice of acknowledgment, redistribution, or future prevention, but that is not supplied here. It does not become stable critique unless follow-up is bound over time. The main risks are **narrative repair drift** and **commentary drift**. Narrative repair drift would occur if the tragic explanation makes participants feel the loss has been morally processed and therefore no longer needs attention. Commentary drift would occur if the discussion remains refined but disconnected from any possible care, repair, or acknowledgment. These are risks, not findings.

MIP must remain careful. If this were a real case, MIP might become relevant because harms, obligations, asymmetries, and responsibility could be present. But this example is intentionally bounded. It does not supply enough information for A-score, M-score, IA classification, or D-profile. MIP would remain a working-note guardrail unless the concrete role-context-time-window were supplied. AHP would be relevant to harden the analysis by asking whether the language of tragedy is being used to preserve non-capture or to avoid responsibility. It would also check whether the analysis is too elegant, whether affected-side adequacy is missing, and whether rival readings are operationalized rather than merely listed.

The forbidden claims are especially important here. The example does not claim that tragedy excuses harm. It does not claim that no one is responsible. It does not claim that structural explanation is morally superior to grief, anger, refusal, or testimony. It does not diagnose anyone as unable to integrate complexity. It does not convert suffering into a model residue. It does not say that PMS, CRITIQUE, MIP, or AHP has solved the tragic situation. It also does not make a metaphysical claim that all moral reality exceeds interpretation.

The non-capture note is that the loss itself remains. The broken promise, the unavailable repair, the unequal burden, or the grief may be structurally interpretable, but not thereby dissolved. A rival tragic, phenomenological, testimony-centered, or ethical reading may preserve the weight of this remainder better than an operator grammar.

The article takeaway is that non-capture can be a disciplined result rather than an analytic failure. The best structural reading may be the one that knows where to stop: it clarifies why closure is unavailable without pretending that clarification is itself repair.

##### Case takeaway

This case shows that AXIOM can render a closure-demand without satisfying it by force. The central movement is not from confusion to solution, but from interpretability to disciplined non-closure. PMS Core maps the structural relation between difference, frame, residue, recontextualization, and distance. PMS-CRITIQUE shows that the case is critique-ready but not correction-complete. MIP refuses the temptation to score or decide where no concrete scene exists. AHP hardens the analysis by marking its own vulnerability to over-smoothness.

The examples make the section reusable across nearby chapters because they show the same structural movement in different registers: organizational process, technical interpretability, and philosophical tragedy. Each example preserves the same boundary: explanation may matter, but it does not itself remove the practical remainder.

The methodological takeaway is therefore precise. Interpretation is not rejected; it is bounded. Coherence is not dismissed; it is prevented from becoming proof. Non-capture is not treated as failure; it is kept visible as the case’s central Λ-residue. The case should be used in the chapter as a reminder that a strong structural reading may be strongest exactly where it knows where to stop.

#### 8.4.3 Epistemic Injustice

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because epistemic injustice generates a distinctive closure demand: a morally difficult situation may not turn on what was explicitly done to a person, but on whether that person is allowed to count as a knower, interpreter, or credible participant in shared understanding. The wrong, if present, does not necessarily appear as a single prohibited act. It may appear through the frame that decides which statements count as knowledge, which interpretations count as relevant, which absences matter, and whose contribution can reorganize a shared field.

This makes the case structurally important for AXIOM. It shows a moral concept whose force is high but whose scene-specific evidence may be low or absent. The case asks for recognition, explanation, and possible correction, but it cannot responsibly be solved merely by naming the concept. “Epistemic injustice” may clarify a morally serious asymmetry; it may also become an over-assimilating label if every disagreement, refusal, non-response, or evidentiary caution is absorbed into it. The case therefore needs mapping rather than closure by assertion.

The completed analysis treats the case as a conceptual projection, not a concrete scene. This matters because operators such as Ω (Asymmetry), Θ (Temporality), Σ (Integration), Ψ (Self-binding), and the derived structures of responsibility or action can be structurally rendered, but they cannot become scene-specific findings without additional evidence. No concrete speaker, listener, institution, exchange, timeline, or repair process is supplied. The case therefore belongs in the chapter not as proof that epistemic injustice occurred, but as a structured demonstration of how AXIOM handles a closure demand where the frame of credibility and interpretation may itself be part of what is at issue.

The specific difficulty is that epistemic injustice is easy to over-close. Once the concept is introduced, there is pressure to move directly from possible non-uptake to injustice, from asymmetry to blame, from recontextualization to truth, and from critique-readability to validated critique. The case is valuable because it resists those shortcuts. PMS Core makes the structural grammar visible; PMS-CRITIQUE shows that the case is only weakly critique-readable, not critique-completed; MIP refuses scoring or IA adjudication; AHP hardens the analysis artefact against false completeness and transmission overreach. The result is a case section about disciplined visibility, not final judgment.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement begins with Δ (Difference). Epistemic injustice becomes legible only once distinctions are drawn between recognized and discounted epistemic standing: knower versus excluded knower, interpreter versus interpreted object, credible participant versus dismissed participant, contribution versus personal aside, shared understanding versus exclusion from that shared field. This is not a ranking of persons. It is the minimal differentiation that allows the case to be structurally discussed at all.

From Δ follows a weak but relevant ∇ (Impulse). The case implies directional pressure toward uptake, recognition, interpretation, correction, or participation. A contribution may ask to be treated as knowledge rather than anecdote; a missing concept may press for recognition; an excluded participant may seek intelligibility. But this remains structural directedness, not a psychological inference. No resentment, defensiveness, motive, affective state, or inner condition is inferred.

The central frame is □ (Frame): the credibility-and-interpretation frame in which shared understanding is organized. This frame determines what counts as testimony, interpretation, relevance, evidence, participation, and uptake. Epistemic injustice is not intelligible without such a frame, because the alleged wrong often concerns how the frame distributes credibility and intelligibility before any explicit judgment is made. This is why the frame cannot be assumed neutral merely because it is shared.

Λ (Non-event / residue) appears as possible non-uptake: not being heard as a knower, not being recognized as an interpreter, not receiving a response where one is expected, or not having an interpretive category available. But Λ is carefully calibrated. Absence is not automatically injustice. Silence may be refusal, caution, irrelevance, disagreement, or non-capture. A non-event only becomes structurally meaningful where a frame establishes an expectation that something should have occurred.

Α (Attractor) remains weak. Epistemic injustice often involves repeated patterns: recurring credibility deficits, stabilized dismissal, institutional scripts, or habitual exclusion from interpretation. But the abstract case material does not establish recurrence. Attractor language therefore marks a possible future specification, not a finding. A concrete case would need repeated events, routines, records, role scripts, or path dependence before Α could carry stronger weight.

Ω (Asymmetry) is conceptually central. The case concerns uneven standing in credibility, interpretation, participation, exposure, and authority. Some roles may grant or withhold uptake; other roles may bear the cost of not being believed or not being interpreted. Yet Ω is not a moral verdict by itself. Asymmetry marks directional imbalance, not guilt, bad faith, domination, or superiority. Because Ω depends on prior patterning in the PMS dependency path, it remains structurally important but dependency-weak unless recurrence or stabilized role structure is supplied.

Θ (Temporality) is also conditional. Epistemic injustice may unfold through delayed recognition, accumulated credibility loss, repeated non-uptake, inherited interpretive gaps, or institutional memory. None of that is established by the abstract material. Θ therefore marks where a later scene would need sequence, duration, recurrence, delay, or trajectory. It should not be read as already proving historical persistence.

Φ (Recontextualization) is active. The case asks how morally difficult situations should be understood when people are wronged in epistemic capacities. This invites a shift from “mere disagreement,” “failed communication,” or “insufficient evidence” toward “possible epistemic wronging under credibility and interpretive asymmetry.” But Φ is not proof. Recontextualization is valid only insofar as it improves discrimination. If it absorbs every rival reading into PMS terms, it becomes capture rather than clarification.

Χ (Distance) is the crucial application discipline. It keeps the analysis from becoming a tribunal. The case can be mapped only if the reading remains reversible, bounded, and non-fused with accusation or sanction. Χ does not mean refusing to name possible injustice. It means not converting structural legibility into verdict. In this case, Χ is what allows epistemic injustice to be examined without immediately declaring guilt, immaturity, legal responsibility, or metaphysical truth.

Σ (Integration) remains weak. The case may require integration of testimony, interpretive resources, absence, disagreement, asymmetry, and rival frames, but no actual integration occurs in the material. There is no repair process, no institutional response, no shared revised frame, and no corrective procedure. Coherence in the analysis is therefore not evidence of integrated praxis. This is one of the main guardrails of the case: PMS can describe the structure elegantly, but elegance is not adequacy.

Ψ (Self-binding) is not established as a case result. It would become relevant if an agent, institution, group, or analytic procedure bound itself over time to norms of credible uptake, interpretive fairness, response, review, or repair. The case gives no such commitment. Ψ remains primarily analytic discipline: the analysis binds itself to non-overclaim, non-coercion, reversibility, non-capture, and Dignity-in-Practice.

The core structural movement can therefore be rendered as:

Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ

The formula is deliberately conditional in its middle operators. It does not claim that Α, Θ, Σ, or Ψ are fully activated by the case material. It says that epistemic injustice is structurally projectable through framed epistemic difference, possible non-uptake, possible stabilization, asymmetry, conditional temporality, recontextualization, and required distance. It stops short of claiming integration or self-binding as achieved.

PMS-CRITIQUE adds a case-specific overlay. PMS Core already maps the structure, but it does not isolate whether the case is critique-readable, critique-productive, or critique-stable. CRITIQUE shows that the case reaches only weak minimal critique-readability. The reduced signature is Δ + □ + Χ: there is a mismatch, a frame, and maintained distance. This makes the case readable as critique, but not validated as critique.

CRITIQUE_PROD is not reached because Σ remains weak. No corrective step consolidates. CRITIQUE_STABLE is not reached because Ψ follow-up binding is absent. No review loop, audit path, repeated correction, or self-bound responsibility appears. The overlay’s final case-specific signature is therefore: **CRITIQUE_MIN_weak: Δ + □ + Χ, with Φ active as conceptual recontextualization, Σ weak, Ψ not established, publicness not applicable, and guarded drift risks around judgment, narrative repair, commentary, and integration bypass.**

MIP is relevant only as downstream discipline. It refuses assignment. No A-band, M-band, IA verdict, or D-profile is warranted because there is no concrete scene, role-holder, time window, evidence basis, publicness field, or power-to-act profile. This refusal is the MIP result, not a failure of analysis. Non-assignment is not a low score, and the MIP layer leaves the case at conceptual working-note status rather than turning it into a verdict, decision artefact, or applied maturity finding.

AHP then hardens the analysis artefact. It does not rescore MIP, activate D, or create new findings. Its case-specific result is that the analysis is precise but still vulnerable to misreading: formal completeness may look like evidential completeness; critique-readability may be mistaken for validated accusation; coherence may substitute for discrimination; dignity language may be quoted without its guardrails; and any future institutional use would need role, context, publicness, evidence, time-window, and response-channel gates.

##### What the analysis shows

The analysis shows that epistemic injustice is structurally visible where a frame of shared understanding distributes epistemic standing unevenly. PMS Core makes visible how differences of knowing and interpretation become frame-bound, how non-uptake may appear as meaningful absence, how asymmetry may organize exposure and credibility, and how recontextualization may shift a scene from ordinary disagreement to possible epistemic wronging.

It also shows why this visibility cannot become closure. The operators most central to the case—Δ (Difference), □ (Frame), Ω (Asymmetry), Φ (Recontextualization), and Χ (Distance)—are strong enough to map the closure demand. But the operators required for stronger scene-specific claims remain conditional or weak. Λ (Non-event / residue) requires a specified expectation. Α (Attractor) requires recurrence. Θ (Temporality) requires sequence or accumulation. Σ (Integration) requires corrective consolidation. Ψ (Self-binding) requires follow-up commitment. Without these, the analysis must remain structural and reversible.

The derived structures follow the same discipline. Awareness_A is weak to active only as framed differentiation; it does not imply phenomenological consciousness or actor awareness. Coherence_C is weak because the analysis is coherent but no concrete praxis sequence is supplied. Responsibility_R is weak because Ψ, Θ, and scene-specific Ω are not established. Action_E is under-specified because no integrated enactment occurs. Dignity_in_Practice_D functions as a praxeological constraint on handling asymmetry, not as ontological dignity or a dignity score. SELF_FIXPOINT remains analytic-only; the case does not establish stable self-relation or institutional self-binding.

The drift-pattern reading is also guarded. AD_A>>E is possible but not established: awareness of epistemic injustice could expand while enactment remains absent, but this abstract case does not show such a pattern. AD_Sigma_low is possible but not established: recontextualization could proliferate faster than integration, but disagreement, refusal, rupture, and non-capture are not low integration by default. Risk remains risk.

The add-on confirms rather than strengthens the verdict status. CRITIQUE shows critique-readability, not critique completion. MIP blocks scoring and governance overreach. AHP hardens the writing against false authority. Together, the layers produce more explicit separation, not stronger adjudication.

The case-specific closure that is possible is therefore limited: AXIOM can show where the concept of epistemic injustice becomes structurally inspectable, where critique can begin, and where a later concrete case would need evidence. The closure that remains impossible is equally important: the article does not decide whether epistemic injustice occurred, who is responsible, what remedy is required, or which rival theory is defeated.

##### Case-specific boundary

This case specifically invites three misuse risks.

First, the phrase “epistemic injustice” can become a verdict too quickly. The analysis must not treat the concept as proof. A frame may be structurally questionable without yet being unjust. Non-uptake may be possible without being established. Asymmetry may be visible without assigning blame.

Second, critique-readability can be mistaken for completed critique. The CRITIQUE overlay prevents this. A framed mismatch that can be interrupted is not yet productive correction, stable follow-up, or justified accusation. CRITIQUE_MIN is not CRITIQUE_PROD, and CRITIQUE_PROD is not CRITIQUE_STABLE.

Third, dignity language can intensify the case if mishandled. Here, Dignity-in-Practice protects against humiliation, ranking, and person-level degradation, but it does not activate a D-profile or prove a dignity violation. The case is especially sensitive because epistemic standing is closely tied to recognition; for that reason, dignity language must remain praxeological and protective, not ontological or accusatory.

The main weakening conditions are case-specific. The PMS reading weakens if the alleged wrong is better explained as ordinary disagreement, evidentiary caution, conceptual mismatch, or legitimate refusal. It weakens if no credibility or interpretation frame can be specified. It weakens if Λ is invoked without a clear expectation of uptake. It weakens if recurrence, temporality, responsibility, or follow-up binding is claimed without evidence. It becomes over-assimilative if every absence, rupture, disagreement, or rival framing is translated into PMS drift or failed integration.

Rival readings remain live. Epistemological accounts may better explain testimony and warrant. Hermeneutical accounts may better capture missing interpretive resources. Speech-act accounts may better analyze uptake. Political and sociological accounts may better capture historical or institutional power. Phenomenological accounts may better preserve lived experience. Anti-capture or resistance-centered readings may show why some refusals should not be integrated into a dominant frame. These are not defects in PMS; they mark where PMS must remain open.

##### Example 1 — The seminar contribution that does not yet count

This worked example shows why a concrete scene must specify frame, expected uptake, and asymmetry before epistemic injustice can be claimed rather than merely queried.

A graduate seminar is discussing a canonical text on testimony. One participant offers an interpretation that connects the text’s argument to a form of exclusion she has encountered in academic discussion: some statements are not rejected explicitly, but are never treated as contributions around which the conversation has to reorganize. The seminar leader responds politely, thanks her for the comment, and returns to the planned interpretive question. Other participants continue as if the comment were an illustrative aside rather than a claim about the structure of the discussion itself.

The vignette is intentionally bounded. It does not establish that epistemic injustice occurred. It does not prove that the seminar leader acted wrongly, that the participant was harmed, that the institution is exclusionary, or that the other participants lacked moral awareness. It only supplies a scene in which the question becomes structurally legible: a contribution is made, the frame of relevance does not shift, and the contribution may have been treated as personal illustration rather than interpretive participation.

What this example demonstrates is the difference between **non-uptake as possible structure** and **non-uptake as established wrong**. The relevant issue is not whether someone “felt ignored” or whether the seminar leader had bad intentions. The PMS question is narrower: within this frame, was the contribution expected to count as an interpretation of the shared object, and did the frame actually allow it to count that way? If the contribution was merely acknowledged but not integrated into the discussion’s relevance structure, then a possible Λ (Non-event / residue) appears: an expected uptake event may not have occurred. But that remains conditional. The comment might also have been unclear, outside the session’s scope, insufficiently connected to the text, or simply one of many contributions that could not all redirect the discussion. The example does not decide among these readings.

The PMS Core hooks are visible. **Δ (Difference)** marks the difference between “comment as personal aside” and “comment as interpretive contribution.” **∇ (Impulse)** appears as pressure toward uptake: the participant’s contribution implicitly asks the seminar to reconsider what counts as relevant interpretation. **□ (Frame)** is the seminar frame: a setting with norms about textual relevance, authority, turn-taking, and legitimate interpretive moves. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** is possible if the expected event—recognition of the comment as a frame-relevant contribution—does not occur. **Α (Attractor)** remains weak unless the same pattern repeats over multiple sessions or across participants. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is present as a possible asymmetry: the seminar leader has more authority to define relevance, while the participant has less control over whether her contribution reshapes the discussion. **Θ (Temporality)** would become stronger if the pattern accumulated over time. **Φ (Recontextualization)** appears if the scene is recontextualized from “a comment that did not fit” to “a possible case of epistemic non-uptake.” **Χ (Distance)** is required so the analysis does not become an accusation. **Σ (Integration)** is weak because no repair or revised discussion structure is shown. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is not established because no one binds themselves to future uptake norms.

The CRITIQUE overlay helps by showing that the scene is at most **weakly critique-readable**. The mismatch between epistemic standing and the seminar’s relevance frame can be interrupted and examined: this is the CRITIQUE_MIN zone. But the example does not reach productive critique. No corrective step occurs. The seminar leader does not reopen the discussion, establish a review norm, or invite clarification in a way that changes the frame. It also does not reach stable critique, because there is no follow-up binding.

MIP remains non-scoring. No A-band or M-band can be assigned. The leader’s knowledge, alternatives, role obligations, time pressure, and actual interpretive options are not sufficiently specified. The IA-box would be only a question: were the frame rules transparent, justified, time-bounded, and reversible? AHP would flag the example as useful but sensitive: a reader might mistake critique-readability for proof that the seminar leader committed epistemic injustice. That is exactly what the example must prevent.

The forbidden claims are therefore strict. The example may not claim discrimination, bad faith, immaturity, guilt, institutional pathology, or legal relevance. It may not diagnose the participant as wounded or the leader as dismissive. It may not treat polite acknowledgment as proof of non-uptake, nor non-integration as proof of injustice.

The non-capture note is equally important. The participant’s lived experience, the affective texture of being heard or not heard, and the broader history of seminar norms may exceed PMS capture. A phenomenological or political reading may preserve aspects that the structural analysis only marks abstractly.

The article takeaway is that epistemic injustice becomes visible only when the frame of uptake is examined, but visibility is not verdict. A PMS reading can identify where the question should be asked without deciding the answer in advance.

##### Example 2 — The review meeting and the missing category

This worked example shows how a missing interpretive category can become structurally relevant without yet proving that the frame is unjust or that formal verification is illegitimate.

A research team is reviewing a classification scheme for field reports. The scheme contains categories for “confirmed incident,” “unconfirmed incident,” and “irrelevant report.” A field worker objects that several reports from local participants do not fit these categories. They describe recurring conditions that are not yet incidents, but that local participants recognize as early warning signs. The project lead replies that the scheme cannot include categories that lack formal verification. The reports are therefore placed under “unconfirmed” or “irrelevant,” and the review proceeds.

This vignette is bounded as a structural illustration. It does not claim that the project lead is wrong, that the field worker is right, or that the classification scheme is unjust. It does not establish that the local participants possess superior knowledge, nor that formal verification is oppressive. It shows a situation in which epistemic and interpretive standing may become structurally difficult: some participants appear to have a category of relevance that the official frame does not yet recognize.

The example demonstrates the relation between **hermeneutical non-capture** and **frame-bound non-uptake**. The key issue is not merely that one person disagrees with a classification scheme. The issue is that the available frame may lack a category for something that matters to some participants. If the missing category prevents certain forms of situated knowledge from becoming intelligible within the review process, then epistemic injustice becomes a possible reading. But it remains possible only. The project lead may have legitimate methodological constraints. The reports may be too ambiguous. The category may be too broad, too risky, or too difficult to validate. PMS must preserve these alternatives.

The PMS Core hooks are especially clear. **Δ (Difference)** marks the difference between recognized report types and the proposed missing category. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as pressure to make the reports usable: the field worker pushes toward recognition; the project lead pushes toward methodological closure. **□ (Frame)** is the classification frame, including evidence rules, verification standards, and decision purposes. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** appears as a possible non-event: the expected category or interpretive resource does not arrive. It is not simply silence; it is a missing structural place within the frame. **Α (Attractor)** would become active if the scheme repeatedly absorbs local early-warning reports into “unconfirmed” or “irrelevant,” thereby stabilizing a pattern. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is visible because the project lead or institution controls the categories, while field participants bear the cost of being unreadable within them. **Θ (Temporality)** becomes relevant if the pattern accumulates across reporting cycles. **Φ (Recontextualization)** appears when the scene is recontextualized from “insufficient evidence” to “possible interpretive exclusion.” **Χ (Distance)** prevents the analysis from declaring the scheme illegitimate too quickly. **Σ (Integration)** would require integration of formal verification needs and local interpretive categories. **Ψ (Self-binding)** would require a future binding: for example, the team commits to reviewing the scheme after a trial period.

The CRITIQUE overlay sharpens the threshold. The field worker’s objection creates a critique-readable mismatch: the frame’s categories do not accommodate a kind of situated report. CRITIQUE_MIN is plausible because Δ, □, and Χ can be maintained: there is a mismatch, a frame, and a reflective pause is possible. CRITIQUE_PROD is not yet reached unless the team creates a corrective step, such as a provisional category, a review protocol, or an explicit test for whether early-warning reports have predictive value. CRITIQUE_STABLE is not reached unless the team binds itself to follow-up: a review date, responsibility assignment, audit trace, or revision condition.

This example also shows why drift risks remain risks, not findings. Judgment drift would occur if “epistemic injustice” were used simply to label the project lead’s refusal as morally defective. Narrative repair drift would occur if the team produces a sympathetic explanation of local knowledge but still changes nothing. Commentary drift would occur if the issue is discussed extensively but no review mechanism is created. Silence-attractor risk would become relevant only if similar objections repeatedly disappear without acknowledgment.

MIP status remains guarded. No A-score or M-score should be assigned. The project lead’s constraints, knowledge, authority, alternatives, and obligations are not fully specified. The IA-box could become useful in a later concrete analysis: were the asymmetrical category rules transparent, justified, time-bounded, and reversible? At present, those remain questions. AHP would recommend hardening the example by requiring future-scene thresholds before calling the frame unjust or mature.

Forbidden claims include legal liability, methodological misconduct, epistemic superiority, bad faith, or proof that local participants were wronged. The example must not become an argument against verification standards as such. It must not imply that every unrecognized category is valid, or that formal categories are inherently oppressive.

The non-capture note is substantial. A hermeneutical reading may better describe the missing category. An empirical reading may be needed to test whether the early-warning reports are reliable. A political reading may be needed if the categories systematically privilege institutional knowledge over local knowledge. PMS can map the structural tension, but it cannot decide all of these questions.

The article takeaway is that epistemic injustice can appear where a frame lacks room for a form of knowing, but PMS must distinguish missing category, methodological caution, and actual exclusion. The example shows why Σ and Ψ cannot be claimed until the frame produces correction and follow-up binding.

##### Example 3 — The public memo that should not become a verdict

This worked example shows why the stack’s guardrails become most important when a structural analysis begins to approach governance, transmission, ranking, or institutional use.

An organization drafts an internal memo after receiving complaints that some contributors’ expertise is not being taken seriously in cross-functional meetings. The memo states that “epistemic injustice may be structurally relevant where credible participation is repeatedly discounted.” It recommends reviewing meeting procedures, clarifying what counts as expertise in different contexts, and creating a reversible process for participants to request that a contribution be reconsidered. Before the memo is finalized, someone suggests naming the teams where the problem is “most severe” and ranking managers by “epistemic maturity.”

This example is designed as a guardrail case. It shows how a structurally useful concept can become dangerous when moved toward governance, publicness, or ranking. The memo’s initial formulation is cautious: it says epistemic injustice may be structurally relevant. It proposes review procedures rather than verdicts. It keeps the focus on frames, criteria, and reversibility. The suggested ranking, by contrast, would turn a conceptual and procedural analysis into person evaluation. That is precisely the move the completed YAML analyses prohibit.

The example demonstrates the difference between **structural use** and **misuse by escalation**. The organization may legitimately ask whether meeting frames distribute credibility unevenly. It may ask whether expertise criteria are explicit. It may create procedures for reconsideration. But it may not use PMS, CRITIQUE, MIP, or AHP to rank managers, diagnose teams, assign moral maturity, or declare epistemic guilt without evidence and due process. Even with evidence, PMS would still not be a person-ranking tool.

The PMS Core hooks are distributed across the memo’s possible uses. **Δ (Difference)** appears in distinctions between recognized and discounted expertise. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as pressure to respond to complaints. **□ (Frame)** is the meeting and organizational decision frame: what counts as expertise, relevance, contribution, and uptake. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may appear where expected reconsideration or acknowledgment does not occur. **Α (Attractor)** would require repeated patterns across meetings, not isolated dissatisfaction. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is central because managers, chairs, or dominant roles may control uptake, agenda, and recognition. **Θ (Temporality)** matters if patterns persist across review cycles or become part of organizational culture. **Φ (Recontextualization)** appears when complaints are recontextualized as possible epistemic wronging rather than interpersonal friction. **Χ (Distance)** is essential: the memo must preserve distance and avoid verdict-fusion. **Σ (Integration)** would appear only if procedures integrate complaints, role duties, meeting design, and review mechanisms into a coherent process. **Ψ (Self-binding)** would appear only if the organization binds itself to a non-coercive follow-up process, such as periodic review, documented criteria, or accountable revision.

The CRITIQUE overlay makes the escalation visible. The memo, if written carefully, may reach weak CRITIQUE_MIN: it identifies a framed mismatch and maintains distance. It may even move toward CRITIQUE_PROD if it creates a concrete corrective step, such as a meeting review protocol. It approaches CRITIQUE_STABLE only if the organization binds itself to follow-up in a transparent, non-punitive way. But the proposed ranking of managers would be critique drift. It risks judgment masquerading as critique, moralization drift, and possibly public pillory if circulated. It would convert critique-readability into verdict-readiness.

MIP is directly relevant here, but still bounded. This is the kind of context where a downstream guardrail reading matters because the artefact may influence institutional practice. Yet the MIP response should not be to assign A-bands or M-bands to managers. It should ask whether the memo’s use is transparent, justified, time-bounded, reversible, and dignity-protective. It should check publicness, role clarity, power-to-act, and transmission status. The D-module should remain off unless a very specific, safe, role-bound and time-windowed dignity analysis is warranted. Even then, D0 remains untouched and no dignity score is assigned.

AHP is also relevant. It would flag several attack surfaces: the memo may look more evidentially complete than it is; “epistemic injustice” may be misread as validated accusation; critique-readability may be mistaken for proof; ranking language may create person-level harm; and transmission beyond the intended audience may turn a working note into a public verdict. AHP would recommend hardening the memo’s scope, adding explicit non-ranking language, defining review thresholds, and repeating that the document is not a decision basis unless further evidence and process are supplied.

The forbidden claims are extensive. The memo must not claim that named managers are epistemically unjust, immature, deficient, oppressive, or morally ranked. It must not treat complaints as proof without review. It must not treat structural explanation as exoneration either. It must not use “maturity” as a hidden score. It must not turn Dignity-in-Practice into a dignity rating. It must not treat AHP attack points as defects in people.

The non-capture note is important because an organizational memo may not capture the lived experience of contributors who were discounted. A procedural review may be necessary but insufficient. Conversely, some complaints may reflect disagreement, role confusion, unclear expertise boundaries, or evidence standards rather than injustice. PMS and MIP can structure the review, but they cannot replace affected perspectives, institutional context, or fair process.

The article takeaway is that the stack is most valuable when it prevents premature escalation. PMS makes the structural issue legible, CRITIQUE distinguishes critique-readability from completed critique, MIP prevents scoring and governance overreach, and AHP hardens the artefact against misuse. The example prepares the later conclusion by showing that the point of the analysis is not to name culprits, but to keep closure, correction, and dignity-protection properly bounded.

This case shows how AXIOM can make a morally forceful concept structurally inspectable without turning it into a verdict machine. PMS Core maps how epistemic standing may be framed, withheld, recontextualized, and held under distance. PMS-CRITIQUE shows that the case is weakly critique-readable, but not productive or stable critique. MIP refuses scoring, IA adjudication, and D-profile activation because the case is not a concrete scene. AHP hardens the analysis against false completeness, misquotation, and governance overreach.

What remains visible is the Λ-residue: the unresolved space between possible non-uptake and established wrong, between critique-readability and completed critique, between structural coherence and proof, and between analytical clarity and operational use.

#### 8.4.4 Legitimacy Without Authority

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because it isolates a distinctive closure-demand: the demand to decide whether legitimacy can stand where no recognized authority is available to certify, ratify, authorize, or settle it. The situation is structurally difficult because it is not merely a dispute about whether a particular authority is valid. It is a dispute about what happens when legitimacy is claimed, granted, or contested in the absence of any recognized authority capable of closing the frame.

That absence invites two opposite but equally premature closures. One closure says: without recognized authority, legitimacy is missing. The other says: if legitimacy appears without authority, authority was never necessary. Both moves decide too quickly. They treat the absence of authority either as a defect or as liberation before the relevant frame has been named. The case therefore belongs in AXIOM because it tests whether a structurally legible pressure toward judgment can be mapped without being resolved into a verdict.

The case is a conceptual projection rather than a concrete scene. It does not supply actors, institutions, jurisdictions, publicness conditions, evidence, chronology, harm, or enforceable outcomes. That has direct consequences for the analysis. Operators such as Ω (Asymmetry), Θ (Temporality), Σ (Integration), Ψ (Self-binding), Responsibility_R, and Action_E may be structurally prepared, but they cannot become scene-specific findings without additional material. The case is mapped rather than solved.

Its value lies in showing how legitimacy can become readable as a pressure field. The analysis must ask: recognized by whom, under which frame, for what purpose, and with what missing authority event? It must also preserve the possibility that legitimacy, authority, recognition, public uptake, legal validity, moral justification, and PMS readability are not the same thing. That is the central reason the case matters for the chapter: it shows how closure is demanded precisely where closure is not yet warranted.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement begins with Δ (Difference). The case is built from distinctions: legitimacy versus authority, recognized versus unrecognized authority, claim versus grant, grant versus contestation, structural readability versus validity. If legitimacy were simply identical with recognized authority, the case would collapse. If absence of recognized authority automatically meant illegitimacy, the case would also collapse. PMS holds the distinction open.

From that distinction follows ∇ (Impulse), the moral-practical pressure to respond. A legitimacy claim made without recognized authority calls for acceptance, refusal, justification, contestation, reframing, or suspension. This impulse is structural, not psychological. It does not license motive attribution, diagnosis, or moral ranking. It only marks that the difference between legitimacy and authority generates directed pressure.

The decisive calibration point is □ (Frame). Legitimacy without authority means different things in different frames. In a legal frame, recognized authority may be indispensable. In a communal-recognition frame, legitimacy may arise from uptake by relevant participants. In an anti-authoritarian frame, absence of recognized authority may not be a deficit at all. In a philosophical frame, the case may remain a conceptual boundary problem. PMS must therefore name the operative frame before reading the absence of authority as meaningful.

That absence is Λ (Non-event / residue): recognized authority is missing where some frame might expect an authorizing, ratifying, certifying, or settling event. Λ is strong in the case, but it is not sovereign. A non-event is only meaningful relative to a frame. If the frame expects authority, the absence matters. If the frame does not require authority, the same absence may not be a failure. This is the case’s central residue: authority absence is visible, but its meaning is not self-deciding.

Α (Attractor) remains weak. The case describes a possible class of situations, but it does not show recurrence, stabilized scripts, repeated recognition practices, or path-dependence. A later scene might show legitimacy without authority becoming a durable pattern; the present case does not. Ω (Asymmetry) is relevant but low-resolution. Legitimacy without recognized authority may distribute exposure unevenly: claimants may bear risks of non-recognition, granters may gain informal recognition power, contesters may bear challenge costs, and affected parties may lack procedural recourse. Yet no direction of asymmetry can be assigned without concrete roles.

Θ (Temporality) is also weak. Legitimacy claims may persist, recognition may be delayed, contestation may harden, or absence may become recurrent. But no sequence is supplied. This matters because stronger structures such as Responsibility_R, Action_E, Σ (Integration), and Ψ (Self-binding) require temporal material. The case does not provide that material.

Φ (Recontextualization) is active conceptually. The case reframes legitimacy apart from recognized authority. It asks whether legitimacy can be read without the usual settling mechanism. But recontextualization is not resolution. It does not decide whether legitimacy is valid, invalid, emancipatory, defective, legal, or morally justified. Χ (Distance) is therefore essential. The analysis must keep legitimacy claim, recognized authority, legal validity, moral truth, critique threshold, and PMS readability separated. Χ is strong as application discipline, but the case does not show case-internal stop-capability by any actor.

Σ (Integration) remains weak. PMS can organize the rival frames, but that organization is not the same as integrating them into a shared practical resolution. Ψ (Self-binding) remains analytic-only. No actor, institution, community, or authority binds itself to a legitimacy procedure over time. The abstract case itself is not a self.

The strongest PMS movement is therefore:

**Χ (Distance) ∘ Φ (Recontextualization) ∘ Θ? (Temporality) ∘ Ω? (Asymmetry) ∘ Α? (Attractor) ∘ Λ (Non-event / residue) ∘ □ (Frame) ∘ ∇ (Impulse) ∘ Δ (Difference)**

The question marks matter. Pattern, asymmetry, and temporality are possible but not established. Integration and self-binding are not part of the primary result. The formula marks a disciplined structural projection, not a closure machine.

PMS-CRITIQUE adds a case-specific threshold reading. The legitimacy/authority mismatch is weakly readable as **CRITIQUE_MIN**: Δ (Difference) + □ (Frame) + Χ (Distance) as framed, interruptible mismatch. This does not make the case productive critique. Productive critique would require Φ (Recontextualization) plus Σ (Integration): a reframed mismatch becoming a coordinatable corrective step. Stable critique would require Ψ (Self-binding): follow-up owned over time without coercing others. None of that is supplied. The overlay therefore sharpens the case by showing that the situation is critique-readable only at a weak threshold level; it is not correction, not follow-up binding, and not public legitimacy.

The MIP layer adds a refusal rather than a score. The case is too abstract for maturity scoring. A-score and M-score are not assigned. IA classification is under-specified and remains a non-classification result, not weak IA or low IA. The D-module remains off because no D1/D2 dignity-in-practice enactments are supplied. This is not a negative result; it is the correct result for a non-concrete case. AHP then hardens the analysis artifact: the main precision vulnerability is that “legitimacy,” “recognized authority,” “claim,” “grant,” and “contestation” remain working terms rather than fully defined scene terms. AHP improves the next iteration; it does not rescore, activate D, or upgrade the analysis.

##### What the analysis shows

The case shows that legitimacy without authority is structurally readable as a frame-dependent pressure field, not as a solved legitimacy theory. PMS Core maps the movement from distinction to pressure, from pressure to frame, from frame to structured absence, from absence to possible asymmetry, from asymmetry to weak temporality, and from there to conceptual recontextualization held under distance.

The central structural result is that Λ (Non-event / residue) remains visible without becoming a verdict. Recognized authority is absent, but absence alone does not decide legitimacy. PMS can show why closure is demanded: the missing authority event creates pressure. It can also show why closure remains unwarranted: the frame that would make authority necessary has not been fixed.

PMS-CRITIQUE shows that the same structure can become weakly critique-readable. A legitimacy claim without recognized authority may be interrupted as a mismatch. But critique readability is not correction. The case does not supply the Σ (Integration) needed for productive critique or the Ψ (Self-binding) needed for stable follow-up. The drift risks are therefore bounded risks: judgment masquerading as critique, narrative repair masquerading as critique, and commentary or analysis paralysis. They are not findings.

MIP shows that full template coverage is not the same as maturity assessment. The case lacks the role, power, time, consequence, and enactment data required for scoring or IA classification. AHP shows that the analysis is strong but still hardenable: especially by defining the key terms more explicitly if the case is later used in a concrete setting.

The possible closure is limited. PMS can close the operator map enough to show where the structural pressure lies. PMS-CRITIQUE can close the critique-threshold reading enough to say “weak CRITIQUE_MIN only.” MIP can close the maturity question enough to refuse scoring. AHP can close the analysis-quality review enough to identify term-definition hardening. None of these closures decides legitimacy itself.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk of this case is **closure-by-substitute-authority**. Because the case concerns legitimacy without recognized authority, the analysis itself could be misused as the authority that settles legitimacy. That would invert the point of the case. PMS, PMS-CRITIQUE, MIP, and AHP must not become the ratifying authority whose absence generated the problem.

A second case-specific risk is **authority-default drift**: treating absence of recognized authority as automatic illegitimacy. The opposite risk is **anti-authority romanticization**: treating absence of recognized authority as automatic legitimacy. Both are invalid shortcuts. The case requires frame calibration before either direction can be assessed.

A third risk is **critique inflation**. A contestation of legitimacy may be critique-readable, but it is not automatically productive critique. Without Σ (Integration), there is no corrective step. Without Ψ (Self-binding), there is no stable follow-up. PMS-CRITIQUE reduced signatures remain overlay handles, not PMS operators.

A fourth risk is **score pressure**. Because MIP is present downstream, the completed template can look evaluative. Here, however, non-assignment is the result. Non-assignment is not a low score. Working-note status is not a verdict. IA under-specification is not hidden IA classification. Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological and guardrail-bound, not ontological or person-ranking.

The final case-specific boundary is that rival readings are not defects. Normative theory may preserve the question of whether legitimacy can be justified without recognized authority. Recognition theory may preserve the question of whose uptake counts. Legal-political analysis may distinguish legitimacy, legality, jurisdiction, mandate, and enforceability more sharply than PMS can within this case. Anti-authoritarian critique may read the absence of recognized authority not as a deficit but as the very condition under which legitimacy becomes possible. Discourse ethics may ask whether non-coercive participation or public reason is the relevant authority substitute. Rhetorical and language-practice readings may focus on what is performed by claiming, granting, or contesting legitimacy. Phenomenological readings may preserve lived exposure or recognition experience that PMS does not claim to access. Anti-capture readings may object that formalizing legitimacy without authority risks installing the analysis itself as a substitute authority. These rival readings remain open; their persistence is not a failure of the analysis.

##### Example 1 — The Informal Mediator

A neighborhood group faces a recurring dispute over shared courtyard use. There is no formal association, no elected board, and no recognized authority empowered to decide binding rules. One resident begins to coordinate discussions, keeps notes, and proposes a shared calendar. Several neighbors treat this person’s proposals as legitimate because the process is useful, transparent enough for practical coordination, and open to revision. Others object: the resident was never elected, has no mandate, and should not be treated as an authority.

The vignette is bounded. It does not establish whether the resident’s role is actually legitimate. It does not decide whether the objectors are right. It does not claim that informal coordination is better or worse than formal authority. It only provides a readable scene in which legitimacy is claimed, partly granted, and contested without recognized authority.

What the example demonstrates is the frame-dependence of authority absence. In a formal legal or institutional frame, the resident has no recognized authority. In a practical coordination frame, the resident’s role may still acquire limited legitimacy because others use the calendar, respond to the notes, and treat the process as provisionally helpful. In a democratic-procedural frame, the same arrangement may appear deficient because there was no authorization procedure. The point is not to select one frame as correct. The point is to show why PMS must ask which frame makes authority absence relevant before treating that absence as a failure.

The PMS Core hooks are clear. **Δ (Difference)** appears in the distinction between legitimacy and authority: the resident may be treated as practically legitimate by some without being formally authorized. **∇ (Impulse)** appears in the pressure to settle the dispute: the group needs coordination, and the absence of authority intensifies the demand for closure. **□ (Frame)** is decisive because different frames alter the meaning of the same conduct. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** appears as the missing authorization event: no election, no mandate, no recognized appointment. But Λ is not automatically a defect; it is a structured absence only relative to frames that expected such authorization. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is possible but not yet a verdict: the note-taking resident may acquire influence, and dissenters may face coordination costs if the calendar becomes de facto binding. **Θ (Temporality)** would become stronger only if the arrangement persisted and hardened over time. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is visible when the group shifts from “who has authority?” to “what makes this coordination acceptable?” **Χ (Distance)** is required so that the analysis does not become either a celebration of informal legitimacy or a condemnation of unauthorized authority. **Σ (Integration)** and **Ψ (Self-binding)** remain conditional unless the group later integrates a shared procedure and binds itself to it.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay adds a useful distinction. The objectors’ challenge may be **weak CRITIQUE_MIN** if it remains framed and interruptible: they identify a mismatch, locate it in the frame of authorization, and preserve the possibility of revision. It is not yet productive critique unless it produces a coordinatable correction, such as a rotating facilitation process or an agreed review meeting. It is not stable critique unless someone or some group binds itself to follow-up over time. The drift risks are only risks. The dispute could slide into judgment drift if “unauthorized” becomes a label that replaces coordination. It could slide into commentary drift if the group discusses legitimacy indefinitely but never decides whether any procedure is needed. Neither is a finding from the vignette alone.

MIP would remain limited. A-score and M-score should not be assigned unless the example supplies role-specific knowledge, power, alternatives, predictability, and consequences. The IA-box could name possible asymmetry, but it should not classify it without more evidence: Can dissenters opt out? Does the calendar affect access to the courtyard? Can the facilitator be replaced? Are affected parties heard? The D-module should remain off unless dignity-in-practice enactments appear. AHP would likely flag term precision: “legitimacy,” “coordination,” “authority,” and “mandate” would need working definitions before the vignette could support stronger claims.

Forbidden claims are therefore important. The example does not prove that informal leadership is legitimate. It does not prove that formal authority is necessary. It does not diagnose the resident as power-seeking or the objectors as immature. It does not establish legal standing, institutional authority, or moral superiority. It does not validate PMS.

The non-capture note is also central. A democratic theory may insist that procedural authorization is the decisive issue. A communitarian reading may emphasize recognition through practice. A legal reading may ask whether any enforceable right exists. PMS can map these frames, but it does not replace them.

The article takeaway is that legitimacy without authority is not a single condition. It is a frame-dependent pressure field. The same absence of recognized authority may be a defect, a neutral feature, or a condition of provisional coordination depending on which frame is operative and how asymmetry, reversibility, and follow-up are handled.

##### Example 2 — The Emergency Repair Decision

A small research lab discovers a water leak late at night. The official facilities manager is unreachable, and no written emergency protocol is available. A junior researcher contacts a plumber, authorizes a temporary repair, and sends a message to the lab mailing list explaining what happened. The next morning, some colleagues thank the researcher for preventing damage. Others object that the researcher had no authority to authorize costs or act on behalf of the lab.

This example is concrete enough to be readable but still bounded. It does not establish legal authority, financial liability, employment responsibility, or moral praise. It also does not decide whether the repair decision was legitimate. It only shows how legitimacy may be claimed, granted, and contested where recognized authority is absent at the moment of action.

PMS Core first separates the relevant distinctions. **Δ (Difference)** marks the difference between formal authority and practical uptake: the researcher lacks recognized authorization but may be treated by some as having acted legitimately under emergency constraints. **∇ (Impulse)** is strong because the leak creates pressure to act. Without recognized authority available, the closure-demand intensifies: someone must decide whether to wait, intervene, or escalate. **□ (Frame)** becomes the hinge. In an emergency-preservation frame, temporary action may appear legitimate because delay risks damage. In a budget-authority frame, the same action may appear unauthorized. In a procedural governance frame, the issue may be less the repair itself than the missing protocol. These frames cannot be silently collapsed.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is the missing expected event: the facilities manager does not respond, the protocol is absent, the authorized channel is unavailable. But Λ must be carefully calibrated. The absence of authority matters because the frame expected an authority to decide. It is not yet proof that the researcher’s action was invalid or valid. **Α (Attractor)** might emerge if emergencies are repeatedly handled by whoever happens to be present, but the vignette does not yet show recurrence. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is relevant because costs and exposure are uneven: the junior researcher may bear employment risk, the lab may bear financial cost, and later reviewers may have authority to approve or criticize after the fact. Still, Ω should not be moralized. **Θ (Temporality)** is partly visible because timing matters: the leak created a narrow window, and later review may reinterpret the action. But a full temporal trajectory is not supplied unless we follow the aftermath.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** appears when the event is reframed after the fact. Was this unauthorized spending, emergency stewardship, procedural failure, or informal legitimacy under constraint? Each recontextualization changes what the action means. **Χ (Distance)** is essential: the analysis must not rush into praise, blame, legality, or diagnosis. It must preserve the difference between a structural emergency, an authority absence, and the legitimacy of action under pressure. **Σ (Integration)** remains conditional. The lab might later integrate the event into a new emergency procedure; until then, no integration is established. **Ψ (Self-binding)** would appear only if the lab binds itself to a revised protocol, or if the researcher explicitly accepts a defined role for future emergencies. Neither follows automatically.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay helps distinguish critique from reaction. A colleague who says “you had no right to do that” may be reacting, judging, or critiquing depending on whether the objection is framed, interruptible, and oriented toward correction. If the objection identifies the mismatch between emergency action and authorization procedure while preserving review and revision, it may reach weak CRITIQUE_MIN. If the lab creates a temporary-spending rule, emergency contact chain, and review procedure, productive critique becomes possible because Φ and Σ are linked to correction. Stable critique would require follow-up binding: someone owns the protocol, updates it, and reviews it over time. The vignette does not yet show that.

MIP status remains cautious. It would be tempting to assign responsibility: the researcher acted, the manager was absent, the lab lacked a protocol. But the MIP analysis must not assign M-score without evidence of knowledge, power-control, predictability, access to alternatives, and serious integration attempts. The IA-box might later ask whether asymmetry was transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible: Did the researcher document the decision? Was the repair limited? Could the cost be reviewed? Were affected parties informed? But these questions require scene data. The D-module remains off unless the aftermath includes humiliation, shaming, standing-stripping, or dignity-protective conduct.

AHP would likely harden the analysis by demanding term definitions. “Emergency,” “authority,” “temporary repair,” “legitimate action,” and “recognized authorization” must be defined before the example is used in a stronger argument. AHP would also warn that full template coverage is not equivalent to a maturity verdict. The example may illustrate the structure, but it does not score the actor.

Forbidden claims include: the junior researcher was legally authorized; the junior researcher was morally superior; the colleagues were obstructive; the absent manager was responsible; the lab is dysfunctional; emergency action is always legitimate; procedural authority is always secondary. None of these follows.

The non-capture note matters because legal, organizational, ethical, and emergency-management frameworks may each preserve features PMS does not decide. A legal reading may focus on spending authority. An organizational reading may focus on protocol design. An ethical reading may focus on preventable damage. PMS maps the interaction of those frames but does not settle them.

The article takeaway is that legitimacy without authority often becomes clearest under temporal pressure. The absence of recognized authority does not dissolve the need for action, but it also does not automatically legitimate whoever acts. PMS preserves the pressure, the absence, the frame conflict, and the need for review without converting them into verdict.

##### Example 3 — The Open Letter Without a Mandate

A group of students publishes an open letter claiming to speak for “the concerned members” of a university department. The letter criticizes a proposed curriculum change and demands that the decision be paused. The group has no elected mandate, no formal representative status, and no recognized authority to speak for the department. Some readers treat the letter as legitimate because it articulates concerns that had not been heard in the official process. Others reject it as illegitimate because the authors claim a collective voice without authorization.

This example is intentionally high-risk and must remain guardrail-bound. It involves publicness-adjacent dynamics, representation, critique, and legitimacy without recognized authority. However, the vignette does not identify real persons, does not decide whether the letter is valid, and does not establish whether the students represent anyone beyond themselves. It is an illustration of the article’s structure, not evidence for a governance conclusion.

At the PMS Core level, **Δ (Difference)** marks several distinctions: speaking as individuals versus speaking for a group; legitimacy versus recognized mandate; critique versus representation; public uptake versus formal authority. **∇ (Impulse)** appears in the pressure to respond to the curriculum change. A decision is pending, and the letter attempts to interrupt it. **□ (Frame)** is crucial. In a formal governance frame, the lack of mandate may weaken the representative claim. In a public critique frame, the letter may not need formal authority to raise concerns. In a recognition frame, legitimacy may depend on whether affected members identify with the concerns. In an institutional process frame, the issue may be whether official channels failed to include relevant voices.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** appears as missing authorization: no election, no formal appointment, no recognized representative procedure. It may also appear as a missing response event if concerns were previously raised but not addressed. Yet PMS must keep these Λ-structures separate. Missing mandate is not the same as missing hearing. Missing hearing is not proof of legitimacy. Missing authorization is not proof of illegitimacy. Each absence is meaningful only within a frame. **Α (Attractor)** might become active if open letters become the default way to interrupt decisions, but the vignette alone does not establish that. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is relevant because public letters can redistribute exposure: authors may gain visibility, named decision-makers may face public pressure, quieter members may feel represented or misrepresented, and institutional actors may control formal response channels. But Ω remains a structure to inspect, not a verdict.

**Θ (Temporality)** is more salient here than in a purely abstract case because timing matters: the proposed decision is pending, the letter asks for a pause, and public circulation may create residue. Still, the example does not supply long-term trajectory. **Φ (Recontextualization)** appears when the curriculum issue is recontextualized from an internal governance matter into a public legitimacy dispute. **Χ (Distance)** is essential to avoid fusing critique with righteousness or unauthorized speech with illegitimacy. **Σ (Integration)** is not established unless the letter leads to a coordinated review, clarified representation procedure, or revised curriculum process. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is not established unless authors, department, or institution bind themselves to follow-up.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay is especially useful. The letter may instantiate weak **CRITIQUE_MIN** if it identifies a mismatch, locates it in a frame, and remains interruptible: “the process is proceeding without adequate recognition of affected concerns; pause and review.” It is not automatically productive critique. Productive critique would require Φ and Σ: a reframed problem plus an actionable correction, such as a defined consultation window, a procedure for collecting views, or a concrete revision pathway. Stable critique would require Ψ: follow-up ownership, review dates, and non-coercive commitment to the process.

The example also shows why CRITIQUE drift risks must remain risks, not findings. **Judgment drift** could occur if the letter labels the curriculum process illegitimate without offering a correction path. **Narrative repair drift** could occur if the dispute becomes endless reframing about who is really represented while no process changes. **Commentary drift** could occur if the institution produces reflective statements without review or action. But none of these is established by the vignette alone. They become visible as possible analysis attack surfaces.

Publicness requires care. The original case analysis marked the publicness overlay P as not applicable because no public scene was supplied. In this example, P may become conditionally relevant because an open letter can amplify exposure, harden residue, and accelerate attractor formation. But P is still not a PMS operator. It is an overlay modulator. Its presence would not create critique or correction by itself. It would only increase the need to track Ω, Θ, and Α.

MIP remains constrained. No A-score or M-score should be assigned from the vignette. The students’ awareness, the institution’s responsibility, and the legitimacy of the process cannot be scored without concrete evidence. The IA-box could become relevant if the open letter creates asymmetry: Who can respond? Is the exposure transparent? Is the pause demand justified? Is it time-bound? Is the reputational effect reversible? But without full scene data, IA classification should remain cautious. The D-module should stay off unless the example includes humiliation, shaming, degradation, dignity-protective restraint, or public standing harm.

AHP would add hardening requirements. Terms such as “speak for,” “concerned members,” “mandate,” “legitimate critique,” and “public representation” need working definitions. AHP would also warn that an article-ready example must not let the completeness of the rendering look like a judgment on the students or institution. The example should remain internal to the article’s explanatory task.

Forbidden claims are numerous: the students legitimately represent the department; the students illegitimately usurp authority; the institution is oppressive; the letter is courageous; the letter is manipulative; the curriculum change is valid or invalid; public critique is correction; lack of mandate defeats critique; public uptake creates authority. None follows from the bounded vignette.

The non-capture note is strong. A democratic theory may prioritize mandate. A social-movement theory may prioritize voice where formal channels fail. A legal-institutional reading may ask who has standing. A discourse-ethical reading may ask whether affected parties can participate. PMS-CRITIQUE maps critique thresholds and drift risks, but it does not settle representation or legitimacy.

The article takeaway is that legitimacy without authority becomes especially delicate when critique enters public or semi-public space. A claim may be critique-readable without being representative, productive, stable, or legitimate. Conversely, lack of formal authority does not automatically invalidate the interruption. The analytic task is to preserve the distinction.

##### Case takeaway

“Legitimacy Without Authority” shows how AXIOM can map a closure-demand without satisfying it prematurely. PMS Core makes the legitimacy/authority distinction structurally readable and preserves Λ (Non-event / residue) as a frame-relative absence rather than a verdict. PMS-CRITIQUE shows that the case reaches only weak critique-threshold readability, not correction or stable follow-up. MIP refuses scoring because the case is not a concrete praxis scene. AHP hardens the analysis by identifying term-definition and transmission risks without creating new findings.

The case should be used in the chapter as a boundary case for structural restraint. It demonstrates that the absence of recognized authority may create pressure, contestability, and residue, but does not by itself decide legitimacy. It also shows why examples must remain illustrative: they can demonstrate how the structure behaves in different frames, but they do not supply evidence for the abstract case.

The remaining Λ-residue is the unresolved authority absence itself. The analysis can show why that absence matters, where it may generate critique, and why stronger claims require additional scene data. It cannot turn that absence into legitimacy, illegitimacy, maturity, immaturity, or proof. The reading would have to be weakened or revised if later material showed that recognized authority is actually present and dispositive, that legitimacy is not claimed, granted, or contested, that no operative frame expects recognized authority, that no asymmetry of exposure or recognition is involved, or that a rival legitimacy grammar discriminates the case better without over-assimilation. It would also fail if PMS readability were used to absorb all disagreement, rupture, resistance, or non-fit as drift, failed integration, or maturity failure. The methodological takeaway is therefore simple but strict: the case is clarified by refusing to let the demand for closure become a substitute authority.

#### 8.4.5 Privacy vs. Publicness

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because privacy and publicness create a strong closure demand while resisting any simple closure. The tension looks familiar: privacy seems to protect what should not be exposed, while publicness seems to open what should be accountable. Yet the completed analyses show that neither term has a stable moral or structural meaning in isolation. Privacy may protect intimacy, confidentiality, safety, discretion, and legitimate opacity. It may also conceal power, block access, or prevent affected parties from knowing what matters to them. Publicness may support accountability, answerability, public reason, and shared intelligibility. It may also become spectacle, surveillance, humiliation, public pillory, reputational lock-in, or coercive exposure.

The case therefore belongs in AXIOM because it is not primarily a request to decide whether privacy or publicness wins. It is a request to map the structures that make premature decision dangerous. The closure demand is not merely “private or public?” but “under what frame does privacy protect, under what frame does it conceal, under what frame does publicness account, and under what frame does it expose?” The case makes visible the need to keep concealment, exposure, access, limitation, accountability, frame, asymmetry, temporality, non-event, and non-capture distinct.

The completed YAML analyses mark this as a conceptual domain projection rather than a concrete scene. That is decisive for the chapter rendering. There is no determinate actor, institution, information object, platform, public, legal standard, temporal sequence, harm structure, consent structure, or outcome. Operators such as responsibility, action, asymmetry, integration, and self-binding can therefore be structurally rendered, but they must not become scene-specific findings. The case is mapped rather than solved.

The case also matters because it tests whether a layered AXIOM reading can remain disciplined when the topic itself invites moral acceleration. Privacy/publicness conflicts easily attract premature labels: privacy becomes evasion, publicness becomes accountability, silence becomes guilt, exposure becomes truth, refusal becomes immaturity, and critique becomes correction. The completed analysis blocks these moves. Its methodological value is not that it decides the tension; it shows what must remain visible before any decision could be warranted.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core reading begins with **Δ (Difference)**. The case is activated by distinctions: privacy/publicness, concealment/exposure, access/limitation, accountability/non-accountability, visibility/opacity, disclosure/non-disclosure. These distinctions are not verdicts. They open the field. If they are pushed too far, the case collapses into a false binary: privacy as secrecy, publicness as truth. The analysis therefore treats Δ as a structural entry point rather than a moral sorting device.

From there, **∇ (Impulse)** marks the directional pressure generated by the distinction. Once privacy and publicness are distinguished, the case carries pressure to conceal, expose, access, withhold, justify, limit, disclose, respond, or demand accountability. This pressure is structural, not psychological. It does not allow inference about motive, sincerity, evasiveness, courage, fear, or domination.

**□ (Frame)** is the central stabilizing operator. Privacy and publicness become meaningful only inside frames. In an interpersonal frame, privacy may protect trust or intimacy. In an institutional frame, it may name confidentiality or process protection. In a public-discursive frame, it may look like opacity or refusal. Similarly, publicness may mean accountability, public reason, procedural transparency, visibility, surveillance, spectacle, or harm depending on the frame. Because the case does not supply a concrete frame, □ is active as a requirement but under-specified as a finding.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is active but fragile. The case concerns concealment, limited access, non-disclosure, delayed explanation, missing accountability, or withheld exposure. Each may become a meaningful absence. But Λ requires an expected event. A missing disclosure is structurally meaningful only if the frame establishes that disclosure, access, explanation, refusal, review, or accountability was due. Without that calibration, absence becomes too easy to moralize. Silence is not automatically evasion; non-disclosure is not automatically guilt; withheld access is not automatically accountability failure.

**Α (Attractor)** remains weak. A privacy/publicness pattern would need recurrence or stabilization: repeated secrecy, repeated exposure, a stable non-response script, routine public shaming, institutional opacity, habitual disclosure, or recurring accountability deferral. The case material does not provide recurrence. Α therefore marks a possible future patterning condition, not a present finding.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is materially salient but dependency-sensitive. Privacy and publicness distribute exposure, access, vulnerability, authority, and obligation unevenly. One role may control access while another bears exposure. One role may demand accountability while another bears reputational cost. One party may be protected by privacy while others remain unable to contest effects. Yet because no concrete scene or stabilized pattern is supplied, Ω cannot become a blame gradient or responsibility finding. It remains a central calibration need.

**Θ (Temporality)** is weak but important. Concealment, disclosure, access, and accountability unfold over time. Delay may protect or obstruct. Publicness may create archive, residue, repetition, reputational memory, or practical irreversibility. But the case gives no sequence, no before/after, no duration, no review window, no accumulated harm, and no follow-up. Θ therefore cannot ground a trajectory claim. It marks what would be required for stronger analysis.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is strongly active. The same structure can be reframed in incompatible ways. Privacy may be protection, intimacy, lawful confidentiality, safety, refusal, privilege, secrecy, or evasion. Publicness may be accountability, public reason, truth-telling, transparency, surveillance, spectacle, coercion, humiliation, or structural harm. Concealment may be care or domination. Exposure may be repair or degradation. Access may be right or violation. Φ is therefore central to the case’s philosophical difficulty, but it must not absorb every rival reading into PMS. Some rival framings may genuinely preserve structures that PMS does not fully capture.

**Χ (Distance)** is the application discipline most visibly required by the case. It inhibits immediate verdict. It prevents forced exposure, forced secrecy, forced accountability, forced neutrality, forced integration, and person-level attribution. In this case, Χ is not an observed actor capacity; it is the analytic stop-capability that keeps the case reversible. It is also not avoidance. In a concrete scene, delay might intensify harm and public accountability might be urgent. Here, because the case is abstract, Χ protects against overclaim.

**Σ (Integration)** remains unresolved. A mature privacy/publicness handling might integrate privacy protection, accountability, access, exposure risk, consent, public reason, and reversibility. But the case does not supply the conditions for such integration. PMS coherence is therefore not proof of adequacy. A smooth structural reading does not establish moral resolution, legal sufficiency, empirical truth, or practical design.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is guardrail-only. Ψ would require an identified agent, role, institution, promise, review obligation, policy, or self-bound follow-up across time. The abstract case supplies none. Ψ therefore applies only to the analysis itself: the reading binds itself to reversibility, distance, non-ranking, and non-overclaim. It does not bind any party and does not generate a SELF_FIXPOINT.

The most compact PMS movement is therefore: privacy/publicness differences activate framed pressures around concealment, exposure, access, limitation, and accountability; possible non-events and asymmetries become visible; rival recontextualizations multiply; reflective distance is required; integration and self-binding remain conditional. The reading is strong as a structural map and weak as a scene finding.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay sharpens what PMS Core alone does not isolate: when a privacy/publicness mismatch becomes critique, and when critique mutates into reaction, judgment, narrative repair, commentary, public pillory, or silence. The overlay’s reduced signatures are shorthand only. They do not redefine PMS operators. The case reaches weak minimal critique because it contains mismatch, frame contestation, and analytic stop-capability. It does not reach productive critique, because no corrective step is consolidated. It does not reach stable critique, because no self-bound follow-up trajectory is supplied.

Publicness overlay **P** is active only as an overlay handle. It amplifies possible asymmetry, temporal residue, and attractor formation: once something becomes public, exposure may become harder to reverse, circulate beyond the initiating frame, and create reputational or symbolic residue. But P is not a PMS operator and does not prove that publicness is accountability. It marks amplification risk.

MIP adds a downstream guardrail result. The case supports maturity-in-practice questions but not maturity scoring. No A-score, M-score, D-score, person-level maturity assessment, or responsibility band is assigned. The IA-box remains conceptual IA-risk only: privacy/publicness conflicts can create asymmetric exposure and limited reversibility, but transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility are not concrete enough to decide. The D-module is active only as a qualitative protection signal because exposure and publicness can implicate Dignity-in-Practice, but no dignity harm, D-score, or person-level D reading follows.

AHP hardens the analysis artefact rather than the case claim. Its most important result is that the analysis is precise, but precision is not evidence. A complete YAML or polished article can still be built on an abstract case without scene data. AHP therefore flags the risk that formal completeness, publicness terminology, IA-box clarity, D-language, or transmission polish could be overread. It does not rescore, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or create new findings.

The PMS drift-pattern result remains deliberately weak. AD_A>>E and AD_Sigma_low are retained only as conditional risk patterns: the case could later show over-expanded awareness without enactment, or repeated recontextualization without integration, but the abstract material does not establish either pattern.

##### What the analysis shows

The case shows that privacy/publicness conflicts are structurally legible but not self-resolving. PMS Core maps the movement from distinction to pressure, frame, non-event, possible patterning, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, unresolved integration, and guardrail-only self-binding. CRITIQUE shows that interruption is possible, but correction and stable follow-up are not established. MIP shows that maturity, responsibility, IA, and D-language must remain non-scoring and protection-oriented. AHP shows that high analytical precision must not be mistaken for evidential fullness.

Where residue remains, it should be described locally as Λ (Non-event / residue), not treated as accusation by absence.

The case-specific formula is best read as a dependency-preserving movement, not as a final chain finding:

**Ψ (Self-binding) guardrail-only ∘ Σ (Integration) unresolved ∘ Χ (Distance) guard ∘ Φ (Recontextualization) contested ∘ Θ (Temporality) weak ∘ Ω (Asymmetry) dependency-sensitive ∘ Α (Attractor) weak ∘ Λ (Non-event / residue) candidate ∘ □ (Frame) ∘ ∇ (Impulse) ∘ Δ (Difference).**

This formula expresses a bounded structural projection. It does not decide whether privacy or publicness should prevail. It shows where closure is possible only conditionally: after frame, expected event, exposure gradient, temporal sequence, recurrence, reversibility, and follow-up obligations are supplied. It also shows where non-event / residue structures must remain visible. Missing disclosure, missing access, missing explanation, or missing publicness cannot be converted directly into verdict.

The analysis further shows that non-capture is not a defect. Privacy as intimacy, opacity, or protected confidentiality may resist publicness grammar. Public accountability may resist reduction to exposure. Legal, institutional, surveillance-critical, consent-based, phenomenological, and pluralist readings may discriminate some concrete cases better than PMS. The value of the case is that these rival readings remain open rather than being absorbed as drift.

##### Case-specific boundary

This case specifically invites several misuses. The first is treating privacy as evasion. The analysis blocks that move. Privacy may protect, conceal, preserve, refuse, or limit; the frame decides which meanings are available.

The second is treating publicness as correction. Public exposure may interrupt silence, but interruption is not productive critique. Publicness may amplify accountability, but it may also amplify humiliation, spectacle, surveillance, and irreversibility.

The third is treating absence as proof. A missing disclosure, access denial, silence, or delayed explanation may be structurally meaningful only if an expected event has been established. Λ (Non-event / residue) must not become accusation by absence.

The fourth is treating drift risk as drift finding. Privacy/publicness cases are drift-prone, especially around public pillory, silence attractors, judgment drift, commentary drift, and integration bypass. But the abstract case does not establish any drift pattern.

The fifth is treating full-stack analysis as stronger verdict. The presence of PMS-CRITIQUE, MIP, and AHP adds layer separation, boundary discipline, and artefact hardening; it does not intensify the case claim. The full-stack result is more explicit restraint, not more authority.

##### Example 1 — Confidential review without public exposure

A staff member reports a concern about a colleague’s conduct to an internal review role. The concern involves a pattern that may affect others, but the reporter asks that their identity and some contextual details remain private. The review role now faces a privacy/publicness tension. If the report remains entirely private, accountability may become impossible or appear impossible. If the report is made public too quickly, the reporter may be exposed, the colleague may be reputationally marked before the frame is clarified, and the situation may become harder to revise.

The bounded vignette is deliberately limited. It does not say whether the concern is true, whether the colleague acted wrongly, whether the reporter is justified, or whether the institution has fulfilled any legal duty. It only creates a structural scene in which privacy, access, exposure, and accountability become mutually entangled.

What the example demonstrates is the difference between **privacy as protection** and **privacy as accountability failure**. A PMS reading must not decide between these too early. The privacy request may protect the reporter from retaliation or unwanted exposure. It may also limit the ability of others to respond, contest, or understand what is being claimed. The same non-disclosure can therefore carry different meanings depending on the frame.

The PMS Core hooks are clear. **Δ (Difference)** appears in the distinction between private report, limited review, and public disclosure. **∇ (Impulse)** appears in the pressure to act: someone wants protection, someone may need accountability, and the reviewing role may feel pressure to decide. **□ (Frame)** is decisive: the active frame might be confidential review, workplace procedure, relational trust, risk assessment, or public accountability. Each frame changes what counts as legitimate access. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** appears where an expected event is absent: perhaps no public explanation is given, no full access is granted, or no response is yet possible. But the example shows why Λ must be calibrated. The absence of public disclosure is not automatically concealment; it may be a protected review condition.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is present as an exposure gradient. The reporter may bear a high cost if identified. The colleague may bear a high cost if publicly named. The review role may control access and timing. **Θ (Temporality)** appears in the review window: delay may protect process integrity, but prolonged delay may also create accountability residue. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is active because the same act can be reframed: confidentiality as care, secrecy as evasion, publicness as accountability, publicness as harm. **Χ (Distance)** is required to prevent immediate collapse into either “publish everything” or “keep everything hidden.” **Σ (Integration)** remains conditional: a limited-access review, staged disclosure, or accountable confidentiality process could integrate competing constraints, but only if a concrete procedure exists. **Ψ (Self-binding)** would require a self-bound follow-up commitment, such as a review deadline or documented responsibility for next steps. The vignette does not itself prove that such Ψ exists.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay is relevant but limited. The case reaches only weak minimal critique if the report interrupts a too-smooth institutional frame. It does not automatically become productive critique. Productive critique would require a consolidated corrective step, not merely a complaint, exposure, or silence. Stable critique would require follow-up binding: for example, a review role binding itself to a time-bounded response. The publicness overlay **P** remains conditional because the case is not yet public. If publicness later appears, it may amplify Ω and Θ by making exposure more durable.

MIP status remains guardrail-only. No A-score, M-score, or D-score is assigned. The IA-box would require transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility before any applied asymmetry reading could be made. Dignity-in-Practice is relevant as a protection signal: the analysis must avoid humiliating exposure and avoid using confidentiality to erase affected parties.

AHP is relevant because even this example can become too smooth. It is easy to make the internal review scenario look like a balanced design problem. But the example is not evidence that confidential review works, that it is legally sufficient, or that it protects all parties. It is only an illustration of the calibration problem.

Forbidden claims are central. This example does not show that confidentiality is good, secrecy is bad, publicness is required, or exposure is harmful. It does not diagnose the reporter, the colleague, or the review role. It does not establish legal duties or institutional adequacy. It does not validate PMS.

The non-capture note is that a legal, institutional, or trauma-informed framework may discriminate this scene better than PMS if real facts are supplied. PMS maps the structural tension, but it does not decide the procedure.

The article takeaway is that privacy/publicness analysis must begin by declaring the frame and the expected event. Without that, absence becomes too easy to moralize: non-disclosure becomes evasion, disclosure becomes accountability, and the case loses the very distinctions the analysis is meant to preserve.

##### Example 2 — Public accusation and the risk of exposure becoming “correction”

A public post claims that an unnamed organization has hidden information that affects a wider community. The post does not identify individuals, but it uses a strong moral vocabulary: people deserve to know, silence is complicity, and privacy is being used as a shield. The post receives attention. Some readers demand full disclosure. Others warn that premature publicness could expose vulnerable participants, distort the record, or make later review impossible.

This vignette is bounded. It does not say whether the public post is accurate, whether the organization has done anything wrong, whether the wider community has a right to know, or whether public disclosure is legally or morally required. It simply stages a publicness-heavy situation in which accountability pressure and exposure risk grow together.

What the example demonstrates is why PMS Core alone is not the final layer for this case. PMS Core can describe the privacy/publicness tension, but PMS-CRITIQUE makes a more specific danger visible: public interruption can be mistaken for productive critique. A public post may interrupt silence, but interruption is not yet correction. A demand for disclosure may be structurally intelligible, but it is not automatically accountable, proportionate, reversible, or dignified.

The PMS Core hooks begin with **Δ (Difference)**: hidden/public, silence/disclosure, community access/organizational privacy. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as pressure toward exposure: readers want the matter clarified, answered, or made visible. **□ (Frame)** is unstable because several frames compete. In one frame, this is public accountability. In another, it is reputational exposure. In another, it is incomplete information circulating without review. In another, it is a protective warning. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** appears in the missing response: no full explanation has yet arrived. But that absence is ambiguous. It may be evasion, process protection, legal caution, or an unavailable event.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** intensifies once attention grows. The organization may have resources and control over information, but the public post may control narrative momentum. Unnamed participants may bear exposure risk without having voice. **Θ (Temporality)** appears as public residue: once the post circulates, screenshots, commentary, and reputational effects may outlast any later correction. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is central because every side can reframe the same move: publicness as accountability, publicness as pressure, privacy as protection, privacy as concealment. **Χ (Distance)** is needed to stop the analysis from siding with whichever frame feels urgent. **Σ (Integration)** is absent unless a corrective pathway appears: for example, a time-bounded public statement, protected review, or accountable disclosure channel. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is absent unless someone binds themselves to follow-up rather than only demanding that others respond.

The CRITIQUE overlay carries the example. **CRITIQUE_MIN** is plausible because there is mismatch, frame contestation, and interruption. But **CRITIQUE_PROD** is not yet reached. The post may make a problem visible, but it does not by itself consolidate a corrective step. **CRITIQUE_STABLE** is also not reached unless the critic, organization, or process binds itself to follow-up. The overlay’s publicness modulator **P** is active: publicness amplifies Ω (Asymmetry), Θ (Temporality), and Α (Attractor) effects. Attention can create an attractor. Readers repeat simplified claims. Silence becomes interpreted as guilt. Response becomes interpreted as damage control. The case can drift before facts are clarified.

The drift risks are not findings, but this example shows why they matter. **Judgment masquerading as critique** is a risk if “privacy” becomes a label for guilt or “publicness” becomes a label for virtue. **Public pillory** is a risk if exposure becomes degradation rather than correction. **Commentary drift** is a risk if the public discussion expands while no corrective path emerges. **Integration bypass** is a risk if moral clarity substitutes for Σ (Integration).

MIP remains non-scoring. No maturity or responsibility band is assigned. The IA-box would ask whether the exposure process is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. Those conditions are not answered by the vignette. Dignity-in-Practice is active only as protection discipline: publicness must not become humiliation, and privacy must not become a tool for erasing affected parties.

AHP is relevant at the article level because this example can be easily overread. The example itself must be presented as an analysis artefact with attack surfaces: publicness is semantically loaded; critique clarity is not critique decidability; exposure risk is not proof of harm; non-response is not proof of guilt.

Forbidden claims must be explicit. The example does not claim that public accusation is wrong. It does not claim that public accountability is illegitimate. It does not claim that the organization is guilty, that the critic is reckless, or that public attention is inherently harmful. It also does not claim that privacy defeats accountability.

The non-capture note is that a public-reason, legal, media-ethics, or surveillance-critical framework may be needed in a concrete case. PMS-CRITIQUE makes drift risk visible; it does not settle public legitimacy.

The article takeaway is that publicness can interrupt, but interruption is not correction. CRITIQUE helps preserve this distinction: a public act may open a frame, but productive critique requires integration, and stable critique requires self-bound follow-up.

##### Example 3 — Access request, refusal, and the ambiguity of “nothing happened”

A researcher asks an institution for access to archived material relevant to a past decision. The institution refuses full access and offers only a short explanation: the material contains private information and cannot be released. The researcher argues that the refusal blocks accountability. The institution argues that disclosure would violate privacy and expose people who did not consent to public review. Observers split: some see a privacy boundary; others see institutional opacity.

This vignette is bounded. It does not say whether the archive should be opened, whether the refusal is lawful, whether accountability is being obstructed, whether the researcher has a right to access, or whether the private material would harm anyone if released. It stages a structurally difficult non-event: requested access does not occur.

What the example demonstrates is the calibration of **Λ (Non-event / residue)**. A request is made; access is not granted. It is tempting to treat the missing access as evidence that accountability has failed. It is equally tempting to treat the refusal as obviously justified by privacy. PMS allows neither shortcut. The non-event becomes meaningful only if the frame establishes what kind of access was expected, why it was expected, what protected good limits it, and what alternative review path exists.

The PMS Core hooks unfold in order. **Δ (Difference)** appears in the distinctions between access and refusal, privacy and accountability, archive and public record, full disclosure and partial explanation. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as pressure to resolve: the researcher seeks access; the institution seeks limitation; observers seek an interpretation. **□ (Frame)** is the decisive missing element. Is the frame scholarly inquiry, institutional accountability, legal access, archival ethics, privacy protection, public-interest review, or internal governance? The refusal changes meaning depending on which frame applies.

**Λ** is the center of the example. The expected event might be access, explanation, review, redaction, appeal, or protected third-party audit. If the expected event is full public release, the refusal is one kind of non-event. If the expected event is accountable review without public disclosure, the refusal may be different. If no access right exists in the operative frame, the absence of access may not be a failure at all. The example therefore shows why Λ must not be moralized.

**Α (Attractor)** remains conditional. A single refusal does not prove an institutional secrecy pattern. If many similar requests were refused without review, an attractor might become visible. But this vignette does not establish that. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is salient because the institution controls the archive, the researcher lacks access, and the persons represented in the archive may bear exposure costs. Yet Ω remains a structural gradient, not a blame finding. **Θ (Temporality)** is relevant because archives preserve past decisions and public access can reopen old effects; but the example gives no timeline for review, delay, or appeal. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is active: the same refusal can be recontextualized as protection, opacity, bureaucratic caution, confidentiality, or accountability failure. **Χ (Distance)** is required to prevent the analysis from converting refusal into guilt or access into justice. **Σ (Integration)** would require a concrete integration, such as redacted access, independent review, staged release, consent process, or public summary. **Ψ (Self-binding)** would require a self-bound follow-up commitment by the institution or reviewer. Neither is supplied by the vignette.

PMS-CRITIQUE is relevant if the researcher’s access request functions as critique. At most, the request may reach weak minimal critique: it identifies a mismatch, invokes a frame, and can be interruptible under Χ. It does not become productive critique unless a correction or review pathway is consolidated. It does not become stable critique unless someone binds themselves to follow-up. The refusal itself is not automatically silence attractor; a pattern would need recurrence.

MIP is relevant as a downstream guardrail. The IA-box would ask whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. Is the refusal explained? Is the privacy good specified? Is there a review window? Is there an appeal or redacted alternative? Are affected parties protected? In the vignette, those questions are not answered. Therefore IA remains a risk structure, not a finding. No M-score can be assigned to the institution, researcher, or observers because knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and serious integration attempts are not established.

AHP is relevant because this example can look cleaner than it is. The analysis can become formally precise while the evidence remains thin. AHP would flag the attack surface: public interest, privacy, access, archive, and accountability are all under-defined unless the frame is specified.

Forbidden claims include: the institution is hiding wrongdoing; the researcher is entitled to the archive; privacy is merely an excuse; accountability requires full public access; refusal proves guilt; access would necessarily harm private persons. None follows from the vignette.

The non-capture note is that archival ethics, privacy law, institutional procedure, research ethics, or public-records doctrine may be necessary in a real case. PMS clarifies structural dependencies; it does not supply the access standard.

The article takeaway is that “nothing happened” is often the most dangerous part of a privacy/publicness case. A missing disclosure, missing access, or missing reply can be structurally significant, but only after the expected event and frame are declared. Without that discipline, Λ becomes accusation by absence.

##### Case takeaway

This case shows that privacy/publicness tensions should be used in the chapter as a test of disciplined non-closure. The structural movement is clear enough to map: distinctions generate pressures; frames determine meaning; absences become dangerous if uncalibrated; asymmetry and temporality matter; recontextualization multiplies rival readings; distance is required; integration and self-binding remain conditional.

The add-on result is equally specific. PMS-CRITIQUE shows weak formal critique-interruptibility, not productive critique or stable critique. Publicness overlay P makes amplification visible without turning publicness into correction. MIP preserves the downstream result that no score, person finding, IA verdict, or D-reading follows. AHP preserves the artefact-level result that precision is not evidence.

The examples show how the analysis travels into bounded scenes without becoming evidentiary. Confidential review demonstrates why privacy may protect without automatically defeating accountability. Public accusation demonstrates why exposure may interrupt without becoming correction. Archive access demonstrates why non-event analysis requires a declared expected event and frame.

The remaining Λ (Non-event / residue) structure is central: missing disclosure, missing access, missing explanation, delayed accountability, or withheld publicness may matter, but they do not decide the case by themselves. The methodological takeaway is that AXIOM’s role is not to solve privacy versus publicness. It is to keep visible the structural conditions under which any responsible solution would have to be argued.

## 8.5 Summary

Part V establishes CRITIQUE as the PMS add-on layer for reach, scale transition, publicness, and the boundary between interruption, correction, exposure, and authority. The cases in this part do not treat critique as truth, moral superiority, or a right to compel. They treat critique as a structural operation: a mismatch becomes visible, seeks interruption, may travel across frames, and may or may not consolidate into correction or stable follow-up.

The common result is not that PMS-CRITIQUE decides which critique is true, justified, legitimate, or publicly warranted. The result is that PMS-CRITIQUE makes visible where critique remains structurally bounded and where it drifts: local mismatch becomes macro accusation; explanation becomes substitute repair; recognition language becomes verdict; legitimacy becomes covert authority; publicness becomes exposure; absence becomes accusation by non-event.

CRITIQUE therefore does not replace PMS core. PMS remains the operator grammar. CRITIQUE adds overlay handles for critique-specific thresholds and risks: reach level, scale transition, publicness, judgment drift, narrative repair, commentary drift, silence attractor, public pillory, integration bypass, reach laundering, and legitimacy laundering. These handles are not verdict categories. They do not decide truth, assign blame, rank persons, validate critique, suppress critique, or authorize sanction.

#### 8.5.1 Baseline result of Part V

The baseline established by Part V is that critique must remain interruptible before it can become corrective.

Across the cases, PMS core makes the mismatch structure visible, while CRITIQUE sharpens the reach discipline. Critique Reach shows that micro, institutional, public, and systemic critique are not the same act at larger volume. Interpretability vs. Reality shows that explanation may clarify without resolving the practical remainder. Epistemic Injustice shows that non-uptake may become structurally inspectable without becoming an immediate verdict. Legitimacy Without Authority shows that absence of recognized authority does not itself decide legitimacy. Privacy vs. Publicness shows that visibility is a regime shift, not a corrective operator.

The strongest shared discipline is:

**Δ + □ + Χ before Σ or Ψ**

A mismatch must be specified. A frame must be declared. Distance must remain available. Only then can integration or follow-up binding be tested. Without that discipline, critique becomes too easy: a difference becomes accusation, publicness becomes proof, explanation becomes closure, legitimacy becomes authority, and non-response becomes guilt.

#### 8.5.2 Cluster-level result

The Part V cases form three CRITIQUE-facing clusters.

**Reach and scale transition** show that critique changes when it travels. A local objection can become an institutional complaint, a public critique, or a systemic frame, but each transition changes the audience, exposure field, temporal residue, evidentiary burden, and correction path. Macro reach may reveal what a local frame hides, but it may also erase local constraints. The task is not to privilege local or systemic framing in advance. The task is to keep the Φ-move visible and test whether Σ and Ψ actually follow.

**Interpretability, epistemic standing, and legitimacy** show that critique often begins where something becomes readable but not yet corrected. An explanation may make a burden intelligible while leaving the burden in place. A credibility frame may make non-uptake visible without proving injustice. A legitimacy claim may be contestable without recognized authority, but the absence of authority does not decide the case. These cases preserve the gap between structural readability and completed critique.

**Privacy and publicness** show the most exposed edge of the chapter. Publicness may interrupt silence, reveal blocked uptake, or make accountability possible. But publicness may also amplify Ω, harden Θ, stabilize Α scripts, reduce Χ, and turn critique into exposure. The case therefore blocks both shortcuts: privacy is not automatically evasion, and publicness is not automatically correction.

#### 8.5.3 Cross-case dependency discipline

The recurring failure mode in Part V is:

**critique → authority**

This occurs when evaluative clarity, interpretability, legitimacy language, recognition language, or public exposure is treated as warrant to bind others. Once that shortcut appears, the operator discipline usually collapses: Λ is converted into accusation by absence, Χ is bypassed, Φ becomes narrative control, Σ is assumed rather than tested, and Ψ is externalized onto others as compliance, deference, or forced uptake.

In Part V this shortcut appears in several forms:

- macro reach becomes higher truth;
- public exposure becomes correction;
- interpretability becomes practical resolution;
- epistemic-injustice language becomes proof of wronging;
- legitimacy without authority becomes substitute authority;
- missing disclosure becomes guilt;
- privacy becomes evasion;
- publicness becomes accountability by default;
- critique-readability becomes completed critique.

Part V blocks these shortcuts without neutralizing critique. It does not say critique must stay local, private, polite, procedural, or correction-oriented. Some critique may function as refusal, testimony, rupture, warning, protest, or public exposure. PMS-CRITIQUE does not erase those possibilities. It only prevents them from being silently redescribed as correction, legitimacy, authority, or stable follow-up when the dependencies are not present.

#### 8.5.4 What Part V carries forward

Part V carries forward the publicness lesson of AXIOM: critique language is powerful because it can clarify and coerce at the same time. It can name a mismatch, open a blocked frame, and make non-uptake visible. But it can also harden into judgment, reputational residue, institutional pressure, or covert authority.

CRITIQUE is justified because PMS core can map mismatch and interruption, but the overlay makes critique-specific thresholds sharper: when reach changes scale, when publicness amplifies exposure, when interpretability bypasses action, when legitimacy tokens bind without mandate, when silence becomes attractor, and when critique claims travel farther than their correction path.

MIP and AHP remain downstream artefact layers. They do not evaluate critics, accused persons, institutions, publics, or maturity. MIP reviews whether critique artefacts could acquire coercive, reputational, institutional, or governance force. AHP hardens the analysis against excerpting, publicness drift, score-like overread, dignity-language confusion, and false transmission readiness.

Part V closes with the CRITIQUE rule for AXIOM: where critique interrupts a mismatch, render the frame; where critique travels, track the scale shift; where critique becomes public, audit exposure; where explanation clarifies, do not call it repair; where legitimacy is claimed, do not treat AXIOM as the missing authority; and where critique lacks integration or follow-up binding, keep Λ visible rather than laundering interruption as correction.
