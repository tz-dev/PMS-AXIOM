# PMS-AXIOM: PART VI - EDEN

## 9. EDEN-Overlay: Relational Regimes, Pseudo-Symmetry, and Reciprocity Under Load

Part VI introduces the EDEN overlay as the PMS add-on layer for relational regimes. The cases in this part remain grounded in PMS core, but they require an additional lens because their central closure-demands concern reciprocity, recognition, trust, boundary handling, repair, and the ways relational coordination can be displaced by parity surfaces, comparison frames, chronic residue, protocol loops, or externalized binding.

The purpose of this part is not to diagnose persons, assign motives, distribute blame, prescribe healthy relationships, or decide who is right in a relational scene. It is to show how relational coordination becomes structurally viable, fragile, simulated, or displaced once differences (Δ) are framed (□), asymmetries (Ω) are acknowledged or tabooed, temporal patterns (Θ) and scripts (Α) harden, and integration (Σ) or self-binding (Ψ) either carry reciprocity or fail to do so.

The EDEN overlay is therefore a non-mixing disciplinary lens. It does not redefine PMS operators. It does not add a new base grammar. It supplies regime handles, drift corridors, reduced signatures, scene-mapping discipline, and relational pattern labels that make EDEN-specific risks visible: pseudo-symmetry, comparison pivots, residue leverage, Σ-simulation, Ψ-externalization, parity-to-exit, boundary erosion, repair-as-narrative substitution, and chronic Λ under relational load.

PMS remains the canonical operator grammar. EDEN only sharpens the reading where the case-specific issue is not merely relation, disagreement, care, or conflict, but relational regime formation: the way coordination succeeds, fails, or is simulated when real asymmetry must be carried without collapsing into domination, denial, exposure, or pseudo-reciprocity.

### 9.1 Case Set and Role in the Paper

The cases addressed in Part VI are:

41. Pseudo-Symmetry
42. Recognition
43. Trust (Relational Regimes)
44. Boundary Erosion
45. Repair Relationships

Together, these cases show how relational terms can function as structural closure devices. Equality-language may close asymmetry too early. Recognition may become demand, withholding, or protocol. Trust may become stance, exposure, leverage, or self-binding. Boundary handling may shift between privacy, publicness, repair, and tribunal frames. Repair may reopen coordination, but it may also become narrative substitution, pressure to resume, or exposure management.

The cases cluster into several EDEN-relevant families:

- **Pseudo-symmetry and recognition** show how parity, acknowledgment, visibility, and reciprocity can either coordinate real asymmetry or conceal it.
- **Trust and relational regimes** show how relational continuity depends on Λ handling, Ω exposure, Θ patterning, Χ interruptibility, and Σ/Ψ carriage.
- **Boundary erosion** shows how relational material can be moved between private repair, public accountability, institutional record, and tribunal-like frames.
- **Repair relationships** shows why repair cannot be reduced to apology, explanation, narrative closure, or return-to-normal; repair requires interface-level coordination, distance, and non-coercive re-binding.

Part VI therefore demonstrates a central AXIOM discipline: relational analysis must remain structural and non-personal. EDEN can classify regime patterns and drift corridors, but it must not become a diagnostic vocabulary, maturity ranking, moral tribunal, or relationship advice system.

### 9.2 Output Contract for Part VI

The admissible outputs in Part VI are PMS + EDEN artefacts. The primary output type is:

- **Regime taxonomy:** a structured, non-prescriptive artefact that classifies relational configurations into operator-defined regimes and drift corridors without converting the taxonomy into diagnosis, blame, virtue language, person-typing, or enforcement.

A case may also include:

- **Viability boundary:** a statement of where reciprocity remains structurally possible, where it is simulated, where it erodes, and where additional scene data would be required.
- **Relational drift map:** a bounded account of possible EDEN drift corridors, such as pseudo-symmetry, comparison pivot, residue leverage, Σ-simulation, Ψ-externalization, parity-to-exit, protocolized avoidance, or repair substitution.
- **Structural reading:** a dependency-sensitive PMS rendering, with EDEN regime labels and reduced signatures used only as overlay handles, not as PMS operators.
- **Transmission guardrail:** a boundary statement for cases where relational analysis could be reused for accusation, shaming, ranking, therapeutic authority, institutional complaint, public exposure, or coercive repair demands.

The EDEN overlay may identify drift risks, but drift risk is not drift finding. A case may show that pseudo-symmetry, boundary erosion, reciprocity loss, repair substitution, or Ψ-externalization is structurally plausible without asserting that any concrete person, partner, family, team, institution, or relationship has committed that drift.

MIP is not part of the default Part VI stack. It becomes relevant only downstream, where a relational artefact is institutionalized, made public, used in HR, education, therapy-like contexts, formal complaints, governance, reputational exposure, binding recommendations, or other dignity-risk zones. Where invoked, MIP evaluates the analysis artefact and its use conditions, not persons, relationships, maturity, worth, or dignity. AHP may harden the analysis artefact where precision, excerptability, publicness, attack surfaces, or claim-boundary risks require it. AHP does not rescore, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or create new findings.

### 9.4 Cases

#### 9.4.1 Pseudo-Symmetry

##### Why this case is here

Pseudo-symmetry is included here because it isolates a closure-demand that cannot be handled by simply affirming or denying equality. The case concerns situations in which parity is verbally asserted while outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations remain unevenly distributed. The pressure point is not merely that words and practice may diverge. The deeper issue is that the language of parity can itself become the frame through which uneven distribution is either made visible, softened, justified, obscured, or declared already resolved.

This makes the case structurally important for AXIOM. Many closure problems arise when a frame openly imposes hierarchy, authority, exclusion, or sanction. Pseudo-symmetry is subtler. It can appear where a structure claims that the relevant asymmetry has already been overcome: “everyone has equal voice,” “we all share responsibility,” “the same procedure applies to all,” or “anyone may request accountability.” Such claims may be true in a formal frame, aspirational in a reform frame, functional in a role-differentiated frame, or misleading in a practical cost frame. The case therefore tests whether PMS can keep parity-language and uneven distribution visible at the same time without becoming a verdict machine.

The case is a conceptual projection rather than a concrete scene. No particular institution, relationship, actor, documented statement, temporal sequence, or evidentiary record is supplied. This matters because responsibility, action, asymmetry, and self-binding can be structurally rendered but cannot become scene-specific findings without additional evidence. The analysis can say that a parity/asymmetry mismatch is structurally legible; it cannot say that a real person or institution is guilty, immature, evasive, manipulative, legally liable, or diagnostically classifiable.

The case belongs in the EDEN layer because EDEN makes a specific feature visible beyond PMS Core: parity surface under uneven cost, exposure, obligation, or responsibility. PMS Core can map the basic operator structure. EDEN sharpens the question of whether parity-language functions as coordinated asymmetry, as a temporary buffer that reopens integration, or as pseudo-symmetry that substitutes for reciprocal cost-bearing. MIP is relevant downstream only because the case raises maturity and asymmetry questions; it remains non-scoring here because there is no concrete scene. AHP hardens the analysis by marking where evidence-depth, transmission, and language-hygiene risks would arise; it does not rescore or add findings.

##### Structural reading

PMS Core begins with **Δ (Difference)**: the difference between asserted parity and uneven distribution. This difference is the entry point, not the conclusion. It does not prove hypocrisy, guilt, injustice, or drift. It simply makes the case intelligible: parity language and distributional structure may not coincide.

From that difference, **∇ (Impulse)** is only weakly present. The mismatch may generate pressure to justify, correct, deny, contest, stabilize, or reinterpret the unevenness. But this remains structural pressure, not psychological motive. The case does not show resentment, intent, denial, manipulation, or desire. It only shows that the mismatch could become practically or interpretively charged.

The decisive early operator is **□ (Frame)**. Pseudo-symmetry can only be evaluated inside a frame that defines what parity means. A parity claim may refer to formal standing, equal access, equal procedural rights, equal burden, equal voice, equal responsibility, or reciprocal recognition. Uneven distribution may be irrelevant in one frame and decisive in another. Therefore the first calibration question is always: which frame makes the parity claim and the uneven distribution mutually relevant?

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** remains weak and conditional. Pseudo-symmetry may involve a missing acknowledgment of asymmetry, missing reciprocity, missing redistribution, missing protection, or missing interface work. But the expected event must be named before Λ can carry weight. The mere fact that equality is asserted and distribution remains uneven does not by itself establish a meaningful absence.

**Α (Attractor)** is also weak. The case title names a pattern-type, but no recurrence is supplied. Pseudo-symmetry becomes structurally stronger only if parity-language and uneven distribution repeatedly coexist, stabilize into a role-script, or become institutional routine. A single conceptual configuration cannot establish that.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is central because uneven distribution is the case material itself. Outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations remain uneven. But Ω must not be moralized. Asymmetry can be unjustified, but it can also be functional, transparent, role-bound, temporary, accepted, necessary, or under review. The analysis must specify the direction and kind of asymmetry: who bears cost, who has access, who controls review, who is exposed, who can exit, and who can contest.

**Θ (Temporality)** is suggested but not established. The wording “remain unevenly distributed” raises a temporal question, but it does not yet show duration, accumulation, delay, recurrence, or permanentization. A strong Θ reading would require evidence across time: repeated parity claims, persistent uneven cost, delayed correction, review failure, or stabilized expectations.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is the most important calibration operator in the case. Parity-language may recontextualize asymmetry as symmetry: unequal exposure may be described as equal access, unequal obligation as shared responsibility, unequal cost as equal procedure. But Φ is ambiguous. Recontextualization may clarify, conceal, soften, justify, normalize, or aspire beyond the current structure. Not every appeal to parity is masking; not every rival frame is drift.

**Χ (Distance)** governs the analysis itself. The case invites premature closure: either parity is accepted as proof of equality, or unevenness is treated as proof of false parity. Χ prevents both. It keeps the mismatch visible without turning it into accusation. It also prevents PMS terminology from becoming authority-language.

**Σ (Integration)** is weak and contested. A parity claim may sound coherent, but PMS integration is not rhetorical smoothness. Σ would require a structure that actually integrates the uneven distribution into coherent praxis: named asymmetry, differentiated responsibility, review, repair, protection, or reciprocal cost-handling. The case supplies the question of integration, not evidence that integration has occurred or failed.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is weak and conditional. A parity claim may declare a commitment, but it is not yet self-binding. Ψ would require durable commitment across time: a role or institution binding itself to handle the named asymmetry responsibly. A slogan, policy phrase, or shared statement is not Ψ unless it is carried into practice through time.

The derived structures follow from this restrained operator reading. Awareness_A is weak: the analysis can distinguish parity claim from uneven distribution, but no role’s actual awareness is evidenced. Coherence_C is weak and contested: parity-language may create apparent coherence while the distributional mismatch remains unresolved. Responsibility_R is conditional: uneven obligation is not the same as blame or mature responsibility. Action_E is under-specified because no concrete enactment or integrated realization is supplied. Dignity-in-Practice_D remains an application constraint: asymmetry must be handled without humiliation, person-ranking, diagnosis, or ontological claims. SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only; the case does not establish durable self-relation or institutional self-binding.

The PMS drift-patterns remain risks, not findings. AD_A>>E, the risk of excessive distance between awareness and enactment, is only possible: the case suggests that parity may be verbally available while enactment remains absent, but no stable knowing-without-doing trajectory is evidenced. AD_Sigma_low, fragmented integration, is also possible but not established: parity-language may simulate coherence, but there is no evidence of repeated recontextualization outpacing integration. In both cases, non-assignment is not a low score. It is the correct result when recurrence, temporality, action, and self-binding are under-specified.

EDEN sharpens the reading by naming the central overlay signature: **PS (Pseudo-Symmetry)** as a parity surface under real or alleged uneven distribution. PS is active at the conceptual signature level because the case directly concerns asserted parity under uneven outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations. But it is not active as a full regime finding. The case does not show punished Ω (Asymmetry)-speech, chronic Λ (Non-event / residue), repeatable Α (Attractor), blocked Σ (Integration), displaced Ψ (Self-binding), comparison governance, or concrete switch activation.

The EDEN asymmetry-switch remains a risk. A structure that asserts parity while retaining uneven distribution may allow asymmetry to be invoked selectively: denied under the parity surface, activated under pressure, or used as an exception. But no actual switch is shown. Parity-to-exit is likewise a risk: equality-language might be used to avoid re-internalizing unequal cost, but the case does not show such an exit. Responsibility-to-authority is not applicable because no conversion of responsibility into control or frame ownership is supplied. **PFO (Postfeminist Override / Over-steering)** is too strong as an active label. Devaluation, humiliation, **NRK (Negative Responsibility Kernel)**, breach, dissatisfaction, and full reciprocity loss are not established. This is an important part of the result: EDEN identifies the pseudo-symmetry signature without importing the full EDEN drift pipeline.

MIP’s case-specific result is non-scoring. The case opens questions about awareness, coherence, responsibility, power-to-act, and asymmetry legitimacy, but no A-score, M-score, IA result, or D-profile can be assigned. The relevant downstream MIP question would be whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. AHP’s case-specific result is hardening, not evaluation: the analysis must keep evidence-depth limits, excerpting risks, D-language risks, and transmission boundaries visible.

##### What the analysis shows

The central movement is: **Δ (Difference)** between parity claim and uneven distribution inside **□ (Frame)**, with **Ω (Asymmetry)** as the core structural pressure and **Φ (Recontextualization)** as the main risk of parity-language converting asymmetry into apparent symmetry. **Χ (Distance)** is required to prevent closure; **Σ (Integration)** and **Ψ (Self-binding)** remain conditional because no concrete integration or durable commitment is supplied.

The EDEN layer adds that this is a bounded PS signature: parity surface under uneven cost, exposure, responsibility, or obligation. It makes visible the difference between formal parity, pseudo-symmetry, and coordinated asymmetry. It also marks possible switch-risk, parity-to-exit risk, and reciprocity-loss risk. But these remain risks. The case does not warrant a full drift pipeline, regime reading, person finding, or institutional verdict.

The strongest positive result is therefore discriminative rather than conclusory. The case teaches how to keep several possibilities apart: a valid formal equality claim, justified functional asymmetry, aspirational parity, transitional reform, administrative shorthand, pseudo-symmetrical masking, and coordinated reciprocity. PMS Core maps the operator field. EDEN sharpens the parity-surface problem. MIP refuses scoring without evidence. AHP hardens the analysis so that the clarity of the structure is not mistaken for evidence depth.

Non-capture remains active. A formal-equality reading may preserve procedural or legal precision. A functional-asymmetry reading may preserve role differentiation. An aspirational-parity reading may preserve reform language. A recognition-theory reading may preserve questions of acknowledgment and misrecognition. A distributive-justice reading may better specify fairness, burden, need, desert, or equality of opportunity. A systems or institutional reading may explain the same pattern through role architecture, compliance, scarcity, or organizational design. These rival readings are not defects in the PMS or EDEN reading; they mark distinctions the case must not flatten.

The PMS reading would weaken if the parity claim and distributional asymmetry cannot be clearly distinguished, if no relevant □ (Frame) makes them mutually salient, if no meaningful Λ (Non-event / residue) can be specified, if Ω (Asymmetry) turns out to be transparent and functionally justified, or if Θ (Temporality) and Α (Attractor) remain absent in any concrete scene. The EDEN reading would weaken if the parity claim is explicitly formal, aspirational, transitional, or review-bound rather than a parity surface that blocks cost recognition. It would also weaken if asymmetry is marked, bounded, re-bound into Σ (Integration) / Ψ (Self-binding), and open to revision.

Closure is possible only at the level of structural mapping. It is not possible at the level of empirical finding, legal classification, diagnostic attribution, maturity scoring, or metaphysical truth. The remaining Λ (Non-event / residue) is not simply “what equality failed to do.” It is the unresolved question of what, if anything, the frame required but did not supply: acknowledgment, reciprocity, redistribution, protection, review, repair, or re-binding.

##### Case-specific boundary

This case invites one distinctive misuse: the temptation to turn “pseudo-symmetry” into an accusation. Once the term is available, it may be used to claim that any parity-language is concealment, that any uneven distribution is illegitimate, or that any appeal to equality is an exit from responsibility. That would violate the analysis.

Here, asymmetry does not automatically mean injustice. Parity-language does not automatically mean deception. A missing equalization does not automatically become Λ (Non-event / residue). A smooth PMS reading does not prove capture. EDEN terms such as PS, asymmetry switch, parity-to-exit, PFO, and reciprocity loss remain overlay handles, not PMS operators and not verdicts.

The most important boundary is the distinction between coordinated asymmetry and pseudo-symmetry. Mature reciprocity may require unequal burdens if those burdens are transparent, justified, time-bound where appropriate, reversible where possible, and integrated through role-specific self-binding. The case becomes pseudo-symmetrical only where parity surface substitutes for that work or prevents the uneven distribution from being named and coordinated.

##### Example 1 — Equal Voice, Unequal Exposure

A small committee adopts a rule that “all members have equal voice.” In meetings, each person is formally permitted to speak, propose revisions, object to drafts, and request clarification. The rule is written neutrally and applies to everyone. Yet one member occupies a precarious role: they are junior, their contract renewal depends partly on the committee chair, and their objections are more likely to be remembered as “difficult” or “not collegial.” Senior members can disagree without comparable exposure. The committee therefore has a parity surface — equal voice — while the cost of using that voice is unevenly distributed.

This example demonstrates the core difficulty of pseudo-symmetry. The parity claim may be formally true. Nobody is explicitly forbidden from speaking. The rule may even be well-intentioned. The problem is that formal permission and practical exposure do not necessarily coincide. If the committee treats the equal-voice rule as sufficient proof that participation is symmetrical, the unequal exposure attached to speaking may become difficult to name. If, however, the analyst immediately declares the committee hypocritical or abusive, the reading overreaches. The structure requires calibration, not a verdict.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ (Difference)** appears in the distinction between formal speaking rights and unequal practical exposure. **∇ (Impulse)** is weakly visible as pressure around whether the junior member should speak, remain silent, or accept the parity frame. **□ (Frame)** is the committee setting: its rules, hierarchy, norms of collegiality, and evaluation channels. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may be present if an expected protection is absent — for example, if the committee says all may speak but provides no way to protect low-status speakers from retaliation or reputational cost. This Λ remains conditional unless the expectation of protection is actually part of the frame. **Α (Attractor)** would require recurrence: repeated meetings where equal-voice language coexists with uneven exposure. A single meeting is not enough.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is central because exposure is uneven. But it must not be moralized too quickly. Unequal exposure may be built into the role hierarchy; it may be recognized, mitigated, ignored, or denied. **Θ (Temporality)** becomes stronger if the pattern persists across meetings, review cycles, or contract decisions. **Φ (Recontextualization)** appears if the equal-voice rule recontextualizes unequal exposure as practical symmetry: “everyone had the same opportunity to speak.” **Χ (Distance)** is required of the analysis: the reading must not collapse into blame or innocence. **Σ (Integration)** is weak unless the committee integrates the exposure problem into actual practice, such as protected channels, chair restraint, or explicit review. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is weak unless the committee binds itself across time to maintain not only formal voice but usable voice.

The EDEN overlay is relevant but must remain bounded. The example fits the **PS (Pseudo-Symmetry)** signature: parity surface under uneven exposure. It also raises a possible **parity-to-exit** risk: if senior members say, “You had the same chance to speak, so the outcome is equally owned,” parity language may become an exit from responsibility for unequal risk. But this is a risk, not a finding. There is no full **PFO (Postfeminist Override / Over-steering)** unless the committee develops a stable regime in which asymmetry is unspeakable, exception-switches are permanentized, and **Σ (Integration)** / **Ψ (Self-binding)** coordination is blocked. There is no **humiliation** finding merely because the junior member is exposed. There is no **NRK (Negative Responsibility Kernel)** unless a concrete realization sequence with Σ/Ψ failure is supplied.

The MIP status would be non-scoring at this stage. The example opens questions about awareness, coherence, responsibility, and real power-to-act, but it does not by itself assign an A-score, M-score, IA result, or D-profile. MIP would ask whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. Does the committee recognize that equal formal voice does not mean equal practical cost? Is the exposure justified by role? Is there a review point? Can the junior member object without disproportionate penalty? AHP or a later quality-hardening pass would harden the analysis by warning against mistaking a clean structural fit for evidence depth.

Forbidden claims are crucial. The example does not prove that the chair is manipulative, that senior members are guilty, that the junior member is harmed, that the institution is legally discriminatory, or that the committee is morally defective. It does not diagnose anyone’s motives. It does not show empirical prevalence. It does not validate PMS or EDEN.

The non-capture note is equally important. A rival reading may show that the equal-voice rule is only one part of a broader structure that already provides private feedback, ombudsperson review, or anonymous objections. Another rival reading may argue that role hierarchy necessarily creates different exposure and that the mature task is not identical risk but coordinated asymmetry. A legal reading may focus on formal procedure. A recognition reading may focus on whether the junior member’s concerns are acknowledged.

The article takeaway is that pseudo-symmetry often appears where equal access is declared but the cost of using access is uneven. PMS Core makes the parity/exposure mismatch visible; EDEN names the PS risk; MIP withholds scoring until the asymmetry’s transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility are known.

##### Example 2 — Shared Responsibility, Unequal Obligation

A small project team misses an internal deadline. In the follow-up meeting, the team lead says, “We all share responsibility for this.” The sentence lowers immediate blame pressure and prevents the discussion from becoming a personal accusation. At that level, it may be constructive. Yet the actual distribution of obligation was not equal. One person had authority to allocate tasks, another had access to the missing information, a third repeatedly asked for clarification but received no answer, and a fourth completed their part on time. The language of shared responsibility creates a parity surface, but the structure of agency, access, and obligation remains uneven.

This example demonstrates why pseudo-symmetry is not simply “false equality.” Shared-responsibility language can be useful. It may reduce shame, preserve group orientation, and keep a team from scapegoating one person. PMS and EDEN must therefore not treat every shared-responsibility statement as drift. The structural question is whether the parity claim reopens coordinated responsibility or closes it. If “we all share responsibility” is followed by a differentiated review of access, authority, timing, and repair, it may support integration. If it prevents that differentiation, it may become pseudo-symmetrical.

The PMS Core hooks begin with **Δ (Difference)**: shared responsibility is verbally asserted, but actual obligations and capacities differ. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as the pressure to resolve the missed deadline without producing a humiliating blame scene. **□ (Frame)** is the project frame: task allocation, authority, access to information, deadlines, and review expectations. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may be the missing clarification, the unanswered request, the absent escalation path, or the missing review of who could act. But each possible Λ must be named. The fact that the deadline failed does not automatically identify the relevant non-event. **Α (Attractor)** would be active only if the same shared-responsibility language repeatedly prevents differentiation across project cycles.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is central because power-to-act, information access, and obligation are unevenly distributed. The team lead may have more authority. The person with missing information may have a bottleneck role. The person who asked for clarification may have had low power to solve the problem. **Θ (Temporality)** matters if the pattern accumulates: repeated missed deadlines, repeated flattening of responsibility, or repeated failure to correct access asymmetry. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is significant because the team lead’s statement may recontextualize differentiated responsibility as equal responsibility. But Φ is ambiguous. It may be a temporary de-escalation frame, not concealment. **Χ (Distance)** is required to preserve this ambiguity. **Σ (Integration)** is active only if the team integrates the differentiated structure into repair: who had what authority, what failed, what changes, who follows up. **Ψ (Self-binding)** appears only if the team binds itself across time to a revised responsibility structure.

EDEN adds a sharper reading of the risk. The phrase “we all share responsibility” may become **PS (Pseudo-Symmetry)** if it preserves parity surface while unequal authority and obligation remain operative. It may become **parity-to-exit** if the person or role with greater authority uses shared responsibility to avoid re-internalizing the cost of their specific leverage. But the same phrase may also be a temporary dignity-preserving buffer if it prevents shaming and then reopens **Σ (Integration)** / **Ψ (Self-binding)** work. EDEN therefore asks: does parity language reopen coordination under named **Ω (Asymmetry)**, or does it replace coordination?

The example also clarifies reciprocity. Reciprocity is not everyone carrying identical blame. Mature reciprocity may require that each role carry the responsibility appropriate to its actual access, authority, and consequence profile. If the lead had allocation authority, their responsibility differs from the person who lacked information. If the information holder had a bottleneck, their responsibility differs from the person who completed their work. Coordinated asymmetry is not pseudo-symmetry. Pseudo-symmetry begins where differentiated responsibility is blocked by a parity surface.

The MIP layer would remain careful. No score is warranted from this vignette alone. MIP would ask what each role knew, what each could control, what consequences were predictable, what alternatives were available, and whether serious integration attempts occurred. The M-score cannot be inferred from the fact that a deadline was missed. IA analysis would ask whether the asymmetry of responsibility was transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. AHP would flag an analysis risk: the phrase “shared responsibility” is rhetorically loaded and can be overread as either mature solidarity or manipulative flattening.

Forbidden claims include: the team lead is not diagnosed as evasive; the team is not declared immature; the missed deadline is not treated as legal wrongdoing; the person with more authority is not automatically guilty; the person with less authority is not automatically absolved. The example does not prove that shared-responsibility language is bad. It shows that the language requires structural calibration.

The non-capture note is strong here. A conflict-resolution reading may say the shared-responsibility statement is a necessary first move to prevent scapegoating. A management reading may focus on process design. A legal reading may distinguish formal accountability from internal responsibility. A virtue-ethical reading may ask whether humility and ownership are being cultivated. PMS and EDEN preserve one structural layer but do not exhaust these readings.

The article takeaway is that pseudo-symmetry can appear not only in equality claims but also in responsibility claims. A parity statement may be constructive if it leads into differentiated repair; it becomes structurally risky if it replaces the work of naming who could know, who could act, who bore cost, and who must bind themselves to change.

##### Example 3 — Equal Access, Unequal Cost of Accountability

An organization introduces an accountability channel. The policy says that anyone may report concerns, request review, or ask for clarification about responsibility allocation. On paper, access is equal. The channel is publicized as evidence that the institution is transparent and accountable. In practice, however, using the channel carries different costs for different roles. Senior staff can file concerns without serious reputational danger. Contractors worry that reporting will affect renewal. People in dependent roles fear being marked as difficult. Those already exposed by the issue may have to reveal private information in order to make the asymmetry legible. The institution can truthfully claim equal access while the practical cost of accountability remains uneven.

This example demonstrates the difference between formal access and usable accountability. It is concrete enough to show the structure but bounded enough to avoid a verdict. The organization may be sincerely attempting to create review. The channel may be better than having no channel. But if equal access is treated as proof that accountability is equally available, pseudo-symmetry may arise. The parity claim is “anyone may report.” The uneven distribution is the cost of reporting, exposure, dependence, and possible retaliation or reputational trace.

The PMS Core hooks are layered. **Δ (Difference)** marks the difference between equal procedural access and unequal practical cost. **∇ (Impulse)** is the pressure around whether to use the channel, remain silent, seek informal support, or accept the institutional parity claim. **□ (Frame)** is the accountability frame: policy language, reporting procedures, confidentiality rules, hierarchy, dependency, and review norms. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may appear if the policy promises protection but does not provide it, or if review exists formally but no safe uptake occurs. The exact non-event must be specified: missing confidentiality, missing anti-retaliation protection, missing follow-up, missing acknowledgment, or missing repair. **Α (Attractor)** would require recurrence: repeated cases where equal-access language coexists with uneven reporting cost.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is central. Exposure, dependency, role security, access to protection, and cost of accountability are uneven. **Θ (Temporality)** matters because reporting consequences may unfold across time: renewal cycles, reputational memory, delayed retaliation, archived complaints, or repeated non-response. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is active if the institution recontextualizes unequal accountability cost as equal accountability access. **Χ (Distance)** must be maintained by the analyst: the existence of the channel should not be dismissed as mere cover, but neither should it be accepted as proof of practical equality. **Σ (Integration)** would require integrated accountability design: confidential options, role-sensitive protections, follow-up, review, and burden-aware procedures. **Ψ (Self-binding)** would require the institution to bind itself across time to those protections, not merely publish the policy.

The EDEN hooks are significant. This is a strong **PS (Pseudo-Symmetry)** example: parity surface under uneven exposure and accountability cost. It may also show **asymmetry-switch risk**. The institution may invoke equality when asked about access — “everyone can report” — but asymmetry becomes operative when vulnerable roles calculate the cost of doing so. **Parity-to-exit** is a risk if the organization uses the existence of the channel to exit from further responsibility: “We provided equal access; if people did not use it, the responsibility is theirs.” That would be a stronger finding only if the organization actually uses the policy in that way. **PFO (Postfeminist Override / Over-steering)** remains too strong unless there is evidence of a regime that suppresses **Ω (Asymmetry)** legibility, permanentizes exception-switch availability, and substitutes procedure for **Σ (Integration)** / **Ψ (Self-binding)**. **Humiliation** and **devaluation** are not to be inferred unless the process actually lowers standing, exposes persons, or stabilizes status regulation.

This example is especially useful for the MIP layer. MIP would not score the organization or any role-holder from the vignette alone. It would ask whether the asymmetry is transparent: does the institution recognize that reporting cost differs by role? Is it justified: are there protected goods that require the design, and are alternatives considered? Is it time-bound: are the risks reviewed, monitored, or reduced over time? Is it reversible: can a reporter withdraw, correct, appeal, or avoid disproportionate exposure? MIP would also ask who knows about the cost asymmetry, who has real power to alter the channel, what consequences are predictable, what alternatives exist, and whether serious integration attempts were made.

AHP or a later quality-hardening pass would add hardening discipline to the analysis. It would warn that this example can easily be misused. A reader may want to conclude that the organization is hypocritical. Another reader may want to conclude that the policy proves accountability. Both are too fast. AHP would require evidence-linking, scope boundaries, reversibility clauses, and language hygiene. It would also flag publicness: if reporting exposes private information, D-P risk may become relevant, but no D-profile should be activated without a safe, role-bound, time-windowed basis.

Forbidden claims are therefore explicit. The example does not prove that the organization is acting in bad faith. It does not prove legal retaliation, discrimination, or institutional wrongdoing. It does not diagnose managers or reporters. It does not claim empirical frequency. It does not validate PMS, EDEN, MIP, or AHP. It does not authorize intervention. It does not treat non-use of the channel as immaturity or cowardice, because the cost of use may be real.

The non-capture note matters. A governance reading may argue that no accountability channel can eliminate all cost and that the proper comparison is not perfect equality but available safeguards. A legal reading may distinguish formal access from protected-class impact or retaliation standards. A design reading may focus on confidentiality architecture. A recognition reading may ask whether vulnerable roles are believed before they expose themselves. A systems reading may focus on organizational trust. PMS and EDEN clarify the parity/cost structure but do not replace these frameworks.

The article takeaway is that pseudo-symmetry often appears at the boundary between access and usability. Equal access is not meaningless, but it is not automatically equal accountability. The structural question is whether the institution integrates unequal cost into its accountability design or uses the formal parity of access to close the issue before exposure, dependency, and reversibility have been examined.

##### Case takeaway

Pseudo-symmetry shows how parity can function both as a genuine structural achievement and as a premature closure device. The case does not teach that equality-language is false, nor that asymmetry is inherently illegitimate. It teaches that parity claims must be read with their cost topology: who bears exposure, who carries obligation, who has access, who can contest, who can exit, and who is bound to repair.

Methodologically, PMS Core maps the parity/asymmetry mismatch without deciding it. EDEN sharpens the pseudo-symmetry signature and distinguishes it from coordinated asymmetry. MIP refuses scoring because the case is not a concrete scene. AHP hardens the analysis by keeping evidence-depth and transmission risks visible. The result is a disciplined map of a closure temptation: parity-language can close too early, but asymmetry-language can also accuse too quickly.

The remaining Λ (Non-event / residue) is the unresolved question of what the relevant frame required but did not yet show: acknowledgment, reciprocity, redistribution, protection, review, or re-binding. That residue must remain visible. This case should be used in the chapter as a calibration case for equality-language under uneven distribution: it demonstrates how AXIOM keeps symmetry, asymmetry, reciprocity, risk, and non-capture distinct without converting structural readability into verdict.

#### 9.4.2 Recognition (Acknowledgment)

##### Why this case is here

Recognition is a deceptively compact closure-demand. To recognize something may mean to notice it, acknowledge it, accept it, validate it, admit it, register it institutionally, respect it, or take responsibility for it. The difficulty is that these meanings often slide into one another. A person may be noticed but not accepted. A claim may be heard but not validated. A situation may be visible but not made explicit. A responsibility may be named but not taken. A legal admission may be confused with interpersonal acknowledgment. A delay may be read as denial, while in another frame it may be procedural caution, respectful restraint, or non-assimilative refusal.

This case is here because AXIOM needs a structurally disciplined way to map that closure-pressure without satisfying it too quickly. Recognition demands status: is the person, claim, situation, or responsibility now acknowledged or not? Has the matter crossed from implicit availability into explicit standing? Has silence become denial? Has delay become refusal? Has noticing become acceptance? Has responsibility been assumed, or merely named? These questions are not merely semantic. They shape relations, obligations, repair expectations, procedural pathways, public standing, and possible dignity exposure.

The case is a conceptual projection rather than a concrete scene. No actor, institution, timeline, publicness level, legal rule, empirical record, responsibility bearer, or role-bound asymmetry is supplied. This means that the analysis may render recognition structurally, but it may not convert that rendering into scene-specific findings. Responsibility, action, asymmetry, integration, and self-binding can be mapped as possibilities or calibration points; they cannot be assigned as facts without further evidence.

The case is therefore mapped rather than solved. It does not answer what recognition “really is” metaphysically, nor whether recognition is owed in any concrete case. It shows how recognition becomes structurally legible, where closure is possible, where residue remains, and where rival readings must stay open.

##### Structural reading

At PMS Core level, the case begins with **Δ (Difference)**. Recognition requires differentiating acknowledged from not-yet-acknowledged, explicit from implicit, accepted from denied, delayed from enacted, responsibility-named from responsibility-unnamed. Without such distinctions, recognition cannot become structurally legible. Yet Δ (Difference) must not be overused. The mere fact that a distinction is visible does not decide whether acknowledgment is due, whether implicitness is failure, or whether responsibility exists.

**∇ (Impulse)** is weak but relevant. Once a person, claim, situation, or responsibility is differentiated, there may be pressure toward response, explicitness, acceptance, refusal, or responsibility uptake. This pressure is structural; it is not a psychological claim about desire, avoidance, guilt, sincerity, or bad faith. The analysis must not infer inner motive from the existence of a recognition-demand.

**□ (Frame)** is central. Recognition only becomes readable within a frame. The same gesture may be conversational acknowledgment, legal notice, institutional receipt, moral response, political recognition, epistemic uptake, or mere awareness depending on the frame. The most important calibration rule is therefore frame discipline. A claim about interpersonal acknowledgment must not silently become a claim about legal admission. A claim about being heard must not silently become a claim about being validated.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is one of the case’s most important operators, but it is conditional. Recognition often becomes visible through what does not happen: an expected acknowledgment does not arrive; responsibility remains unnamed; a situation remains implicit; a claim is noticed but not accepted. Yet absence is not automatically Λ (Non-event / residue). A concrete frame must define what acknowledgment-event was expected, by whom, when, and under which conditions. Otherwise silence, delay, or refusal may remain non-capture, rival framing, legitimate caution, or simply insufficiently specified absence.

**Α (Attractor)** is not active as a finding. Repeated delays, ritualized omissions, default non-response scripts, institutional deferrals, or habitual pseudo-acknowledgments could become attractors in a concrete scene. The present case supplies no recurrence, script, or stabilized pattern. Α (Attractor) therefore remains a possible later calibration point, not an established structure.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is weak and under-specified. Recognition can involve asymmetry because one role may grant, withhold, define, or institutionalize acknowledgment while another role bears the cost of non-recognition. A frame-holder may control what counts. A responsibility-bearer may have more capacity to acknowledge than the affected role has to compel acknowledgment. Publicness may amplify exposure. But the case material supplies no concrete capacity, vulnerability, authority, leverage, or obligation gradient. Ω (Asymmetry) is therefore possible, not found.

**Θ (Temporality)** is conceptually active. Recognition can be delayed, made explicit later, deferred, carried, or left unresolved. A non-response may matter differently after one hour, one week, or one year. Yet no timeline is supplied. Θ (Temporality) marks that acknowledgment-status may unfold across time; it does not establish chronic delay, accumulated harm, or irreversible trajectory.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is relevant because recognition often changes meaning when the frame changes: from noticing to accepting, from acceptance to validation, from validation to legal admission, from responsibility naming to responsibility-taking, from private exchange to public standing. Φ (Recontextualization) can clarify these shifts, but it can also over-assimilate them. A refusal, for example, may not be incomplete recognition; it may be a valid refusal under another frame.

**Χ (Distance)** is active as application discipline. It keeps the analysis from fusing recognition with verdict, denial with guilt, delay with immaturity, or acknowledgment-demand with coercion. In the case itself, object-level distance cannot be assessed because no actor is supplied. But analysis-level Χ (Distance) is mandatory: the reading must remain reversible, non-tribunal, and non-person-ranking.

**Σ (Integration)** is weak and conditional. Recognition may become integrated when a claim, situation, or responsibility is incorporated into a coherent praxis interface: a review is opened, a repair path is created, a role changes, a responsibility is taken up, or a refusal is explicitly justified within the relevant frame. The abstract case supplies none of this. Explicit wording alone is not Σ (Integration).

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is also weak and not established. Acknowledgment becomes Ψ (Self-binding) only when a role durably binds itself to the acknowledged status, responsibility, or relation across time. A statement such as “we recognize this” or “we take responsibility” may point toward such binding, but it does not establish it by itself.

The resulting PMS movement is therefore conditional:

Χ[(Θ ∘ □ ∘ Δ) ∩ (Θ ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇) + Ω? + Φ?] → Σ? → Ψ?

This formula states only that recognition-as-acknowledgment can be mapped as framed differentiation across time, with possible non-event structure, possible asymmetry, possible recontextualization, and conditional integration or self-binding. The question marks matter. They prevent the case from turning conceptual fit into a verdict.

PMS-EDEN sharpens one specific feature beyond PMS Core: **threshold explicitness**. Recognition becomes EDEN-relevant when acknowledgment-status becomes selectable within a frame across time. One may acknowledge, refuse, delay, leave implicit, name responsibility, or avoid naming it. The threshold marker is active because this case centrally concerns what becomes noticed, accepted, denied, delayed, or made explicit. But EDEN’s threshold is not breach. It does not prove that acknowledgment is owed. It does not prove that denial has occurred. It only marks that the recognition-field has crossed from undifferentiated availability into selectable acknowledgment-status.

Other EDEN handles remain weak, risk-level, or not applicable. Breach-like chain interruption would require a specified expected acknowledgment-event, a real option-set, consequence-bearing Ω (Asymmetry), and failure of Σ (Integration) or Ψ (Self-binding). NRK remains weak because visible responsibility is not yet responsibility carried or failed. False comparison is only a risk where acknowledgment shifts into rank, parity, or value-competition. Pseudo-symmetry is not active without comparison-dominant framing and real asymmetry. PFO is not applicable. Reciprocity loss, devaluation, and humiliation remain possible later drift risks only if a concrete scene supplies the missing markers.

MIP adds a downstream result rather than a stronger case finding. Because the case is not concrete, MIP assigns no A-score, no M-score, no IA-box result, and no D-profile. This is not a low score; it is non-assignment. There is no role-context-time evidence from which to assess knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, asymmetry transparency, time-boundedness, reversibility, or dignity-in-practice enactments. The D-module remains off: D is a guardrail here, not an object-level D-profile.

AHP then hardens the analysis artefact. It does not rescore the case, activate D, or upgrade transmission status. Its case-specific contribution is to warn that a complete YAML or polished article can be mistaken for evidentiary completeness. The correct hardening sentence is: template completeness does not increase evidence completeness.

##### What the analysis shows

The analysis shows that recognition is structurally legible as a layered closure problem. PMS Core maps the movement from difference and frame to possible non-event, temporality, asymmetry, recontextualization, integration, and self-binding. EDEN sharpens the threshold at which acknowledgment-status becomes selectable. MIP refuses downstream scoring because the scene is not concrete. AHP hardens the artefact against over-reading.

The case-specific result is not that recognition succeeds or fails. The result is that recognition must be disaggregated. Noticing is not acceptance. Acceptance is not validation. Validation is not legal admission. Responsibility naming is not responsibility-taking. Explicit acknowledgment is not yet integration. Integration is not yet self-binding.

The PMS drift-pattern status remains risk-only. `AD_A>>E` is relevant only as a warning against inflating structural awareness into enacted acknowledgment: a concern may be visible without being carried as Action_E. `AD_Sigma_low` is relevant only as a warning against treating explicit acknowledgment-language as Σ (Integration): a statement may sound coherent without changing the praxis interface. Neither pattern is active as a finding. Under-specification is not failure, and non-assignment is not a low score.

The central closure possible here is structural: the case can show where acknowledgment-status becomes readable. The closure not possible here is evaluative: the case cannot decide whether a concrete acknowledgment was owed, withheld, delayed irresponsibly, legally required, ethically sufficient, or psychologically resisted.

The most important residue remains Λ (Non-event / residue), but only conditionally. A missing acknowledgment may be a meaningful non-event, but only if the relevant frame defines an expected acknowledgment-event. Without that, absence remains ambiguous. This is the case’s main methodological contribution: it makes the demand for recognition visible without converting the absence of recognition into a finding.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk in this case is recognition-enforcement. Because recognition language is normatively charged, the analysis could be misused to pressure acknowledgment, shame refusal, diagnose denial, or treat delay as evasion. This case therefore needs a local boundary even if the Part opening already states the general AXIOM discipline.

Here, Χ (Distance) prevents the analysis from becoming a tribunal. Λ (Non-event / residue) must not be assigned unless an expected acknowledgment-event is frame-defined. Ω (Asymmetry) must not be inferred from the mere presence of a claim. Σ (Integration) must not be inferred from explicit acknowledgment. Ψ (Self-binding) must not be inferred from responsibility-language. EDEN threshold visibility must not be treated as breach, and EDEN drift handles must not become verdicts.

The MIP refusal to score is also case-specific. Non-assignment is not a low score. Working-note status is not a verdict. The D-module being off is not indifference to dignity; it is the correct result when no concrete, safe, role-bound, time-windowed scene is supplied.

##### Example 1 — A concern is heard, but not yet acknowledged

A colleague says during a project meeting, “The rollout plan leaves the support team exposed after launch.” The project lead replies, “I hear that,” and moves on to the next agenda item. After the meeting, no follow-up occurs. Two days later, the support team asks whether the risk has been accepted, rejected, deferred, or assigned to anyone. The project lead says, “We noted the concern, but we have not made a decision.”

This vignette is intentionally bounded. It does not establish that the project lead acted wrongly. It does not establish that the support team’s concern was correct. It does not establish negligence, denial, evasion, or bad faith. It only gives a small scene in which recognition-related distinctions become visible: hearing, noting, acknowledging, accepting, deciding, and assuming responsibility are not the same act.

The example demonstrates why the case cannot treat awareness as completed recognition. The concern has entered the frame: it has been spoken, heard, and minimally noticed. That activates a **Δ (Difference)** distinction between an unraised and a raised concern. The meeting provides **□ (Frame)**, the frame in which the concern becomes relevant. There is also weak **∇ (Impulse)**, because the concern carries directional pressure toward response, clarification, or responsibility assignment. But the phrase “I hear that” does not by itself establish **Σ (Integration)** or **Ψ (Self-binding)**. It may be a minimal receipt, a polite conversational marker, a deferral, an implicit acknowledgment, or a procedural holding phrase.

The PMS Core hook is strongest around **Λ (Non-event / residue)** and **Θ (Temporality)**. If the meeting frame includes an expectation that raised rollout risks must be assigned, recorded, or reviewed, then the absence of follow-up may become a structured non-event. If no such expectation exists, the absence may not be Λ at all. Temporality matters because the difference between immediate non-response, brief deferral, and chronic non-acknowledgment cannot be collapsed. PMS therefore asks: what acknowledgment-event was expected, by whom, within which time window, and under which role responsibility?

**Ω (Asymmetry)** remains conditional. The project lead may have decision authority, but the example does not specify formal mandate, capacity, consequence-bearing, or available alternatives. If the support team bears post-launch costs while the project lead controls acknowledgment of risk, Ω may become relevant. But that requires additional scene data. **Φ (Recontextualization)** may appear if the concern is later reframed: “This is not a support exposure; it is a communication issue,” or “This is now a launch-readiness blocker.” Recontextualization may clarify the case, but it may also redirect or dilute the original concern. Σ would require the concern to be integrated into the project interface, for instance through an assigned review, decision log, mitigation plan, or explicit refusal with reasons. Ψ would require durable commitment, such as the lead binding the project to a risk decision across time.

The EDEN overlay hook is threshold explicitness. The concern crosses from background availability into selectable acknowledgment-status. The project lead can acknowledge, deny, delay, assign, reframe, or leave it implicit. EDEN helps name that moment without turning it into breach. Breach-like interruption would require more: a defined expected response, a real option-set, consequence-bearing Ω, and failure of Σ/Ψ carriage. NRK is only a risk if responsibility becomes visible yet is externalized, displaced, or simulated. Pseudo-symmetry is not active unless the lead later says something like “all teams carry equal risk” while support actually bears a disproportionate burden.

MIP would not assign A-score, M-score, IA-box result, or D-profile from this vignette alone. More information would be needed: the lead’s mandate, the meeting rules, the expected response window, available alternatives, predictability of harm, and whether the support team had realistic channels for review. AHP would flag excerpt risk: if this example is quoted without its boundaries, readers may wrongly treat the lead’s “I hear that” as evidence of evasion or the support team’s concern as proven.

The forbidden claims are therefore clear. The example does not show immaturity. It does not show denial. It does not show responsibility failure. It does not show legal or managerial negligence. It does not prove that acknowledgment was owed. It only shows how recognition can remain suspended between awareness and integration.

The non-capture note is important. In a real organization, this scene might be better analyzed through project governance, workplace hierarchy, labor distribution, risk management, or speech-act norms. PMS maps the recognition structure, but it does not exhaust the practical or institutional meaning of the exchange.

**Article takeaway:** The example shows why “being heard” is structurally weaker than acknowledgment, why delay is not automatically denial, and why Σ or Ψ cannot be inferred from polite receipt language.

##### Example 2 — Institutional receipt is not legal or practical recognition

A resident submits a written complaint to a housing office about persistent heating outages. The office sends an automated message: “Your request has been received.” A week later, the resident receives another message: “Your request is under review.” No repair occurs during that week. The resident says, “They recognized the problem and still did nothing.” The office says, “We acknowledged receipt, not responsibility.”

This example is useful because it places several recognition meanings close together without letting them collapse. The office has recognized receipt of a message. It has not necessarily recognized the truth of the complaint. It has not necessarily accepted legal responsibility. It has not necessarily integrated the problem into a repair plan. It has not necessarily refused responsibility either. The phrase “acknowledged receipt” belongs to one frame; “recognized the problem” belongs to another; “accepted responsibility” belongs to a third.

PMS Core begins with **Δ (Difference)**: receipt, review, acceptance, responsibility, repair, and refusal must be distinguished. The institutional communication supplies a **□ (Frame)**, but it is ambiguous. Is the relevant frame administrative tracking, tenant protection, legal notice, maintenance procedure, interpersonal acknowledgment, or emergency repair? The answer determines whether a **Λ (Non-event / residue)** exists. If the office’s rules require immediate repair after heating outage reports, the absence of repair may become a meaningful non-event. If the rules require triage, inspection, and verification first, “under review” may be a legitimate procedural interval rather than denial.

**Θ (Temporality)** is central. One week is not structurally meaningful in the abstract. It becomes meaningful only in relation to weather, habitability rules, risk severity, office procedures, available repair capacity, and expected response windows. PMS does not decide those facts. It asks what temporal structure the frame supplies. Without that, the analysis cannot responsibly classify delay as neglect, caution, bureaucracy, or denial.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** may become significant, but only with scene data. A housing office usually has more procedural power than a resident; the resident may bear material exposure while the office controls repair scheduling. But the example as given does not establish legal duty, actual capacity, knowledge of severity, or available alternatives. Ω is plausible, not proven. **Χ (Distance)** is required because the scene invites immediate moral closure: “they knew and did nothing.” PMS must pause that closure until the frame, evidence, and capacities are specified.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** appears in the office’s distinction between “receipt” and “responsibility.” This may be a valid frame clarification or an evasive recontextualization. PMS cannot decide without more information. The key is not to treat all recontextualization as drift. A procedural office may need to distinguish receipt from liability. But if that distinction is used to keep Λ open indefinitely while real exposure accumulates, a later analysis may have grounds for stronger critique.

**Σ (Integration)** would require the complaint to enter a coherent response interface: inspection scheduled, emergency criteria checked, temporary heat offered, repair ordered, or reasons for non-action given. **Ψ (Self-binding)** would require durable institutional binding: the office accepts an obligation and carries it across time. Neither is shown by receipt alone.

The EDEN overlay makes visible the threshold structure. The complaint crosses into institutional visibility: it is no longer merely private. The office can receive, review, deny, repair, defer, escalate, or accept responsibility. This is threshold explicitness. But EDEN must not convert receipt into breach. NRK becomes relevant only if responsibility is visible, options are real, Ω is effective, and Σ/Ψ fail or are simulated. False comparison would appear if the office reframed the situation into generic parity—“all requests are treated equally”—while the resident’s material exposure is unequal and urgent. Pseudo-symmetry would require more: a parity surface masking real asymmetry. None of that is established by the vignette alone.

MIP would keep scores suspended unless further evidence is supplied. To assign M-score, one would need knowledge, control, predictability, alternatives, and whether a serious integration attempt occurred. The IA-box would require concrete asymmetry, transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. D remains a guardrail, not a D-profile: the example may involve dignity-relevant exposure if heat failure affects living conditions, but the vignette alone does not license a dignity judgment about the office or resident.

AHP would warn that this example is easy to overread. The sentence “Your request has been received” may look like recognition, and the lack of repair may look like failure. But the analysis must preserve the distinction between administrative receipt, factual validation, legal responsibility, practical repair, and self-binding institutional obligation.

Forbidden claims include legal negligence, moral bad faith, institutional cruelty, empirical harm severity, or procedural violation. The example does not establish any of these. It also does not prove the resident’s interpretation wrong. It keeps both frames open until the evidence, rules, and time window are specified.

The non-capture note is substantial. A legal analysis may be primary if housing law defines notice and repair duties. A public-health analysis may be primary if the outage creates safety risk. A bureaucratic-process analysis may explain delay better than PMS. A phenomenological account may better describe the resident’s felt abandonment. PMS maps recognition-status and dependency risks, but it does not replace these frameworks.

**Article takeaway:** The example shows why acknowledgment of receipt, recognition of a problem, acceptance of responsibility, and integrated repair must remain distinct.

##### Example 3 — “We take responsibility” without integration or self-binding

A small organization publishes a statement after a failed event: “We recognize that the event did not meet expectations, and we take responsibility.” The statement thanks participants for feedback and promises internal reflection. It does not specify what failed, who will review it, what will change, whether refunds or repairs are available, or when follow-up will occur. Some participants feel acknowledged. Others say the statement is only reputational language.

This example demonstrates why explicit acknowledgment is not automatically integration, and why responsibility-language is not automatically self-binding. The statement is not empty by definition. It may be an important threshold crossing: the organization does not ignore the event, does not deny that something went wrong, and publicly names responsibility in a broad sense. But the statement does not yet show how the acknowledged issue is integrated into future action.

PMS Core begins with **Δ (Difference)** distinctions: recognizing disappointment is not the same as specifying failure; taking responsibility is not the same as assigning repair; thanking participants is not the same as creating review; promising reflection is not the same as binding future conduct. The public statement supplies **□ (Frame)**, a communication frame. Yet the frame remains ambiguous. Is this a public-relations statement, a moral acknowledgment, an operational review notice, a contractual response, or a repair commitment?

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** may appear if the statement creates expectations it does not satisfy. If “we take responsibility” conventionally requires concrete next steps in this organizational context, then the absence of such steps may become a structured non-event. If the statement is only an initial notice, the absence may be a temporary under-specification rather than failure. **Θ (Temporality)** matters because follow-up could still occur. A statement without immediate details is different from a statement that permanently substitutes for action.

**Α (Attractor)** is not active unless this becomes a repeated pattern: broad acknowledgment, no specification, no follow-up, renewed public language after each failure. One statement is not an attractor. **Ω (Asymmetry)** may be relevant because the organization has frame-setting power and participants may have limited ability to compel repair. But again, this depends on the event’s terms, expectations, publicness, material consequences, and available channels.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is prominent. The organization recontextualizes the failed event as a learning moment or responsibility moment. This may be constructive. It may also be reputation management. PMS cannot decide from the wording alone. **Χ (Distance)** requires the analysis to hold both possibilities without jumping to cynicism or endorsement.

**Σ (Integration)** is the central question. Has the organization integrated the acknowledged failure into a coherent interface? Integration would require something like a review process, named responsibility, altered procedure, repair channel, timeline, or explicit explanation of why no further action is possible. The statement alone gestures toward Σ but does not establish it. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is even more demanding. “We take responsibility” becomes self-binding only if the organization durably binds itself to consequences across time. A generic sentence does not yet do that.

The EDEN overlay is useful here because the statement crosses the threshold of explicitness. Responsibility is no longer entirely implicit. The organization makes acknowledgment selectable and publicly visible. This activates the threshold marker. But it also opens several EDEN risks without proving them. NRK is a risk if responsibility becomes visible but is not carried by Σ or Ψ. Σ-simulation is a risk if the statement performs coherence without interface change. Ψ-externalization is a risk if the organization shifts responsibility back to participants through vague “feedback” language while appearing accountable. False comparison is possible if the organization later equalizes all perspectives to avoid addressing specific failure. Pseudo-symmetry is possible if a parity surface replaces real asymmetry: “we all learned together,” while only participants carried the cost.

None of these EDEN risks is a finding. The statement may be a responsible first step. It may be early, cautious, legally constrained, or awaiting internal review. EDEN sharpens the questions; it does not decide the case. PFO, humiliation, devaluation, and permanentized exception-switch are not applicable unless repeated regime-level patterns appear.

MIP would again suspend scoring. A-score cannot be assigned to the organization as a whole from one statement. M-score would require knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and evidence of whether serious integration was attempted. The IA-box would require specifying asymmetry between organization and participants, including transparency, justification, time limits, and reversibility. D remains a guardrail: public criticism of the statement must not become humiliation or person ranking, and the organization’s public language must not be treated as proof of dignity failure.

AHP would flag the main artefact risk: the example is rhetorically compelling and therefore easy to over-close. Readers may treat “we take responsibility” as either obviously sufficient or obviously empty. The analysis must resist both. Its task is to show what would have to follow for Σ or Ψ to become warranted.

Forbidden claims include fraud, insincerity, legal admission, organizational immaturity, moral failure, or successful repair. The example does not establish any of these. It also does not deny participant frustration. It leaves open that the statement may be meaningful, insufficient, strategic, constrained, or merely preliminary.

The non-capture note is important. Public apology theory, crisis communication, contract law, event governance, and organizational ethics may each offer sharper criteria. PMS and EDEN map the structural gap between explicit acknowledgment and integrated self-binding, but they do not replace those domain frameworks.

**Article takeaway:** The example shows why explicit responsibility-language is not yet Responsibility_R, Action_E, Σ, or Ψ, and why EDEN threshold visibility must not be mistaken for breach or repair.

##### Case takeaway

Recognition (Acknowledgment) shows that closure can be structurally demanded before it is analytically warranted. PMS Core maps the demand: recognition begins with differentiated acknowledgment-status inside a frame, may involve possible non-event residue across time, may involve asymmetry, and may later require integration or self-binding. PMS-EDEN sharpens the threshold: acknowledgment, denial, delay, refusal, and responsibility naming become selectable options. MIP refuses downstream scoring because the case lacks role-context-time evidence. AHP hardens the analysis against the mistake of treating formal completeness as evidentiary completeness.

The examples show the case’s practical use. Hearing is not acknowledgment. Receipt is not responsibility. Public responsibility-language is not integration or self-binding. Each example illustrates the same methodological result: the analysis can make recognition structures visible without turning them into verdicts.

The remaining Λ (Non-event / residue) is not a problem to be eliminated. It is the case’s central boundary. A missing acknowledgment may be meaningful, but only under a specified frame. A delay may be failure, caution, procedure, refusal, or non-capture. A responsibility statement may be real, simulated, preliminary, or legally constrained. AXIOM keeps those possibilities visible rather than forcing premature closure.

#### 9.4.3 Trust (Relational Regimes)

##### Why this case is here

Trust belongs in this chapter because it is a high-closure relational concept. It appears to ask for immediate settlement: either trust remains, or it is broken; either a silence is ordinary, or it is betrayal; either repair restores the relation, or the relation has failed. Yet the case shows why AXIOM cannot allow that closure-demand to become a verdict machine. Trust is structurally dense because it combines reliance, vulnerability, expectation, confidence, doubt, withdrawal, repair, and betrayal inside frames that differ across interpersonal, institutional, testimonial, procedural, and role-based contexts.

The case is a conceptual projection rather than a concrete scene. That matters. No actor, institution, timeline, promise, repair attempt, evidentiary record, legal duty, or clinical context is supplied. The analysis can therefore map the structural conditions under which trust becomes legible, but it cannot determine whether any actual trust relation succeeded, failed, matured, broke down, or required repair. Responsibility, action, asymmetry, and self-binding can be structurally rendered, but they must not become scene-specific findings without additional evidence.

Trust matters for AXIOM because it sits exactly where closure, vulnerability, and interpretation meet. A reply that does not come may be ordinary delay, incapacity, refusal, boundary, neglect, or betrayal. A withdrawal may be avoidance, but it may also preserve dignity. A repair attempt may clarify a rupture, but it may also pressure one side to restore trust prematurely. A formally reciprocal relation may hide unequal vulnerability. Trust therefore makes visible a core AXIOM problem: where closure is desired, where closure is possible, and where Λ (Non-event / residue) must remain visible.

The case is mapped rather than solved. PMS Core can show how trust depends on framed expectation, meaningful absence, asymmetry, temporality, distance, and possible integration. PMS-EDEN can sharpen threshold, breach, reciprocity, and reciprocity-loss risks. MIP can preserve non-scoring and working-note discipline where downstream governance language might otherwise overreach. AHP can harden the analysis artifact against misreading. None of these layers turns trust into an empirical finding, diagnosis, legal conclusion, metaphysical account, or person-ranking device.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement begins with Δ (Difference): trust only becomes legible where distinctions are made between trust and non-trust, reliance and non-reliance, confidence and doubt, repair and non-repair, expectation and absence, betrayal and ordinary failure. These distinctions do not yet prove that a trust relation exists; they only make a trust-reading possible. A key calibration problem is therefore under-discrimination. If trust cannot be distinguished from prediction, dependency, credibility, loyalty, institutional compliance, fear, faith, or procedural reliability, the reading becomes too broad.

∇ (Impulse) marks the directional movement within the relation. Trust can move toward reliance, renewed confidence, clarification, or repair; it can also move away through doubt, suspension, withdrawal, or refusal. This directedness is structural. It is not a claim about inner motive or psychology. The analysis must not infer desire, pathology, avoidance, manipulation, or emotional maturity from directional movement alone.

□ (Frame) is decisive. Trust does not mean the same thing in friendship, institutional procedure, public testimony, professional role, fiduciary relation, or intimate care. The same delay, silence, omission, or repair offer may carry different meaning depending on the active frame. The trust case therefore cannot be read without frame specification. The frame determines what counts as expectation, what counts as response, what counts as repair, and what counts as residue.

Λ (Non-event / residue) is one of the strongest operators in this case. Much of trust is shaped by what does not happen: the reply that never comes, the expected protection not provided, the explanation withheld, the promised repair not attempted, the institution not responding, the fidelity not enacted. But absence alone is not betrayal. A non-event becomes trust-relevant only where a framed expectation exists. This is the central calibration point: the expected occurrence must be named before absence can carry structural weight.

Α (Attractor) is relevant but not fully established in the abstract case. Trust often stabilizes through repeated reliability, repeated non-response, repeated repair, or repeated disappointment. Yet one event is not already a pattern. The case names trust as a relational regime, but it does not supply recurrence evidence. Α (Attractor) therefore remains structurally relevant but scene-dependent.

Ω (Asymmetry) is central. Trust involves exposure, dependence, capacity differences, informational imbalance, authority, role obligation, or institutional power. A relying role may bear more risk than the role relied upon. A trusted institution may control the channel through which its own response is judged. Yet Ω (Asymmetry) is not blame. It does not establish guilt, moral superiority, legal liability, victim ranking, or diagnosis. The necessary question is: who is exposed, who can act, who can define the frame, who bears repair cost, who can withdraw, and who can contest the interpretation?

Θ (Temporality) is also central. Trust unfolds across time. Confidence accumulates, doubt grows, betrayal is remembered, repair takes time, and institutions acquire histories. A one-hour silence, a one-day delay, and a six-month absence do not carry the same structure. Θ (Temporality) prevents the analysis from treating trust as a punctual state. It asks how expectation persists, when absence becomes meaningful, whether repair remains open, and when withdrawal becomes structurally appropriate.

Φ (Recontextualization) becomes relevant where trust is repaired, reframed, renegotiated, or reinterpreted. A missed response may be reframed as misunderstanding, overload, refusal, procedural failure, or betrayal. But Φ (Recontextualization) must not be used to smooth away rupture. Reframing can clarify; it can also erase vulnerability or pressure a relation back into coherence. The source frame and target frame must be named. Otherwise, recontextualization becomes narrative laundering.

Χ (Distance) governs both the trust relation and the analysis. In the relation, distance may appear as hesitation, doubt, suspension, withdrawal, or refusal to renew reliance too quickly. In the analysis, Χ (Distance) prevents premature verdict. It blocks the slide from silence to betrayal, from vulnerability to blame, from repair to required reconciliation, and from refusal to immaturity. This is especially important because trust produces strong closure pressure.

Σ (Integration) remains conditional. A concrete trust relation may integrate confidence and doubt, reliance and vulnerability, memory and repair, role and person, institution and lived exposure. But the abstract case does not show such integration. PMS coherence is not Σ (Integration). A formally elegant reading does not prove that the relation itself is integrated.

Ψ (Self-binding) is also conditional. It becomes relevant where trust involves promise, durable role ownership, accountability, institutional commitment, or self-binding to repair, refusal, or responsibility across time. The abstract case does not establish these. Ψ (Self-binding) therefore remains available but not activated as a finding. It applies most clearly to the analysis itself: the reading must remain bound to its own limits.

The compact PMS Core movement can be rendered as framed, expectation-sensitive trust becoming temporally extended and asymmetrically exposed through recurring or potentially recurring patterns. In formula shorthand, after the operators have been specified, the primary movement is: Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ. This formula deliberately does not include Σ (Integration) or Ψ (Self-binding) as primary results. They remain conditional extensions.

PMS-EDEN adds a focused overlay. It is not used because PMS Core is insufficient, but because trust contains EDEN-relevant questions that PMS Core makes visible without naming in EDEN’s drift vocabulary. EDEN also keeps a weak, analogical ground-state question in view: whether trust can appear as a non-deficit relation before suspicion, comparison pressure, coercive verification, or anticipated betrayal becomes dominant. This is not an active finding, but it helps frame why threshold and breach questions matter. EDEN sharpens four case-specific issues.

First, trust may have a weak **threshold** structure. A relation changes when betrayal, refusal, withdrawal, non-repair, or public accusation becomes an explicit option. That does not mean breach has occurred. It means option-visibility has changed.

Second, EDEN treats possible breach as **chain interruption**, not as knowledge gain or moral revelation. Betrayal, if later shown, would need a framed expectation, action or omission, consequence-field legibility, and failed or absent integration or self-binding. The abstract case does not supply this.

Third, EDEN distinguishes trust as simple symmetry from trust as **coordinated asymmetry**. Trust is often called reciprocal, but reciprocity is not mere equality. It may require unequal vulnerability, unequal repair burden, or unequal role responsibility to be made legible and coordinated. This is the most constructive EDEN contribution to the case.

Fourth, EDEN marks **reciprocity loss** as a risk only. Reciprocity loss would require chronic Λ (Non-event / residue), stabilized Α (Attractor), real Ω (Asymmetry), Θ (Temporality) erosion, and failed or displaced Σ (Integration) / Ψ (Self-binding). The abstract case does not show that. PFO, permanentized exception switch, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, devaluation, and humiliation are not active readings here.

The MIP layer remains downstream. Its case-specific result is not a score but a refusal of premature scoring. The case has conceptual awareness and internal coherence, but no concrete scene, actor knowledge, power-to-act, predictability, alternatives, or integration attempts. No A-score, no M-score, and no IA finding are assigned. Non-assignment is not a low score; it is correct score hygiene. Dignity language remains protection-oriented only.

AHP hardens the analysis artifact rather than the case. Its case-specific result is that the artifact is a high-precision working note, but vulnerable to communicative misreading: cross-model acronym density, D/AHP boundary confusion, transmission wording, M-ceiling phrasing, IA threshold precision, and the possible over-reading of “confirmation” as validation. AHP does not rescore, does not activate D, does not upgrade transmission status, and does not create new case findings.

##### What the analysis shows

The case shows that trust is structurally legible where framed expectation, meaningful absence, vulnerability, temporality, and distance are held together without closure-by-authority. It does not show that trust is solved. PMS Core maps the basic movement: differences become directionally charged within a frame; expected events may fail or remain delayed; patterns may stabilize; vulnerability and asymmetry become visible; temporal arcs shape whether confidence, doubt, repair, or refusal can be understood.

The central closure possible here is structural, not adjudicative. The analysis can say that trust requires calibration of expectation, absence, asymmetry, time, distance, and possible repair. It can say that Σ (Integration) and Ψ (Self-binding) are not available cheaply. It can say that non-response, refusal, rupture, and disagreement must not automatically become drift. It can say that EDEN makes reciprocity-as-coordinated-asymmetry visible. It cannot say that any actual relation has failed, matured, healed, betrayed, or become reciprocal.

The analysis also shows why trust is vulnerable to over-smooth modeling. PMS fits the case well: nearly every operator can be made relevant. That fit is useful, but dangerous. If every trust phenomenon maps too easily, the reading may lose discrimination. The case therefore keeps non-capture open. Lived vulnerability, affective texture, grief, fear, hope, shame, cultural meanings, theological trust, legal trust, empirical trust dynamics, and linguistic-pragmatic trust may remain partly outside PMS or EDEN.

The main drift-pattern result is conservative. AD_A>>E and AD_Sigma_low are possible risk checks, not findings. The case does not show awareness expanding while action is chronically suppressed. It does not show volatile coherence or inconsistent responsibility. It shows under-specification. Under-specification is not failure.

The MIP and AHP layers reinforce this result. MIP refuses score assignment because the case lacks concrete role evidence. AHP identifies how the analysis could be misread, but does not strengthen the case claim. Full-stack treatment therefore does not mean stronger verdict. It means more explicit layer separation.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk in this case is the conversion of trust-language into verdict-language. Silence may be treated as betrayal; withdrawal as immaturity; repair as required restoration; vulnerability as guilt; reciprocity as symmetry; asymmetry as moral rank; refusal as drift. These are the case-specific dangers.

Here, Λ (Non-event / residue) must not be read as betrayal without a framed expectation. Ω (Asymmetry) must not be read as blame or legal responsibility. Θ (Temporality) must not be read as proof that a relation is developing toward maturity or failure. Φ (Recontextualization) must not be read as repair by default. Χ (Distance) must not be treated as avoidance or refusal to mature. Σ (Integration) must not be inferred from coherent prose. Ψ (Self-binding) must not be inferred from trust-language alone.

EDEN terms remain overlay handles. Threshold, breach, NRK, pseudo-symmetry, asymmetry switch, reciprocity loss, PFO, devaluation, and humiliation do not become PMS operators and do not become verdicts. In this case, most EDEN drift terms are weak, risk-only, or not applicable.

The examples are illustrative only. They demonstrate the calibration discipline; they do not provide evidence for the case as a whole.

##### Example 1 — The unanswered message and the frame of expectation

A person sends a message after a difficult conversation: “Can we talk tomorrow?” No reply comes that day. On its own, this non-reply is not yet betrayal, avoidance, immaturity, or proof that trust has failed. It becomes structurally meaningful only if a frame of expectation already exists. If the relation normally allows delayed answers, the silence may remain ordinary. If the prior conversation ended with an explicit promise to respond, the same silence may become a trust-relevant Λ (Non-event / residue), a meaningful non-event inside a frame.

The example demonstrates why trust cannot be analyzed from absence alone. PMS Core first requires Δ (Difference): reply / non-reply, expectation / no expectation, ordinary delay / meaningful omission. ∇ (Impulse) appears as the movement toward renewed relation, clarification, or withdrawal. □ (Frame) is decisive: the same missing reply means different things in friendship, workplace procedure, care relation, institutional complaint, or casual acquaintance. Λ (Non-event / residue) becomes active only if the non-reply fails a framed expectation. Ω (Asymmetry) becomes relevant only if the silence exposes one role to vulnerability, uncertainty, dependency, or repair cost; without that exposure, the scene may remain ordinary communication delay rather than trust-relevant non-event. Θ (Temporality) matters because the meaning of silence changes with time: one hour, one day, and one month do not carry the same structural weight. Χ (Distance) remains necessary because the analyst must not rush from silence to verdict.

The EDEN overlay is only weakly relevant here. The example may show threshold explicitness if the silence makes the option of withdrawal, distrust, or non-repair newly visible. It does not show breach-as-chain-interruption unless the expected reply, the relevant action, and the failure of Σ (Integration) / Ψ (Self-binding) are concretely established. It does not activate pseudo-symmetry, PFO, reciprocity loss, devaluation, or humiliation.

MIP status remains non-scored. No A-score, M-score, IA finding, or D-profile is warranted. AHP would treat this only as a useful example if its wording preserves the boundary: “silence may become trust-relevant” is acceptable; “the silence proves betrayal” is not.

Forbidden claims are therefore clear. The vignette does not diagnose avoidance, emotional immaturity, manipulation, or bad faith. It does not assign blame. It does not establish legal breach. It does not prove that repair is owed. It does not show that the person who waited is right, or that the person who did not answer is wrong.

The non-capture note is also important. The lived feeling of waiting may involve anxiety, grief, hope, anger, or shame. PMS can map the structural non-event, but it does not exhaust the affective experience of being left without response.

**Article takeaway:** the same absence can be ordinary, ambiguous, protective, negligent, or trust-relevant depending on frame, expectation, and time. Λ (Non-event / residue) is powerful in trust analysis, but only when calibrated.

##### Example 2 — Repair without automatic restoration

A project coordinator misses an agreed internal deadline. A colleague had relied on that deadline to prepare a presentation. The coordinator later explains that another urgent task intervened and offers to help repair the delay. The colleague accepts the explanation but does not immediately return to the previous level of reliance. For the next phase, the colleague asks for written checkpoints rather than informal assurances.

This vignette is concrete enough to show the structure of trust, but bounded enough to avoid adjudication. It does not ask whether the coordinator is trustworthy as a person. It asks how reliance, expectation, non-event, explanation, repair, and modified future reliance become structurally related.

PMS Core hooks are visible throughout. Δ (Difference) distinguishes missed deadline, explanation, repair offer, and modified future reliance. ∇ (Impulse) appears in two directions: the coordinator moves toward repair, while the colleague moves toward guarded reliance rather than full restoration. □ (Frame) is the project frame, not a total moral frame. Λ (Non-event / residue) is the missed deadline as meaningful non-event, but only because the deadline was agreed and relied upon. Α (Attractor) may become relevant only if such delays repeat; one missed deadline is not yet an attractor. Ω (Asymmetry) appears because the colleague bore downstream presentation risk. Θ (Temporality) matters because trust does not reset instantly after explanation; repair unfolds across later checkpoints. Φ (Recontextualization) appears when the missed deadline is recontextualized as an intervening-task problem rather than immediate betrayal. Χ (Distance) appears in the colleague’s refusal to restore informal trust too quickly. Σ (Integration) is possible but not complete: the new checkpoint structure may coordinate reliability and caution. Ψ (Self-binding) remains conditional unless the coordinator durably binds themselves to the revised responsibility structure.

The EDEN overlay sharpens the example by preventing two opposite overreadings. First, the missed deadline should not automatically become “Fall” or breach-as-chain-interruption. The repair offer and explanation matter. Second, the colleague’s guarded response should not be treated as failed reciprocity. Trust as coordinated asymmetry may require a changed burden structure: written checkpoints make Ω (Asymmetry) legible instead of pretending the relation is simply symmetrical. NRK is not active unless the scene shows realization under ∇ (Impulse) with Σ (Integration) failing and Ψ (Self-binding) absent or displaced. Pseudo-symmetry would become relevant only if both parties pretended nothing had changed while the colleague continued silently carrying the cost.

MIP status remains qualitative. No score should be assigned from the vignette. It could support a later MIP analysis only if the actual role conditions were known: what each party knew, what alternatives were available, whether the deadline was realistically controllable, whether the repair offer was viable, and whether the colleague’s checkpoint request was proportionate. AHP would flag any wording that turned “modified reliance” into a maturity judgment.

Forbidden claims include: the coordinator is irresponsible; the colleague is distrustful; written checkpoints are punitive; repair requires renewed informal trust; the missed deadline is legal breach; the scene proves institutional dysfunction. None follows from the bounded vignette.

The non-capture note is that ordinary workplace context may include workload, hierarchy, organizational culture, private stress, or team norms not captured by the structural reading. These factors may change the interpretation without invalidating the structural hooks.

**Article takeaway:** repair does not automatically mean restoration. A mature trust structure may preserve distance, revise expectations, and coordinate asymmetry without turning caution into blame or repair into coercion.

##### Example 3 — Institutional trust, publicness, and the danger of over-reading drift

A small community group submits a formal request to a local institution. The institution’s public materials say that such requests will receive a response within four weeks. Six weeks pass without reply. Members of the group begin to disagree about what the silence means. One member says the institution has betrayed public trust. Another says the delay may be administrative overload. A third wants to publish a public accusation. A fourth suggests sending a clarification request first and documenting the timeline before drawing conclusions.

This example demonstrates why the full stack is needed, but also why the stack must remain restrained. It contains trust, expectation, asymmetry, publicness, possible non-event, possible repair, and possible escalation. Yet it does not establish betrayal, legal breach, institutional bad faith, PFO, humiliation, or reciprocity loss.

PMS Core begins with Δ (Difference). The analysis must distinguish formal expectation from informal hope, delay from refusal, silence from rejection, administrative overload from avoidance, and public accusation from clarification request. ∇ (Impulse) appears in competing directions: escalation, patience, clarification, withdrawal, or documentation. □ (Frame) is institutional-public rather than intimate or clinical. The four-week response policy frames the expectation. Λ (Non-event / residue) becomes relevant because no response occurs after the expected window, but the meaning of that non-event remains open. Α (Attractor) is not yet active unless the institution repeatedly misses such timelines. Ω (Asymmetry) is significant because the institution controls response channels and public legitimacy, while the community group bears uncertainty and possible dependence on institutional recognition. Θ (Temporality) is central: four weeks, six weeks, and repeated silence over months would not mean the same thing.

Φ (Recontextualization) may appear if the group reframes the silence. It might become an administrative delay, a procedural failure, a public trust problem, or a reason for formal complaint. But Φ (Recontextualization) must not be used to smooth away the issue. Reframing silence as “just bureaucracy” could under-read vulnerability; reframing it as “betrayal” could over-read the evidence. Χ (Distance) is the key discipline: the fourth member’s proposal to document and ask for clarification preserves distance. It delays public closure without denying the trust-relevant residue. Σ (Integration) remains conditional. The group may later integrate concern, evidence, and action into a coherent response, but that has not yet occurred. Ψ (Self-binding) is also conditional. The institution may have a self-binding policy obligation, but the example does not establish whether the four-week statement is binding, aspirational, legally relevant, or operationally constrained.

The EDEN overlay adds useful caution. Threshold explicitness is visible: the group now recognizes that public accusation, formal complaint, renewed waiting, or withdrawal are selectable options. Breach-as-chain-interruption is possible but not established. NRK is not active, because the example does not show realization under ∇ (Impulse) with failed Σ (Integration) and displaced Ψ (Self-binding). Pseudo-symmetry is a risk if the institution publicly presents itself as equally responsive while retaining all interpretive and procedural control. But this is not yet a finding. PFO is not applicable. There is no evidence of the EDEN-specific regime conditions. Parity-to-exit and responsibility-to-authority are also not active. Reciprocity loss is only a risk: it would require chronic Λ (Non-event / residue), stabilized Α (Attractor), real Ω (Asymmetry), Θ (Temporality) erosion, and failed or displaced Σ (Integration) / Ψ (Self-binding). Six weeks of silence may be concerning, but it is not enough by itself to prove such a drift profile.

MIP becomes relevant because publicness appears. The group’s possible public accusation increases D-risk. A public claim may be warranted in some concrete cases, but MIP would require role clarity, information basis, proportionality, reviewability, and distinction between structural criticism and reputational attack. No A-score or M-score is assigned. The institution is not scored; the group is not scored; the members are not ranked. The IA-box remains under-specified: transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility all require more evidence. D is relevant only as protection. Public dignity risks arise if the institution is personified into individual blame, or if members of the group are shamed for caution or urgency.

AHP would treat the example as high-risk for language hygiene. It would flag terms like “betrayal,” “bad faith,” “institutional humiliation,” or “reciprocity loss” unless the article clearly marks them as possible readings, not findings. AHP would recommend precise wording: “the silence creates a trust-relevant Λ (Non-event / residue) under a public institutional frame” is safer than “the institution betrayed the group.”

Forbidden claims are extensive. The vignette does not prove legal breach. It does not prove institutional bad faith. It does not diagnose bureaucratic dysfunction. It does not show that public accusation is wrong or right. It does not show that caution is mature and urgency immature. It does not establish PFO, pseudo-symmetry, NRK, humiliation, devaluation, or reciprocity loss. It does not validate PMS, EDEN, MIP, or AHP.

The non-capture note matters because institutions have operational constraints that may not be visible from the outside: staffing, jurisdiction, emergency workload, unclear submission status, procedural backlog, or legal limits. Conversely, affected groups may experience silence as exclusion, disrespect, or structural vulnerability in ways that administrative explanation alone does not capture. PMS can map the structural residue, but it cannot replace affected-party account or institutional evidence.

**Article takeaway:** institutional trust cases are structurally tempting because public silence easily invites closure. The disciplined reading preserves the trust-relevant non-event while refusing premature drift labels, legal conclusions, or public shaming.

##### Case takeaway

Trust is a high-closure case because it invites the analysis to decide too quickly what absence, doubt, withdrawal, repair, or betrayal means. The chapter use of this case should therefore be methodological: it shows how AXIOM can map the structural movement of trust without deciding the relation by authority.

PMS Core makes trust visible as framed, temporally extended, asymmetrically exposed, expectation-sensitive praxis. EDEN adds a restrained overlay by sharpening threshold, breach, reciprocity, and reciprocity-loss questions. MIP preserves non-scoring and working-note discipline where governance language might otherwise overreach. AHP hardens the analysis artifact by identifying possible misreadings without changing the case result.

The remaining Λ (Non-event / residue) is not a defect. It is part of the result. Trust can be structurally mapped, but not exhausted. The case should be used to show how a relation can be made more legible without becoming a verdict about persons, institutions, maturity, betrayal, or repair.

#### 9.4.4 Boundary erosion (Privacy → Publicness)

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because it exposes a closure demand that is especially tempting in public-facing disputes: the demand to decide whether a movement from confidentiality into publicness is accountability or betrayal. Material made for a bounded repair frame — confidential feedback, internal resolution language, mediation notes, complaint material, apology drafts, institutional responses — can become materially different when moved into a public or tribunal-like frame. What was initially framed as repair material may become evidence; what was framed as an internal accountability step may become public accusation; what was framed as confidentiality may become either protection or concealment.

The case therefore belongs in the EDEN part because the PMS Core structure alone already makes the frame transfer legible, but EDEN makes the publicness threshold more precise. Once bounded material becomes selectable as public material, comparison, pseudo-symmetry, switch-risk, reciprocity strain, and cost externalization become easier to see. Publicness does not automatically mean erosion. Confidentiality does not automatically mean repair. The structural difficulty is that both frames can be legitimate and both can become drift-prone.

This is a conceptual projection rather than a concrete praxis scene. No actor, chronology, consent record, legal duty, institutional procedure, repair history, audience scale, or harm context is supplied. That matters: responsibility, action, asymmetry, self-binding, and drift can be structurally rendered, but they must not become scene-specific findings without additional evidence. The case is mapped rather than solved because the available material supports a high-risk structural reading, not a verdict about whether disclosure, silence, public accountability, or confidentiality was warranted.

##### Structural reading

At PMS Core level, the central movement is a frame transfer. Δ (Difference) makes the basic distinction visible: confidential/public, internal/external, repair/accountability, bounded/open, original-use/secondary-use. ∇ (Impulse) appears only as structural pressure: pressure toward repair, pressure toward disclosure, pressure toward accountability, pressure toward containment, pressure toward exposure. No motive follows from this. The analysis must not infer courage, retaliation, avoidance, bad faith, fear, or moral urgency from the mere presence of publicness or confidentiality.

□ (Frame) is central. The same material functions differently inside different frames. In a confidential repair frame, it may support candor, learning, non-humiliating correction, and bounded response. In a public accountability frame, it may support warning, public learning, answerability, or democratic scrutiny. But either frame can also become distorted. Confidentiality can become silence or closure. Publicness can become tribunalization or reputational punishment. PMS therefore does not ask which frame is “really” right. It asks what the frame permits, what it excludes, what it protects, and what cost distribution it creates.

Λ (Non-event / residue) is relevant but must remain candidate-only. The move into publicness may be driven by an absent repair, an absent response, an absent accountability step, an absent right of reply, an absent proportionality check, or an absent confidentiality boundary. But the abstract case does not specify which absence is active. A missing repair process cannot be invented merely because publicness appears; a confidentiality breach cannot be asserted merely because bounded material becomes public. Λ-residue remains visible precisely because the case does not provide the scene data needed to close it.

Α (Attractor) is weak. The language of drift suggests possible stabilization, but a single abstract frame transfer does not establish repeated patterning. Α would become stronger if there were recurring uses of confidentiality to block accountability, recurring publicization of repair material as pressure, recurring tribunalization of internal feedback, or repeated repair processes that never produce accountable change. Without recurrence or path-dependence evidence, attractor language remains a risk marker, not a finding.

Ω (Asymmetry) is structurally active. Publicness changes exposure, audience scale, reputational risk, reply capacity, and interpretive control. Confidentiality changes access, timing, context control, and procedural leverage. But Ω cannot be assigned to one side in advance. The confidentiality-holder may hold power; the publicizing actor may hold power; the institution may hold power; the audience may acquire diffuse power; the affected or exposed role may carry disproportionate costs. This is why the case cannot be resolved by preferring privacy or publicness as such.

Θ (Temporality) matters because boundary erosion is not a static contrast. It is a movement over time: material is created or held in one frame and later becomes available in another. Expectations accumulate. Repair may fail or succeed. Public-interest thresholds may emerge. Consequences may become irreversible. Silence may harden; exposure may harden. Without chronology, Θ can mark the transition but cannot generate a narrative of delay, escalation, recurrence, or culpability.

Φ (Recontextualization) is the strongest PMS Core operator in the case. Existing material is placed into a new frame, changing its audience, relevance, and practical force. But Φ does not decide whether the transfer is erosion, correction, disclosure, distortion, protection, or warranted escalation. It names the structural transformation. The analysis must distinguish frame transfer from mere quotation, consented publication, anonymized learning, legal disclosure, protected warning, or independently generated public criticism.

Χ (Distance) is mandatory as application discipline. The analysis must not fuse with either the public accountability frame or the confidentiality frame. It must not become a tribunal in structural language, and it must not defend containment by default. Χ preserves reversibility: the case remains readable without becoming a judgment.

Σ (Integration) is weak and at risk. The core structural question is whether repair and accountability can be integrated: privacy without impunity, publicness without humiliation, disclosure without context collapse, confidentiality without silence, accountability without spectacle. The danger is that Φ outpaces Σ: material is recontextualized before the competing burdens are carried together. But Σ is not mere smoothness. A coherent PMS reading is not proof that the scene itself is integrated.

Ψ (Self-binding) is conditional. It would require specified commitments: confidentiality promises, repair obligations, accountability duties, public-interest duties, non-retaliation commitments, right-of-reply procedures, or restraint norms carried across time. The case does not provide those commitments. The analysis itself must therefore remain self-bound: no verdict, no person-ranking, no drift finding, no legal conclusion.

The derived structures remain formula-derived rather than primitive. Awareness_A is active only as structural differentiation across time and frame, not as a claim about actor-awareness. Coherence_C remains weak because the private and public frames may each be internally coherent while their transition is not yet integrated. Responsibility_R is conditional because the case lacks specified commitments, knowledge, power, alternatives, and role duties. Action_E is not established because no Σ (Integration)-carried enactment across Θ (Temporality) is shown. Dignity_in_Practice_D is active only as a Ψ (Self-binding) / Χ (Distance) / Ω (Asymmetry) application constraint against humiliation, person-ranking, and standing loss. SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only: this is not a selfhood case, and any institutional self-binding reading would require concrete scene evidence.

EDEN sharpens this structure by making the publicness threshold more visible. Once the material becomes selectable for public use, several overlay handles become relevant. Threshold visibility is active: the option of publicizing, quoting, framing as evidence, or inviting public judgment appears. Breach-as-chain-interruption is weak: a repair chain or confidentiality chain may have been interrupted, but this is not shown. The Negative Responsibility Kernel remains weak and must not be treated as Action_E. Fig-leaf or legibility management is a risk: confidentiality can manage exposure without repair, and publicness can manage legitimacy without context. False comparison is a significant risk: the issue can shift from “how are repair and accountability coordinated?” to “who is right, credible, failed, or disqualified?” Pseudo-Symmetry remains a risk, not a finding: privacy and public accountability may be presented as formally equal options while real exposure and voice burdens are unequal.

Asymmetry-switch availability is especially important. Either frame can activate asymmetry under pressure. Confidentiality may invoke trust, process, fairness, vulnerability, or legal duty to restrict publicness. Public accountability may invoke harm, public interest, institutional failure, or protection to justify exposure. Either activation can be valid if marked, bounded, and re-bound into integration. Either can become drift if unmarked, repeatable, and responsibility-shifting. Permanentized Exception Switch and PFO are not active in this case: there is no evidence of a standing regime, protocolized exception lever, Ω-taboo governance, or full drift catalogue activation. EDEN’s correct contribution is local threshold and switch-risk, not regime diagnosis.

MIP adds a case-specific maturity-in-practice result: the configuration is high-publicness and under-specified. Awareness is conceptually present because the frame transfer is named. Coherence remains fragile. Responsibility is role-specific and cannot be assigned globally. Power-to-act is unknown because the case lacks concrete levers. IA-risk is visible because publicness and confidentiality can both distribute burdens asymmetrically, but IA-risk is not a verdict. Dignity-in-Practice remains qualitative and protective: publicness must not become humiliation, and confidentiality must not become silencing. AHP hardens the analysis artefact rather than rescoring it. Its case-specific warning is that a polished structural article can itself be mistaken for a public-ready decision document unless transmission boundaries, score hygiene, D-profile restraint, and evidence-chain limits remain explicit.

##### What the analysis shows

The central structural movement can be stated compactly: a bounded repair or confidential feedback frame becomes available to public accountability or tribunal-like publicness through Φ (Recontextualization), under Δ (Difference), □ (Frame), Ω (Asymmetry), and Θ (Temporality), with Λ (Non-event / residue) and Α (Attractor) conditional, Σ (Integration) at risk, Χ (Distance) required, and Ψ (Self-binding) conditional.

The analysis shows that boundary erosion is not identifiable merely by the fact that material becomes public. It becomes analytically plausible only when the frame transfer externalizes costs, suppresses context, weakens reply capacity, converts repair material into standing judgment, or uses publicness to bypass integration. Conversely, confidentiality becomes structurally suspect only when it blocks repair, suppresses accountability, closes Λ-residue prematurely, or protects asymmetry without review. The closure that PMS can provide is therefore not a verdict. It is a bounded structural clarification: this is a publicness-threshold case in which frame, time, asymmetry, recontextualization, and integration must be tracked before any stronger claim is made.

EDEN adds that the case is vulnerable to comparison drift. Public discourse can shift from praxis coordination to value comparison; privacy discourse can shift from repair protection to closure management. The overlay makes these risks visible without making them findings. MIP adds that non-assignment is itself the result: no global maturity score, no person finding, no dignity ranking, and no actionable governance conclusion follow from the abstract case material. AHP adds that the analysis must remain internally protected against becoming more authoritative than its evidence permits.

Λ-residue remains visible at several points: whether internal repair failed; whether confidentiality was legitimate; whether publicness was warranted; whether consent, anonymization, public-interest threshold, legal duty, or right of reply existed; whether public consequences were reversible; and whether rival frameworks discriminate the case better than PMS or EDEN.

The reading weakens if the material was never actually bounded, was public from the start, was consented for public use, was legally required to be disclosed, was sufficiently anonymized, or was part of the original accountability frame. It also weakens if publicness remains praxis-coordinative rather than comparison-dominant, if asymmetry activation is marked and bounded, if robust Σ and Ψ are carried, or if confidentiality demonstrably protects repair without suppressing accountability. These weakening conditions are not defects in the model. They are the conditions under which the analysis must remain reversible or yield to legal, procedural, information-ethical, restorative, institutional, public-sphere, or phenomenological readings.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk in this case is public-tribunal conversion. Structural language can easily become another form of public judgment: “boundary erosion,” “pseudo-symmetry,” “switch-risk,” “devaluation,” “humiliation,” or “IA-risk” can be misread as findings about actors. This must not happen. In this case, risk remains risk. Publicness is not automatically drift. Confidentiality is not automatically repair. Resistance, refusal, rupture, non-fit, disagreement, or absence are not automatically incomplete integration or maturity failure.

The second distinctive boundary is dignity language. Dignity-in-Practice is praxeological, not ontological. It protects against humiliation, person-ranking, and irreversible standing loss; it does not rank persons, frames, or publics by dignity. The case is precisely the kind of configuration in which dignity vocabulary can be weaponized from either side: publicness can claim dignity through recognition while producing exposure; confidentiality can claim dignity through protection while silencing accountability.

The third boundary is legal and procedural. Real cases of confidentiality, protected disclosure, defamation, professional privilege, retaliation, duty to warn, restorative process, or public-interest disclosure require external analysis. PMS and EDEN can map structural pressures, but they do not decide legality, admissibility, culpability, or institutional duty.

##### Example 1 — The internal feedback note that becomes public evidence

A small organization runs an internal review after a difficult project. Participants are invited to submit confidential feedback. The stated purpose is repair: identify what went wrong, reduce future harm, and let people speak candidly without fear of immediate public exposure. One participant writes that the project lead repeatedly ignored warnings, dismissed concerns, and created a climate in which junior staff stopped speaking. The feedback is submitted through the internal channel.

Several weeks pass. The organization announces that the project is closed and that “all concerns were addressed internally.” One participant believes this is false. They quote part of the confidential feedback in a public post, arguing that the organization is using confidentiality to avoid accountability. The quoted passage circulates widely. Some readers treat it as evidence that the project lead is unfit for future work. Others say the public quotation violated the repair process and turned confidential feedback into a public tribunal.

This vignette demonstrates the core structure of boundary erosion without deciding whether the public quotation was justified. The material changes frame. In the first frame, the feedback note belongs to a bounded repair process. Its audience, purpose, and expected use are constrained. In the second frame, the same material becomes public evidence in an accountability discourse. The public move may be warranted if the internal process failed, concealed harm, or falsely claimed resolution. It may also be erosive if it strips context, bypasses reply, or converts repair material into reputational judgment. The example is therefore not a verdict about disclosure. It is a readable scene in which the frame-transfer problem becomes visible.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. Δ (Difference) appears in the distinction between confidential/internal and public/accountability-facing use. ∇ (Impulse) appears only weakly as structural pressure: repair pressure, disclosure pressure, accountability pressure, and exposure pressure. No motive is inferred. □ (Frame) is central because the same sentence functions differently inside an internal review and a public post. Λ (Non-event / residue) is a candidate because the participant may be responding to an absent repair, an absent truthful institutional account, or an absent accountability step; but this must be specified. Α (Attractor) is not established unless the organization repeatedly closes internal complaints without repair, or participants repeatedly publicize confidential material as the only available route. Ω (Asymmetry) is active as risk: the project lead may bear public reputational exposure; junior staff may have carried unequal silence or dependency; the organization may control the internal record; the public may exert diffuse judgment power. Θ (Temporality) appears in the sequence from internal submission to public quotation after a period of alleged non-response. Φ (Recontextualization) is the strongest operator: the note is recontextualized from repair material into public evidence. Χ (Distance) is required from the analysis: the reader must not fuse with either the public post or the confidentiality claim. Σ (Integration) is weak and at risk because repair and accountability are not yet integrated. Ψ (Self-binding) is conditional: confidentiality promises, repair duties, public-interest obligations, and reply duties would need to be specified before responsibility could be assigned.

The EDEN overlay helps sharpen the threshold. The feedback note becomes selectable as public material. That threshold does not itself create truth, legitimacy, or wrongdoing. It only marks that a new option is now available: quotation, public framing, audience expansion, and accountability pressure. EDEN also marks false comparison risk. Public readers may compare standing: who is credible, who failed, who should be blamed. That may replace praxis coordination. Pseudo-symmetry may appear if people treat “confidentiality” and “public accountability” as formally equal options while real costs differ. Asymmetry-switch risk is also bidirectional: the organization may invoke confidentiality to block accountability, while the publicizing actor may invoke harm or public interest to bypass proportionality and reply. None of these risks is a finding.

MIP renders the vignette as high-publicness and under-specified unless more facts are supplied. It would ask what each role knew, what each role could do, what was predictable, what alternatives existed, and whether any serious integration attempt occurred. The internal process-holder may have had power to repair or respond. The publicizing participant may have had power to anonymize, contextualize, delay, or seek bounded disclosure. The exposed project lead may or may not have had reply capacity. The public audience may amplify consequences without carrying procedural responsibility. MIP would not assign a global maturity score. AHP would warn that a polished analysis of this example could itself become a public judgment if transmitted without guardrails.

Forbidden claims are essential here. The example does not show that the publicizing participant acted maturely or immaturely. It does not show that the project lead is guilty, unsafe, incompetent, or morally deficient. It does not show that the organization committed legal wrongdoing. It does not show that confidentiality is manipulation or that publicness is shaming. It does not diagnose anyone and does not rank dignity.

The non-capture note is equally important. A legal whistleblowing frame might be decisive. A procedural justice analysis might ask whether the project lead had notice and reply rights. An institutional-power analysis might focus on whether the internal process was structurally incapable of accountability. A restorative-practice reading might ask whether the feedback process had any genuine repair mechanism. PMS and EDEN illuminate the frame transfer; they do not exhaust the case.

The article takeaway is that boundary erosion becomes analytically visible when a material crosses from bounded repair into public accountability, but the crossing alone does not decide legitimacy. The mature question is whether the transition carries its asymmetries: repair without concealment, accountability without humiliation, publicness without context collapse, and confidentiality without impunity.

##### Example 2 — The anonymized lesson that nearly becomes a tribunal

A university department holds a confidential mediation after a conflict between a supervisor and several graduate students. The mediation produces a short internal resolution document. It names communication failures, unclear expectations, delayed responses, and a need for better supervisory structures. The document is not meant to identify individuals publicly. Its purpose is to support local repair and prevent recurrence.

Months later, the department wants to publish a public-facing “lessons learned” memo. The memo is intended to acknowledge that supervisory structures failed and to explain new safeguards. A draft includes several details from the confidential mediation. Although names are removed, the small size of the department makes the supervisor and students easily identifiable to insiders. Some faculty argue that transparency is necessary because the department has a public responsibility to show that it has changed. Others object that the memo converts a bounded repair process into public reputational exposure. A revised draft removes identifying details, describes structural changes rather than personal failures, adds a right-of-reply process for affected participants, and states what will remain confidential.

This example demonstrates that publicness is not automatically boundary erosion. The case is still structurally risky because material from a bounded repair process is being moved toward public communication. But the vignette includes a possible integrative path: anonymization, structural framing, reduction of identifying details, explicit limits, and reply capacity. The example therefore helps the article avoid a false binary. Public communication may be necessary and still require strict boundary work. Confidentiality may protect participants and still allow public learning if the transition is properly carried.

PMS Core hooks remain visible. Δ (Difference) distinguishes confidential mediation from public lessons learned. ∇ (Impulse) appears as pressure toward transparency and pressure toward protection. □ (Frame) is again central: the same mediation findings function differently as internal repair material and public institutional communication. Λ (Non-event / residue) may include a public expectation of accountability after prior failure, but it may also include an expectation that mediation details remain bounded. Α (Attractor) remains weak unless the department has a repeated pattern of either overexposing mediation material or hiding behind confidentiality. Ω (Asymmetry) is strong as a structural concern: the supervisor, students, department, and public have different exposure, authority, dependency, and reply capacities. Θ (Temporality) appears in the movement from mediation to later public memo. Φ (Recontextualization) is active because mediation findings are recontextualized into public institutional learning. Χ (Distance) appears not only as analytic distance but also as a possible case-level practice: the revision process pauses before publication and distinguishes structural learning from public identification. Σ (Integration) is partially visible as a risk and possible achievement: confidentiality, accountability, learning, and dignity protection are being coordinated. Ψ (Self-binding) remains conditional but more plausible than in the first example because the revised process begins to articulate commitments: what will be disclosed, what will remain confidential, how reply will be handled, and how future supervision will change.

EDEN is especially useful here because it distinguishes threshold activation from drift outcome. The threshold is active: confidential material becomes selectable for public use. But the example shows an attempt to prevent the threshold from becoming false comparison. The revised memo avoids turning the mediation into a ranking of persons. It shifts away from “who failed” and toward “which structures failed and what will change.” That is an EDEN-relevant move from value-relation back toward praxis-relation.

Pseudo-symmetry risk is present but contained. One side might say, “The public has a right to know everything.” Another might say, “Mediation confidentiality means nothing can be public.” Both can become parity-to-exit moves if used to avoid carrying unequal burdens. The revised memo attempts a different structure: public responsibility is carried through structural learning, while confidentiality is carried through non-identification and bounded detail. Asymmetry activation becomes more marked and bounded. The department does not simply invoke public accountability to justify exposure; nor does it invoke confidentiality to avoid all public answerability. This is not proof of mature integration, but it demonstrates what EDEN means by non-externalization of unequal burdens.

MIP would treat this example as more operationally promising than the first, but still not as a person-level maturity finding. Awareness is higher because publicness risk is explicitly named. Coherence is more visible because the memo attempts to align public communication with confidentiality constraints. Responsibility is still role-specific: the department has process power; participants have dignity and reply interests; the public has an accountability interest but not unlimited entitlement to mediation detail. Power-to-act is more concrete: revise, anonymize, contextualize, create reply procedure, define confidentiality boundary. The IA-box would still require transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility checks. AHP would likely mark lower transmission risk if the memo itself included clear boundaries, but it would still warn that public-facing text can be overread.

Forbidden claims must remain explicit. This example does not prove that anonymization is always enough. It does not prove that public lessons learned are always legitimate. It does not prove that the department acted maturely or that objectors were immature. It does not decide whether the mediation details were legally protected. It does not diagnose the supervisor or students. It does not rank the dignity of participants against the public interest.

The non-capture note is strong. Information ethics may better decide whether the anonymization is sufficient in a small community. Legal confidentiality rules may prohibit even anonymized disclosure. Restorative practice may ask whether participants consented to public learning. Institutional accountability theory may ask whether structural language is being used to avoid naming actual power abuse. PMS and EDEN can describe the transition and its risks, but they cannot settle those external questions.

The article takeaway is that not every privacy-to-publicness movement is erosion. A bounded public communication may preserve the core repair frame while carrying accountability, but only if the transition names asymmetry, limits exposure, preserves reply, avoids person-ranking, and does not use public learning as a substitute for actual repair.

##### Example 3 — The public accusation that reveals a failed private frame

A professional association receives several confidential complaints about a senior member’s conduct at events. The association tells complainants that the matter will be handled internally. Months pass. No clear outcome is communicated. Some complainants believe the senior member continues to hold visible roles and that others remain at risk. One complainant writes a public statement saying that internal channels have failed. The statement does not publish the full complaint file, but it describes the pattern in general terms and names the association’s non-response. The association replies that the public statement undermines confidentiality and damages the fairness of the internal process.

This vignette is high-risk because it looks like boundary erosion and may also be warranted public accountability. It is useful precisely because the article must not collapse publicness into drift. The complainant moves from a bounded internal complaint process to public discourse. That is a frame transfer. But the public move may be a response to a failed internal frame. If internal confidentiality protects inaction, the public statement may be the first available accountability route. If the public statement overstates, identifies people unnecessarily, or bypasses fair process, it may create exposure and tribunal risk. The example forces the analysis to preserve both possibilities.

The PMS Core hooks are strong. Δ (Difference) distinguishes internal complaint handling from public accountability statement. ∇ (Impulse) includes pressure for protection, warning, answerability, and perhaps frustration, though no motive is inferred. □ (Frame) is contested: the association frames the matter as internal process; the complainant frames it as public accountability after internal failure. Λ (Non-event / residue) is highly relevant as a candidate: delayed response, absent outcome, absent protection, absent communication, or absent accountability. But even here, Λ must be evidenced. The mere belief that nothing happened is not the same as proof that nothing happened. Α (Attractor) remains unproven unless the association repeatedly uses confidentiality to avoid accountability. Ω (Asymmetry) is central: the senior member may have status and institutional protection; complainants may bear vulnerability or retaliation risk; the association controls internal process; public discourse creates new exposure. Θ (Temporality) is visible in months of delay. Φ (Recontextualization) is active as recontextualization of internal complaint material into public accountability language. Χ (Distance) is difficult but essential: the analysis must not become either a public accusation or an institutional defense. Σ (Integration) is weak because internal process and public accountability are not integrated. Ψ (Self-binding) is conditional: the association’s process duties, the complainant’s public-interest obligations, and the senior member’s reply rights would need specification.

EDEN’s strongest contribution here is the distinction between boundary transfer and local switch drift without full PFO. The complainant may activate asymmetry by invoking risk, vulnerability, and institutional failure to justify publicness. The association may activate asymmetry by invoking confidentiality, fairness, and process integrity to demand silence. Either activation may be valid if marked, bounded, and re-bound into responsibility. Either may become drift if it becomes an unmarked lever: publicness as exposure without safeguards, or confidentiality as closure without accountability.

Pseudo-symmetry risk is also sharp. The association may say all complaints are handled equally through confidential procedure, while real asymmetry leaves complainants without voice and the senior member with continued public standing. The public statement may say public accountability applies equally, while real asymmetry exposes some parties to reputational or procedural costs they cannot answer. EDEN does not decide which side is right. It shows that formal parity can hide unequal burden distribution.

PFO is still not applicable unless a broader regime is shown. The example may show local switch-risk, but not a regime of permanentized exception, Ω-taboo governance, or protocolized comparison. Devaluation and humiliation remain risks: public accusation may lower standing-in-practice before process is complete; internal confidentiality may lower complainants’ standing by treating public concern as improper. But neither humiliation nor devaluation is automatically found.

MIP would emphasize that responsibility cannot be assigned without role-specific knowledge and power. What did the association know? What could it disclose? What legal constraints applied? What risks did complainants face? What alternatives existed? Was there a serious internal integration attempt? Did the public statement preserve proportionality, context, and right of reply? Did the association preserve safety and accountability, or only procedure? Without these answers, MIP can identify IA-risk but cannot assign M. It can say that publicness and confidentiality both create burden distributions requiring transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. AHP would warn that any article rendering of this example could easily become a covert verdict if it uses vivid details, identifies roles too closely, or lets public failure language outrun evidence.

Forbidden claims are especially important. The example does not prove that the senior member did anything. It does not prove that the association concealed misconduct. It does not prove that the complainant acted responsibly or irresponsibly. It does not determine whether public disclosure was protected, defamatory, necessary, or excessive. It does not diagnose institutional bad faith. It does not label resistance as immaturity. It does not convert delay into guilt.

The non-capture note is central. A legal analysis may dominate this scene. Confidentiality obligations, defamation risk, protected disclosure, duty of care, and process fairness may determine what can be said. A trauma-informed or safety-oriented framework may better capture why internal silence can be harmful. A procedural justice framework may better specify reply rights and evidentiary thresholds. A public-interest framework may better determine whether naming the association’s non-response is warranted. PMS, EDEN, and MIP provide structural discipline, but they must remain open to these grammars.

The article takeaway is that boundary erosion cannot be identified merely by the fact that confidential material becomes public. Sometimes publicness may reveal that the private frame had already failed. The mature structural question is not “privacy or publicness?” but whether the transition carries the unequal burdens of protection, accountability, process, exposure, reply, and reversibility without turning either confidentiality or public accountability into an authority that escapes its own costs.

##### Case takeaway

This case shows that privacy-to-publicness transfer is not self-interpreting. The same movement can be erosive, corrective, protective, necessary, disproportionate, restorative, or tribunalizing depending on the missing scene structure. PMS Core maps the formal movement: bounded material is recontextualized into publicness under frame, time, asymmetry, and integration pressure. EDEN sharpens the publicness-specific risks: threshold selection, pseudo-symmetry, switch activation, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, reciprocity strain, and cost externalization. MIP preserves the downstream result that no person finding, maturity score, dignity score, or IA verdict follows. AHP preserves the artefact-level caution that even a precise analysis must not become a public decision instrument.

The methodological point is not that boundary erosion has occurred. The point is that a closure-demand has become structurally legible: either confidentiality must show how it carries repair and accountability, or publicness must show how it carries context, proportionality, reply, and reversibility. Where neither burden is carried, Λ (Non-event / residue) remains visible. Where rival legal, procedural, information-ethical, restorative, institutional, public-sphere, or phenomenological readings discriminate the case better, PMS and EDEN must yield or remain secondary.

The case should therefore be used in the chapter as a publicness-threshold stress test. It demonstrates how AXIOM can map a high-risk closure demand without solving it by authority, and how add-on visibility can sharpen a case without turning overlay language into verdict.

#### 9.4.5 Repair Relationships

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because trust repair creates one of the strongest closure-demands in the AXIOM field. A damaged or uncertain trust relation almost immediately asks for a verdict: was there betrayal, misunderstanding, justified refusal, failed repair, coercive repair pressure, or only unresolved expectation? The case concerns how trust is tested, damaged, withdrawn, or restored through reliance, vulnerability, expectation, confidence, doubt, repair, or betrayal between persons, roles, claims, or institutions. That makes it structurally rich, but also dangerous: the vocabulary of repair can quickly become a tribunal vocabulary.

The case is not a concrete praxis scene. It is a conceptual projection of a trust-repair field. No actor, institution, promise, omission, apology, refusal, repair attempt, evidence sequence, or outcome is supplied. This matters because the operators can be structurally rendered, but they must not become scene-specific findings. Responsibility, action, asymmetry, integration, and self-binding can be mapped as possible structures, but they cannot be assigned to any person, group, role, or institution without additional evidence.

The closure-demand is philosophically important because trust appears to combine expectation and exposure. Trust is not merely a belief that someone or something will behave reliably. It often places a role-position in a vulnerable relation to a person, claim, institution, norm, or promise. Once vulnerability is involved, absence can become meaningful: silence, delay, non-response, missing acknowledgement, absent repair, or failure to repeat a prior reliability signal can carry structural weight. Yet these absences do not interpret themselves. They require a frame, a temporal horizon, a relevant expectation, and a calibrated relation of exposure.

This is why the case belongs in AXIOM. It shows the difference between mapping closure pressure and claiming closure. A trust rupture can demand moral certainty even when the scene has not supplied enough structure for certainty. The mere presence of disappointment, silence, delay, disagreement, withdrawal, or non-fit does not establish betrayal. The mere presence of apology, explanation, reassurance, or institutional messaging does not establish repair. The case therefore demonstrates a central AXIOM discipline: closure must remain conditional where Λ (Non-event / residue) is still structurally open.

##### Structural reading

At the PMS Core level, the trust-repair field begins with **Δ (Difference)**. The case requires distinctions between trust and distrust, reliance and non-reliance, confidence and doubt, repair and non-repair, betrayal and misunderstanding, person and role, claim and response, institution and actor. These distinctions make the field legible, but they do not decide it. A later scene may show that what looked like betrayal was better described as non-fit, justified refusal, institutional delay, misunderstanding, or a rival frame.

From Δ (Difference), **∇ (Impulse)** becomes visible as directional tension. A role may move toward reliance, withdraw reliance, seek repair, doubt a claim, demand acknowledgement, protect itself, accuse, re-enter trust, or refuse reconciliation. This remains structural directedness, not psychological motive. The analysis does not infer sincerity, avoidance, intent, fear, manipulation, immaturity, or pathology.

The decisive early operator is **□ (Frame)**. Trust changes meaning by frame. Interpersonal trust is not institutional trust; epistemic credibility is not contractual reliance; practical handover is not reconciliation; apology is not necessarily procedural repair. The frame must be named before absence, delay, rupture, or refusal can be interpreted. If the frame is wrong, the whole case reading becomes over-assimilative.

Within a frame, **Λ (Non-event / residue)** becomes central. Trust is often tested by what does not happen: the reply that never arrives, the promised repair that does not occur, the acknowledgement withheld, the missing confirmation of reliability, the absent review channel. But absence becomes structurally meaningful only where a framed expectation exists. Silence without a warranted expectation is not yet betrayal. Delay without a relevant temporal window is not yet drift. Non-response without a role obligation is not yet repair failure.

**Α (Attractor)** remains weak unless repetition or stabilization is shown. Trust, doubt, avoidance, apology-performance, institutional messaging, non-response, or comparison may stabilize into recurring scripts. But the conceptual case does not provide recurrence. A single rupture is not an attractor. A named absence is not a pattern. The attractor layer would require evidence of repeated interaction, path dependence, or role-script formation.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is highly relevant but under-specified. Reliance and vulnerability imply possible exposure gradients: one role may depend, another may decide; one may withhold, another may wait; one may control institutional procedure, another may bear uncertainty. Yet asymmetry is not wrongdoing. Vulnerability is not proof of correctness. Authority is not guilt. The structural question is who bears which exposure, capacity, obligation, publicness, or exit constraint. Without that mapping, Ω (Asymmetry) remains possible but not assigned.

**Θ (Temporality)** is central because trust repair is never purely instantaneous. Trust may be built through repeated reliability, damaged through delay, eroded through cumulative non-response, or restored through sustained enactment. But the case gives no sequence. There is no before, rupture, response, delay, recurrence, repair attempt, or aftermath. Θ (Temporality) therefore marks a required dimension, not an established trajectory.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is relevant because repair often works through reframing. A rupture may be recontextualized as misunderstanding; refusal may be recontextualized as boundary-setting; silence may be recontextualized as incapacity; institutional failure may be recontextualized as procedural gap. But Φ (Recontextualization) is risky. Reframing can clarify, but it can also erase. A repair frame that dissolves the damaged expectation too quickly becomes over-assimilative.

**Χ (Distance)** is the analytical safeguard. It prevents the case from becoming a small tribunal. Χ (Distance) suspends immediate verdict, keeps the reading reversible, and prevents repair-language from becoming pressure toward reconciliation. It also prevents refusal, rupture, or non-fit from being automatically redescribed as immaturity, failed integration, or drift.

**Σ (Integration)** is conditional. Repair would require more than explanation or coherent narrative. It would require integration of rupture, expectation, vulnerability, asymmetry, future practice, and revised frame. An apology is not necessarily Σ (Integration). A policy statement is not necessarily Σ (Integration). A coherent account of the problem is not necessarily Σ (Integration). In this case, Σ (Integration) functions as a repair threshold, not an achieved result.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is also conditional. Restored trust may require a person, role, or institution to bind itself across time to changed conduct, clarified responsibility, or renewed reliability. But a promise, apology, declaration, or reassurance is not yet Ψ (Self-binding). It must be integrated, temporally maintained, and reflectively constrained in the relevant frame.

The case-specific PMS movement can therefore be rendered as:

Σ? ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ

The question mark on Σ (Integration) is essential. Integration is structurally relevant to repair, but it is not achieved by the case material. Ψ (Self-binding) remains downstream and unclaimed unless a concrete scene shows durable commitment across time.

PMS dependency discipline matters strongly here. Later operators cannot be used cheaply. Λ (Non-event / residue) cannot prove betrayal without a framed expectation. Ω (Asymmetry) cannot prove wrongdoing without a mapped exposure gradient. Θ (Temporality) cannot invent a history. Φ (Recontextualization) cannot erase rupture. Χ (Distance) cannot become avoidance. Σ (Integration) cannot become forced reconciliation. Ψ (Self-binding) cannot be inferred from apology alone.

The derived structures remain formula-derived, not primitive. **Awareness_A** is active as sustained, framed differentiation across time: the analysis can hold distinctions between trust, doubt, repair, betrayal, absence, and role-position. **Coherence_C** is active but risky: the case is structurally coherent, but coherence is not proof of repair, truth, or adequacy. **Responsibility_R** is weak because no actor, knowledge state, asymmetry, option set, or self-binding structure is supplied. **Action_E** is under-specified because no integrated enactment is given. **Dignity_in_Practice_D** is active only as a praxeological constraint on handling vulnerability and asymmetry without shaming, ranking, or coercive closure. **SELF_FIXPOINT** remains analytic-only: no stable self-binding chain is assigned to any person, role, or institution.

The PMS drift-patterns remain risks, not findings. **AD_A>>E** would become relevant if awareness, interpretation, or evaluation expanded while enactment remained chronically delayed. In this conceptual case, that is only a possible future distortion. **AD_Sigma_low** would become relevant if recontextualization repeatedly outpaced integration, leaving volatile coherence and inconsistent responsibility. Here, weak Σ (Integration) means only that integration is not established. It does not mean failed integration. Under-specification is not failure.

PMS-EDEN sharpens the repair-specific drift risks that PMS Core alone does not isolate. EDEN remains an overlay and introduces no new PMS operators. ##### Example 1 — The unanswered reliance request

A project member asks a colleague to confirm whether a shared document can be used in a meeting. The request is not casual: the meeting depends on the document, and the requesting role has relied on the colleague’s earlier statement that the document would be ready. No reply arrives before the meeting. The meeting proceeds with uncertainty, and afterwards the requester describes the silence as a breach of trust. The colleague later says that the request was seen too late and that no promise of same-day confirmation had been made.

The example is deliberately bounded. It does not decide whether betrayal occurred. It does not decide whether the colleague failed a duty. It does not decide whether the requester is overreacting, whether the colleague is unreliable, or whether the relationship has been damaged in any durable way. It simply gives enough scene structure to show why PMS must treat trust repair through frames, expectations, non-events, asymmetry, temporality, and distance.

The first PMS Core hook is **Δ (Difference)**. Several differences become relevant: reliance versus non-reliance, confirmation versus non-confirmation, silence versus response, prior statement versus current expectation, breach versus misunderstanding. Without those distinctions, the trust question cannot even be posed. But Δ alone does not settle the case. The distinction between “silence” and “breach” is not self-interpreting.

The second hook is **∇ (Impulse)**. The requester’s practical orientation moves toward reliance before the meeting and toward doubt after the non-response. The colleague’s later explanation may move toward repair, clarification, or boundary-setting. PMS does not infer motives from this. It only identifies directional tensions: reliance, doubt, explanation, and possible repair.

The decisive hook is **□ (Frame)**, the frame. If the working frame included a clear expectation that the colleague would confirm availability before the meeting, the silence has one meaning. If the prior statement was loose, exploratory, or not tied to the meeting, the same silence has another meaning. The frame must be specified before the absence can be judged structurally.

This leads to **Λ (Non-event / residue)**, the non-event. The missing reply may be a meaningful non-event only if a reply was expected within the frame. A silence without a framed expectation is not yet a breach. The example shows why PMS does not translate absence into failure automatically. Λ requires an expected occurrence, a relevant frame, and temporal placement.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** appears as possible asymmetry, but not automatically as wrongdoing. The requester may have borne more meeting risk. The colleague may have controlled access to the needed document. The requester may have had fewer alternatives. Yet these asymmetries must be mapped; they cannot be assumed from frustration alone. If the requester had other access options, Ω is different. If the colleague had no reasonable opportunity to respond, Ω is different again.

**Θ (Temporality)** matters because the meaning of the silence depends on timing. A reply one hour late, one day late, or after repeated earlier non-responses does not carry the same structure. If this is a single missed message, **Α (Attractor)** remains weak. If this repeats across multiple meetings, an attractor may form around unreliable response or anxious monitoring. The example therefore shows why recurrence must not be invented.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** may enter when the colleague later reframes the event: “I saw it too late,” “I did not understand this as urgent,” or “I never promised same-day confirmation.” Such recontextualization may clarify the frame. It may also erase the requester’s reliance if used too quickly. PMS must distinguish genuine frame clarification from repair-language that dissolves the damaged expectation.

**Χ (Distance)** is the analytical safeguard. A PMS reading should not immediately say “betrayal” or “overreaction.” It should hold distance long enough to ask what the expectation was, what the time window was, what alternatives existed, and what asymmetry was actually present. Without Χ, the example becomes a small tribunal.

**Σ (Integration)** remains conditional. The relationship is not repaired merely because an explanation is offered. Integration would require the relevant expectation to be coordinated: perhaps future urgent requests are marked explicitly, the document owner confirms availability rules, or the requester stops relying on ambiguous statements. **Ψ (Self-binding)** would require a role-position to bind itself across time to that clarified arrangement. No such self-binding is established by the vignette alone.

The EDEN overlay is relevant only lightly. A false comparison risk could emerge if the discussion becomes “who was more wrong” rather than how reliance should be coordinated. Pseudo-symmetry could appear if both parties are declared “equally responsible” while one role actually bore the meeting consequence. A weak parity-to-exit risk would arise only if equality-language were used to avoid a differentiated repair burden after the expectation had been clarified. But those are risks, not findings. The example is too small and too under-specified for NRK, PFO, permanentized exception, humiliation, devaluation, or reciprocity loss. EDEN therefore functions here mainly as a boundary lens: it prevents the missed reply from becoming either a moral verdict or a premature symmetry claim.

MIP would not score this example from the vignette alone. A-score, M-score, and IA classification would require more data: what each role knew, what alternatives existed, what was predictable, what authority or responsibility each role had, and whether any serious integration attempt occurred. AHP would warn that a structurally elegant reading of the missed reply is not evidence that betrayal occurred.

The forbidden claims are clear: no diagnosis of avoidance, no finding of betrayal, no legal breach, no maturity ranking, no dignity score, no claim that repair succeeded, and no claim that silence necessarily means drift.

The non-capture note is equally important. The requester may have felt exposed or embarrassed in a way PMS only partially captures. The colleague may have faced overload, competing duties, or institutional constraints not visible in the scene. A language-practice reading of what “ready” meant may be more decisive than PMS.

**Article takeaway:** this example shows why Λ (Non-event / residue) is powerful but dangerous. A missing response can matter structurally, but only inside a frame of expectation, across time, under calibrated asymmetry, and with Χ (Distance) preserving reversibility.

##### Example 2 — The repair statement that does not yet repair

A small institution receives complaints that members relied on a published support process, but when several members requested help, responses were delayed or absent. After criticism, the institution releases a statement: “We recognize that communication has not always met expectations. We value every member equally and are committed to improving trust.” The statement is received with mixed reactions. Some members feel reassured. Others say the institution has acknowledged concern without explaining what happened, who can rely on what in the future, or how delayed responses will be prevented.

This example is not a finding against the institution. It does not claim deception, bad faith, legal breach, clinical harm, or institutional guilt. It is a bounded vignette designed to show the difference between repair-language and integrated repair.

At the PMS Core level, **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes support from non-support, expectation from non-response, acknowledgement from repair, reassurance from self-binding, and equality-language from actual asymmetry. **∇ (Impulse)** appears in the movement toward complaint, reassurance, renewed trust, doubt, or institutional repair. **□ (Frame)** is the institutional support frame: members relied on a process that created expectations. Without this frame, delayed response may be inconvenient but not necessarily trust-damaging.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is central. The relevant non-events are delayed or absent responses within a published support process. Yet PMS still asks: what exactly was promised? What response time was reasonable? Were the delays exceptional, systemic, or unavoidable? Which requests fell inside the process? The example does not answer these questions. It only shows why they matter.

**Α (Attractor)** may become relevant if delayed responses repeat. If the support process repeatedly produces non-response, an attractor may form around institutional unreliability or member distrust. But the vignette does not establish recurrence strongly enough to claim a stable attractor. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is possible because institutions often hold procedural power: they define channels, timelines, visibility, and review. Members may bear uncertainty while the institution controls information. Still, Ω must be mapped rather than presumed.

**Θ (Temporality)** matters because trust in a support process is temporal. A single delay, a repeated pattern, and a long-standing failure have different structures. Repair also unfolds over time; it cannot be completed by one statement. **Φ (Recontextualization)** appears in the institution’s recontextualization: “communication has not always met expectations.” This may open repair, but it may also soften or blur the specific Λ. PMS asks whether the statement preserves the damaged expectation or dissolves it into general concern.

**Χ (Distance)** requires the analysis not to decide too quickly. The statement might be an honest first step. It might be inadequate but not deceptive. It might be constrained by privacy, procedure, or incomplete review. Distance prevents the article from converting dissatisfaction into verdict.

The crucial issue is **Σ (Integration)**. The statement sounds coherent, but integration is not yet shown. A repair that genuinely carries Σ would have to coordinate the frame, the non-events, the asymmetry, and future practice. For example, the institution might specify response windows, review backlogs, name escalation routes, distinguish urgent from non-urgent requests, and create a way to audit whether the process changes. Without such coordination, the statement may remain Φ without Σ.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is also conditional. “We are committed to improving trust” is not yet self-binding. Ψ would require the institution to bind itself across time to specific, reviewable practices. The promise must become stable normativity, not only reassurance.

This is where PMS-EDEN becomes especially useful. EDEN names the risk of **Σ-simulation**: coherence-display that sounds like integration while Λ persists. The institutional statement may simulate repair if it produces reassurance without interface change. EDEN also identifies **pseudo-symmetry** risk in the phrase “we value every member equally.” Equality-language may be appropriate, but it can become pseudo-symmetrical if it hides real asymmetry between the institution that controls the support process and members who depend on it.

A **false comparison pivot** could also occur if the discussion becomes about whether members are too demanding or whether the institution is being unfairly criticized, instead of how reliance should be coordinated. **Responsibility-to-authority** could occur if the institution uses its responsibility for repair to control the meaning, timing, or legitimacy of member complaints. These are risks only. The vignette does not establish them.

MIP would treat this as a strong candidate for later role-based analysis but would still not assign scores from the vignette alone. A-score might be document-level high because many distinctions are visible, but no enactment score follows. M-score would require knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and serious integration attempt data. IA-box classification would require clarity about the institutional asymmetry: was it transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible? D would be relevant as a protection guardrail because public institutional statements can affect member standing, voice, and exposure. AHP would flag a likely attack surface: formal repair-language may be over-read as actual repair.

Forbidden claims include: the institution betrayed members; the statement is manipulation; the members are immature; the process is legally defective; repair succeeded; reciprocity is lost; pseudo-symmetry is active; PFO applies. None follows from the vignette alone.

The non-capture note matters. Institutional constraints may be real. Privacy rules may prevent full explanation. Resource scarcity may explain delay. Legal duties may determine what can be said. Members’ lived experience of abandonment may also exceed the structural account. PMS and EDEN do not exhaust the case.

**Article takeaway:** repair-language is not repair. A statement can begin Φ (Recontextualization), but Σ (Integration) requires coordinated practice, and Ψ (Self-binding) requires durable self-binding across time. EDEN helps name the risk that apparent repair becomes appearance-management, but it must not turn that risk into a finding.

##### Example 3 — The refusal that must not be forced into repair

Two former collaborators are invited to “repair the relationship” after a conflict. One collaborator says: “I understand why repair is being requested, but I do not want to resume the collaboration. I am willing to clarify practical handover issues, but I do not want reconciliation.” The other collaborator experiences this as withdrawal and says the refusal proves that trust has been abandoned rather than repaired.

This vignette is intentionally high-risk because repair language can become coercive. It shows why AXIOM must preserve non-capture and why non-repair is not automatically failure. The example does not decide whether the refusal is justified, whether the collaboration should resume, or whether either collaborator acted well. It only marks how a refusal can be structurally legible without being converted into immaturity or drift.

At the PMS Core level, **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes repair from reconciliation, practical handover from restored relationship, refusal from betrayal, withdrawal from boundary-setting, and non-repair from failed maturity. These distinctions are essential. If “repair” is treated as identical with renewed closeness, the refusing role is already framed negatively. PMS prevents that collapse.

**∇ (Impulse)** appears in competing directions: one role moves toward relational repair; the other moves toward bounded closure without renewed collaboration. This does not reveal inner motive. The refusal might express protection, fatigue, principled boundary, lack of trust, strategic avoidance, or something else. PMS does not diagnose.

**□ (Frame)** is decisive. What is the frame of the requested repair? Is it interpersonal reconciliation, professional handover, institutional mediation, reputational restoration, apology, future collaboration, or practical closure? The refusing collaborator accepts one frame — handover clarification — but rejects another — reconciliation. This is not automatically incoherent. It may be a precise frame boundary.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** appears as non-reconciliation. The desired renewed relation does not occur. But this non-event is not automatically a breach. If no role is obligated to reconcile, the absence of reconciliation may be structurally valid. The example therefore protects the distinction between missing repair and refused reconciliation.

**Α (Attractor)** is not established. A single refusal does not prove a pattern of avoidance. **Ω (Asymmetry)** may be present if one collaborator has more reputational power, institutional backing, public voice, or dependency exposure. But the vignette does not specify this. The refusal may protect a vulnerable role; it may also burden another role. Ω must be mapped.

**Θ (Temporality)** matters because refusal after repeated failed repair attempts differs from refusal immediately after a minor misunderstanding. The vignette supplies no history. Therefore temporal interpretation must remain open.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is present in the refusing collaborator’s recontextualization: repair is separated from reconciliation. This may clarify the frame. It may also be contested. PMS asks whether the recontextualization preserves the relevant obligations or erases them. It does not decide in advance.

**Χ (Distance)** is crucial. Without distance, the analyst might say: refusal is immature, or refusal is courageous, or the repair-seeking party is coercive. All three could be overclaims. Χ keeps the reading reversible.

**Σ (Integration)** remains conditional. Practical handover may become a limited integration: the collaborators coordinate remaining obligations without restoring the relationship. That may be a valid Σ (Integration) at the practical frame while no Σ (Integration) exists at the relational-reconciliation frame. This is a powerful point for the article: integration is frame-specific. **Ψ (Self-binding)** would require role-specific self-binding, perhaps to complete the handover cleanly or to respect the boundary. It would not require renewed trust unless the frame warrants it.

PMS-EDEN adds important guardrails. The case could drift into **responsibility-to-authority** if the repair-seeking side treats the need for repair as authority to define what the refusing side must do. It could drift into **parity-to-exit** if the refusing side uses equality-language to avoid genuine remaining obligations. It could drift into **pseudo-symmetry** if both sides are declared equally positioned while one bears greater exposure. But none of these is established. EDEN’s most important contribution here is not to classify drift, but to prevent repair from becoming a hidden command.

MIP would not assign A-score, M-score, or IA classification. The refusal might be mature, immature, protective, avoidant, necessary, or insufficient depending on what was known, what options existed, what consequences were predictable, and what obligations remained. D is relevant because forced reconciliation can violate dignity-in-practice, while refusal can also be delivered in humiliating or devaluing ways. No D score follows. AHP would warn that an example like this is excerpt-sensitive: readers may import their own moral preference for reconciliation or boundary-setting.

Forbidden claims are especially important: the refusing collaborator is not diagnosed as avoidant; the repair-seeking collaborator is not labeled coercive; non-reconciliation is not low integration; refusal is not automatically dignity-protecting; renewed collaboration is not automatically mature; no legal or ethical duty is established.

The non-capture note is central. There may be emotional, historical, cultural, legal, institutional, or safety reasons for refusal that PMS cannot infer. There may also be obligations that the refusal does not satisfy. A relational-ethical framework may ask what acknowledgement is still owed. A legal framework may ask what duties survive. A phenomenological account may ask how the refusal is lived. PMS does not absorb these alternatives.

**Article takeaway:** repair is not always reconciliation. A bounded refusal can be structurally legible without being maturity failure, drift, or betrayal. The analysis must preserve the possibility that non-repair is the responsible outcome, while still asking whether remaining obligations are integrated and self-bound.

Repair Relationships shows that the demand for repair must itself be mapped. The case does not ask AXIOM to decide whether a relationship should be restored. It asks how trust, absence, vulnerability, repair-language, refusal, and responsibility become structurally legible without becoming verdicts.

The three examples demonstrate the same point at scene level: missing response is not automatically betrayal; repair-language is not repair; refusal is not automatically immaturity.

The methodological takeaway is simple but demanding: trust-repair analysis becomes useful only when it preserves the Λ-residue it is tempted to close. Repair may be possible, but not presumed. Betrayal may be real, but not inferred from absence alone. Reconciliation may be desirable, but not mandated. Refusal may be protective, but not automatically vindicated. ### 9.5 Summary

Part VI establishes EDEN as the PMS add-on layer for relational regimes. The cases in this part do not treat relationships as inner states, moral qualities, diagnoses, or symmetry disputes. They treat relational scenes as structural coordination problems: differences must be framed, asymmetries must be carried rather than denied, residues must remain visible, and coordination must be borne by integration (Σ) and durable self-binding (Ψ) rather than by comparison, exposure, apology-language, or parity surfaces.

The common result is not that PMS-EDEN decides who is right, who is mature, who failed, or which relationship should be repaired. The result is that PMS-EDEN makes visible where relational language becomes structurally risky: equality-language may conceal unequal cost; recognition may become enforcement; trust may turn absence into betrayal too quickly; boundary material may shift from repair into public tribunal; and repair-language may become pressure to resume, forgive, or reconcile before Σ and Ψ are actually available.

EDEN therefore does not replace PMS core. PMS remains the operator grammar. EDEN adds overlay handles for relational regime risks: pseudo-symmetry, recognition threshold, trust breach pressure, boundary erosion, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, Σ-simulation, Ψ-externalization, reciprocity loss, publicness transfer, and repair-as-narrative substitution. These handles are not person labels, relationship diagnoses, maturity scores, or verdict categories.

#### 9.5.1 Baseline result of Part VI

The baseline established by Part VI is that relational viability depends on carrier structure, not on relational vocabulary.

Across the cases, PMS core makes the relational closure-demand visible, while EDEN sharpens the regime discipline. Pseudo-Symmetry shows that parity-language is not enough where cost, exposure, voice, or obligation remain uneven. Recognition shows that being heard, noticed, received, or named is not yet integration or self-binding. Trust shows that absence, delay, doubt, and repair must be read through frame, expectation, temporality, and asymmetry before any breach claim is possible. Boundary Erosion shows that privacy-to-publicness transfer is a frame shift, not automatically accountability or betrayal. Repair Relationships shows that repair is not apology, explanation, reconciliation, or return-to-normal; repair requires coordinated practice and durable follow-up, and refusal may remain structurally valid.

The strongest shared discipline is:

**named Ω + preserved Χ + tested Σ + non-coercive Ψ**

Asymmetry must be named rather than hidden by parity language. Distance must remain available rather than priced as betrayal, avoidance, or immaturity. Integration must be tested at the interface, not simulated by narrative coherence. Self-binding must be owned by the relevant role or actor, not externalized as a demand that the other trust, forgive, resume, disclose, or repair.

#### 9.5.2 Cluster-level result

The Part VI cases form three EDEN-facing clusters.

**Pseudo-symmetry and recognition** show how relational closure often begins with surface equality or acknowledgment. Equal voice, shared responsibility, equal access, being heard, receiving a reply, or seeing a concern named may all matter. But none of them proves reciprocal coordination. The key question is whether the relevant frame carries real asymmetry, expected acknowledgment, follow-up, and differentiated responsibility — or whether parity and recognition language close the problem too early.

**Trust and repair** show how relational continuity depends on calibrated absence. A missing reply, a delayed response, a failed deadline, an apology, a repair offer, or a refusal does not interpret itself. Trust repair requires a declared frame, an expected event, temporal placement, asymmetry mapping, and distance before breach, repair, or renewed reliance can be responsibly assessed. Repair may be possible, but not presumed. Reconciliation may be desirable in some frames, but it is never structurally mandated by the word “repair.”

**Boundary erosion and publicness** show how relational material changes when it travels. Confidential feedback, repair notes, internal complaints, apologies, mediation outcomes, or accountability language can become different artefacts once moved into publicness, institutional record, or tribunal-like frames. Publicness may reveal failed private repair, but it may also produce exposure, comparison, reputational hardening, and context collapse. Confidentiality may protect repair, but it may also conceal non-response. EDEN keeps both risks visible without deciding them by label.

#### 9.5.3 Cross-case dependency discipline

The recurring failure mode in Part VI is:

**relationship language → forced closure**

This occurs when relational terms are used to bind before the carrier structure is present. Once that shortcut appears, the dependency chain usually breaks: Ω is denied or moralized, Λ is converted into accusation or leverage, Χ is priced, Σ is simulated by explanation or performance, and Ψ is externalized onto the other side as obligation.

In Part VI this shortcut appears in several forms:

- parity language becomes proof of reciprocity;
- shared responsibility hides unequal authority or exposure;
- being heard becomes acknowledgment;
- acknowledgment becomes repair;
- silence becomes betrayal;
- apology becomes integration;
- “we take responsibility” becomes self-binding;
- publicness becomes accountability;
- confidentiality becomes repair;
- refusal becomes immaturity;
- repair becomes reconciliation mandate.

Part VI blocks these shortcuts without denying relational seriousness. Recognition can matter. Trust can be damaged. Repair can be needed. Publicness can be justified. Refusal can be protective. Asymmetry can be real. But none of these may be converted into a verdict without the frame, expected event, cost topology, temporal sequence, integration path, and self-binding structure needed to support it.

#### 9.5.4 What Part VI carries forward

Part VI carries forward the relational lesson of AXIOM: reciprocity is not symmetry. Reciprocity is coordinated asymmetry under a frame that can carry difference, exposure, residue, revision, and follow-up without turning them into domination, denial, comparison, or coercive repair.

EDEN is justified because PMS core can map relational closure-demands, but the overlay makes EDEN-specific regime risks sharper: when equality becomes pseudo-symmetry, when recognition becomes enforcement, when trust becomes verdictive, when publicness becomes tribunal, when repair becomes performance, and when responsibility becomes authority over the other.

MIP is not part of the default Part VI stack. It becomes relevant only downstream where relational artefacts enter institutionalization, publicness, HR, education, therapy-like contexts, formal complaints, reputational exposure, binding recommendations, or other dignity-risk zones. AHP likewise remains analysis-artifact hardening only. Neither MIP nor AHP scores persons, diagnoses relationships, ranks maturity, activates D as a finding, or upgrades a relational reading into authority.

Part VI closes with the EDEN rule for AXIOM: where relational coordination is possible, test whether Σ and Ψ actually carry it; where asymmetry is real, do not hide it behind parity; where repair is claimed, do not confuse language with restoration; where refusal appears, do not force it into failure; and where relational material becomes public, keep the boundary, exposure, and misuse risks attached.
