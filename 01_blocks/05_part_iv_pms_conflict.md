# PMS-AXIOM: PART IV - CONFLICT

## 7. CONFLICT-Overlay: Conflict as Stabilized Incompatibility (Without “Solutions”)

Part IV introduces the CONFLICT overlay as the PMS add-on layer for structurally stabilized incompatibility. The cases in this part remain grounded in PMS core, but they require an additional lens because their central closure-demands concern conflict, repair limits, tragic structure, forced choice, asymmetry lock-in, and situations where integration (Σ) cannot simply be assumed as the proper or available solution.

The purpose of this part is not to offer mediation techniques, decision advice, institutional remedies, moral verdicts, or conflict-resolution doctrine. It is to show how conflict becomes structurally legible when differentiated trajectories remain locally coherent inside their own frames (□), but cannot be jointly integrated at acceptable cost under temporality (Θ), binding persistence (Ψ), asymmetry (Ω), and constrained or priced distance (Χ).

The CONFLICT overlay is therefore a non-mixing disciplinary lens. It does not redefine PMS operators. It does not add a new base grammar. It supplies reduced signatures, threshold distinctions, drift catalogues, publicness/misuse gradients, and finite-output forms that make conflict-specific risks visible: Σ-incompatibility clash, Ψ-collision, Ω-driven lock-in, Φ-driven exposure capture, repair-talk drift, D-collapse, and the conversion of conflict language into authority, blame, or governance permission.

PMS remains the canonical operator grammar. CONFLICT only sharpens the reading where the case-specific issue is not merely disagreement or difference, but stabilized incompatibility: the point at which local coherences, durable bindings, temporal hardening, exposure gradients, and reduced interruptibility make “integration” itself contested, unavailable, coercive, or tragic.

The cases addressed in Part IV are:

Together, these cases show how conflict differs from ordinary difference, disagreement, plurality, or uncertainty. They are not grouped here because CONFLICT “solves” them. They are grouped here because each case contains a closure-demand that asks for settlement, decision, assignment, repair, sacrifice, or termination under conditions where shared integration may be structurally blocked or ethically dangerous to force.

The cases cluster into several CONFLICT-relevant families:

- **Structural incompatibility and escalation/repair** show how locally coherent trajectories can fail to consolidate into shared integration without thereby becoming defects, diagnoses, or maturity failures.
- **Trolley dilemmas, tragedy, and dirty hands** show how forced-choice structures can make loss or residue visible without converting tragic structure into moral permission or verdict authority.
- **Structural injustice and asymmetry lock-in** show how exposure, capacity, publicness, and exit costs can harden conflict trajectories under Ω and Θ.
- **Repair-limit cases** show why repair language must be guarded: “repair” can become a coercive fantasy, an exposure vector, or a laundering device if Χ is unavailable or asymmetric.

Part IV therefore demonstrates a central AXIOM discipline: not every visible incompatibility should be narrated as solvable, reparable, integrable, or assignable. Where the frame cannot support shared closure, CONFLICT makes the endpoint explicit rather than hiding coercion inside solution language.

### 7.2 Output Contract for Part IV

The admissible outputs in Part IV are PMS + CONFLICT artefacts. A case may yield:

- **Tragedy formalism:** a structured, non-prescriptive artefact that makes non-integrability, forced loss, binding collision, temporal trap, or terminal residue legible without converting it into a solution mandate.
- **Repair policy:** a boundary artefact specifying when repair talk is structurally valid, when it becomes coercive, and where interruption, exit, non-continuation, or refusal must remain available without shaming.
- **Conflict taxonomy:** a classification of conflict types, including weak conflict-legibility, local/shared Σ tension, binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, and terminal conflict thresholds.
- **Structural reading:** a dependency-sensitive PMS rendering, with CONFLICT reduced signatures used only as overlay handles, not as PMS operators.
- **Transmission guardrail:** a boundary statement for cases where conflict language could be reused as justification, reputational assignment, institutional pressure, governance authority, or person-near evaluation.

The CONFLICT overlay may identify drift risks, but drift risk is not drift finding. A case may show that Σ-clash, Ψ-collision, Ω-lock-in, Φ exposure capture, D-collapse, or repair-talk drift is structurally plausible without asserting that any concrete person, institution, group, or practice has committed that drift.

MIP is not a person-evaluation layer in this part. It becomes relevant as a downstream governance and transmission guardrail where conflict artefacts are public, institutional, decision-facing, reputationally sensitive, or otherwise likely to acquire coercive force. In those cases, MIP evaluates the analysis artefact and its use conditions, not persons, teams, parties, or maturity as such. AHP may harden the analysis artefact where precision, attack surfaces, excerptability, publicness, or claim-boundary risks require it. AHP does not rescore, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or create new case findings.

### 7.4 Cases

#### 7.4.1 Conflict as structural incompatibility

##### Why this case is here

This case examines conflict as a philosophical structure rather than as a concrete dispute, diagnosis, institutional failure, or practical decision problem. Its central closure demand is this: when two practical, conceptual, or institutional trajectories remain locally coherent within their own frames but become incompatible when carried together, should that incompatibility be treated as failed integration, or can it remain visible without being converted into defect?

The case belongs here because conflict often attracts premature closure. If two structures do not fit, one is easily treated as irrational, immature, incoherent, excessive, under-integrated, or obstructive. This case resists that conversion. It asks whether structural incompatibility can be mapped as a relation between frames and trajectories while leaving open that some incompatibilities may be genuine, tragic, semantic, institutional, material, political, or otherwise not fully captured by PMS.

The case is a conceptual projection, not a concrete scene. No actor, institution, event sequence, public record, legal setting, clinical context, or implementation environment is supplied. This has a direct consequence for the analysis: responsibility, action, asymmetry, and self-binding can be structurally rendered, but they cannot become scene-specific findings without additional evidence.

The case is mapped rather than solved. PMS Core renders the basic structure: locally coherent trajectories can become incompatible across frames. PMS-CONFLICT sharpens the distinction between weak conflict-legibility and terminal conflict classification. MIP contributes only the case-specific guardrail result: no A/M score is assigned, IA is not a case-content verdict, and D remains inactive as case content. AHP hardens the analysis artefact against misuse, especially against the risk that a polished structural reading becomes too authoritative when excerpted or transmitted.

##### Structural reading

The case-specific PMS movement begins with the canonical operator path: Δ (Difference), ∇ (Impulse), □ (Frame), Λ (Non-event / residue), Α (Attractor), Ω (Asymmetry), Θ (Temporality), Φ (Recontextualization), Χ (Distance), Σ (Integration), and Ψ (Self-binding). The order matters because later operators cannot be used cheaply. In this case, the early operators make the structure legible, while several later operators remain conditional.

Δ (Difference) is active because the case requires distinguishable trajectories: different practical commitments, conceptual schemes, institutional mandates, rule systems, or interpretive logics. But difference alone is not yet conflict. The analysis must not treat distinction as opposition. A conflict reading only begins when differentiated trajectories acquire direction, frame, temporal persistence, and cross-frame incompatibility.

∇ (Impulse) names the directional pressure of each trajectory. A practical trajectory may press toward enactment; a conceptual trajectory toward explanatory consistency; an institutional trajectory toward rule-following, procedure, or mandate preservation. This is structural directedness, not psychology. The case does not infer desire, intention, affect, motivation, or pathology from the presence of trajectory.

□ (Frame) is central. The case explicitly depends on local coherence: each trajectory remains coherent within its own frame. A frame supplies relevance conditions, admissible distinctions, standards of continuity, and criteria for what counts as repair, failure, preservation, or violation. A conceptual frame may preserve inferential consistency; an institutional frame may preserve mandate coherence; a practical frame may preserve role continuity. The problem arises when these local coherences cannot simply be merged.

Λ (Non-event / residue) remains conditional. It would become active if an expected mediation, translation, recognition, convergence, or shared rule failed to occur. But incompatibility alone does not prove a non-event. A missing integration may be a true residue inside a frame, or it may mark a boundary where no shared expectation was warranted. This distinction prevents every unresolved conflict from being redescribed as failed coordination.

Α (Attractor) is plausible but under-specified. Trajectories often stabilize over time, especially in institutions, theories, and practices. Yet the case material does not show recurrence, path dependence, repeated interaction, or sedimented patterning. Attractor language can be used as a possible structural handle, but not as a finding of stabilized conflict.

Ω (Asymmetry) remains weak. Many real conflicts involve unequal power, exposure, capacity, vulnerability, obligation, or exit cost. This case does not specify any such distribution. It would therefore be overreach to infer asymmetry from conflict itself. A structural incompatibility may become asymmetrical in a concrete scene, but this abstract case does not yet establish that.

Θ (Temporality) is active in the limited sense that the case speaks of trajectories rather than static propositions. Conflict appears as something carried over time: a frame persists, a practice continues, an institutional logic accumulates, or a conceptual scheme develops. Still, no temporal scale is supplied. There is no evidence of lock-in rate, compressed history, accumulated cost, closing reversibility window, or recurrence band. Temporality supports trajectory analysis, not temporal classification.

Φ (Recontextualization) is one of the most important operators in the case. A trajectory that is coherent in one frame can become unstable, incompatible, or newly visible when placed in another frame. Recontextualization does not automatically repair conflict. It may clarify incompatibility, reveal a translation problem, expose a false synthesis, or show that the receiving frame cannot absorb the original structure without distortion. The case therefore treats recontextualization as a cross-frame test, not as a solution device.

Χ (Distance) is the main application discipline. Because conflict invites adjudication, distance prevents the analysis from becoming a tribunal. It suspends the urge to decide which trajectory is more mature, more coherent, more rational, or more PMS-compatible. Distance does not mean indifference or avoidance. It means that the structural reading remains reversible and does not convert incompatibility into verdict.

Σ (Integration) is weak and contested. The case is precisely about whether local coherences can or should become shared coherence. Integration may be possible, but it is not guaranteed. It may be partial, false, coercive, unstable, or unavailable. PMS coherence is therefore not enough. A smooth PMS rendering does not prove that the conflict has been adequately integrated, or that integration is the right standard.

Ψ (Self-binding) remains conditional as case content. It would become relevant if a trajectory were bound into a durable commitment, institutional self-relation, role obligation, policy constraint, or stable normative identity. The abstract case does not supply that. Self-binding is most securely present as a discipline on the analysis itself: the analysis must bind itself to its own guardrails and not use structural elegance to force closure.

The derived structures follow the same restraint. Awareness_A is active in a structural, non-phenomenological sense: the case sustains framed distinctions across time. Coherence_C is locally visible but not globally established: each trajectory may be coherent within its own frame while shared coherence remains unresolved. Responsibility_R is not established because the required configuration of Ψ (Self-binding), Φ (Recontextualization), Θ (Temporality), and Ω (Asymmetry) is not supplied. Action_E is under-specified because no concrete enactment or implementation path appears. Dignity_in_Practice_D remains an application constraint, not a case-content dignity profile. SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only: the case does not model a subject, person, institution, or role binding itself into stable self-relation.

The PMS drift-pattern status is equally restrained. AD_A>>E is not established: there is no concrete pattern in which awareness expands while enactment is suppressed. AD_Sigma_low is a possible but weak candidate: the case concerns local coherence without shared integration, but this does not prove under-integration. It may instead indicate genuine non-integrability, semantic incommensurability, institutional rule collision, material scarcity, or tragic pluralism. Risk remains risk; non-assignment is not a low score.

PMS-CONFLICT sharpens the case without escalating it. PMS Core makes structural incompatibility legible; PMS-CONFLICT distinguishes weak conflict-legibility from terminal conflict classification. In the overlay’s terms, the case most strongly resembles a local/shared Σ tension: locally coherent trajectories may fail to consolidate into shared integration. But the full CONFLICT terminal regime is not reached. Ψ is not established; Ω is not established; Χ is not shown to be priced or asymmetric; exposure capture is absent; terminal exhaustion of Σ is not shown.

The strongest CONFLICT-relevant drift risk is Σ-incompatibility clash, but even that remains conditional. The case shows something like Σ_local without established Σ_shared. It does not show failed shared integration under Ψ-binding, Ω-asymmetry, priced Χ-distance, recurrence, unacceptable integration cost, or terminal load. PMS-CONFLICT therefore strengthens discrimination rather than classification. It prevents the case from being read too weakly as mere disagreement, but also prevents it from being read too strongly as terminal conflict.

MIP adds a case-specific refusal rather than a new finding. Because the case lacks concrete actors, roles, decision sequences, alternatives, predictability, and dignity-in-practice enactments, A-score and M-score are not assigned. IA is not a case-content verdict; it is relevant only as an analysis-transmission check. D remains inactive as case content. This refusal is not a failure of MIP. It is the MIP result: the case remains a structural working note, not a maturity profile.

AHP hardens the MIP output rather than changing the case. Its main finding is that the analysis artefact is comparatively precise, but vulnerable to overreading if excerpted. Full-template density can simulate empirical substance; D-language can be mistaken for a hidden dignity profile; IA language can be mistaken for a case-content asymmetry finding; hypothetical M-ceiling language can be mistaken for a hidden score. These are artefact attack surfaces, not case findings.

##### What the analysis shows

The central structural result is that conflict as structural incompatibility may be rendered as differentiated, directionally organized, frame-bound trajectories that remain locally coherent while becoming incompatible under temporal extension, cross-frame comparison, coordination pressure, or attempted recontextualization.

PMS Core shows the local/global coherence split. PMS-CONFLICT shows why that split is conflict-legible without being terminal. MIP shows why no maturity score, responsibility score, IA verdict, or D-profile follows from the abstract case. AHP shows that the analysis text itself must be guarded against over-authoritative transmission.

The analysis allows a bounded form of closure: the case can be read as a local/shared integration problem. It does not allow stronger closure: no terminal CONFLICT classification, no drift finding, no diagnosis, no responsibility verdict, no person or institution ranking, no legal/forensic result, no implementation claim, and no validation of PMS or the overlay.

Λ-residue remains visible in a limited way. There may be absent mediation, failed translation, missing convergence, or non-occurring coordination, but the case does not specify such a non-event. More broadly, non-capture remains visible wherever the incompatibility is better read as logical contradiction, semantic incommensurability, institutional rule collision, material scarcity, systems differentiation, political antagonism, or tragic pluralism. Those rival readings are not defects in the PMS analysis; they are part of its boundary.

The reading would weaken if no distinct frames or trajectories can be specified, if the conflict is only a static contradiction, if no directional pressure or temporal extension is present, if asymmetry is inferred merely from incompatibility, or if integration is treated as the expected solution without evidence that shared integration is available, non-coercive, and structurally adequate. It would also weaken if recontextualization became an assimilation device that absorbs every rival framing into PMS rather than preserving possible non-capture.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk in this case is escalation by vocabulary. Because the word “conflict” already carries urgency, the analysis could easily be overread as diagnosing a terminal state, assigning responsibility, or identifying failed maturity. PMS-CONFLICT is added precisely to prevent that escalation. It shows that weak conflict-legibility is not terminal conflict.

A second case-specific boundary concerns Σ (Integration). The case invites the thought that conflict must be solved by integration. Here, Σ (Integration) must remain a question, not a mandate. Integration may be possible, partial, false, coercive, or unavailable. Non-integration is not automatically failure.

A third boundary concerns the artefact itself. The final example shows that a careful structural note can become part of the conflict structure if excerpted without its boundaries. This is why MIP and AHP matter here: not because they produce stronger case findings, but because they keep the analysis from becoming an unauthorized closure device.

##### Example 1 — Local mandates, shared impossibility

A public archive and a privacy office share responsibility for a collection of historically important records. The archive’s mandate is locally coherent: preserve access, maintain continuity of public memory, and prevent institutional history from disappearing. Its procedures favor stable cataloguing, durable references, and long-term discoverability. Within that frame, removing or heavily obscuring records can look like damage to the archive’s purpose.

The privacy office’s mandate is also locally coherent: reduce exposure, prevent unnecessary circulation of sensitive personal data, and make sure that records do not continue to harm people merely because they were once collected. Its procedures favor minimization, restricted access, and periodic review. Within that frame, unrestricted preservation can look like an avoidable continuation of exposure.

The conflict appears when the same record must be handled under both mandates. The archive is not simply “careless,” and the privacy office is not simply “censorious.” Each trajectory is coherent inside its own frame. The incompatibility arises when both are asked to generate a single shared handling rule: preserve the record fully enough for historical continuity, yet restrict it fully enough to prevent renewed exposure.

What this example demonstrates is the core distinction between local coherence and shared integration. The archive trajectory and the privacy trajectory are not incoherent merely because they collide. Their conflict is not automatically a maturity failure, communication failure, or lack of goodwill. It is a structural incompatibility produced by different frames carrying different protected constraints.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ (Difference)** appears in the distinction between preservation and minimization. **∇ (Impulse)** appears in the directional pressure of each mandate: one moves toward access and continuity, the other toward restriction and exposure reduction. **□ (Frame)** is central because each mandate is locally framed. **Θ (Temporality)** appears because the record is not a one-time object; it persists across time and continues to generate access, memory, exposure, and revision problems. **Φ (Recontextualization)** becomes relevant when the same record is moved from the archive frame into the privacy frame, or back again. The meaning of “responsible handling” changes with the frame. **Χ (Distance)** is required so the analysis does not prematurely declare one mandate the mature one and the other the obstacle. **Σ (Integration)** remains contested: a shared policy may be possible, but it may also be partial, unstable, or costly. **Ψ (Self-binding)** remains conditional unless a durable institutional commitment is specified.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay sharpens the example without escalating it. The case is conflict-legible because it shows local/shared **Σ** tension: each office may have a locally integrated practice, while shared integration remains unresolved. But the example does not yet establish terminal CONFLICT. It does not show that **Χ** is priced or asymmetric, that binding collision is irreversible, that exposure capture is active, or that **Σ** has terminally failed. The right overlay status is therefore weak CONFLICT projection, not terminal conflict classification.

MIP and AHP would remain mostly guardrail-level here unless the vignette were developed into an applied institutional case. No A-score, M-score, IA verdict, or D-profile should be assigned from this example. A concrete analysis would need actual roles, authority structures, publicness, affected perspectives, records of decision, review channels, and evidence of exposure or harm. AHP would be relevant only if the written analysis were to be transmitted, because terms like “exposure,” “restriction,” or “public memory” could harden into blame or institutional positioning.

The forbidden claims are important. The example does not prove that archival access should win. It does not prove that privacy should win. It does not diagnose either office. It does not establish legal liability, moral guilt, institutional bad faith, or empirical harm. It does not validate PMS or PMS-CONFLICT. It also does not show that every such conflict can be repaired by better framing.

The non-capture note is equally important. A legal analysis might be more appropriate if the question concerns statutory obligations. A political theory reading might better capture public memory and historical accountability. An ethics of care reading might foreground affected persons in ways PMS should not absorb. The PMS analysis preserves these rival readings rather than treating them as defects.

The article takeaway is that structural conflict can exist without local incoherence. PMS makes the frame relation visible; PMS-CONFLICT clarifies why the case is conflict-legible but not automatically terminal. The example prepares the reader to see conflict as a disciplined boundary problem, not as an immediate verdict about which trajectory is wrong.

##### Example 2 — The interface that cannot satisfy both clients

A software team maintains a shared interface used by two internal systems. The first system needs stable field names because it archives transactions over long periods. Its local logic values backward compatibility: if a field name changes, old records become harder to interpret, audit, or compare. From that frame, interface stability is not conservatism; it is the condition for continuity.

The second system needs the same interface to reflect a new classification scheme. Its local logic values semantic accuracy: if the interface continues using outdated field names, new outputs will be misleading or require translation layers that introduce errors. From that frame, changing the field names is not disruption; it is the condition for correctness.

Both systems are locally coherent. The archive system is not irrational for resisting change. The classification system is not reckless for demanding it. The conflict emerges because one shared interface is expected to carry two different continuities: historical stability and updated semantic fit. If the interface remains unchanged, the second system carries misfit. If it changes, the first system carries migration cost and possible audit discontinuity. The practical object is singular; the frames are not.

This example demonstrates why recontextualization is not repair by default. When the interface is viewed from the archive frame, “same field name” means continuity. When it is viewed from the classification frame, “same field name” may mean semantic drift. The same structure changes meaning when placed in a different frame. **Φ (Recontextualization)** therefore makes the conflict visible, but it does not automatically solve it.

The PMS Core hooks are especially clear. **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes two requirements: stability and semantic revision. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as directional pressure in both systems: preserve continuity versus update classification. **□ (Frame)** defines what counts as acceptable change in each system. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may become active if an expected translation layer, migration path, or shared naming convention fails to appear, but it should not be assumed merely because the systems disagree. **Α (Attractor)** may become relevant if interface conventions have stabilized through repeated use. **Ω (Asymmetry)** remains conditional: if one team can force changes while another bears the migration burden, asymmetry becomes active; if no such distribution is given, it must remain weak. **Θ (Temporality)** is central because both requirements unfold across time: archives depend on long-term persistence, while classification schemes evolve. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is active because the same interface is re-read under different frames. **Χ (Distance)** is required so the analyst does not declare one system more mature simply because it appears more modern or more stable. **Σ (Integration)** is the contested operator: a versioned interface, aliasing scheme, or migration layer may integrate the requirements, but that must be shown. **Ψ (Self-binding)** becomes relevant only if the organization binds itself to a durable interface policy.

The CONFLICT overlay adds threshold discipline. The example may show a local/shared **Σ** tension: each system can integrate its own requirements locally, while the shared interface cannot yet integrate both. It may also become a binding collision if each system is durably committed to incompatible interface contracts. But that stronger reading requires evidence: formal commitments, non-negotiable dependencies, asymmetric migration cost, time pressure, and exhaustion of shared **Σ**. Without those, the example remains weakly conflict-legible, not terminal.

MIP status remains non-scoring. It would be misuse to assign maturity to either team based on this vignette. A MIP analysis would first ask for role mapping, publicness, responsibility levers, alternatives, predictability, and real power to act. If one team had authority to impose a change while another bore unacknowledged costs, IA might become relevant. But in this bounded example, IA is not a verdict; it is only a possible future asymmetry check. AHP would be useful if a report about the teams were circulated, because “resistance to change” or “semantic correctness” could become person-near or team-near labels.

Forbidden claims must be excluded. The example does not show that backward compatibility is superior to semantic accuracy. It does not show that innovation beats stability. It does not establish negligence, incompetence, irrationality, or bad faith. It does not prescribe a software architecture. It does not validate PMS as an implementation method. It is not an engineering decision rule.

The non-capture note is straightforward. A software architecture analysis might solve the technical interface problem more directly. A cost analysis might determine migration feasibility. A governance analysis might decide authority and deadlines. PMS does not replace those. It clarifies the structural form of the incompatibility: two locally coherent trajectories become incompatible when forced through one shared object.

The article takeaway is that **Φ** can expose conflict without resolving it, and **Σ** must remain a tested possibility rather than an assumed obligation. The example makes visible why PMS must not become a closure machine: sometimes the responsible result is not “integrate now,” but “name the frame collision, preserve non-capture, and specify what additional evidence would be needed before integration or binding is claimed.”

##### Example 3 — The report that becomes too authoritative

An internal analyst writes a careful structural note about a recurring coordination problem between two departments. The note says that Department A and Department B operate under different frames: one is optimized for speed of response, the other for verification and documentation. The note explicitly states that no person is being evaluated, no diagnosis is made, no legal conclusion follows, and no maturity score is assigned. It uses PMS Core to distinguish local coherence from shared integration, and it uses the CONFLICT overlay to mark only a weak local/shared **Σ (Integration)** tension.

Inside the analyst’s private working context, the note is disciplined. It remains reversible, bounded, and non-personal. It does not say that either department is immature. It does not claim terminal conflict. It does not prescribe intervention. It keeps non-capture open: perhaps the problem is resource scarcity, perhaps incompatible incentives, perhaps unclear governance, perhaps a technical workflow issue.

The risk appears when a short excerpt from the note is copied into a management slide: “Department A and Department B show structural incompatibility and weak conflict projection.” The slide omits the boundary language. It omits that PMS-CONFLICT did not find terminal conflict. It omits that MIP did not assign A or M scores. It omits that D was inactive. The phrase “structural incompatibility” now begins to function as an organizational label. A bounded analysis artefact starts to look like an authoritative classification of teams.

This example demonstrates why the MIP and AHP layers matter. The original structural analysis may be correct within its scope, but transmission changes the situation. A phrase that was safe inside a private working note can become risky when excerpted into a public, semi-public, or institutional decision environment. The danger is not that PMS Core was wrong. The danger is that the artefact’s boundary conditions are removed.

The PMS Core hooks are indirect but important. **Χ (Distance)** is the central operator: distance is preserved in the original note but weakened by excerpting. **□ (Frame)** shifts: the text moves from analysis frame to management frame. **Φ (Recontextualization)** occurs as recontextualization of the report itself. **Ω (Asymmetry)** may become active if management has authority over the departments and the excerpt affects reputation, resources, or evaluation. **Θ (Temporality)** matters because a label inserted into slides or records can persist. **Ψ (Self-binding)** becomes a risk if the organization binds the phrase into policy, performance review, or official narrative. **Σ (Integration)** may be falsely assumed: a management audience may read “structural incompatibility” as if the analysis had already integrated all relevant evidence.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay hooks are transmission-related. Publicness and misuse gradient are not PMS operators, but they matter as overlay controls. Exposure capture could become relevant if the excerpt hardens interpretation into institutional pressure. Dignity collapse could become a risk if the language shifts toward humiliation, ranking, or person-near verdict. But even here, these are risks, not findings. The example does not claim that management has acted coercively; it shows how an artefact can drift if its boundaries are stripped.

MIP status is central. The original note should remain a working note unless roles, publicness, affected perspectives, evidence, review conditions, and reversibility are checked. No A-score or M-score should be inferred. The IA-box, if used, applies to the analysis artefact and transmission conditions, not to the departments as case content. D remains guardrail-only unless a concrete dignity-in-practice scene is supplied.

AHP status is also central. This is exactly the kind of case where AHP attack points matter: density may simulate more substance than the abstract material supports; D-language can be overread; IA-box language can be mistaken for a case-content finding; conclusion language can be read as validation; and a non-operative ceiling rule can be mistaken for a hidden score. AHP does not change the underlying analysis. It hardens the artefact against foreseeable misuse.

Forbidden claims must be kept explicit. The example does not show that the departments are dysfunctional. It does not show that management is acting in bad faith. It does not establish organizational liability, clinical harm, legal risk, or empirical fact. It does not prove that PMS language is unsafe in general. It shows only that bounded analysis can become misleading when recontextualized without its safeguards.

The non-capture note is that organizational communication, management practice, labor relations, legal compliance, and workplace ethics may all be needed in a real case. PMS, CONFLICT, MIP, and AHP do not replace those domains. They help identify when a structural note is being moved into a higher-risk transmission environment.

The article takeaway is that the analysis artefact itself can become part of the conflict structure. This is why AXIOM requires claim boundaries, add-on discipline, MIP guardrails, and AHP hardening. A good structural reading is not only one that maps the case; it is one that prevents its own language from becoming an unauthorized closure device.

##### Case takeaway

This case shows how AXIOM can map conflict without making conflict into a verdict. PMS Core renders the structural movement from differentiated trajectories to frame-relative local coherence and contested shared integration. PMS-CONFLICT sharpens the threshold: the case is conflict-legible, but not terminal. MIP confirms that no score, person finding, IA verdict, or D-profile follows from the abstract material. AHP identifies where the analysis artefact itself must be hardened against overreading.

The case should be used in the chapter as a model of disciplined non-closure. It shows that a case can be structurally substantial without becoming evidentiary, diagnostic, legal, metaphysical, or prescriptive. Its central residue remains the gap between local coherence and shared integration. That gap may later be resolved, governed, litigated, redesigned, or left tragic in a concrete setting, but this case does not decide that. It keeps the incompatibility visible while refusing to turn visibility into final capture.

#### 7.4.2 Escalation vs Repair

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because it isolates a closure-demand that is easy to settle too quickly: when a decision has consequences, does the trajectory escalate, or can it still be repaired? The difficulty is that both readings are structurally dangerous if they become premature. If consequence is read as fixed escalation, repair becomes impossible or merely cosmetic. If repair is read as full reversal, consequence is erased. If interruption is read as maturity, delay or refusal may be too quickly absorbed into a repair narrative. If escalation is read as proof of irreversibility, temporality becomes destiny.

The case belongs in this chapter because it shows AXIOM’s core function: it maps a closure-pressure without resolving it by authority. The problem is not whether repair is morally good or whether escalation is bad. The problem is how consequence, interruption, revision, and repair can remain simultaneously visible without collapsing into fate, excuse, erasure, or forced coherence.

The YAML marks this case as a conceptual domain projection rather than a concrete scene. That matters. No concrete actor, affected party, institution, decision event, repair attempt, or evidentiary record is supplied. Operators such as Responsibility, Action, Asymmetry, and Self-binding can therefore be structurally rendered, but they cannot become scene-specific findings without additional evidence. The case can show what would need to be true for repair, conflict, responsibility, or terminality to become legible. It does not show that any of them has occurred.

This is also why PMS-CONFLICT is useful here. PMS Core can map the temporal repair structure, but the CONFLICT overlay sharpens the threshold at which repairable consequence would become stabilized incompatibility under binding, asymmetry, time, and costly or asymmetric distance. MIP and AHP then matter only as downstream handling layers: they keep maturity, responsibility, dignity, and artefact-precision language from becoming person-level findings or stronger claims than the case supports.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement of the case is:

Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ

Read from right to left, this movement runs through Δ (Difference), ∇ (Impulse), □ (Frame), Λ (Non-event / residue), Θ (Temporality), Φ (Recontextualization), Χ (Distance), and Σ (Integration). The formula is not a proof of repair. It names the core structural pathway: a distinction becomes legible; closure pressure appears; a frame holds the problem; an expected continuation is interrupted; consequence unfolds through time; recontextualization becomes possible; distance prevents premature verdict; integration remains conditional.

The opening operator is **Δ (Difference)**. The case depends on keeping several distinctions apart: decision is not consequence, consequence is not fate, interruption is not avoidance, revision is not erasure, and repair is not fixed escalation. These distinctions make the case readable, but they do not solve it. The mere fact that repair can be distinguished from erasure does not prove that repair has occurred.

**∇ (Impulse)** appears as directional pressure. The case is pulled between escalation and repair: either the consequence hardens into a path that cannot be reintegrated, or the trajectory remains open enough for interruption, revision, and repair. This is not a psychological claim about what anyone wants. It names a structural pressure in the problem.

**□ (Frame)** is decisive because “repair” changes meaning across contexts. Repair might mean preventing recurrence, acknowledging harm, restoring trust, compensating loss, changing governance, accepting responsibility, or enabling future non-repetition. Without a declared frame, repair language becomes unstable and easily overclaims. The same is true of escalation: a worsening sequence, a conflict attractor, a legal dispute, a relational rupture, and a tragic non-integration are not the same thing.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is one of the most important operators in the case. The expected continuation does not simply proceed. A pause, interruption, refusal, missing response, or non-finalization can become structurally meaningful. But Λ must be handled carefully. Not every absence is repair. Silence may be avoidance. Delay may be harmful. Refusal may be legitimate. Rupture may remain outside integration. PMS must not automatically redescribe every non-event as productive recontextualization.

**Θ (Temporality)** carries the case. A decision has consequences across time. Repair, if it occurs, comes later and must relate to what already happened. This prevents repair from becoming simple reversal. The consequence remains part of the trajectory. Θ therefore keeps consequence real even when revision remains possible.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** makes repair thinkable. A prior decision-consequence relation can be placed into a new frame: not consequence-as-fate, but consequence-as-something that may still be interrupted, revised, acknowledged, or repaired. Yet Φ is not automatically repair. Reframing can clarify, but it can also excuse, minimize, smooth, or control the narrative. The case therefore requires Φ to remain bound to Θ: the past consequence is not erased by the new frame.

**Χ (Distance)** prevents premature verdict. It keeps the analysis from saying either “the decision fixes everything” or “repair resolves everything.” Χ is not indifference and not avoidance. It is the reflective spacing required to prevent consequence, repair, conflict, responsibility, and dignity language from becoming coercive closure.

**Σ (Integration)** is active only as a conditional demand. The case asks whether consequence, interruption, revision, and repair can be held together without erasure or forced coherence. But no concrete integration is shown. A coherent reading is not proof of repair. Repair must remain distinguishable from reversal, evasion, apology, forgiveness, tragedy, refusal, and unresolved rupture.

The remaining operators are weaker. **Α (Attractor)** is not active as a finding because no recurrence or stabilized pattern is supplied. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is possible but weak: a concrete repair scene would need to specify who decides, who bears consequences, who can interrupt, who can exit, and who controls framing. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is analytic-only because no actor, role, promise, obligation, or institutional commitment is specified. Without concrete Ψ, the case cannot assign responsibility or claim durable repair commitment.

PMS-CONFLICT sharpens the threshold conditions. It adds reduced signatures and non-operator handles such as conflict attractor, binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, and dignity collapse. These remain overlay handles, not PMS operators. In this case, they are mostly risk or threshold markers. The CONFLICT terminal-core signature is weak because Θ, Χ, and conditional Σ are present, but Ψ and Ω are not active. The stabilized-pattern signature is also weak because Α, Ω, and Ψ remain weak. Binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, and dignity collapse are visible as risks under later concretization or transmission, not as findings.

MIP preserves the same restraint in governance terms. It can read the analysis artefact as structurally aware and coherent, but it does not assign actor-level maturity or responsibility. Its A/M language is case-structure readability only. M is not assignable as an actor score because knowledge, control, predictability, alternatives, and serious integration attempt are absent. IA remains asymmetry monitoring rather than verdict. The D-module remains off; Dignity-in-Practice functions only as a protective guardrail.

AHP then hardens the analysis artefact. Its case-specific result is that the analysis is relatively precise but transmission-sensitive. Technical shorthand, A/M band language, D-language, and public excerpting could all be misread as stronger than the case warrants. AHP therefore adds hardening pressure without rescoring, activating D, upgrading transmission status, or creating new case findings.

##### What the analysis shows

The analysis shows that repair is structurally thinkable only if consequence remains temporally real. Repair cannot mean that the decision did not matter. It cannot mean that the consequence is dissolved by reinterpretation. It cannot mean that future correction automatically repairs prior burden. Repair must relate to the trajectory without erasing it.

The analysis also shows that escalation is not automatically conflict. Escalation pressure may be visible, but a conflict regime requires more: stabilized incompatibility, binding collision, asymmetry, temporality, and costly or asymmetric distance. Those conditions are not supplied here. PMS-CONFLICT therefore contributes a threshold watch, not a terminal finding.

The central closure possible in this case is a boundary closure: consequence and repair can be held together as a structural problem, but not resolved as an achieved repair. Λ-residue remains visible in several forms: the missing concrete scene, the missing affected-party standing, the missing repair criterion, the missing self-binding commitment, the missing asymmetry evidence, and the possibility that refusal, tragedy, rupture, or non-capture may remain outside integration.

The case also shows why coherence must be treated carefully. The Core pathway is elegant: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Θ, Φ, Χ, and conditional Σ form a strong structural reading. But precisely because the pathway is elegant, it risks over-smoothing. The analysis must therefore keep repair, reversal, evasion, excuse, forgiveness, tragedy, refusal, and unresolved rupture distinct.

Several rival readings remain genuinely open. A metaphysical determinism reading could treat consequence as fixed in a way PMS does not decide. A pragmatist reading could treat decisions as provisional commitments tested by later consequences. A restorative reading could insist that repair requires affected-party standing, material restitution, recognition, or acceptance. A tragic reading could hold that some consequences remain unrepaired even under awareness and good-faith effort. A language-practice reading could focus on how “repair,” “escalation,” and “responsibility” function in discourse. These readings are not defects in the PMS analysis; they mark what the case does not claim to exhaust.

The reading would weaken if repair and erasure could no longer be kept apart, if interruption were automatically treated as responsible revision, if every refusal or rupture were absorbed as incomplete integration, or if CONFLICT threshold markers were treated as active conflict findings without evidence of binding, asymmetry, recurrence, priced distance, and failed shared Σ. Stronger claims would require a concrete scene: actor roles, affected positions, consequence trajectory, repair criteria, asymmetry evidence, possible self-binding, and some enacted repair or refusal.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk in this case is **repair-language overreach**. “Repair” can easily become a closure device: a way to say that consequence has been handled, that further refusal is unreasonable, that escalation has ended, or that the affected position should move on. The article must therefore preserve the difference between repair as a structural possibility and repair as a concrete achievement.

A second risk is **conflict-language overreach**. PMS-CONFLICT makes stabilized incompatibility visible, but it does not show that this case is terminal conflict. CONFLICT handles such as binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, and dignity collapse are not findings here. They are threshold and misuse markers.

A third risk is **maturity-language overreach**. The MIP layer may describe the analysis artefact as structurally aware or coherent, but it does not evaluate any person. Non-assignment of responsibility is not a low score. D remaining off is not a dignity deficit. Working-note status is not a verdict.

A final risk is **example overreach**. The examples below demonstrate distinctions. They do not supply evidence for the case, prove repair, prove evasion, establish conflict, or authorize applied judgment.

##### Example 1 — The paused reply

The following examples are bounded illustrations. They demonstrate how the article’s distinctions work, but they do not supply evidence for the case, establish findings, or replace the YAML analyses. Each example must therefore be read as a reusable structural vignette, not as a verdict about the roles it describes.

A person receives a message pointing out that an earlier decision caused difficulty for someone else. The immediate expectation is a quick answer: either defense, apology, justification, or counter-accusation. Instead, the person pauses and says, “I need to understand what consequence this had before I answer.” No further facts are supplied. We do not know whether the pause becomes repair, avoidance, evasion, or genuine reflection.

The example demonstrates the case’s central boundary: interruption is structurally meaningful, but it is not automatically repair. The pause is a Λ-event in the article’s sense: an expected continuation does not simply occur. The decision-consequence trajectory is interrupted. That interruption opens a possible Φ-recontextualization, because the earlier act may now be understood through its consequence rather than through the decision-maker’s original frame. Χ is also present as reflective distance: the scene does not immediately collapse into blame, defense, apology, or verdict.

But Σ remains conditional. Nothing has yet been integrated. The earlier consequence has not been repaired. The affected position has not accepted anything. No material response has occurred. No commitment has been made. Ψ is therefore not active as self-binding. Ω also remains unspecified: we do not know who has more power, who bears the cost, or who can exit the exchange.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant only as a threshold watch. If the pause were later used to delay indefinitely, avoid responsibility, control timing, or shift cost to the affected position, asymmetry lock-in might become relevant. If the pause became public and hardened into reputational pressure, exposure capture might become relevant. In the bounded vignette, however, those are only risks, not findings.

MIP and AHP remain guardrail layers here. MIP would not assign maturity or responsibility scores from the pause. AHP would flag any article wording that made the pause look like proof of repair or proof of evasion.

The forbidden claims are straightforward: the example does not show that the person is mature, avoidant, guilty, repaired, forgiven, or conflictual. It does not establish legal liability or psychological state. The non-capture note is equally important: the affected position may later refuse repair, and that refusal must not be redescribed as incomplete integration.

The article takeaway is that interruption creates a structural opening, not a conclusion. Repair begins only if consequence remains real and later action integrates it without erasure.

##### Example 2 — The revised procedure that may not repair the harm

A small team adopts a procedure that unintentionally burdens one role more than the others. After noticing the effect, the team changes the procedure. The new procedure prevents the same burden from recurring. At first glance, this seems like repair: a consequence was recognized, the frame changed, and the future trajectory was altered. But the earlier burden remains. The person who carried it has not been asked whether the revision addresses what happened, and no one has clarified whether repair means prevention, acknowledgment, redistribution, compensation, apology, or changed governance.

This vignette demonstrates why the article refuses to treat recontextualization as repair by itself. PMS Core can identify the initial Δ: the difference between intended coordination and actual burden. ∇ appears as pressure to stabilize the situation quickly. □ matters because “repair” changes meaning depending on the frame. In a process frame, preventing recurrence may count as repair. In a relational frame, acknowledgment may be required. In an institutional frame, redistribution or documented correction may be necessary. In an ethical frame, the affected position may need standing to accept or refuse the adequacy of the response.

Θ is central because the earlier burden remains part of the trajectory. The new procedure changes the future, but it does not erase the past. Φ is active because the team reinterprets the original procedure through its effects. Χ is needed to prevent a premature statement such as “the issue is fixed.” Σ remains conditional: the team has integrated one dimension of the problem, recurrence prevention, but not necessarily the whole consequence structure. Ψ would become active only if the team binds itself to a durable obligation, review practice, or repair commitment.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay becomes useful as a guard against false closure. If the team’s revision is treated as final while the affected role continues to bear unrepaired costs, a Σ-incompatibility risk appears: local procedural coherence may fail to integrate the affected position’s consequence. If the team controls the timing, language, and record of “repair,” while the exposed role has little contest capacity, asymmetry lock-in could become relevant. Still, the vignette does not establish conflict. It only shows what would need to be checked.

MIP would keep responsibility unassigned unless the scene supplied knowledge, control, alternatives, predictability, and role obligations. It would also keep any A/M reading case-bound: a revised procedure may show structural awareness or procedural coherence, but it does not by itself show actor maturity, responsibility, or repair adequacy. AHP would likely harden the analysis by requiring a local definition of “repair,” a second-order evidence notice, and a boundary statement preventing “procedure changed” from being treated as “harm repaired.”

The forbidden claims are that the team has repaired the harm, failed morally, acted negligently, or entered terminal conflict. The example also does not rank the affected role, the team, or the procedure designers. It does not prove empirical effectiveness or legal sufficiency.

The non-capture note is that the affected role may regard the revision as useful but insufficient. That remainder is not a defect in the model. It is exactly the Λ-residue the article insists must remain visible.

The article takeaway is that future-oriented correction can be structurally important without being identical to repair. A repair claim requires more than a changed trajectory; it must preserve the reality of the earlier consequence and specify what kind of integration is being claimed.

##### Example 3 — The public “repair” statement

An organization publishes a short public statement after a disputed decision. The statement says that “the matter has been repaired,” that “lessons have been learned,” and that “everyone should now move forward.” The statement names no concrete affected party, no repair criterion, no time window, and no mechanism for contesting whether the repair is adequate. Internally, there may or may not have been serious work. Externally, the statement produces a new situation: the public record now contains the organization’s claim that repair has occurred.

This example is useful because it shows why the article distinguishes repair, recontextualization, integration, and exposure. The organization’s statement is a Φ-move: it recontextualizes the earlier decision through a repair narrative. It may also attempt Σ: it tries to integrate decision, consequence, learning, and forward movement into a coherent public account. Θ is active because the statement changes the trajectory; from this point on, future discussion occurs under the shadow of a declared repair. Χ is required but fragile: readers need distance from the organization’s closure claim, especially because the statement may pre-empt contest.

Yet the example does not show that repair occurred. It does not show that consequence was acknowledged adequately, that affected parties participated, that material conditions changed, or that the repair was accepted. It also does not show bad faith. The article’s discipline is precisely to avoid both premature vindication and premature condemnation.

The PMS Core hooks are therefore clear. Δ distinguishes public repair claim from actual repair. ∇ marks the closure pressure: public statements often aim to stop escalation, stabilize reputation, and restore coordination. □ asks what frame the statement belongs to: public relations, governance, relational repair, legal risk management, institutional accountability, or internal learning. Λ appears in the missing events: no affected-party voice, no repair criterion, no review path, no evidence of material change. Φ is active as public recontextualization. Χ is necessary to prevent the repair statement from becoming authoritative closure. Σ remains conditional. Ψ may become relevant only if the organization binds itself to a durable review, remedy, or accountability structure.

The PMS-CONFLICT hooks are especially important. Exposure capture is a risk because the statement moves the repair claim into public record. If publicness amplifies Ω and Θ, the statement may make later contest more costly: anyone who says “this is not repaired” may now appear to be reopening what has officially been closed. Asymmetry lock-in may become relevant if the organization controls the platform, timing, wording, and archive while affected positions have limited contest capacity. Dignity collapse is a misuse risk if the statement implies that continued discomfort, refusal, or disagreement is unreasonable, immature, or obstructive. Still, these remain risks in the vignette unless further scene evidence shows them active.

MIP would treat this as higher transmission risk than the private examples. It would not assign maturity scores to the organization or affected parties. It would ask whether the statement is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. It would also ask whether participation or contest channels exist. D remains a guardrail: no dignity profile is produced, but the analysis must prevent public repair language from becoming humiliation or standing-stripping.

AHP would likely identify several attack points in any article rendering of this example. “Repair” would need a local definition. “Public statement” would need a publicness note. “Lessons learned” would need to be distinguished from enacted change. The article would need to avoid treating polished language as evidence of repair. AHP would also warn that the example itself could be misread as an organizational verdict unless its boundaries travel with it.

The forbidden claims are extensive. The example does not prove deception, liability, manipulation, maturity, immaturity, successful repair, failed repair, or terminal conflict. It does not diagnose organizational culture. It does not decide whether the affected party should accept the statement. It does not authorize sanction or public shaming.

The non-capture note is that an affected party may reject the public repair frame altogether. That rejection may preserve a truth the organization’s coherence cannot absorb. PMS must not automatically convert refusal into unresolved Σ-work, CONFLICT must not automatically convert it into incompatibility, and MIP must not convert it into low maturity.

The article takeaway is that public repair language can change the structure of a case without proving repair. Once repair is publicly declared, the analysis must track not only consequence and revision, but also exposure, contest capacity, reversibility, and dignity risk.

##### Case takeaway

“Escalation vs Repair” shows how AXIOM can hold a fragile middle space without converting it into a verdict. PMS Core makes the temporal repair structure intelligible: consequence remains real, interruption becomes meaningful, recontextualization becomes possible, distance prevents premature closure, and integration remains conditional. PMS-CONFLICT sharpens the point at which this structure would become conflict-legible, but it does not claim that the threshold is crossed.

The case’s main methodological value is its refusal to let either pole dominate. Repair remains possible, but not proven. Conflict remains visible, but not found. Responsibility remains structurally thinkable, but not assigned. Maturity and dignity language remain guarded, not evaluative. The examples show the same movement in concrete miniature: a pause is not repair, a revised procedure is not necessarily repair, and a public repair statement is not proof of repair.

The remaining Λ-residue is not a defect. It is the central output. Affected-party refusal, unrepaired consequence, material irreversibility, tragedy, rival framings, and non-capture remain possible. The case should therefore be used in the chapter as a boundary section: it preserves the possibility of repair while refusing to let repair become erasure, and it preserves the visibility of conflict while refusing to let conflict become verdict. Its methodological value lies in holding open that fragile space without pretending to own it.

#### 7.4.3 Trolley dilemmas

##### Why this case is here

Trolley dilemmas belong here because they concentrate a powerful philosophical closure-demand into a minimal forced-choice form. Someone appears to face a situation in which action or omission affects who is harmed, who is saved, and how responsibility for foreseeable consequences is assigned. The case is familiar because it looks as if it should force an answer: act or refrain, redirect or do nothing, minimize harm or refuse direct involvement, accept responsibility or deny it. Precisely for that reason, it is useful for AXIOM. It shows how quickly a structured dilemma can become a demand for moral closure, and how easily that demand can outrun the material actually given.

The case is not treated as a concrete praxis scene. It is a conceptual projection: no actual chooser, institution, jurisdiction, evidentiary record, technical mechanism, time pressure, probability distribution, legal duty, role mandate, or affected-party voice is supplied. This matters because operators such as responsibility, action, asymmetry, and self-binding can be structurally rendered, but they cannot become scene-specific findings without additional evidence. The case can show how the grammar of a forced-choice dilemma works; it cannot decide what a real person, institution, court, machine, or policy should do.

The closure demand arises because the structure appears simple enough to decide and grave enough to require decision. An action or omission seems to alter the harm distribution. A foreseeable result seems to place pressure on the chooser-position. The analysis therefore has to resist two opposite failures. It must not evade the structural pressure by saying that nothing can be mapped. But it must also not convert structural legibility into a verdict. Trolley dilemmas are mapped rather than solved because the case’s importance lies in the tension between legibility and remainder.

This is also why the case is suitable for a layered reading. PMS Core makes visible the operator movement that gives the dilemma its structure. PMS-CONFLICT sharpens the weak incompatibility between non-jointly-realizable harm branches without treating the case as terminal conflict. MIP becomes relevant only as a guardrail reading of the analysis artefact: no concrete chooser is scored, but the handling of asymmetry, responsibility pressure, publicness, and dignity-risk can be checked. AHP then hardens the analysis artefact against misreading, especially where a careful structural reading might be excerpted as a public rule, maturity judgment, or validation stamp. The stack does not increase certainty. It increases boundary control.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement begins with **Δ (Difference)**. Trolley dilemmas are made of distinctions: action and omission, harmed and saved, intervention and non-intervention, direct and indirect consequence, responsibility assigned and responsibility withheld. Without these differences, the dilemma has no shape. But Δ does not settle the problem. The distinction between action and omission may itself be contested. “Causing” and “allowing” may be framed differently. A branch outcome may be clear while the meaning of the act remains unclear. Difference opens the field; it does not decide it.

From Δ follows **∇ (Impulse)** as directional pressure. Once the branch differences become visible, the case generates pressure to choose, prevent harm, redirect harm, justify non-action, or assign responsibility. This is not a psychological claim about a real agent. No fear, compassion, cruelty, courage, utilitarian motive, deontological rigidity, or moral maturity is inferred. ∇ names the structural pressure produced by a differentiated field under a forced-choice frame.

That pressure becomes intelligible only within **□ (Frame)**. The trolley case is a frame before it is a verdict. The frame makes certain options visible and others invisible. It asks the reader to compare action and omission, harm and rescue, foreseeable consequence and responsibility. But the frame is not self-validating. A critic may contest it as artificial, morally distorting, or too narrow. A consequentialist, deontological, causal, legal, phenomenological, or tragedy-oriented reading may preserve a different boundary. The PMS reading therefore keeps □ explicit: the dilemma is structurally legible only because a frame has already selected what counts as relevant.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is central because omission is one of the case’s major pressure points. Not switching, not intervening, not preventing, not rescuing, not refusing the frame, or not choosing a possible alternative can all become meaningful non-events. But Λ is not mere absence. Absence becomes structurally meaningful only within a frame that generates expectation. Therefore the analysis cannot move directly from “nothing was done” to “there was a culpable omission.” The frame must specify why the non-event matters. In this case, Λ helps show why omission is visible, but it does not decide whether omission is morally equivalent to action, legally culpable, tragically unavoidable, or outside the frame.

**Α (Attractor)** is weak. Trolley dilemmas recur as a recognizable philosophical problem-form. They stabilize a familiar script: forced choice, branch harms, action/omission distinction, and responsibility pressure. In that limited sense, the case has attractor-like form. But this is not a concrete behavioural pattern, institutional routine, or repeated role-script. No agent repeatedly chooses under actual trolley conditions. No institution is shown to reproduce the same conflict. Α therefore marks the recurrence of the philosophical form, not an empirical attractor.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is active but conceptual. The chooser-position appears to have some consequence-shaping capacity, while affected parties are exposed to harm or rescue. This creates a structural imbalance in capacity and exposure. There may also be asymmetry between those harmed and those saved, and between analyst and downstream reader. But Ω must be calibrated carefully. Is the asymmetry one of power, duty, knowledge, vulnerability, role, causal control, or accidental position? The abstract case does not say. Ω therefore cannot become blame, authority, superiority, inferiority, or person-ranking. It shows structural imbalance; it does not assign moral status.

**Θ (Temporality)** is active because the dilemma depends on sequence. Action or omission precedes harm or rescue. Foreseeability concerns a future consequence. Irreversibility matters because once harm occurs it cannot simply be undone. But the temporality remains conceptual. The case does not provide actual timing, probability, causal reliability, delay, or evidence. Θ therefore cannot be treated as empirical predictability or legal foreseeability. It marks consequence-trajectory within a thought-structure.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is one of the case’s most important operators. The same minimal structure can be reframed as outcome comparison, doing versus allowing, rescue versus sacrifice, duty versus consequence, causal intervention, role responsibility, or tragedy. Φ is active because trolley dilemmas are frame-mobile. But Φ must not become an assimilation machine. Rival readings are not automatically incomplete PMS readings. A refusal of the trolley frame may preserve something the frame excludes. A tragedy reading is not simply low integration. A deontological reading is not a defective consequentialist reading. Recontextualization makes rivalry visible; it does not dissolve rivalry.

**Χ (Distance)** is required for the analysis itself. Without Χ, the case becomes a tribunal: pull or do not pull, blame or excuse, solve or fail. Distance keeps the reading reversible and prevents structural legibility from becoming moral closure. Yet the analysis must distinguish its own reflective distance from the imagined situation. A real emergency may not give a chooser the reflective space that an analyst has. Χ is therefore an application constraint, not a claim that the agent had calm deliberative access.

**Σ (Integration)** remains weak. The case demands integration across consequences, duties, omissions, causal pathways, affected parties, and responsibility frames, but no integrated resolution is supplied. A coherent PMS chain is not enough. If Σ were pushed too far, PMS would become a closure machine: it would smooth the dilemma into an elegant formula and risk losing the very differences the case is meant to expose. Here, integration is a demand and a possible task, not an achieved result.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is also weak. Responsibility is thematically present, but PMS Responsibility_R requires more than the fact that consequences are foreseeable. It requires a self-binding relation across asymmetry, time, and recontextualization. The abstract case provides no concrete agent, role duty, promise, institutional obligation, professional mandate, or stable commitment. Ψ therefore cannot be applied to the imagined chooser as a finding. It remains relevant only as a condition for possible stronger readings and as a discipline on the analysis itself.

The dependency lesson is straightforward. The analysis cannot leap from Δ and ∇ directly to Ψ. It cannot move from Λ to blame, from Ω to culpability, from Θ to certainty, or from coherence to proof. Later operators require calibration. Stronger responsibility claims would need role, knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, obligation, and temporal sequence. Stronger action claims would need an actual enactment. Stronger conflict claims would need concrete binding, sustained load, and priced or asymmetric distance. The abstract trolley form gives enough for structural reading, not enough for verdict.

PMS-CONFLICT sharpens one specific aspect: the case is weakly conflict-legible because the branch outcomes are not jointly realizable. One cannot simply realize all goods, avoid all harms, and preserve all frames at once. But this is not terminal CONFLICT. The terminal core would require Σ under load with binding Ψ and priced or asymmetric Χ under Ω/Θ. The case does not establish binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, terminal Σ-failure, or a stabilized conflict regime. The overlay therefore marks weak dilemma-legibility, not terminal classification.

The active CONFLICT contribution is modest: a weak stabilized dilemma-pattern, plus transfer risks around exposure capture and dignity collapse. Exposure capture becomes relevant if a reversible structural reading hardens into public pressure, rule-like authority, or enforcement affordance. Dignity-collapse risk becomes relevant if the case is used to rank lives, shame choosers, or treat disagreement as maturity failure. These remain risks, not findings.

MIP adds only the case-specific guardrail result. The case is not scored. No A-score or M-score is assigned to a chooser. The IA-box remains a risk check because asymmetry is visible but only partially transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible in an abstract frame. The D-module remains off; dignity language functions as protection against ranking, shaming, degradation, and public misuse. MIP therefore confirms that non-assignment is the result, not a deficiency.

AHP adds a second-order hardening result. The previous MIP output is precise as an analysis artefact, but its transmission is sensitive. “Structurally transmittable” could be overread if detached from “bounded working note.” Numeric-looking analytic band language could be mistaken for a score. Dignity language could be excerpted as a dignity judgment. Layered coherence could be mistaken for framework validation. AHP hardens those points; it does not rescore the case, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or create new findings.

The reading would weaken if the case ceased to contain a genuine forced-choice structure, if action or omission no longer affected harm or rescue branches, if no frame made omission meaningful, if asymmetry of capacity or exposure disappeared, or if responsibility-ascription were not actually at issue. It would also weaken if a concrete variant supplied legal, empirical, institutional, or role-specific material that displaced the abstract frame. Stronger claims would require concrete scene data: agent role, duty, knowledge, timing, probabilities, causal mechanism, available alternatives, affected-party perspectives, and review conditions. Without such data, operationalization remains unavailable.

##### What the analysis shows

The case-specific structural movement is:

**Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ weak(Α) ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ, with Σ and Ψ conditional rather than established.**

This formula should not be read as a solution. It says that trolley dilemmas are structurally readable as differentiated, framed, omission-sensitive, asymmetrical, temporally extended, recontextualized forced-choice structures that require reflective distance. Α is weak because the recurrence is philosophical rather than concrete. Σ is weak because no integrated resolution is supplied. Ψ is weak because no self-binding role, obligation, or agent is specified.

The CONFLICT overlay adds:

**CONFLICT_weak_dilemma_legibility = weak(CONFLICT_stabilized_pattern) + risk(DRIFT_exposure_capture) + risk(DRIFT_dignity_collapse), with CONFLICT_terminal_core, DRIFT_binding_collision, and DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in not applicable as established case structures.**

The case therefore shows a distinction between incompatibility and terminality. A dilemma may be conflict-legible without being terminal conflict. It may be unresolved without proving that integration has failed under sustained binding. It may be tragic in a broad philosophical sense without satisfying the PMS-CONFLICT terminal threshold.

The PMS drift-pattern status is similarly cautious. AD_A>>E is a weak risk because analysis can expand while no enactment is present, but this is not a finding about avoidance or failed action. AD_Sigma_low is a weak risk because rival frames may remain unresolved, but unresolved plurality is not automatically fragmented integration. The case’s main internal danger is under-discrimination: PMS maps the case fluently enough that different variants could be flattened unless role, causation, omission, probability, and duty are recalibrated.

The analysis shows that closure is possible only at the level of structural legibility. It can say why the dilemma feels forceful. It can say why omission matters only within a frame. It can say why asymmetry and temporality generate responsibility pressure. It can say why rival frames remain open. It can say why weak conflict-legibility is not terminal conflict. It cannot say what the morally correct choice is.

Λ-residue remains visible in several places: omission without verdict, tragedy without solution, responsibility pressure without responsibility assignment, conflict-legibility without terminality, dignity-protection without dignity scoring, and precision without validation. Rival readings remain open rather than absorbed as model defects. A consequentialist reading may preserve outcome comparison; a deontological reading may preserve the distinction between doing and allowing; a causal reading may preserve intervention structure; a role-duty reading may preserve institutional mandate; a legal reading may preserve jurisdiction and evidentiary standards; a phenomenological reading may preserve lived burden; a tragedy reading may preserve irreducible remainder; and an anti-capture reading may reject the trolley frame itself. PMS can render why these readings arise, but it does not defeat them by translating them.

##### Case-specific boundary

This case specifically tempts several overreadings.

First, **Λ (Non-event / residue)** must not be read as automatic culpable omission. Not acting becomes meaningful only within a frame that establishes expectation, capacity, duty, or available intervention.

Second, **Θ (Temporality)** must not be read as empirical or legal foreseeability. The case supplies conceptual consequence structure, not probability evidence or legal standards.

Third, **Ω (Asymmetry)** must not become ranking. The chooser-position’s possible capacity and the affected parties’ exposure create structural asymmetry, not a hierarchy of persons or lives.

Fourth, **Σ (Integration)** must not be inflated. A coherent reading of the dilemma is not a moral solution. Local coherence within one ethical frame does not defeat rival frames.

Fifth, **Ψ (Self-binding)** must not be read as ultimate authorship, guilt, legal responsibility, or mature identity. No concrete role or commitment is supplied.

Sixth, PMS-CONFLICT terms must remain overlay handles. Conflict-legibility is not terminal CONFLICT. Binding collision and asymmetry lock-in are not active without concrete bindings and cost structures.

Seventh, MIP and AHP must remain artefact-bound. The non-assignment of A-score, M-score, IA-verdict, or D-profile is not a low score. It is the correct result for a non-concrete case. AHP precision is not validation.

##### Example 1 — The switch as frame-dependent action

A bounded version of the trolley case can be stated without deciding it. A track branches ahead. On one branch, several persons are exposed to harm; on another branch, one person is exposed to harm. A chooser-position is imagined near a switch. Pulling the switch would redirect the trolley. Not pulling the switch would leave the trolley on its current path. The vignette is deliberately abstract: no jurisdiction, no role duty, no actual probabilities, no institutional mandate, no testimony, no emergency protocol, and no identifiable persons are supplied.

This example demonstrates why the frame matters before any responsibility claim can be made. In one frame, the switch appears as an available action that changes the outcome. In another frame, pulling the switch may be described as direct intervention into harm. In another, not pulling the switch may be described as omission. In yet another, the whole setup may be rejected as an artificial forced-choice frame that excludes other possible responses. The point is not to decide between these readings. The point is to show that the case does not become structurally legible without a declared frame.

The PMS Core hooks are immediate. **Δ (Difference)** marks the relevant distinctions: pulling or not pulling, redirected harm or non-redirected harm, harmed or saved, intervention or non-intervention. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as the pressure to choose, prevent, justify, or assign responsibility. **□ (Frame)** is the forced-choice frame that makes the switch meaningful in the first place. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** appears because not pulling can become a meaningful non-event only if the frame treats intervention as expected, available, or relevant. **Ω (Asymmetry)** appears as a conceptual asymmetry between a chooser-position with possible consequence-shaping capacity and affected parties exposed to harm. **Θ (Temporality)** appears in the sequence from possible action or omission to foreseeable consequence. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is active because the same switch can be recontextualized as rescue, sacrifice, causal redirection, omission, non-interference, or tragic exposure. **Χ (Distance)** is required so that the analysis does not become a verdict. **Σ (Integration)** remains weak because no integrated resolution is supplied. **Ψ (Self-binding)** remains weak because no concrete self-binding role, duty, promise, or institutional obligation is specified.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant only weakly. The vignette is conflict-legible because the branches cannot both be realized: the imagined outcome-space contains incompatible harm distributions. But this does not activate terminal CONFLICT. No binding collision is established. No concrete role commitment collides with another. No priced or asymmetric distance structure is supplied. No terminal Σ-failure under sustained Θ/Ω/Ψ load is demonstrated. The overlay therefore helps name weak incompatibility without escalating it into a terminal conflict regime.

MIP remains artefact-bound here. No A-score or M-score is assigned to the chooser-position. The vignette lacks the factors required for responsibility scoring: concrete knowledge, power, predictability, real alternatives, and role-defined obligation. The IA-box would remain a risk check only: asymmetry is visible, but transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility are only partially available in the abstract frame. The D-module remains off. Dignity functions only as a guardrail: the example must not rank lives or shame the imagined chooser.

The forbidden claims are therefore clear. This example does not show that pulling is right, that not pulling is wrong, that the chooser is guilty, that one person counts less than several, that omission is equivalent to action, or that PMS decides the case. It does not establish legal liability, moral desert, clinical meaning, empirical probability, or implementation validity.

The non-capture note is equally important. A deontological reading may preserve the difference between doing and allowing. A consequentialist reading may preserve outcome comparison. A tragedy reading may preserve the remainder that no branch is clean. A refusal of the switch-frame may preserve the concern that the setup is morally artificial. PMS can map these frame pressures, but it does not erase their rivalry.

The article takeaway is that the switch example shows that trolley dilemmas are not simply “choice problems.” They are frame-dependent structures in which action, omission, harm, and responsibility become legible only under declared boundaries. PMS clarifies the frame-pressure; it does not convert the switch into a moral verdict.

##### Example 2 — The operator, the bystander, and the missing role

Consider two versions of the same abstract trolley structure. In the first, the chooser-position is a random bystander near a switch. In the second, the chooser-position is a trained railway operator working inside an explicit safety role. The visible branch structure may look similar: action or omission appears to affect who is harmed and who is saved. Yet the responsibility structure cannot be assumed to be the same.

This example demonstrates why later PMS operators cannot be used cheaply. In both versions, **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes action and omission, harmed and saved, intervention and non-intervention. **∇ (Impulse)** creates pressure to respond. **□ (Frame)** frames the situation as one in which the switch or control mechanism matters. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** can mark non-intervention as a possible non-event. **Ω (Asymmetry)** marks asymmetry between a decision-position and exposed parties. **Θ (Temporality)** marks the temporal sequence from response to consequence. But the operator version may introduce further structures that the bystander version lacks: role duty, training, mandate, institutional expectation, prior commitment, safety protocol, and documented responsibility channels. Those would matter for **Σ (Integration)** and **Ψ (Self-binding)**.

In the bystander version, Ψ remains weak. The bystander may have capacity, but capacity alone is not self-binding. The case does not yet show a role-owned obligation, a promise, a professional duty, or a stable commitment across time. Responsibility-ascription is structurally salient, but PMS Responsibility_R is not fully derived. In the operator version, Ψ might become stronger if the role includes explicit duty, accepted responsibility, procedural knowledge, and temporal obligation. Even then, the analysis would need concrete facts: what the operator knew, what the protocol required, what alternatives existed, what the timing allowed, and whether action or non-action was actually available.

This is the key dependency lesson. PMS does not permit a leap from “someone could affect the outcome” to “someone is fully responsible.” That would be a Δ/∇/Ω-to-Ψ shortcut. It bypasses frame declaration, omission calibration, temporality, recontextualization, reflective distance, and integration. A responsible PMS reading must ask what kind of role the chooser-position occupies and whether any self-binding structure is actually present.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay also changes with the role structure. In the bystander version, conflict-legibility remains weak: there are incompatible branch outcomes, but no binding collision. In the operator version, a binding collision might become possible if concrete duties conflict: for example, a duty to minimize system harm collides with a duty not to redirect harm onto a protected zone. But that cannot be asserted from the abstract vignette alone. The overlay can say what would need to be shown; it cannot supply the missing role data.

MIP’s relevance becomes clearer here. It would still not score a person from the abstract example. But it can explain why high M is blocked in the bystander case unless knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and role obligations are established. In the operator case, MIP could become more relevant, but only after concrete scene material is supplied. The M-score would still describe shared structural responsibility, not guilt. The IA-box would need renewed calibration because institutional asymmetry, duty, and contest capacity may be stronger. The D-module would still not activate automatically; Dignity-in-Practice would remain a guardrail against shaming or irreversible labeling.

AHP would treat this example as a hardening reminder. If the article uses “chooser” too generally, readers may miss the role difference. AHP would likely flag a precision issue: the analysis should not let a bystander and an operator collapse into the same responsibility formula. The hardening action would be to specify that role data can strengthen Ψ only when supplied, and that abstract capacity is not enough.

The forbidden claims are specific. This example does not say the operator is liable, the bystander is innocent, the professional role always creates responsibility, or the absence of role always removes responsibility. It does not decide whether action is morally required. It does not rank the affected parties. It does not diagnose hesitation or refusal.

The non-capture note is that legal, professional, and institutional readings may preserve distinctions PMS Core cannot fill without data. A legal doctrine might treat duties differently from moral philosophy. A professional safety frame might specify obligations not visible in the generic trolley version. A tragedy reading might still say that even role-bound action leaves moral residue.

The article takeaway is that the bystander/operator contrast shows why PMS dependency discipline matters. Later structures such as Responsibility_R, Action_E, and Ψ cannot be activated merely because harm is foreseeable. Role, duty, knowledge, alternatives, and temporal commitment must be calibrated before stronger claims become legitimate.

##### Example 3 — When a classroom example becomes a public rule

Imagine that a teacher uses the trolley dilemma in a private seminar. The purpose is reflection: students compare frames, examine action and omission, and discuss why responsibility-ascription remains difficult. In that setting, the example remains reversible. Interpretations can be revised. No real person is scored. No institution acts on the result. The analysis stays within a low-publicness, descriptive setting.

Now imagine that a simplified excerpt travels outside the seminar: “PMS-CONFLICT shows trolley cases are conflict structures; MIP confirms the mature handling is to preserve the greater number; AHP gives the analysis high precision.” This sentence is wrong, but it is a plausible misuse. It converts weak conflict-legibility into a decision rule, MIP guardrail review into moral maturity judgment, and AHP precision into validation. It turns a bounded analysis into public pressure.

This example demonstrates why transmission is part of the case architecture. PMS Core may be disciplined in the original article, and the CONFLICT overlay may be carefully bounded, but public reuse changes the risk environment. The same wording that is safe inside a private working note can become dangerous when detached from its boundaries. Publicness can harden interpretation into authority. A reversible structural reading can become an enforcement affordance.

The PMS Core hooks remain the same, but their misuse changes. **□ (Frame)** is silently changed: the frame shifts from philosophical reflection to public rule-like statement. **Φ (Recontextualization)** occurs without being declared. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** is suppressed because the excerpt hides non-capture, rival readings, and weak Σ/Ψ. **Ω (Asymmetry)** increases because the speaker, institution, platform, or publication may gain authority over how the dilemma is received. **Θ (Temporality)** matters because the excerpt can persist as a record, teaching note, policy prompt, or public claim. **Χ (Distance)** is weakened if readers are not invited to preserve distance and reversibility. **Σ (Integration)** is falsely inflated if the excerpt implies a settled integration. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is falsely implied if the excerpt makes the analysis sound binding.

PMS-CONFLICT is especially relevant here. The original overlay marks **exposure capture** and **dignity collapse** only as risks. In the public excerpt, those risks become more salient. Exposure capture occurs when a scene-bound interpretation hardens into pressure. Dignity-collapse risk appears if the excerpt encourages ranking lives, shaming students who resist the calculus, or presenting disagreement as immature. Yet even here the article should be careful: the example demonstrates transmission risk, not an established drift finding unless a concrete public event is documented.

MIP clarifies why the public move is not harmless. The original MIP status is a private structural working note. It does not assign A-score or M-score to a chooser. It does not activate the D-module. It does not authorize public D-readings. If the analysis is reused in a public or institutional setting, publicness must be recalibrated. The IA-box must be redone. Transmission status must be reviewed. A phrase like “structurally transmittable” cannot be detached from “only as a bounded working note.”

AHP is directly relevant. It already flags the main attack point: transmission ambiguity. “High Precision Heuristic” means the MIP output is well-structured as an analysis artefact. It does not mean the case is solved, the result is public-ready, or the analysis can be operationalized. In this example, AHP does not add a new moral claim about the seminar, the teacher, the students, or the trolley dilemma. It identifies a weakness in the artefact’s possible travel path: a careful private formulation can become misleading when compressed into a public rule-like sentence. AHP’s recommended hardening banner fits here: working note only; no rescore; no D-module activation; no verdict; no operational use.

The forbidden claims are therefore explicit. This example does not show that public teaching is wrong, that trolley dilemmas should not be used pedagogically, that one moral theory is immature, or that public discussion is a drift by itself. It does not claim empirical harm from classroom use. It does not say that AHP detects wrongdoing. It only shows how a bounded analysis can become unsafe when excerpted without its frame.

The non-capture note is that pedagogy, public philosophy, and governance each have their own norms. PMS, CONFLICT, MIP, and AHP can identify transmission boundaries, but they do not replace educational judgment, democratic deliberation, legal standards, or institutional review. A classroom may legitimately use trolley dilemmas to teach moral conflict, but the article’s layered analysis must not be converted into a public decision procedure.

The article takeaway is that the classroom-to-public-rule example shows why AHP belongs at the end of the stack. The danger is not only analytical error inside the case; it is also transmission error after the case. A careful structural reading can still be misused if its boundaries do not travel with it.

##### Case takeaway

Trolley dilemmas show how a minimal forced-choice structure can generate maximal closure pressure. PMS Core maps the pressure without resolving it: difference, frame, omission, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, and distance are active, while integration and self-binding remain conditional. PMS-CONFLICT sharpens the weak incompatibility of non-jointly-realizable branches without activating terminal conflict. MIP preserves non-assignment as the correct result for a non-concrete case. AHP identifies transmission vulnerabilities in the analysis artefact without turning precision into validation.

The case should be used in the chapter as a demonstration of disciplined non-closure. It shows how AXIOM can render a classical philosophical problem readable without making the model a verdict machine. The remaining Λ-residue is not accidental: omission, responsibility, tragedy, rival ethical frames, role duty, public transmission, and dignity-risk all remain partially open. The methodological result is not that the trolley problem is solved. It is that the analysis can show why the demand for solution arises, where it can be structurally clarified, and where it must remain bounded.

#### 7.4.4 Tragedy as a category

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because tragedy places one of the strongest closure-demands on AXIOM. It asks how loss, conflict, guilt, responsibility, or irreversibility can remain meaningful when no fully resolving action, interpretation, reconciliation, or integration is available. The difficulty is not simply that tragedy involves suffering or conflict. The difficulty is that tragedy invites interpretation while resisting the kind of interpretation that would make the tragic remainder disappear.

For that reason, the case is mapped rather than solved. A tragic structure is not adequately handled if the analysis merely says that something sad occurred, that someone failed, or that a system broke down. Nor is it adequately handled if the analysis elevates tragedy into a metaphysical doctrine about fate, being, mortality, evil, or finitude. The case instead asks for a structural rendering: what must be present for tragedy to become legible as a category without turning that legibility into closure?

The case is a conceptual projection, not a concrete scene. No particular actors, roles, institutions, legal claims, action sequence, or evidentiary record are supplied. This matters because several PMS structures can be rendered only in a limited way. Responsibility, action, asymmetry, and self-binding can be structurally discussed, but they must not become scene-specific findings without additional evidence. The point is to show how the tragic category carries closure-pressure and residue, not to decide a concrete conflict, assign guilt, evaluate maturity, or diagnose anyone.

The case belongs in AXIOM because it tests whether PMS can remain non-totalizing under pressure. A weak use of PMS would dissolve tragedy into coherence: every loss becomes a structured Λ-field, every non-resolution becomes incomplete integration, every resistance becomes immature praxis, and every rival reading becomes merely under-calibrated PMS. The disciplined use does the opposite. It makes tragedy legible while preserving the place where legibility does not become resolution.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement begins with Δ (Difference). Tragedy requires distinctions: loss and non-loss, conflict and reconciliation, guilt and innocence, responsibility and non-responsibility, reversible and irreversible, resolved and unresolved, repair and non-repair. Without these distinctions, the tragic category has no structural entry point. Yet Δ alone is not enough. Not every difference is tragic. A distinction becomes relevant only when it is framed as meaningful under loss, conflict, responsibility, or irreversibility.

∇ (Impulse) appears weakly as closure-pressure. The case implies a directed tension toward repair, explanation, recognition, reconciliation, or responsibility. But because no concrete actor is supplied, ∇ cannot be read as psychological desire, avoidance, guilt-feeling, or motivation. It is only the structural pressure generated by a difference that calls for closure while closure may remain unavailable.

□ (Frame) is central. The active frame is “tragedy as a category.” Inside this frame are meaningful loss, conflict, guilt, responsibility, irreversibility, unavailable repair, unavailable reconciliation, and non-resolving interpretation. Outside this frame remain metaphysical doctrines of tragedy, empirical accounts of grief, clinical interpretations, legal culpability, and aesthetic genre theory as such. These rival domains may be relevant in other analyses, but they are not claimed here.

Λ (Non-event / residue) is one of the strongest operators in the case. Tragedy is not merely the presence of loss; it is also the structured absence of what would be expected or demanded within the frame. The expected event might be repair, reconciliation, restitution, forgiveness, recognition, explanation, or a resolving action. The tragic force lies in the fact that this event may not be available while its absence remains meaningful. Λ must therefore be calibrated carefully. A mere lack, delay, or missing detail is not yet tragedy. The absence must be framed, expected, and structurally consequential.

Α (Attractor) remains weak. “Tragedy” may function as a stabilized category across many possible situations, but the case supplies no repeated interaction, no role-script, no institutional routine, and no path-dependent scene. Α is therefore present only as weak category-level stabilization. This matters because Ω (Asymmetry) and Θ (Temporality) depend downstream on stabilized patterning. The analysis may discuss them, but it must not pretend that a full scene has been supplied.

Ω (Asymmetry) is active but under-specified. Guilt, responsibility, exposure, and loss imply possible asymmetries: who bears the loss, who can act, who is vulnerable, who is obligated, who can demand closure, who must live with non-closure. But no concrete distribution is given. Ω therefore cannot become blame, culpability, hierarchy, or moral ranking. The correct reading is that tragedy often invites asymmetry analysis, but this case does not provide enough scene data to assign asymmetry.

Θ (Temporality) is central because irreversibility is explicit. Tragedy depends on what has happened, what cannot be undone, what persists, and what later action cannot fully repair. Θ is not merely chronology. It marks irreversible residue and temporal consequence. The case is therefore temporally structured even without a concrete timeline. At the same time, because no scene is supplied, Θ cannot become a responsibility horizon, a lock-in sequence, or a measurable trajectory.

Φ (Recontextualization) is active but bounded. The case itself asks how situations of loss, conflict, guilt, responsibility, and irreversibility can be recontextualized under the tragic category. But Φ must not become a cure. To call something tragic may clarify why closure is unavailable; it does not thereby repair the loss. The major risk is over-assimilation: every rupture, resistance, or rival framing could be treated as something PMS has already absorbed. That would erase the very residue the case is designed to preserve.

Χ (Distance) is the main application discipline. The case involves language that can easily harden into verdict: guilt, responsibility, tragedy, irreversibility, non-resolution. Χ prevents the analysis from becoming a tribunal or a closure machine. It allows the category to be examined without immediately assigning blame, ranking persons, or declaring finality. Yet Χ is not indifference. It does not neutralize loss or responsibility. It holds them without forcing them into premature resolution.

Σ (Integration) remains limited. PMS can produce a coherent reading of tragedy: framed difference, weak closure-pressure, meaningful non-event, weak attractor status, possible asymmetry, irreversible temporality, bounded recontextualization, and reflective distance. But tragic coherence is not tragic resolution. If Σ is treated as success, the case is misread. The analysis can integrate enough to map the structure, but it must not claim that the tragic residue has been reconciled.

Ψ (Self-binding) is weak and conditional. Responsibility is named in the case material, but no concrete agent, promise, obligation, role, or self-relation is supplied. Ψ can therefore appear only as a possible future operator if a concrete scene provides self-binding commitments. It also applies reflexively to the analysis: the analysis must remain self-bound by its own limits. But SELF_FIXPOINT cannot be derived from the category itself.

The resulting PMS movement can be rendered as:

Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ

Α and Ω appear in the formula for dependency hygiene, but both remain weak or under-specified. Σ and Ψ are not part of the minimal final movement because integration and self-binding require stronger conditions than the case supplies. This formula shows tragedy as framed difference under weak closure-pressure, meaningful non-event, weak category-level stabilization, under-specified asymmetry, and irreversible temporality, held through bounded recontextualization and reflective distance.

The same discipline governs the derived structures. Awareness_A is available only as structural awareness of framed distinctions across time, not as phenomenological consciousness. Coherence_C is active but limited: the tragic configuration can be intelligible without being resolved. Responsibility_R remains weak because responsibility-language does not by itself establish calibrated Ω or concrete Ψ. Action_E is under-specified because the case concerns situations where no fully resolving action may be available. Dignity_in_Practice_D functions only as an application constraint against shaming, ranking, or person-near closure, not as an ontological dignity claim. SELF_FIXPOINT is not derivable because the case supplies no concrete self-binding agent, role-continuity, or autobiographical structure.

PMS-CONFLICT sharpens one part of this structure. PMS Core shows tragedy as meaningful non-resolution under irreversibility. PMS-CONFLICT asks whether that non-resolution becomes conflict-legible as stabilized incompatibility under binding, time, cost, exposure, or terminal descriptive positioning. In this case, the answer is weak and conceptual. The material does not show concrete bindings, asymmetric exit costs, exposure structures, publicness dynamics, role-specific obligations, or a stabilized conflict attractor.

The **CONFLICT terminal core** signature is therefore weak. It would require Σ under load with Ψ-binding, Ω-asymmetry, Θ-temporality, and priced or asymmetric Χ. Here, Θ is central, Σ is limited, Ω is under-specified, Ψ is weak, and Χ is analytic distance rather than priced exit. The signature can illuminate possible non-integrability, but it cannot establish a terminal conflict regime.

The **CONFLICT stabilized pattern** signature is also weak. A stabilized conflict attractor would require recurrent incompatibility under asymmetry, time, binding, and costly distance. The category of tragedy may stabilize interpretively, but no repeated conflict field is supplied. The overlay must not treat the tragic category itself as a demonstrated attractor.

The **binding collision** signature is risk-only. Tragedy often evokes incompatible obligations, responsibility without repair, guilt without reconciliation, or duty under loss. But no concrete commitments are supplied. Binding collision may be illustrated, but it cannot be found. **Asymmetry lock-in** is not applicable as a finding because no unequal exit or distance cost is supplied. **Exposure capture** is a transfer risk rather than a case finding: tragedy-language may harden if excerpted publicly. **Dignity collapse** is likewise a misuse risk, not an active structure.

The overlay also adds scale and boundary sensitivities that matter even though they do not become active findings. Θ-history scaling is not applicable because the case gives no concrete regime history, acceleration pattern, or young attractor field. Institutionalized Χ scaling is not applicable because no institutional distance regime, mandate, or coercive distance structure is supplied. Application-boundary scaling, however, is active as a safety interface: the case remains descriptive, private, reversible, and non-person-near, while any public, institutional, legal, diagnostic, disciplinary, governance, or person-near reuse would require a new mode declaration and misuse-gradient review. This is why publicness and transmission are not afterthoughts. A phrase that is safe inside a bounded conceptual artefact may become unsafe if it travels without the boundary clauses that make it reversible.

The downstream MIP result is also case-specific: MIP does not score the case. A-score, M-score, IA-box verdict, D-profile, and D-Quick-Grid are not assigned because the case is not an enactment and supplies no role-holder, evidence, time window, publicness field, or concrete asymmetry. This non-assignment is not a low score. It is the correct result of the case type. MIP adds working-note discipline: the analysis is structurally mature only insofar as it refuses to become a verdict.

AHP then hardens the analysis artefact rather than the case itself. Its main case-specific result is that the prior MIP output is high-precision as a private working note, but remains vulnerable to public excerpting, term hardening, false evidence-completeness, missing concrete-scene transition gates, untested rival readings, score-hygiene confusion, and D-profile boundary drift. AHP does not rescore, does not activate D, does not upgrade transmission status, and does not create new case findings. Its contribution is to protect the article’s language from becoming the closure-by-authority that the case itself resists.

##### What the analysis shows

The analysis shows that tragedy can be mapped as meaningful non-resolution without being solved. PMS Core makes visible the structure by which a category can hold loss, conflict, guilt, responsibility, and irreversibility together without making them fully integrable. The closure that is possible is structural legibility: the case can show why the tragic frame is not arbitrary, why absence matters, why irreversibility matters, and why repair may remain meaningful without becoming total.

The analysis also shows where closure is not possible. Λ-residue remains visible where the expected event does not occur and cannot simply be replaced by explanation. Σ remains limited because coherence is not reconciliation. Ψ remains conditional because responsibility-language does not by itself establish self-binding. Ω remains under-specified because asymmetry is thematically present but not scene-calibrated. PMS-CONFLICT can sharpen possible non-integrability, but it cannot turn weak terminality into finality.

The case-specific weakening conditions follow from this structure. The PMS reading would weaken if no structured expectation exists, if no temporal irreversibility or persistence is present, if no asymmetry of exposure or obligation can be identified in a concrete case, or if the situation is fully resolvable without remainder. It would become under-discriminating if every unresolved difficulty were called tragic. It would become over-assimilative if every rival reading were treated as merely incomplete PMS.

The CONFLICT overlay would weaken if no stabilized incompatibility appears. A full CONFLICT regime would require concrete bindings, role structures, repeated incompatibility, asymmetry, temporal load, and costly or asymmetric distance. Without those, conflict language must remain weak or illustrative. Terminality must not be confused with finality.

The MIP and AHP layers show that formal completeness is not evidence completeness. A full template can be filled while still refusing score, verdict, and D activation. A high-precision artefact can still require hardening against transmission risk. The result is not stronger authority but clearer boundary discipline.

The main rival readings remain open. Tragedy may be read aesthetically as genre, ethically as collision among legitimate obligations, metaphysically as finitude or fate, theologically as divine silence or fallenness, phenomenologically as lived grief or rupture, legally as culpability or accountability, linguistically as a practice of naming, or anti-formally as what resists capture. PMS does not defeat these readings. It preserves one structural projection among them.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive misuse risk in this case is that tragedy-language can become closure-language. Terms such as “tragic,” “irreversible,” “guilt,” “responsibility,” “terminal,” and “non-integrable” can sound final even when they are used structurally and weakly. This case therefore requires a specific boundary: tragic legibility is not tragic proof; possible non-integrability is not final impossibility; responsibility-pressure is not culpability; terminality is not finality.

The case also requires a specific score-hygiene boundary. Because MIP appears in the stack, non-assignment must not be mistaken for a low score. “Weak,” “under-specified,” “not applicable,” and “risk-only” are not negative ratings. They mark the absence of concrete scene conditions.

Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological. It protects the analysis from shaming, ranking, humiliation, and person-near labeling. It does not become an ontological dignity claim, a D-profile, or a D-Quick-Grid result. This is especially important because tragedy-language can easily attach to persons as a public label.

The examples below are illustrative. They demonstrate the structural movement, but they do not supply evidence for PMS, do not decide concrete cases, and do not create new claims.

##### Example 1 — The apology that cannot restore

A person breaks an object that had significance for another person: not because of malice, not because of a dramatic betrayal, but through an ordinary failure of attention. The object can be replaced materially, and an apology can be offered sincerely. Yet the original object, with its history and singular relation to the other person, cannot be restored. The apology matters. The replacement may matter. A new practice of care may matter. But none of these actions makes the loss disappear as loss.

The example demonstrates why tragedy cannot be reduced to the absence of action. There is action: apology, repair attempt, changed conduct. But the action does not fully resolve the structure. The tragic element lies in the persistence of meaningful non-restoration. The case is not tragic because no one does anything; it is tragic because what can be done does not undo what cannot be undone.

At the same time, the vignette must not be overread. Not every broken object is tragic, and not every irreversible loss warrants the tragic category. The object becomes structurally relevant only because it is framed by a relation, a history, and an expectation that cannot be reproduced by replacement. The example therefore illustrates the minimum PMS pressure: Δ distinguishes replacement from restoration, □ makes the distinction matter, Λ marks the unavailable return of the original, and Θ preserves the irreversible residue. Without that framed residue, the example would be only an ordinary accident.

The PMS Core hooks are clear. **Δ** appears in the distinction between material replacement and historical restoration. **∇** appears as the pressure to repair. **□** is the relational frame in which the object matters. **Λ** is the missing event: the original cannot return. **Θ** marks irreversibility. **Φ** may recontextualize the event as accident, negligence, apology, or learning, but no recontextualization fully restores the original relation. **Χ** is required so the analysis does not rush into blame, exoneration, or sentimental closure. **Σ** remains limited: a coherent account can be formed, but coherence is not restoration. **Ψ** would require further concrete commitments and cannot be inferred from the vignette alone.

The CONFLICT overlay is only weakly relevant. There may be conflict, but no stabilized conflict attractor, binding collision, or asymmetry lock-in is established. MIP would not assign scores from this vignette; AHP would warn that terms such as “responsibility” or “irreversibility” must not be excerpted into blame.

Forbidden claims are therefore central. The example does not establish legal liability, moral guilt, psychological defect, or metaphysical tragedy. It does not rank either person. It does not show that apology is useless. It shows only that meaningful repair and irreversible residue can coexist.

The non-capture note is that lived grief over the object may exceed PMS rendering. The article takeaway is simple: tragedy becomes visible where repair remains meaningful but not total.

##### Example 2 — The two duties that cannot both be carried

A caregiver has promised to remain present for an aging parent during a difficult medical transition. At the same time, the caregiver has a professional obligation to appear for a critical responsibility that cannot easily be transferred. Both obligations are real within their frames. Neither is trivial. The caregiver does not face a simple preference conflict between comfort and inconvenience, nor a clear moral hierarchy where one duty obviously cancels the other. Whichever path is taken, something remains uncarried.

This vignette demonstrates the kind of structure that can make tragedy conflict-legible without turning the example into a verdict. There is loss even if the caregiver acts with seriousness. There may be guilt even if no malicious intention exists. There may be responsibility even if full satisfaction of all obligations is impossible. The point is not to decide what the caregiver should do, but to show how tragedy can emerge where multiple meaningful bindings cannot be jointly integrated without remainder.

PMS Core hooks begin with **Δ**, the distinction between the two obligations and their incompatible demands. **∇** appears as the pressure to decide and to restore coherence. **□** matters because each duty belongs to a different frame: care, family, profession, institutional reliability. **Λ** appears as the missing event of full joint fulfilment. **Α** could become relevant if such conflicts recur, but it is not established from one vignette. **Ω** is possible but must be calibrated: who has flexibility, who bears cost, who can forgive, who cannot? **Θ** is active because the obligations unfold over time and because some missed presence cannot be recovered later. **Φ** may reframe the situation as sacrifice, failure, necessity, or misaligned systems. **Χ** is crucial because the analysis must not become a tribunal. **Σ** is limited: the caregiver may integrate reasons enough to act, but not enough to erase remainder. **Ψ** is possible because promises and role commitments are involved, but a full self-binding analysis would require a richer scene.

PMS-CONFLICT adds a useful but bounded lens. This could be read as a possible **binding collision**, but only as an illustrative structure. It is not a finding of drift, immaturity, betrayal, or guilt. The vignette does not yet show whether the obligations are truly non-integrable, whether one frame has priority, whether others could share the burden, whether institutional design produced the conflict, or whether a later action could partially reopen the field. The **CONFLICT terminal core** therefore remains unavailable unless the scene shows sustained incompatibility under asymmetry, time, binding pressure, and priced or asymmetric distance. MIP would require concrete evidence before any A/M/IA/D analysis. AHP would mark public retelling as risky if the caregiver were identifiable, because a compact illustration of tragic pressure could quickly harden into a judgment about loyalty, failure, or responsibility.

Forbidden claims are explicit: the example does not say which duty is higher, who is blameworthy, whether the caregiver acted maturely, or whether the conflict is legally relevant. The non-capture note is that ethical theory may preserve normative features PMS does not decide. The article takeaway is that tragedy can arise where responsibility is not absent, yet no available integration fully carries every binding.

##### Example 3 — The public sentence that hardens too quickly

A short passage from a structural analysis says: “the situation is tragically legible as possible non-integrability.” Within the full article, the sentence is careful. It means that, under the PMS-CONFLICT overlay, a configuration may become understandable as a case where integration does not fully restore shared continuation. It is not a verdict. It is not a diagnosis. It is not a claim that repair is impossible. It is not a judgment about persons.

Now imagine the sentence copied into a public discussion without its surrounding boundary clauses. The phrase “tragically legible” is read as “the people involved are trapped.” “Possible non-integrability” is read as “nothing more can be done.” A weak conceptual marker becomes a public conclusion. A guardrail-bound phrase becomes an authority-sounding label. The analysis has not changed internally, but its transmission context has changed its risk.

This example demonstrates why MIP and AHP were included in the stack. PMS Core can mark **Λ** and **Θ**; PMS-CONFLICT can mark weak terminality or non-integrability; but MIP and AHP are needed to protect the analysis artefact from misuse in transmission. The vignette is not about the original tragic situation. It is about the analysis itself becoming risky when excerpted.

The PMS hooks are indirect but important. **□** shifts: a private conceptual working note becomes public discourse. **Ω** shifts: the person quoting the analysis may gain interpretive authority over those described. **Θ** shifts: a sentence can persist as record. **Φ** shifts: structural language becomes social judgment. **Χ** may fail if the excerpt no longer carries distance, reversibility, and boundary discipline. **Σ** is falsely overread as completed interpretation. **Ψ** may appear if the label hardens into an institutional or reputational commitment.

The CONFLICT overlay marks this as **exposure capture risk**, not exposure capture finding. MIP would keep the artefact in working-note status unless publicness and misuse-gradient review were performed. AHP directly identifies this kind of public excerpting as a high-severity attack surface and recommends an excerpt boundary: the passage is not a score, diagnosis, responsibility finding, D-profile, IA verdict, legal claim, implementation claim, or PMS/MIP validation.

Forbidden claims are the lesson. The example does not show that public discussion is always invalid, that tragedy-language must never be used, or that structural analysis should remain private forever. It shows only that transmission changes risk. The non-capture note is that public language has rhetorical and social effects no structural formula fully controls. The article takeaway is that tragedy-language must travel with its guardrails, or it can become the closure-by-authority the analysis was designed to prevent.

##### Case takeaway

This case shows that tragedy can be made structurally legible without being solved. PMS Core maps the movement from difference and closure-pressure to framed non-event, irreversibility, bounded recontextualization, and distance. PMS-CONFLICT sharpens the possibility of non-integrability without turning that possibility into a terminal finding. MIP and AHP preserve the working-note and transmission discipline required when tragedy-language could otherwise harden into verdict.

The examples show the same methodological result at smaller scales. Repair may matter without restoring what is lost. Responsibility may remain meaningful without yielding a clean integration or blame finding. A careful sentence may become unsafe if transmission strips its boundaries. These examples prepare the later conclusion by showing why tragedy requires disciplined legibility rather than stronger closure.

The case should be used in the chapter as a test of non-totalizing analysis. It shows where PMS can map a closure-demand, where an overlay can sharpen a specific risk, and where downstream review must prevent the analysis from becoming authority. The remaining Λ-residue is not a failure of the method. It is the point at which the method must stop claiming more than it has shown.

#### 7.4.5 Structural injustice

##### Why this case is here

Structural injustice is included because it tests a closure demand that cannot be satisfied by returning too quickly to a single agent, a single intention, or a single wrongful act. The case asks how harms, disadvantages, responsibilities, or constraints can arise from social, institutional, or historical arrangements without becoming reducible to one culpable moment. It is therefore a central AXIOM case: the task is not to solve injustice metaphysically, empirically, legally, or diagnostically, but to map the kind of structure in which ordinary closure demands become unstable.

The difficulty is that structural injustice pulls in two directions at once. If the analysis stays only with individual action, it risks missing the force of repeated arrangements, institutional routines, inherited exclusions, delayed remedies, and asymmetrically distributed options. If the analysis moves too quickly into “structure,” it risks dissolving responsibility into background conditions or turning all participants into undifferentiated carriers of blame. The case therefore requires a grammar that can show arrangement-mediated harm without either collapsing into tribunal logic or evacuating responsibility entirely.

The case is a conceptual projection, not a concrete scene. No institution, legal record, affected group, historical dataset, policy mechanism, or named actor is supplied. This matters: PMS can render the structural movement of the category, but it cannot turn responsibility, action, asymmetry, or self-binding into scene-specific findings without additional evidence. Structural injustice can be made legible here; it cannot be adjudicated.

The case belongs in AXIOM because it makes visible a distinctive form of non-closure. The category demands more than agent-centered moral judgement, but less than a full metaphysical theory of collective responsibility. It requires attention to frames, absences, recurrences, asymmetries, temporal accumulation, recontextualization, reflective distance, weak integration, and conditional responsibility. It also requires preserving rival readings: legal, empirical, phenomenological, political, moral, and metaphysical accounts may each capture something not exhausted by PMS.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement begins with **Δ (Difference)**: the case distinguishes isolated wrongdoing from arrangement-mediated disadvantage. That difference is not merely descriptive. It changes the type of question being asked. In an isolated wrong, the analysis may ask who acted, with what intention, and in which moment. In structural injustice, the analysis must ask how frames, routines, histories, positions, institutions, and asymmetries distribute effects over time. This is the first structural displacement.

**∇ (Impulse)** is present only weakly. The case creates pressure to explain how injustice can be real when not reducible to one agent or act, and it creates pressure to understand responsibility without collapsing into guilt. But because no concrete action scene is supplied, ∇ remains conceptual pressure, not a live demand for decision, intervention, or verdict.

**□ (Frame)** is the main organizing operator. The frame is “structural injustice” as an arrangement-mediated category. It includes social, institutional, and historical arrangements; harms, disadvantages, responsibilities, and constraints; and the refusal to reduce the case to one intention or isolated act. It excludes, at this level, concrete legal liability, empirical causation, clinical diagnosis, person-ranking, metaphysical proof, and implementation design. The frame is strong enough to orient the analysis, but it is not a concrete institution or policy environment.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is especially important. Structural injustice often appears not only through what happens but through what fails to happen: missing recognition, missing remedy, missing access, missing review, missing explanation, missing participation, or missing correction within a frame that makes such events expectable. In this case, Λ remains partial. The material does not identify a specific expected event whose absence is established. It only shows why structured absence is relevant to the category. This prevents the analysis from treating every absence, disagreement, or missing piece of evidence as proof of structural injustice.

**Α (Attractor)** is likewise partial. Structural injustice usually requires more than a single bad outcome; it suggests recurrence, stabilization, role-script reproduction, or path-dependence. But the case supplies no empirical recurrence or concrete routine. Α therefore marks what a stronger case would need to show: repeated arrangement-mediated effects, not merely conceptual plausibility.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is active and central. The case concerns harms, disadvantages, responsibilities, and constraints, all of which imply possible asymmetry in exposure, credibility, access, vulnerability, authority, burden of proof, or capacity to contest. Yet Ω must remain structural. It cannot become blame, dignity-ranking, guilt, legal liability, or moral hierarchy. A stronger Ω analysis would have to identify who bears which costs, who controls which frame, who can exit, who can contest, who is heard, and who is delayed. The abstract case does not supply those details.

**Θ (Temporality)** is also active. Structural injustice is rarely legible as a single moment. It involves accumulation, inheritance, delay, institutional memory, path-dependence, repeated exposure, and the gradual closing or opening of revision windows. Here Θ is conceptually central, but unscaled: no timeline, duration, rate of hardening, or historical sequence is supplied.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is the structural move the case itself performs. It shifts analysis from isolated agent-intention or isolated act toward social, institutional, or historical arrangement. This makes the category visible, but it does not prove that the structural frame defeats all rival frames. The recontextualization must remain reversible: agent-centered, legal, empirical, phenomenological, political, and metaphysical readings may still preserve elements PMS does not capture.

**Χ (Distance)** is the operator that prevents the reading from becoming a closure machine. It holds the analysis away from verdict, blame, legal conclusion, diagnosis, public label, or implementation claim. This is not avoidance. It is the condition under which structural legibility remains usable rather than coercive. In this case, Χ is one of the strongest calibrations: the analysis repeatedly refuses to let the structural reading become empirical proof, legal judgement, person evaluation, or PMS validation.

**Σ (Integration)** is weak. PMS can produce a coherent structural reading, but coherence is not the same as full integration. The case does not supply enough material to integrate agency, causation, harm, responsibility, history, evidence, remedy, and rival theories into one stable account. Weak Σ is therefore not a defect; it is the correct result for an abstract conceptual case.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is also weak and conditional. The case mentions responsibilities, but no self-binding bearer is specified. Responsibility may later attach to a person, role, institution, office, collective, or procedure, but the current material does not identify such a bearer. Ψ cannot be inferred from the mere presence of harm or asymmetry. Structural responsibility remains possible, not assigned.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay adds a second layer where the case becomes conflict-sensitive. It does not replace PMS Core. It asks whether the structural configuration crosses thresholds of stabilized incompatibility: binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, terminal regime, or downstream docking. In this case, those thresholds are not crossed. **CONFLICT_terminal_core** is not applicable because Σ has not failed under concrete Ψ, Ω, Θ, and priced or asymmetric Χ. **CONFLICT_stabilized_pattern** is weak because recurrence of structural arrangements is conceptually relevant, but recurrence of conflict itself is not shown. **DRIFT_binding_collision** is not applicable because no concrete Ψ-bindings collide. **DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in**, **DRIFT_exposure_capture**, and **DRIFT_dignity_collapse** remain risk-only categories. They show what could become visible under publicness, institutionalization, or concrete role data; they are not findings.

MIP adds only the case-specific downstream result: the analysis remains a working note. No A-score or M-score is assigned, because there are no concrete roles, time windows, knowledge states, alternatives, or responsibility bearers. The IA-box remains unclear because no specific arrangement is available for assessing transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. Dignity-in-Practice is active only as protection: D₀ remains untouched, D₁/D₂ are not scored, and no public D-reading follows. This non-assignment is not a low score; it is the structurally correct refusal to score an abstract case.

AHP hardens the MIP analysis artefact rather than changing the case result. It marks the analysis as high precision because the artefact defines its terms, states its scope, names thresholds, links evidence and inference, preserves reversibility, and maintains language hygiene. It also identifies attack surfaces: the stack may look more authoritative than the material warrants; IA “unclear” may be misread as analytic weakness; D activation may be misread as a D-profile; and public excerpting could strip away boundaries. AHP therefore sharpens transmission discipline; it does not rescore, activate D, upgrade the working note, or create new findings. In this case, that hardening result is itself case-specific because the phrase “structural injustice” can travel as if it were already a finding. AHP’s value is to keep that travel visible as a transmission risk rather than allowing precision to become authority.

##### What the analysis shows

The central structural movement is this: structural injustice is readable as a frame-bound category in which arrangement-mediated patterns may stabilize asymmetries across time, while recontextualization shifts analysis away from isolated agent-intention models and distance prevents that shift from becoming verdict, proof, or authority.

PMS Core shows how the category becomes legible. Δ separates isolated wrongdoing from arrangement-mediated harm. □ holds the structural frame. Λ and Α indicate possible structured absence and recurrence. Ω and Θ carry asymmetry and temporal accumulation. Φ performs the shift into structural explanation. Χ protects against collapse into judgement. Σ and Ψ remain weak because integration and self-binding require more than conceptual fit.

PMS-CONFLICT shows how the case could become conflict-sensitive without establishing conflict. Structural injustice may involve mutually incompatible frames of agency, responsibility, harm, institutional history, recognition, and distribution. But the current case does not establish terminality, binding collision, active exposure capture, or priced distance. The overlay’s value lies in marking those thresholds clearly.

MIP shows that the case should remain a working note rather than a scored or transmissible judgement. It refuses A-score, M-score, IA classification, and D-profile because the required concrete material is absent. AHP then confirms that the artefact is strong as a bounded analysis but still vulnerable to overreading if its boundaries are stripped away.

The possible closure is therefore methodological and structural: the case can be mapped. It cannot be settled. Λ-residue remains visible wherever the analysis lacks concrete scene data, affected perspectives, empirical evidence, responsibility bearers, legal standards, or rival-framework comparison.

The reading would weaken if no relevant social, institutional, historical, or normative frame could be specified beyond vague background. It would become under-discriminating if it could not distinguish structural injustice from isolated wrongdoing, accidental aggregation, ordinary inequality, lawful but non-injurious asymmetry, legal liability, moral blame, or metaphysical accounts of justice. It would become over-assimilative if resistance, disagreement, rupture, rival framing, missing evidence, or unresolved absence were automatically translated into PMS drift, failed integration, incomplete calibration, or recontextualization. Stronger claims would require concrete scene data: actual arrangements, affected positions, recurrence, temporality, role-bearers, publicness conditions, responsibility pathways, and domain-specific standards.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive danger of this case is not merely generic misuse; it is **blame transfer through structural language**. “Structure” can be used to dissolve all responsibility into background conditions, or it can be used to accuse everyone associated with a structure. Both moves are invalid. The case must preserve the difference between structural explanation, responsibility calibration, guilt, legal liability, and public accusation.

A second case-specific danger is **coherence inflation**. Structural injustice fits PMS well because it is already structurally formulated. That fit must not be mistaken for proof. A smooth PMS chain may still be under-discriminating if it cannot separate structural injustice from ordinary inequality, accidental aggregation, vague background harm, legal liability, moral blame, or metaphysical accounts of justice.

A third danger is **transmission hardening**. A careful private analysis of structural injustice can become a public label if excerpted without its boundaries. This is why PMS-CONFLICT’s exposure-capture risk, MIP’s working-note status, and AHP’s attack-surface notes are case-relevant rather than generic. The phrase “structural injustice” can travel faster than its evidence and become accusation, stigma, or institutional pressure.

Finally, rival readings remain open. Legal, empirical, phenomenological, political, moral, and metaphysical accounts are not failed PMS readings. They may preserve duties, data, lived harm, domination, recognition, or collective agency questions that PMS maps but does not settle.

##### Example 1 — The admissions rule that never names anyone

A university department uses an admissions procedure that appears formally neutral. Applicants are ranked by prior institutional affiliation, recommendation letters, uninterrupted academic trajectory, and evidence of early access to research networks. No member of the admissions committee intends to exclude anyone. No rule explicitly names class background, disability, migration history, care obligations, or regional inequality. Each reviewer can plausibly say that they assessed only the documents before them.

Over several years, however, the same pattern recurs. Applicants from less well-connected institutions are rarely admitted. Applicants with interrupted trajectories are treated as “less competitive” even where the interruption reflects care work, illness, displacement, or lack of early access to academic mentoring. Recommendation letters from prestigious networks carry implicit credibility that other letters do not. The result is not easily reducible to one malicious decision, one explicit discriminatory rule, or one isolated wrongful act. Yet the arrangement may still produce patterned disadvantage.

This vignette demonstrates the central category problem of structural injustice. The question is not whether one reviewer secretly intended exclusion. Nor is it whether a legal violation has already been established. The question is how an arrangement can distribute opportunity, credibility, risk, and burden without a single decisive wrongdoer. The example makes visible why the case cannot be handled only through the agent-intention frame. If the analysis asks only, “Who intended to exclude?”, it may miss the way the frame itself filters relevance.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. Δ (Difference) distinguishes isolated wrongful act from arrangement-mediated disadvantage. □ (Frame) is the admissions frame: the rules, credibility markers, institutional expectations, and role-space in which applications are read. Λ (Non-event / residue) appears as possible structured absence: missing recognition of interrupted trajectories, missing access to recommendation networks, missing review of whether prestige is being used as proxy for merit. Α (Attractor) appears if the pattern recurs over cycles and stabilizes into routine. Ω (Asymmetry) appears in unequal credibility, unequal access to preparatory resources, unequal ability to absorb interruption, and unequal burden of proof. Θ (Temporality) appears because the disadvantage is cumulative: early access, institutional prestige, and uninterrupted trajectory compound over time. Φ (Recontextualization) appears when the case is recontextualized from “neutral individual assessment” to “arrangement-mediated opportunity sorting.” Χ (Distance) is required so that the analysis does not become a tribunal against individual reviewers. Σ (Integration) remains conditional because a full integrated account would require data, institutional history, comparison groups, and procedural analysis. Ψ (Self-binding) remains conditional because no self-binding responsibility bearer is yet specified: it may later be a committee, a faculty, an institution, a policy office, or no single bearer in the strong PMS sense.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant only as risk visibility. The example could become conflict-relevant if merit, fairness, institutional autonomy, historical exclusion, and responsibility attribution stabilize into mutually incompatible frames. But the vignette does not establish terminal conflict. There is no binding collision finding, no priced exit finding, and no active exposure capture. If the example were used publicly to shame named committee members, that would be a misuse of the overlay, not a valid CONFLICT application.

MIP would treat this as a possible future concrete case only if additional information were supplied. No A-score or M-score follows from the vignette. The IA-box cannot be completed without evidence about transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility of the admissions rule. Dignity-in-Practice would matter as a protection constraint: applicants must not be represented as deficient persons, and reviewers must not be converted into global blame objects without role-specific calibration. AHP would likely flag provenance and transmission risk if this example were inserted into a public article: the example must remain illustrative unless data are provided.

The forbidden claims are important. This example does not prove unlawful discrimination. It does not prove that any reviewer is biased, immoral, negligent, or diagnostically deficient. It does not prove that the admissions system is empirically unjust. It does not prove that prestige should never matter. It does not establish legal liability, metaphysical responsibility, or policy remedy. It shows only how structural injustice becomes intelligible when formally neutral procedures distribute opportunity through recurrent frames, asymmetries, and temporal accumulation.

The non-capture note is equally important. A legal theorist might ask whether protected-category discrimination can be shown. A sociologist might demand longitudinal data. A political theorist might focus on class reproduction or institutional legitimacy. A phenomenological account might focus on how rejected applicants experience invisibility or humiliation. PMS does not defeat those readings. It prepares the structural map on which their questions can be located.

The article takeaway is that structural injustice becomes visible when the analysis shifts from intention alone to frame, recurrence, asymmetry, and time — but that shift still does not authorize blame, liability, diagnosis, scoring, or closure.

##### Example 2 — The complaint channel that formally exists

A public institution has a complaint procedure for residents who believe they were treated unfairly. The procedure is documented on the website, the form is available, and a response deadline exists. Formally, therefore, the institution can say that remedy is available. No one is barred from filing a complaint. No official explicitly refuses recognition. No policy states that some residents matter less than others.

In practice, the procedure is difficult to use. The form requires technical language. Supporting documents must be uploaded in a format that many residents cannot easily produce. Responses are brief and cite internal categories that are not explained. Appeals are possible but must be filed within a short deadline, while the institution itself may take months to answer. Residents who have stable time, language skills, administrative experience, and legal support can navigate the channel. Others can formally enter the procedure but cannot realistically make it work.

This vignette demonstrates why Λ, structured absence, is crucial but dangerous. The complaint channel exists; therefore the injustice, if any, is not simply that there is no procedure. The relevant absence is more subtle: missing intelligibility, missing practical access, missing explanation, missing time symmetry, missing support, missing effective reversibility. PMS allows such absences to be analyzed only if they are anchored in a frame. Here the frame is the institution’s own promise of review. The absence matters because the procedure creates an expectation: complaints should be not only formally receivable but practically contestable.

The PMS Core hooks unfold in order. Δ (Difference) distinguishes formal availability from real accessibility. ∇ (Impulse) appears as the pressure to contest a decision or seek review. □ (Frame) is the complaint procedure as institutional frame. Λ (Non-event / residue) appears where expected explanation, access, or remedy fails to materialize in practice. Α (Attractor) would be established only if this pattern recurs across cases rather than appearing once. Ω (Asymmetry) appears in the asymmetry between institutional time and resident time, institutional language and resident language, institutional record control and resident evidence burdens. Θ (Temporality) appears in delay, appeal deadlines, accumulated fatigue, and the way procedural burdens compound. Φ (Recontextualization) recontextualizes the issue from “a remedy exists” to “the remedy may be structurally unavailable to some positions.” Χ (Distance) prevents the analysis from immediately declaring bad faith or legal violation. Σ (Integration) remains conditional because an integrated account would need records, user experiences, procedural data, and comparison with administrative constraints. Ψ (Self-binding) remains conditional because responsibility requires specifying whether the institution, office, administrators, designers, policymakers, or another role is self-bound to remedy access.

This example also shows why dependency shortcuts would be invalid. It would be too quick to move from “some residents struggle” to “the institution is guilty.” It would also be too quick to move from “the procedure exists” to “the institution is fair.” PMS forces the analysis to stay between these closures. The structural question is whether the frame’s own promise generates expectations that are not practically fulfilled, whether this failure recurs, whether it distributes burdens asymmetrically, and whether it persists over time.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant as a risk layer. Conflict may arise if institutional actors frame the procedure as adequate because it exists, while residents frame it as inaccessible because it cannot be effectively used. These frames can become locally coherent yet mutually non-integrable. Still, the example does not establish CONFLICT_terminal_core. It does not show failed Σ under binding, costly Χ, and sustained Ψ collision. DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in is a risk only: if residents bear high costs for contesting while the institution bears low costs for delay, lock-in may become relevant. DRIFT_exposure_capture is also only a transfer risk: if complaints become public records or residents are exposed through the process, the analysis would require a stronger publicness and misuse-gradient review.

MIP would treat the example as requiring careful IA-box work. Transparency may be partial: the procedure is published, but its categories may be opaque. Justification may be unclear: the institution may have reasons for document requirements, but those reasons must be tested against access burdens. Time-boundedness is asymmetrical if resident deadlines are short while institutional delays are long. Reversibility may be formal rather than real if appeals are too hard to use. Yet no IA verdict follows from the vignette alone. The example supplies a structure, not evidence. No A-score or M-score should be assigned. Dignity-in-Practice remains a guardrail: residents should not be portrayed as incompetent, and officials should not be portrayed as malicious without further evidence.

AHP would likely recommend hardening if this example were used in the article. The article should clearly distinguish illustrative structure from empirical finding. It should state that “formal availability versus practical accessibility” is the analytic distinction, not a conclusion that this particular institution is unjust. It should also prevent high coherence from becoming authority: the example is readable, but readability is not proof.

Forbidden claims include legal invalidity, administrative misconduct, intentional exclusion, clinical stress claims, moral inferiority, dignity loss, or policy prescription. The example does not prove that the complaint channel is unlawful or unjust in a given real-world setting. It does not show that any official intended harm. It does not establish that all procedural complexity is injustice. It does not prescribe reform. It shows what a structural analysis would need to ask.

The non-capture note should remain visible. Administrative law might frame the issue through due process, standing, deadlines, and reason-giving. Empirical research might measure who uses the procedure and with what outcomes. A phenomenological account might describe exhaustion, humiliation, or distrust. A political account might treat the procedure as legitimacy management. PMS maps the structural conditions but does not absorb these rival analyses.

The article takeaway is that structural injustice may appear not where remedy is absent in name, but where the practical conditions of remedy are asymmetrically distributed. Even then, the correct output is not verdict but calibrated inquiry.

##### Example 3 — The public label that hardens faster than the analysis

A research group prepares an internal structural analysis of a city housing process. The draft says that certain residents may face arrangement-mediated disadvantage because eligibility categories, documentation requirements, waiting lists, and appeal procedures interact over time. The draft is careful. It states that no legal conclusion is being made, no person is being blamed, no empirical finding is final, and further data are required.

A short excerpt is then shared publicly without those boundaries. The excerpt says: “The housing office reproduces structural injustice.” In public discussion, this becomes a label. Some readers treat it as proof of wrongdoing by specific staff. Others treat it as an accusation that the whole institution is morally corrupt. Staff respond defensively. Residents who were supposed to be protected by the analysis become visible as examples of harm. The structural question hardens into a public conflict before the evidence, roles, and responsibility pathways have been calibrated.

This vignette demonstrates why the later layers of the completed analysis matter. PMS Core can make structural injustice legible, but legibility is not harmless. Once a structural reading travels into publicness, the analysis may change function. What was a reversible working note can become a durable public label. What was a conceptual frame can become an accusation. What was a cautious Φ recontextualization can become exposure capture. This is why the completed CONFLICT, MIP, and AHP analyses repeatedly emphasize transmission boundaries.

The PMS Core hooks begin with Δ (Difference): internal working note versus public label; structural description versus verdict; arrangement-mediated disadvantage versus staff blame. □ (Frame) shifts from research frame to public discourse frame. Λ (Non-event / residue) appears if the excerpt omits the original caveats, review conditions, and uncertainty markers. Α (Attractor) may form if the public label repeats and stabilizes. Ω (Asymmetry) appears in unequal exposure: residents, staff, and institutions do not bear the same costs of being named or framed. Θ (Temporality) appears as the label persists in records, search results, institutional memory, or public debate. Φ (Recontextualization) is central because the analysis is recontextualized from a bounded research note to public accusation. Χ (Distance) is endangered because public circulation reduces reflective distance. Σ (Integration) may become harder because positions polarize. Ψ (Self-binding) remains conditional: no binding collision is established merely because public conflict begins.

The PMS-CONFLICT hooks are especially important. This is a clean illustration of exposure capture as risk, not as automatic finding. In the original internal draft, publicness P is low and MG is low. After public excerpting, P rises. The misuse gradient rises because the language becomes person-near, institution-near, and potentially record-like. DRIFT_exposure_capture becomes a serious risk if interpretation hardens into enforcement affordance or reputational pressure before calibration. DRIFT_dignity_collapse becomes a guarded misuse risk if staff, residents, or groups are shamed, ranked, humiliated, or converted into moral symbols. DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in may become relevant if one side bears higher costs of correction or response. Still, terminal conflict is not established. The vignette shows a transmission danger, not a completed terminal regime.

MIP clarifies the use-status problem. The original analysis should have remained a working note until concrete evidence, publicness, role mapping, and IA conditions were reviewed. No A-score or M-score should be assigned from the public excerpt. The IA-box would require transparent criteria, justified use, time-boundedness, and reversibility. If the excerpt removes reversibility, the IA risk increases. Dignity-in-Practice becomes active as protection: residents must not be exposed as case material, staff must not be converted into blame objects, and public discourse must not turn structural analysis into humiliation.

AHP would identify several attack surfaces. First, the excerpt creates a model-stack authority effect: the phrase “structural injustice” sounds like a completed finding even if the original analysis was cautious. Second, it creates transmission-status ambiguity: readers no longer know that the analysis was a working note. Third, it creates D-language risk if harm and dignity language are used publicly without guardrails. Fourth, it creates a provenance problem: the public cannot see which claims came from data, which from structural theory, and which were hypotheses. The hardening response would be to require an excerpt preface: this is a private structural working note; it contains no person finding, no score, no legal conclusion, and no transmission upgrade.

Forbidden claims are extensive. The example does not show that the housing office is legally liable. It does not show that staff are blameworthy. It does not prove that residents were harmed in the described way. It does not establish that public criticism is always wrong. It does not establish exposure capture merely because a text becomes public. It does not show that structural-injustice language should be avoided. It shows only that public transfer changes the structural status of an analysis and can create risks that were not present in private review.

The non-capture note matters here because public conflict may also be politically legitimate. A political theorist might argue that public naming is necessary where institutions repeatedly avoid accountability. A legal advocate might argue that public pressure is part of remedy. A phenomenological account might argue that silence itself preserves humiliation. PMS-CONFLICT and MIP do not defeat those readings. They mark the additional burden: if one moves into publicness, one must own the change in exposure, reversibility, dignity risk, and misuse gradient.

The article takeaway is that structural legibility is not the same as safe transmission. A careful analysis can become a harmful or overreaching object if its boundaries are stripped away. For this case, that is not a side issue; it is part of the structural problem.

#### 7.4.6 Dirty hands / forced choice

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because dirty hands / forced choice is one of the clearest philosophical pressure points for AXIOM’s handling of non-closure. The case asks how morally difficult choices should be understood when loss, conflict, guilt, responsibility, or irreversibility may remain significant even after every available action, interpretation, or reconciliation has been exhausted. It is not simply a question about choosing between options. It is a question about what remains when the available options do not produce moral cleanliness.

The closure demand is strong. The case invites the reader to ask which action is right, who bears responsibility, whether guilt remains, whether a later interpretation can repair the situation, and whether the remainder can be integrated into a coherent account. AXIOM maps this demand rather than resolving it. The point is not to settle dirty hands metaphysically, ethically, legally, clinically, or empirically. The point is to show what kind of structure appears when action may be required and yet the situation remains marked by residue.

The case is a conceptual projection rather than a concrete scene. No actor, institution, action trace, harm distribution, role mandate, publicness regime, or evidence base is supplied. This matters for the whole reading. Operators such as responsibility, action, asymmetry, and self-binding can be rendered structurally, but they must not become scene-specific findings without additional evidence. The case therefore belongs in AXIOM as a disciplined test of structural visibility under non-resolution: it shows where PMS Core can clarify the field, where CONFLICT sharpens the possibility of stabilized incompatibility, and where downstream MIP / AHP layers must refuse scoring or verdict.

Dirty hands / forced choice is difficult because it resists the comforting separation between “the correct act” and “the remaining loss.” A choice may be necessary and still leave residue. A role may be responsible without being simply blameworthy. A frame may clarify the situation without removing tragedy. A coherent analysis may be possible without becoming morally sufficient. This is the central methodological value of the case: it forces the model to show that structural legibility is not the same as moral purification.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement is best rendered as a framed difference-field under residue, asymmetry, and time. In compressed form, the case uses Χ (Distance) around Φ (Recontextualization) of Λ (Non-event / residue), Ω (Asymmetry), and Θ (Temporality) within □ (Frame) over Δ (Difference), while Σ (Integration) and Ψ (Self-binding) remain conditional:

**Χ[Φ(Λ + Ω + Θ within □ over Δ)] → Σ? / Ψ?**

This formula is not a new dependency chain and does not replace PMS Base. It states the case movement: differentiated alternatives appear within a moral frame; the absence of a clean resolution becomes structurally meaningful; asymmetry and temporality intensify the field; recontextualization and distance are required; integration and self-binding remain conditional.

The field begins with **Δ (Difference)**. Dirty hands requires distinctions: possible actions and unavailable clean actions, loss and non-loss, responsibility and non-responsibility, guilt and absence of guilt, reconciliation and non-resolution. Without such distinctions, there is no forced choice. Yet Δ is abstract here. The case does not specify the alternatives or their feasibility, so difference is active but not concretely mapped.

**∇ (Impulse)** is weak-to-active. Forced choice implies pressure toward action, interpretation, or decision, but no motive, desire, urgency, psychological drive, or enacted movement is specified. The pressure is structural, not diagnostic. It would be overclaim to infer that an actor wanted, avoided, intended, or chose in a determinate way.

**□ (Frame)** is active as a moral-praxeological frame. Loss, guilt, conflict, responsibility, irreversibility, and reconciliation matter because the frame makes them relevant. But the frame is not a full moral theory. Dirty hands may be read tragically, deontologically, consequentially, existentially, politically, phenomenologically, or legally. PMS does not collapse these frames into one true account.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is the central operator. The case turns on what does not occur: no available action fully resolves the situation, no interpretation fully dissolves guilt or loss, no reconciliation fully repairs the conflict, and no final closure arrives. Λ is not merely missing information. It is meaningful absence inside the frame. The case becomes dirty-hands-like precisely because the expected clean event fails to arrive.

**Α (Attractor)** remains weak. The case material does not specify recurrence, pattern stabilization, routine, role-script formation, or repeated conflict. Dirty-hands discourse may culturally stabilize around guilt, necessity, sacrifice, or residue, but this abstract case does not show an attractor. Therefore later claims about stable patterns, lock-in, or conflict attractors must remain limited.

**Ω (Asymmetry)** is active but under-specified. Forced choice under responsibility and loss implies imbalance: one role may have power to act while another bears harm; one party may be more exposed; one obligation may dominate; one position may carry greater decision burden. But the case does not specify the type or direction of asymmetry. Ω may concern power, vulnerability, obligation, capacity, exposure, dependency, role burden, or liability. It must not be moralized as blame, superiority, guilt, authority, or victimhood.

**Θ (Temporality)** is active. Irreversibility, remaining guilt, continuing responsibility, and aftermath make the case more than a momentary choice. Dirty hands concerns what persists after action: consequence, memory, burden, residue, and possible non-repair. But no sequence is supplied. We do not know what happened before or after the choice, whether repair was attempted, or how quickly consequences locked in. Θ supports temporal extension, not a developmental or maturity finding.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is active because the case asks how such choices should be understood. The same forced-choice structure may be reframed as tragedy, necessity, culpability, sacrifice, office, existential responsibility, legal duress, or moral remainder. Φ makes these frame movements visible. It must not, however, absorb rival readings into PMS as if all resistance or residue were merely incomplete calibration.

**Χ (Distance)** is required. The case strongly tempts verdict: guilt, blame, heroism, corruption, necessity, sacrifice. Χ interrupts that rush. It keeps the analysis reversible and prevents responsibility language from becoming legal culpability, guilt-language from becoming diagnosis, asymmetry from becoming blame, and coherence from becoming solution. Χ is not detachment from loss; it is reflective restraint under pressure.

**Σ (Integration)** is weak-to-active but unresolved. The case invites integration of conflicting obligations, harms, reasons, and interpretations. Yet the very point of dirty hands is that integration may not fully resolve the remainder. A coherent structural map does not prove moral integration. Σ therefore remains marked with a question mark.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is possible but not established. Responsibility may persist across time and may involve binding oneself to ownership, repair, memory, or future restraint. But no actor, promise, role, duty, confession, institutional policy, or stable self-relation is supplied. Ψ applies most clearly to the analysis itself: the reading must self-bind to its own restraint.

The dependency discipline is crucial. Later operators cannot be used cheaply. Σ cannot be asserted because the analysis is coherent. Ψ cannot be asserted because responsibility is thematically present. Ω and Θ are conceptually active, but dependency-limited by weak Α. Non-capture is not failure; it is part of the case’s structure. PMS must not become a closure machine.

The derived structures follow the same discipline. **Awareness_A** is active at the abstract analysis level: the case sustains framed distinctions across time. But this is not actor awareness. **Coherence_C** is weak and unstable: the analysis may be coherent while the situation remains morally unresolved. **Responsibility_R** is active as a question but weak as attribution: it cannot become blame, liability, or guilt assignment. **Action_E** is under-specified because no action trace is provided. **Dignity_in_Practice_D** functions as an application constraint: asymmetry must be handled without humiliation, ranking, or person-level verdict. **SELF_FIXPOINT** remains analytic-only; the case does not establish stable self-binding.

PMS drift patterns remain risks, not findings. **AD_A>>E** is possible only in the sense that reflection may outgrow enactment in some concrete dirty-hands scenes, but no chronic delay or suppressed action is shown here. **AD_Sigma_low** is possible because integration is difficult, but weak integration is not automatically drift; it may be faithful to the phenomenon. Non-assignment is not a low score. Under-specification is not failure.

PMS-CONFLICT adds a threshold lens. It asks whether the case is merely a difficult choice, or whether it approaches stabilized incompatibility under binding, time, cost, and exposure. Its answer is restrained. **CONFLICT terminal core** is weak: Σ is unresolved, Ω and Θ are active, Χ is required, and Ψ is possible, but no concrete binding collision, priced Χ, asymmetric exit, exposure channel, recurrence, or terminal conflict scene is supplied. **DRIFT_binding_collision** is a risk, not a finding. **Asymmetry lock-in** is a risk, not a finding. **Exposure capture** is mainly a transmission risk if dirty-hands language travels into public or institutional contexts. **Dignity collapse** is not applicable internally, but remains a prohibited misuse pathway.

The CONFLICT layer therefore makes visible what PMS Core alone does not isolate: the threshold between residue and stabilized conflict. It prevents the analysis from moving too quickly from “there is moral remainder” to “there is terminal conflict,” or from “there are competing obligations” to “a binding collision has been found.” CONFLICT adds specificity by requiring specified bindings, specified cost, specified exit or distance conditions, specified exposure channels, and some evidence of stabilization or failed shared Σ.

The reading would weaken if the case turned out to be an ordinary solvable dilemma, a purely semantic dispute, a tragic accident without agency or responsibility structure, or a legal-forensic problem requiring external criteria. It would also weaken if no meaningful asymmetry, temporal persistence, structured absence, or unavailable clean resolution could be identified. A stronger reading would require concrete scene data: actors, role obligations, available alternatives, knowledge conditions, real power-to-act, predictability, action traces, affected parties, cost distributions, and evidence of integration attempts. Without such data, the correct status remains structural projection, not finding.

MIP adds a downstream guardrail rather than a score. It marks the case as too under-specified for an A-band, M-band, IA result, or D-profile. There is no concrete actor awareness, no role trajectory, no knowledge profile, no real-alternative map, no predictability assessment, no action trace, and no D1/D2 enactment. The MIP result is therefore refusal of assignment, not a hidden negative assessment. AHP then hardens the analysis artefact: its high Precision Heuristic means that the analysis is well-scoped and guarded, not that the case reading is validated.

##### What the analysis shows

The case shows that dirty hands / forced choice is structurally legible without becoming structurally purified. PMS Core maps the pressure field: differentiated alternatives inside a moral frame, meaningful non-resolution, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, and required distance. The central movement is not “problem → solution,” but “action-pressure → residue → bounded interpretation.”

The case also shows why integration must remain conditional. Σ is the desired but unstable site. The case may become more coherent as an analysis, yet still remain unresolved as a moral situation. This is not a defect of the reading. It is the phenomenon being mapped.

CONFLICT shows that dirty hands may approach conflict-legibility but does not thereby become terminal conflict. The overlay sharpens the question of binding collision, priced distance, exposure, and terminality, but the abstract case does not supply enough markers to activate those findings. Its final overlay signature remains weak:

**weak_CONFLICT_terminal_core / risk_DRIFT_binding_collision with active_Χ_guardrail and preserved_non_capture**

MIP shows that maturity and responsibility language must remain evidence-bound. A conceptual case can raise responsibility, but it cannot assign maturity, blame, IA status, or dignity profile. AHP shows that even a careful analysis needs hardening against overread: formal completeness may be mistaken for evidence, and precision may be mistaken for validation.

The closure that is possible here is cartographic closure: we can say what the structure is, what it requires, what it risks, and what evidence is missing. The closure that is not possible is moral, metaphysical, legal, clinical, empirical, or operational settlement.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive risk of this case is that dirty-hands language easily hardens into verdict. “Guilt” may be read as psychological fact. “Responsibility” may be read as legal culpability. “Conflict” may be read as pathology. “Residue” may be read as failure. “Terminality” may be read as finality. “Maturity” may be read as person evaluation. The case section must prevent each of these shifts.

Here, **Λ (Non-event / residue)** marks meaningful non-resolution; it does not prove tragedy as metaphysical fact. **Ω (Asymmetry)** marks structural imbalance; it does not assign blame. **Θ (Temporality)** marks persistence and irreversibility; it does not prove determinism or developmental maturity. **Σ (Integration)** remains unresolved; this does not mean immaturity or failure. **Ψ (Self-binding)** remains possible but unestablished; it does not prove guilt acceptance, ultimate authorship, or legal responsibility.

PMS-CONFLICT handles such as binding collision, exposure capture, and terminal regime remain overlay handles. They are not PMS operators and not verdict engines. MIP non-assignment is not a low score. AHP high precision is not validation of the case reading. The examples below illustrate these distinctions; they do not supply evidence for the abstract case.

##### Example 1 — The rescue choice that leaves a remainder

A rescue coordinator receives two simultaneous distress signals during a severe storm. One signal comes from a small vessel closer to shore; the other comes from a larger vessel farther out, carrying more people but requiring longer travel time. The available team cannot reach both in time. Weather conditions are worsening, communication is intermittent, and every delay changes the likely outcome. The coordinator must choose a route, knowing that the choice may leave some people without help.

This vignette is not a legal case, not an empirical claim, and not a moral verdict about the coordinator. It is a bounded illustration of dirty hands / forced choice: a situation where action is required, but no available action fully resolves the loss. Even if the coordinator chooses the route that appears best under the information available, the remaining loss does not automatically disappear. The example demonstrates the difference between structural legibility and moral purification.

In PMS Core terms, **Δ** appears in the distinction between the two possible rescue paths, between reachable and unreachable lives, and between action and delay. **∇** appears as pressure to decide under deteriorating conditions. **□** is the emergency-response frame: the relevant question is not abstract moral purity, but action under constrained rescue capacity. **Λ** is central because the expected clean event — saving everyone, or making a choice without remainder — does not occur. The absent rescue is not just missing information; it is a meaningful non-event inside the frame. **Ω** appears in unequal exposure: the coordinator has decision power, while those in danger bear the direct consequences. **Θ** appears because the decision unfolds under time pressure, worsening conditions, and irreversible aftermath.

The example also shows why **Φ**, **Χ**, **Σ**, and **Ψ** must be handled carefully. The event could later be recontextualized as tragedy, necessity, failure, courage, negligence, or institutional under-resourcing. That is a Φ problem. Χ is required so that the analysis does not collapse into a quick verdict. Σ may be attempted through an after-action review, but no integration can be presumed to erase the loss. Ψ might become relevant if the coordinator or institution takes responsibility for review, communication, or future preparedness, but the vignette alone does not establish a stable self-binding structure.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant only as a weak or threshold lens. There may be a possible binding collision: save the nearer vessel, or prioritize the larger number farther out. But the vignette does not prove a terminal conflict regime, stabilized incompatibility, or asymmetry lock-in. It merely illustrates why CONFLICT can ask sharper questions about binding, time, cost, and exposure.

MIP would not assign an A-band, M-band, IA result, or D-profile from this vignette alone. A maturity-in-practice assessment would require concrete data: what the coordinator knew, what alternatives were real, what protocols applied, what resources were available, what was predictable, and whether serious integration or review occurred. AHP, if applied, would harden the later analysis artefact; it would not validate the choice itself.

The forbidden claims are clear. The example does not show that the coordinator is guilty, innocent, mature, immature, negligent, heroic, or morally contaminated. It does not establish legal liability, clinical state, metaphysical tragedy, or policy validity. It does not validate PMS.

The non-capture zone remains important. A person affected by the loss may experience grief or anger that is not exhausted by operator mapping. A theological, deontological, consequentialist, or tragic-ethical account may preserve dimensions that PMS only marks structurally. The article takeaway is therefore restrained: the vignette shows how dirty hands can be structurally legible without becoming morally clean, legally settled, or fully integrated.

##### Example 2 — The confidentiality conflict in a care institution

A senior member of a care institution receives confidential information from one resident suggesting that another resident may soon be harmed. The information is incomplete. Revealing it may violate confidentiality and damage trust in the care relationship. Keeping it confidential may leave another person exposed to risk. The institution has general policies, but they do not clearly settle this specific configuration. The senior member must decide whether to disclose, escalate, delay, seek more information, or intervene indirectly.

This vignette is useful because it shows why PMS Core alone is not always the final analytic layer, while also showing why the add-on must remain restrained. PMS Core can map the case as a framed difference-field under structured absence, asymmetry, temporality, and contested integration. PMS-CONFLICT adds a more specific question: are two bindings colliding in a way that no shared Σ can integrate without unacceptable cost?

The possible bindings are visible but not automatically settled. One binding concerns confidentiality: the care role depends on trust, privacy, and non-exposure. Another binding concerns protection: the institution may have a responsibility to prevent foreseeable harm. These bindings may collide, but the example does not by itself prove that they are absolutely incompatible. More facts would be needed: the severity and likelihood of harm, the resident’s capacity, policy thresholds, available protective alternatives, consent possibilities, supervisory channels, and whether disclosure could be limited.

The PMS Core hooks are clear. **Δ** distinguishes confidentiality, disclosure, delay, and protection routes. **∇** is the pressure to act before harm occurs. **□** is the care-institution frame, with confidentiality and protection as relevant constraints. **Λ** appears in missing information: unclear likelihood, absent consent, absent policy clarity, and no clean option. **Ω** appears because the staff member and institution have role power, while residents may bear exposure and consequence. **Θ** appears because delay, disclosure, trust erosion, and harm risk unfold over time. **Φ** appears when the same act is reframed: disclosure may be seen as betrayal in one frame and protection in another. **Χ** is necessary to prevent urgency from becoming coercive certainty. **Σ** is attempted through policy interpretation and practical coordination, but may remain incomplete. **Ψ** may appear only if the institution binds itself to a decision, review process, or revised protocol.

The CONFLICT hooks are stronger here than in a purely abstract example, but still bounded. The vignette illustrates **risk_DRIFT_binding_collision**: two commitments may become jointly non-viable. It also illustrates possible **asymmetry lock-in** if one role can externalize risk while another bears exposure. It may contain **exposure capture** if the disclosure becomes a durable record or public accusation. Yet none of these is automatically a finding. The vignette must not be read as proof of terminal conflict, dignity collapse, negligence, or institutional guilt.

The example also shows why the CONFLICT layer is a threshold discipline rather than a stronger moral language. If the staff member discloses, the analysis must not immediately call the disclosure betrayal. If the staff member does not disclose, the analysis must not immediately call the non-disclosure abandonment. If the staff member delays, the analysis must not immediately call the delay avoidance. Each of those readings would require evidence about knowledge, policy, feasible alternatives, time pressure, probability of harm, available consultation, and the reversibility or irreversibility of each path. CONFLICT makes the incompatibility legible; it does not decide which binding wins.

MIP would be relevant only if the vignette were expanded into a concrete case. It would ask what the staff member knew, what alternatives were realistically available, what was predictable, what authority and resources existed, and whether serious integration attempts occurred. It would also check IA conditions: transparency, justification, time-boundness, and reversibility. The D-module should remain off unless concrete D1/D2 enactments are visible and the communication space is safe, role-bound, and time-windowed. AHP would only harden the later analysis artefact: it might flag whether the analysis separates observation from inference, whether “confidentiality” and “protection” are defined with thresholds, and whether the language avoids converting institutional difficulty into person-near fault.

Forbidden claims include: that disclosure is legally required; that non-disclosure is culpable; that the staff member is mature or immature; that the resident who gave information is reliable or unreliable; that institutional policy is valid or invalid; or that PMS-CONFLICT has solved the care dilemma. Those would require facts and external frameworks.

The non-capture note is essential. The lived trust relation, fear, vulnerability, and institutional history may matter in ways the structural example does not fully capture. Legal duties, professional ethics, and trauma-informed practice would require separate frameworks. The article takeaway is that CONFLICT sharpens the visibility of binding collision without converting it into verdict. It shows what would need to be specified before stronger claims could be made.

##### Example 3 — When “dirty hands” becomes a public label

A policy committee privately discusses a difficult decision that produced serious loss despite constrained options. In the internal review, one member describes the case as involving “dirty hands”: not to accuse a person, but to mark that the decision may have been necessary while still leaving moral residue. Later, a fragment of that internal phrase appears in a public summary without the surrounding boundaries. The phrase is then quoted as if the committee had admitted guilt, moral corruption, or legal wrongdoing.

This example demonstrates a transmission risk rather than a new first-order case finding. The original phrase may have been structurally careful inside a private review frame. Once excerpted publicly, however, the same phrase can change function. It can move from philosophical description to reputational pressure, from non-resolution to accusation, from structural residue to person-near verdict. That shift is precisely why the completed analyses treat publicness, misuse gradient, exposure capture, and Dignity-in-Practice as serious boundary concerns.

In PMS Core terms, **□** shifts. Inside the internal frame, “dirty hands” may have meant: a difficult forced choice left Λ-residue despite action. In the public frame, the same phrase may be received as confession, blame, or evidence. **Φ** is therefore central: the phrase is recontextualized. **Ω** changes because public audiences, media dynamics, institutions, and affected parties may have unequal power to impose meanings or bear consequences. **Θ** matters because public records persist; an excerpt can harden over time. **Λ** appears because the full context is absent. **Χ** is required but endangered: public pressure may reduce reflective distance. **Σ** may be replaced by slogan-level coherence, and **Ψ** may harden into institutional memory or reputational label.

The CONFLICT overlay is directly relevant. This is a potential **exposure capture** pattern: recontextualization under scaled exposure converts interpretation into enforcement affordance. It may also create a **misuse gradient** increase, because the phrase travels without mode declaration, publicness boundary, reversibility clause, or non-person guardrail. But even here, the example must remain careful. It does not prove that exposure capture has occurred in every public reading. It illustrates a pathway by which it can occur.

MIP would treat this as a transmission and publicness problem, not as a maturity score. It would ask whether the analysis is still private, whether it has become public or media-amplified, whether identifiable persons are exposed, whether contest and correction channels exist, and whether the phrase creates shame or irreversible labeling. It would not assign an A-band, M-band, IA result, or D-profile without concrete evidence. AHP would be relevant as an analysis-artifact hardening layer: it would flag attack surfaces such as excerpt sensitivity, validation overread, and formal-completeness risk.

Forbidden claims are especially important here. The public excerpt does not prove legal guilt. It does not prove moral corruption. It does not prove that the committee, a member, or any affected party has a defect, diagnosis, or maturity failure. It does not prove that the original analysis was invalid. It shows that transmission can change the function of language under publicness and asymmetry.

The non-capture note cuts both ways. Public outrage may include genuine harm, grief, distrust, or political accountability that the structural analysis does not settle. At the same time, structural terminology may be misused to intensify accusation beyond what the original frame warranted. PMS and CONFLICT do not decide which public response is justified; they make visible the shift in frame, exposure, and risk.

The article takeaway is that dirty-hands language is not safe merely because it is analytically careful at the point of production. Under Ω, Θ, Φ, and publicness amplification, even bounded language can harden into verdict. This is why the case requires misuse boundaries, transmission discipline, and AHP-style artefact hardening before any public or institutional reuse.

##### Case takeaway

Dirty hands / forced choice shows how AXIOM can preserve moral seriousness without converting it into closure. PMS Core makes the unresolved structure visible: Δ, □, Λ, Ω, Θ, Φ, and Χ carry the main movement, while Σ and Ψ remain conditional. PMS-CONFLICT sharpens the possible transition from residue to conflict-legibility, but keeps terminality, binding collision, and exposure capture below finding status unless additional markers are supplied. MIP refuses score-like conclusions because the case is not a concrete scene. AHP hardens the analysis against overread without validating the case.

The examples show three reusable forms of the same discipline. The rescue choice shows action under residue. The care-institution example shows possible binding collision without verdict. The public-label example shows how dirty-hands language can harden under exposure. Together they prepare the later chapter conclusion: structural legibility can increase without moral closure increasing with it.

The methodological result is restrained. This case should be used to show how closure-demand, responsibility pressure, and Λ-residue can be mapped without being dissolved. It leaves open rival tragic, deontological, consequentialist, existential, phenomenological, legal, theological, and anti-capture readings. These rivals are not defects in the PMS/CONFLICT rendering. A tragic reading may preserve the sense that some loss is not repaired by coherence. A deontological reading may preserve the claim that some actions remain wrong even under necessity. A consequentialist reading may preserve attention to loss minimization under constraint. A phenomenological reading may preserve lived guilt or anguish that PMS does not model as inner experience. A legal reading would require external legal criteria rather than PMS responsibility language. An anti-capture reading may insist that the residue is not merely an unintegrated structural remainder. The final point is not that PMS or CONFLICT solves dirty hands. The point is that the stack can show where the remainder must remain visible and where no framework should be allowed to make it disappear too cheaply.

#### 7.4.7 Asymmetry lock-in

##### Why this case is here

This case is here because it names a closure demand that cannot be handled by asking only what should be done or who is responsible. The structural difficulty is prior to that: some situations become morally difficult because positions within them do not share the same exposure, dependency, responsibility, or available exit across time. A situation may look formally revisable while, in practice, revision, interruption, distance, or exit becomes increasingly costly for one position and comparatively cheap for another.

The case therefore belongs in AXIOM as a map of **asymmetry under temporal hardening**. It shows why formal openness is not enough. A role may appear able to leave, object, pause, reframe, or withdraw, while the cost of doing so is distributed unevenly. That makes the case structurally difficult without automatically making it diagnostic, legal, empirical, or metaphysical. The task is not to solve asymmetry lock-in as a general moral problem. The task is to make visible how closure is demanded, where closure remains unwarranted, and where Λ-residue must remain in view.

The completed YAML marks this as a conceptual projection, not a concrete scene. That matters. There are no actors, events, institutions, communications, obligations, harms, or observed action sequences. PMS can render responsibility, action, asymmetry, and self-binding as structural questions, but they must not become scene-specific findings without additional evidence. This is why the case is mapped rather than solved.

##### Structural reading

The PMS Core movement begins with **Δ (Difference)**. The case depends on keeping distinctions apart: position is not exposure; exposure is not dependency; dependency is not responsibility; responsibility is not exit; formal exit is not real exit; revision is not interruption; interruption is not leaving. Without Δ, the case collapses into a vague moral difficulty. With Δ, the analysis can ask what kind of difference matters and why.

**∇ (Impulse)** appears as directional pressure: pressure to remain, leave, revise, interrupt, assume responsibility, delay, or stabilize. This remains structural rather than psychological. The case does not infer desire, fear, motive, avoidance, or bad faith. **□ (Frame)** then determines what these pressures mean. The same non-exit can appear differently in a care frame, legal frame, organizational frame, dependency frame, domination frame, or tragedy frame. The frame must therefore be specified before the analysis can become more than a conceptual map.

**Λ (Non-event / residue)** is relevant where revision, interruption, response, recognition, or exit is expected but does not occur. Yet Λ is not activated by absence alone. A missed exit is meaningful only if an exit was expected, promised, required, materially possible, formally available, or structurally relevant within the active frame. This calibration is central: silence, delay, refusal, rupture, or non-fit must not automatically become drift, failed integration, evasion, or immaturity.

**Α (Attractor)** is conceptually important because “lock-in” implies stabilization, recurrence, role-script formation, or path-dependence. But in this case Α remains conditional. The abstract material does not supply recurrence evidence. No sequence, repeated act, institutional routine, or stabilized role-script is documented. The analysis can say that lock-in would require attractor formation; it cannot say that such formation has occurred.

The central operators are **Ω (Asymmetry)** and **Θ (Temporality)**. Ω is explicit in the case material: asymmetries of position, exposure, dependency, responsibility, and available exit are named directly. But Ω is structural, not automatically condemnatory. Asymmetry may be harmful, protective, necessary, tragic, institutional, temporary, justified, unjustified, or contested. Θ is equally central because lock-in is temporal. The relevant question is not only who stands where, but how a configuration becomes harder to revise, interrupt, or leave over time. Still, duration is not lock-in by itself. Delay, commitment, care, duty, institutional continuity, or dependency may be temporally extended without becoming the kind of hardening the case is concerned with.

**Φ (Recontextualization)** is active as a calibration question. A situation may need to be reframed from duty to burden displacement, from care to dependency, from formal exit to asymmetrically priced exit, or from disagreement to structural exposure. But Φ can also overreach. Recontextualization must not absorb every resistance, rival framing, rupture, or non-fit into PMS vocabulary. Sometimes non-fit marks the limit of PMS capture rather than the need for more PMS.

**Χ (Distance)** is active both as an application condition and as a possible scene operator. The analysis must retain reflective distance: no fusion into verdict, urgency, enforcement, diagnosis, or person-level attribution. In a future concrete scene, Χ would also ask whether roles can step back, pause, refuse, reframe, or exit. But distance is not false neutrality. If one role can pause cheaply and another pays heavily for the same pause, Χ itself becomes asymmetrically distributed.

**Σ (Integration)** remains a question, not an achievement. The case involves elements that would need integration: dependency, responsibility, exposure, exit, temporality, possible non-events, rival frames, and dignity constraints. But a coherent PMS formula is not proof that integration has occurred. The analysis can be coherent while the moral situation remains unresolved, tragic, materially constrained, or better described by another grammar.

**Ψ (Self-binding)** is weak. Responsibility is thematically present, but self-binding is not established. Ψ would require a stable commitment to integrated structures across time. The abstract case provides no agent, promise, role ownership, self-relation, or enacted responsibility. Responsibility_R remains a question rather than a result, and SELF_FIXPOINT is not available as a case finding.

The compact PMS Core movement is therefore:

**Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ**, with **Φ, Χ, and Σ** as calibration structures and **Ψ** as a weak watch condition.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay sharpens one specific aspect of this movement: the possibility that distance, delay, revision, interruption, or exit becomes **asymmetrically priced** under Ω and Θ. Its strongest case-specific handle is **DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in**, but this remains an overlay shorthand, not a PMS operator and not a verdict. It names the structure in which Χ-availability becomes a power gradient rather than a shared stabilizing resource. This is the overlay’s contribution beyond PMS Core: it isolates the conflict-relevant risk that formal distance or formal exit may not be real distance or real exit for all positions.

Other CONFLICT handles remain weaker. Conflict attractor is not established because recurrence is not shown. Binding collision is not established because Ψ-level commitments are not specified. Exposure capture is not a feature of the abstract case, though it becomes relevant in the third example, where public circulation changes the function of the analysis. Dignity collapse remains a misuse risk, not a result. Terminal conflict is not reached: there is no failed Σ under sustained Θ/Ω/Ψ with costly or asymmetric Χ.

MIP adds only the case-specific maturity-in-practice result. Awareness is conceptually strong because the case structure is well differentiated. Coherence is useful but risky because the analysis can become too smooth. Responsibility is thematically relevant but not assignable. Power-to-act is central but under-specified because real levers, options, and exit conditions are not known. The MIP A-band should be read only as a **case-structure analysis-readiness band**, not as an actor A-score. M is not assignable to any actor; non-assignment is not a low score. The IA-box yields conceptual asymmetry risk, especially around time-boundedness and reversibility, but it is not a verdict. The D-module remains off as a person profile; Dignity-in-Practice remains a qualitative guardrail.

AHP hardens the artefact rather than changing the case result. Its specific finding is that the analysis is high-precision as a private working note, but its completeness is also its danger. A polished YAML-derived article can look more evidentially grounded than the abstract case permits. For that reason, any later excerpt or reuse should carry the substance of the AHP boundary: **abstract case-type; no actor score; M not assignable; D-module off; IA risk not verdict; working note only.**

##### What the analysis shows

The case shows how a closure demand can arise when asymmetry and time interact. PMS Core maps the movement from distinction, pressure, and frame toward possible non-event, stabilization, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and self-binding. But it also shows why later operators cannot be used cheaply. Σ cannot be inferred from coherence. Ψ cannot be inferred from responsibility language. Α cannot be inferred from one difficult moment. Λ cannot be inferred from absence alone.

The add-on reading shows that asymmetry lock-in is especially sensitive to Χ. If one position can delay, withdraw, reframe, or leave at lower cost than another, then distance itself becomes asymmetrical. PMS-CONFLICT makes this visible without converting it into proof of coercion, domination, legal captivity, or terminal conflict.

The MIP reading shows that the case is structurally analyzable but not assignable. It supports awareness and asymmetry hygiene, but it refuses person scoring, M-assignment, D-profile activation, and transmission as a decision basis. The AHP reading then shows that the article’s own form must remain guarded: structural completeness can become misleading if detached from its boundaries.

The closure available here is therefore limited and methodological. The case can be structurally rendered. It can be made more discriminating. It can identify what would need to be shown in a concrete scene. But it cannot close the moral, empirical, legal, phenomenological, or metaphysical problem.

This also preserves the main weakening conditions from the longer analysis. The PMS reading would weaken if no meaningful asymmetry of position, exposure, dependency, responsibility, or exit could be shown; if revision, interruption, or leaving were merely formally difficult but practically equal across positions; if no expected non-event were frame-relevant; if no recurrence or stabilization supported Α (Attractor); or if no temporal hardening supported Θ (Temporality). The CONFLICT overlay would weaken if distance, delay, revision, or exit were not asymmetrically priced. Rival readings remain open: care ethics may better preserve dependency and obligation; domination theory may better capture arbitrary power; legal analysis may be required for consent, coercion, rights, duty, or liability; phenomenology may better capture lived exposure; systems theory may better explain institutional path-dependence; and tragedy may preserve loss that no integration resolves. These are not defects in the PMS reading. They mark the boundary of what this chapter section can responsibly close.

##### Case-specific boundary

The distinctive risk of this case is that the phrase “asymmetry lock-in” can sound like a conclusion before the analysis has earned one. It can imply that someone was trapped, that someone trapped them, that exit was impossible, that responsibility failed, or that the situation was coercive. None of that follows from the abstract case.

The case-specific boundary is therefore: asymmetry language must not become blame language; lock-in language must not become proof of entrapment; non-exit must not become immaturity; coherence must not become adequacy; and CONFLICT shorthand must not become a PMS operator or verdict category. Dignity-in-Practice protects the handling of asymmetry; it does not rank persons or establish ontological dignity claims.

This boundary is not generic decoration. It is structurally necessary because the case itself is about unequal exposure, available exit, and the hardening of interpretations over time. The analysis can itself become part of the asymmetry if circulated without scope, evidence, reversibility, and dignity discipline.

##### Example 1 — Formal exit, real exposure

A small organization introduces a formal review channel for employees who feel overburdened by project assignments. On paper, anyone may request a review, pause a task, or ask for reassignment. The procedure is written clearly, and no rule explicitly punishes use of the channel.

A junior employee, however, depends on the same manager for references, future assignments, and contract renewal. The employee’s workload has become difficult to revise because each missed review point creates additional obligations: deadlines accumulate, other team members depend on the work, and the employee becomes the person who “knows the project best.” The manager can delay a decision without much visible cost. The employee can also delay, but delay increases exposure: the task remains theirs, the burden becomes more visible, and asking for review later may look like unreliability.

This vignette demonstrates the difference between **formal availability** and **real availability**. It does not establish coercion, exploitation, illegality, or blame. It shows only why “available exit” cannot be read from formal procedure alone. An exit route may exist as a rule while remaining unevenly priced as practice.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes formal exit from real exit, manager position from junior position, and procedural permission from practical exposure. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as pressure to continue, delay, request review, or remain silent. **□ (Frame)** is the organizational frame in which the review channel has meaning. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may appear if review is expected but repeatedly not used, not offered, or not made practically reachable; however, this remains a possible non-event, not an automatic finding. **Α (Attractor)** becomes relevant only if the pattern repeats and the junior employee’s role stabilizes around being the person who absorbs delay. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is central because exposure, dependency, and exit-cost are unevenly distributed. **Θ (Temporality)** matters because the asymmetry hardens over time as deadlines and role-dependence accumulate. **Φ (Recontextualization)** would concern whether the situation can be recontextualized from “individual workload problem” to “asymmetrically priced review structure.” **Χ (Distance)** is the key calibration operator: stepping back is formally allowed, but the cost of stepping back is not equal. **Σ (Integration)** is not established merely because the organization has a coherent review procedure. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is not established unless the employee or manager is shown to bind themselves to a stable responsibility structure across time.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay sharpens the example by naming the reduced signature without turning it into a verdict. The relevant shorthand is **DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in**, because distance, delay, and exit may be asymmetrically priced under Ω and Θ. Yet the vignette does not establish a full CONFLICT terminal regime. It does not show failed Σ under binding collision. It does not show that the manager acted wrongly. It does not show legal coercion. It only illustrates how a review channel can remain formally open while becoming practically difficult for one role to use.

MIP would treat this as an IA-box prompt rather than a judgment. Transparency may be partial if the review channel is written down. Justification remains under-specified unless the organization can explain why the burdens and timing are distributed as they are. Time-boundedness is a risk if review windows are vague. Reversibility is a risk if delayed review increases burden for one side. M-score should not be assigned from the vignette alone. AHP would flag a likely artefact risk: a polished example may make the organization look guilty even though the vignette lacks full evidence.

The forbidden claims are therefore explicit. The example does not prove exploitation, immaturity, negligence, retaliation, coercion, or legal wrongdoing. It does not diagnose the manager or the employee. It does not rank dignity. It does not validate PMS, PMS-CONFLICT, or MIP.

The non-capture note is equally important. A legal analysis might ask whether the review procedure satisfies labor obligations. A care or organizational ethics reading might ask whether dependency and mentorship duties are being handled responsibly. A phenomenological reading might ask how it feels to carry exposure under formal permission. PMS makes the structure readable, but it does not exhaust these questions.

The article takeaway is this: **asymmetry lock-in begins to matter when what is formally revisable becomes practically uneven, temporally burdened, and costly to interrupt.**

##### Example 2 — Care, obligation, and the danger of over-reading non-exit

A person remains in a demanding caregiving arrangement for a family member whose condition requires regular attention. Friends sometimes describe the situation as “trapping,” because the caregiver has little free time, postpones plans, and cannot easily leave for extended periods. The caregiver also sometimes says that leaving is “not really an option.”

This could look like asymmetry lock-in: one person is dependent, another carries responsibility, exit is difficult, and time intensifies the structure. But the example is intentionally bounded so that the article does not over-read non-exit as drift or coercion. The caregiver may experience real burden and still understand the arrangement as meaningful care. The difficulty of leaving may arise from love, responsibility, practical necessity, lack of services, family expectation, material constraint, or several of these at once. None of those possibilities should be collapsed into a single PMS finding.

What the example demonstrates is the importance of **non-capture**. PMS Core can make the structure visible, but it must not force closure. **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes dependency, care, burden, obligation, and exit. **∇ (Impulse)** marks pressures toward staying, resting, asking for help, or reorganizing care. **□ (Frame)** is crucial: the frame may be family care, moral obligation, public welfare failure, gendered expectation, economic constraint, or emotional attachment. Each frame changes what the non-exit means. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** might become relevant if support that should exist does not appear, such as absent institutional care, missing family help, or delayed respite. Yet Λ must be calibrated: absence is meaningful only when an expectation is established. **Α (Attractor)** may be relevant if the caregiving pattern stabilizes over time. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is present because dependency and responsibility are asymmetric. **Θ (Temporality)** is active because care trajectories accumulate. **Φ (Recontextualization)** may recontextualize “personal sacrifice” as “unsupported care structure,” but this reframing must not erase the caregiver’s own interpretation. **Χ (Distance)** requires reflective distance from both romanticizing care and condemning it as entrapment. **Σ (Integration)** remains conditional: integration may be possible, partial, costly, or impossible. **Ψ (Self-binding)** may become relevant if the caregiver has self-bound to a role, promise, or identity, but it cannot be inferred simply from remaining.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant only with caution. **DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in** may become a risk if distance, respite, or exit are unequally available and temporally harden. But the example must not be treated as a CONFLICT finding. Caregiving is not automatically conflict. Dependency is not automatically domination. Non-exit is not automatically immaturity or coercion. Binding collision may become relevant if commitments become jointly non-viable, but that is not shown here.

MIP is especially useful in this example because it blocks premature responsibility scoring. A-awareness may be conceptually strong if the burdens are visible, but actor-level A remains unknown. M-score cannot be assigned without knowing the caregiver’s knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and attempts at integration. The IA-box could ask whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible, but even that must be handled carefully. Some care asymmetries are not fully reversible; the question is then not whether asymmetry exists, but whether it is made less humiliating, less isolating, less unsupported, and less falsely individualized.

AHP would warn that this example has high misuse risk if written too dramatically. It could become sentimental, accusatory, or covertly diagnostic. It should therefore remain structurally modest.

Forbidden claims include: the caregiver is trapped; the dependent person is a burden in a dignity-reducing sense; the family is guilty; the situation is pathological; the caregiver is immature for staying; the caregiver is morally superior for staying; non-exit proves coercion; difficulty proves tragedy. None of these follows.

The non-capture note is central. Care ethics may preserve features that PMS does not fully capture: attentiveness, responsiveness, love, obligation, grief, fatigue, and the moral texture of dependency. Political theory may ask whether public systems offload care. Legal analysis may ask about entitlement to support. Phenomenology may ask how burden and meaning coexist.

The article takeaway is this: **asymmetry lock-in analysis must be able to see exit difficulty without automatically condemning staying, pathologizing dependency, or converting care into drift.**

##### Example 3 — A public interpretation that hardens too quickly

A small conflict in an academic or professional community is first discussed privately as a disagreement about role expectations. One person says they felt unable to object earlier because the other had more status and controlled access to future opportunities. Another person says they did not know the situation was being experienced that way and believed there were open channels for raising concerns.

A short excerpt from the discussion is later circulated publicly. It says that the situation shows “asymmetry lock-in.” The phrase travels quickly. Readers who do not know the context begin treating the phrase as if it proves that one person trapped another, that the higher-status person acted coercively, or that the lower-status person had no real agency. Others reject the phrase as exaggerated and insist that formal channels existed. The original structural question is replaced by a public contest over guilt, innocence, credibility, and status.

This example demonstrates why the article needs PMS-CONFLICT, MIP, and AHP after PMS Core. PMS Core can map the operators in the underlying case, but the public circulation of the phrase introduces a new risk: the analysis itself can become part of the asymmetry. A scene-bound structural term can harden into a reputational object. The vignette is not introduced as evidence that such a public hardening occurred in the actual case; it is a bounded demonstration of how an otherwise careful structural phrase can change function when it leaves its original frame.

The PMS Core hooks are layered. **Δ (Difference)** distinguishes the original disagreement, the private interpretation, the public excerpt, and the later audience reaction. **∇ (Impulse)** appears as pressure to interpret, defend, accuse, deny, or align. **□ (Frame)** shifts: what began as a private role-context frame becomes a public reputational frame. **Λ (Non-event / residue)** may appear as missing context, absent reply, unprovided evidence, or failed opportunity for clarification. **Α (Attractor)** becomes relevant if the public narrative stabilizes into a repeatable role-script. **Ω (Asymmetry)** is intensified because status, exposure, and audience access become uneven. **Θ (Temporality)** matters because public records persist and revision becomes harder over time. **Φ (Recontextualization)** is central because recontextualization occurs: a structural term is moved from private analysis to public interpretation. **Χ (Distance)** is endangered because reflective distance collapses under public pressure. **Σ (Integration)** is not achieved merely because audiences settle on a coherent story. **Ψ (Self-binding)** is not established; public attribution is not self-binding.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay makes visible **exposure capture** as a risk, although in the original abstract case it was not applicable. Here, once publicness is introduced, P and MG become relevant. Recontextualization under scaled exposure can turn interpretation into enforcement affordance. The phrase “asymmetry lock-in” may become a reputational marker rather than a reversible analytic description. **Dignity collapse drift** also becomes a risk if language shifts toward shaming, ranking, humiliation, or person-near verdict. Still, these are risks, not findings. Public circulation does not automatically prove misuse; it makes the boundary problem visible.

MIP would treat this as a transmission failure risk. The IA-box cannot be skipped. Was the asymmetry transparent? Was the public claim justified? Was the interpretation time-bound and reversible? Did affected parties have voice? Were formal and real options distinguished? M-score should not be assigned to the public participants based on the vignette. The D-module would remain highly guarded: public dignity risk is elevated, but dignity must not be scored. AHP would likely mark this as an excerpt-risk case: the analytic artefact has been detached from its boundary language.

Forbidden claims are especially important here. The example does not prove that the original higher-status person did wrong. It does not prove that the lower-status person misrepresented anything. It does not prove that the public audience is malicious. It does not prove legal coercion, reputational harm, institutional negligence, or clinical injury. It does not validate or invalidate the phrase “asymmetry lock-in.” It shows how a structurally careful term can become unsafe when detached from scope, evidence, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice.

The non-capture note is broad. Media theory may better explain circulation. Legal analysis may be needed if defamation, confidentiality, or institutional process is implicated. Phenomenology may better capture the experience of exposure. Political theory may better analyze status and public contestability. PMS-CONFLICT shows the structural risk of exposure capture, but it does not exhaust the public meaning of the event.

The article takeaway is this: **the language of asymmetry lock-in must remain scene-bound and reversible, because once it travels publicly, the analysis itself may become part of the asymmetry it was designed to clarify.**

##### Case takeaway

This case shows how AXIOM can map a morally difficult structure without turning structural legibility into closure. PMS Core renders the movement from differentiated positions to temporally hardened asymmetry. PMS-CONFLICT sharpens the specific danger of asymmetrically priced distance, exit, delay, revision, or interruption. MIP refuses person scoring and keeps responsibility, action, and maturity non-assignable without concrete scene evidence. AHP hardens the article against its own strongest misuse risk: the appearance of evidential completeness.

The examples show how the same structure can appear in different directions: formal exit may not be real exit; care-based non-exit must not be over-read as drift; and public circulation can transform structural language into a new exposure field. None of the examples is evidence. They are calibration devices.

The methodological result is therefore modest but important: **Asymmetry lock-in is PMS-/CONFLICT-/MIP-legible, but its legibility is bounded by non-capture, rival readings, unfulfilled operationalization, and the refusal to turn risk into finding.** The case should be used in the chapter as a disciplined map of closure-pressure under asymmetry and time, not as a resolution of that pressure. Its final value is cartographic: it shows where closure becomes tempting, where stronger claims would need concrete scene evidence, and where Λ-residue must remain visible.

### 7.5 Summary

Part IV establishes CONFLICT as the PMS add-on layer for stabilized incompatibility. The cases in this part do not treat conflict as a defect in persons, a lack of maturity, or a problem waiting for the right solution technique. They treat conflict as a structural regime: differentiated trajectories may remain locally coherent, yet fail to become jointly integrable without remainder, coercion, erasure, exposure, or unacceptable cost.

The common result is not that PMS-CONFLICT resolves conflict. The result is that PMS-CONFLICT makes visible where resolution language becomes unsafe: where integration is assumed too quickly, where repair talk erases consequence, where tragedy is converted into finality, where structural injustice becomes accusation or dilution, where dirty-hands language becomes verdict, or where formal exit hides asymmetrically priced distance.

CONFLICT therefore does not replace PMS core. PMS remains the operator grammar. CONFLICT adds overlay handles for conflict-specific thresholds and risks: local/shared Σ tension, binding collision, terminality pressure, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, repair-talk drift, dignity-collapse risk, and transmission hardening. These handles are not verdict categories. They do not diagnose parties, rank persons, assign blame, prescribe governance, or prove terminal conflict.

#### 7.5.1 Baseline result of Part IV

The baseline established by Part IV is that not every incompatibility should be narrated as solvable.

Across the cases, PMS core makes the closure-pressure legible, while CONFLICT sharpens the threshold discipline. Structural incompatibility shows the gap between local coherence and shared integration. Escalation vs repair shows that interruption or revision is not yet repair. Trolley dilemmas show forced-choice pressure without moral settlement. Tragedy shows meaningful non-resolution without final capture. Structural injustice shows arrangement-mediated asymmetry without immediate blame assignment. Dirty hands shows action under residue without moral purification. Asymmetry lock-in shows that formal exit or revision may not be practically equal across positions.

The strongest shared discipline is:

**local Σ is not shared Σ**

A trajectory may be coherent inside its own frame. A repair statement may be coherent inside a public narrative. A procedure may be coherent inside an institution. A choice may be coherent inside one moral frame. But none of these local coherences proves shared integration, terminal resolution, responsibility assignment, or legitimacy.

#### 7.5.2 Cluster-level result

The Part IV cases form three CONFLICT-facing clusters.

**Structural incompatibility and repair pressure** show how conflict becomes legible before it becomes terminal. A frame collision, procedure clash, or repair attempt may reveal contested shared integration, but this is not yet proof of terminal conflict. The key discipline is to keep Σ as a tested possibility, not a mandate. Repair remains possible, but it is not proven by interruption, apology, changed procedure, or public closure language.

**Forced choice, trolley dilemmas, dirty hands, and tragedy** show how loss and residue remain visible under pressure to decide. These cases are not moral-ranking engines. They show that action, omission, responsibility-pressure, and irreversible consequence can be mapped without producing clean verdicts. Tragic or dirty-hands legibility does not mean guilt, necessity, moral contamination, or metaphysical finality. It means that Λ remains structurally meaningful even where action or interpretation proceeds.

**Structural injustice and asymmetry lock-in** show how Ω and Θ can harden conflict fields without yielding immediate person-level findings. Structural language can reveal arrangement-mediated disadvantage, formal/practical access gaps, and asymmetrically priced exit. But it can also travel too quickly into accusation, stigma, or institutional pressure. For that reason, publicness, misuse gradient, MIP guardrails, and AHP hardening are not decorative additions; they protect the analysis from becoming part of the conflict structure.

#### 7.5.3 Cross-case dependency discipline

The recurring failure mode in Part IV is:

**Σ → solution / authority**

This occurs when coherence, repair language, public explanation, procedural order, or structural description is treated as proof that conflict has been resolved or authoritatively classified. Once that shortcut appears, Λ is suppressed, Χ is weakened, Φ becomes narrative control, Ω distributes who can impose the reading, and Θ hardens the result into record, precedent, role, or expectation.

In Part IV this shortcut appears in several forms:

- local coherence becomes shared integration;
- a pause becomes repair;
- a revised procedure becomes restored harm;
- a trolley frame becomes a moral rule;
- tragedy becomes finality;
- structural injustice becomes public accusation or background dilution;
- dirty-hands language becomes guilt;
- formal exit becomes real exit;
- asymmetry lock-in becomes a verdict before evidence supports it.

Part IV blocks these shortcuts without denying conflict, repair, responsibility, or tragedy. It allows conflict to be visible, but not automatically terminal. It allows repair to be possible, but not automatically achieved. It allows responsibility to be thinkable, but not automatically assigned. It allows asymmetry to be mapped, but not automatically moralized.

#### 7.5.4 What Part IV carries forward

Part IV carries forward the strongest boundary lesson of the overlay chapters so far: add-ons become most necessary where ordinary language is already close to authority. “Conflict,” “repair,” “tragedy,” “injustice,” “dirty hands,” and “lock-in” are powerful labels. They can clarify, but they can also prosecute, shame, freeze, or authorize.

CONFLICT is justified because PMS core can map incompatibility, but the overlay makes conflict-specific thresholds sharper: when Σ is contested, when Χ is priced, when Ψ-binding would be required before responsibility can be claimed, when Ω changes the cost of exit, and when public transmission turns analysis into exposure.

MIP and AHP remain downstream artefact layers. They do not score parties, diagnose conflict maturity, assign dignity status, or authorize governance. MIP reviews whether the analysis artefact could acquire coercive, institutional, reputational, or public force. AHP hardens the analysis against excerpting, score-like overread, publicness drift, D-language confusion, and false transmission readiness.

Part IV closes with the CONFLICT rule for AXIOM: where incompatibility is structurally visible, render it; where repair is only possible, do not call it achieved; where tragedy leaves residue, do not call coherence resolution; where asymmetry is plausible, do not call it proof; and where conflict language could travel as authority, keep the misuse boundary attached.
