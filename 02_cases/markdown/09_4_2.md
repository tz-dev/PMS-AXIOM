### Why this case matters

This case matters because recognition, especially when rendered as acknowledgment, places unusually strong pressure on closure. Recognition appears to promise a transition from ambiguity to settlement. Something was unseen and becomes seen; something was unnamed and becomes named; something was denied and becomes accepted; something was implicit and becomes explicit; something was evaded and becomes responsibility. The philosophical and practical danger is that this transition can look more complete than it is. Once the word “acknowledgment” appears, a reader may assume that the relevant question is simply whether acknowledgment occurred or failed. The completed YAML stack resists that closure-demand.

The closure-demand is especially strong because acknowledgment has a quasi-performative quality. It does not merely describe a prior condition; it can alter the standing of the condition it names. To acknowledge a contribution, a harm, a responsibility, a relationship, a dependence, or an omission is not simply to observe it. It may change what can be said next, what is owed next, who can contest next, what counts as denial, and what counts as repair. This makes recognition structurally powerful, but also analytically dangerous. The more powerful the frame, the more easily it can become coercive.

In ordinary use, acknowledgment can appear morally transparent. If something is true, one should acknowledge it. If someone has been harmed, the harm should be acknowledged. If responsibility exists, it should be named. Yet the case shows that this apparent transparency hides several structural uncertainties. What exactly is the object of acknowledgment? A fact, a feeling, a contribution, a role, a legal status, a responsibility, a public injury, a private experience, or a contested interpretation? Who is positioned to acknowledge it? What counts as adequate acknowledgment? What if explicit acknowledgment would be premature, false, legally unsafe, procedurally improper, or assimilative? What if refusal preserves non-capture? What if delay is a failure in one frame and responsibility in another?

The case belongs in AXIOM because it tests whether a layered analytical stack can increase precision without increasing unauthorized certainty. PMS Core can map the operator structure by which recognition becomes analyzable: differentiation, directedness, frame, non-event, possible attractor, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and self-binding. EDEN can ask whether recognition language falsely coordinates real asymmetry, displaced responsibility, blocked integration, or externalized self-binding. MIP can ask what maturity-in-practice would require in awareness, coherence, responsibility, power-to-act, asymmetry hygiene, and dignity protection. AHP can then ask whether the analysis artefact itself is too polished, too formal, or too complete-looking for the limited claims it is actually allowed to make.

This makes the case structurally difficult. Recognition is not one stable object. It may mean noticing, accepting, validating, admitting, naming, receiving, legally acknowledging, ethically responding, politically recognizing, or institutionally registering. Each of these has a different frame. A legal acknowledgment is not an interpersonal apology. A procedural receipt is not substantive validation. A political recognition is not emotional attunement. A private acceptance is not public naming. If these are collapsed, the analysis becomes too broad to discriminate.

The case is also difficult because non-recognition is not automatically failure. Denial, delay, silence, refusal, non-explicitness, non-binding, or non-integration may be failures in some scenes. They may also be legitimate boundaries, evidence discipline, procedural restraint, non-capture, uncertainty, incapacity, or refusal of a false frame. The YAML stack preserves this tension rather than resolving it prematurely.

The case is not being solved metaphysically. The analysis does not claim that PMS reveals the essence of recognition, that acknowledgment completes subjectivity, or that recognition can be reduced without remainder to the PMS operator chain. SELF_FIXPOINT remains analytic-only. PMS does not prove what selfhood is, what recognition is in itself, or what dignity is ontologically. It gives a reversible structural projection.

The case is not being solved empirically. No scene packet gives actors, evidence, timing, consequences, documents, testimony, institutional rules, or observed effects. Because no concrete scene is supplied, the analysis cannot say that recognition happened, failed, was withheld, was owed, was delayed unjustly, was sincerely given, or was falsely coordinated. The YAMLs are explicit that the case is not empirical and not operationalized for empirical use.

The case is not being solved clinically or diagnostically. No inner state is inferred. No motive, pathology, avoidance pattern, maturity deficit, narcissistic structure, trauma response, or personality claim is made. The PMS operator ∇ is treated as structural directedness, not subjective desire. Λ is structured absence, not a symptom. Ω is asymmetry, not culpability. Χ prevents the analysis from becoming a tribunal.

The case is not being solved legally or forensically. Acknowledgment can resemble admission, notice, acceptance of responsibility, or liability, but the completed analyses explicitly exclude legal and forensic status. The case does not decide whether an acknowledgment would count as admission, whether silence would count as legal refusal, or whether responsibility is legally operative. Those determinations require domain-specific evidence and authority outside PMS, EDEN, MIP, and AHP.

The case is also not solved by implementation. The stack does not prescribe an intervention. It does not tell anyone to confront, demand acknowledgment, refuse acknowledgment, apologize, escalate, repair, document, litigate, reconcile, or make a public statement. The description-to-action firewall remains active. The output is a working-note analytical rendering, not an application protocol.

The case matters, then, because it demonstrates that a powerful analytical architecture can become more exact by withholding closure. The result is not weak because it suspends findings. It is strong because it knows which findings would require more than the available material can support.

### Scene and frame structure

The scene is a conceptual scene rather than a concrete one. Its boundary begins when a recognition object or responsibility claim becomes differentiated enough to be acknowledgment-relevant. The object may be a person, claim, situation, responsibility, denial, delay, acceptance, or explicit formulation. The scene ends before any concrete finding of recurrence, durable self-binding, responsibility displacement, actual cost distribution, repair, admission, or moral failure.

The frame, □, is recognition as acknowledgment. Within this frame, recognition means that something becomes legible as possibly noticed, accepted, denied, delayed, refused, made explicit, or integrated. The frame determines what counts as acknowledgment and what counts as non-acknowledgment. It also determines what kinds of absences matter. A silence matters only if the frame contains an expectation of speech. A delay matters only if the frame contains temporal expectation. A refusal matters only if the frame distinguishes refusal from non-involvement, incapacity, legitimate boundary, or non-capture.

Inside the frame are the minimal structural elements of acknowledgment: differentiated object, possible directedness toward response, contextual criteria for what acknowledgment would be, possible non-event, temporality, possible recontextualization, and the application discipline of distance. The frame can include acceptance, denial, delay, explicitness, responsibility-naming, or movement from implicit to explicit. Outside the frame are all claims that would require a different authority or evidence basis. The analysis does not include psychological access to inner experience. It does not include legal admission, institutional notice, political status-conferral, clinical diagnosis, empirical verification, or moral tribunal judgment. It also leaves outside the frame any claim that a refusal, silence, or delay is automatically illegitimate. Those may be failures in some scenes and legitimate non-capture in others.

The protected constraints are reversibility, non-capture, and Dignity-in-Practice. The analysis must remain able to retract, weaken, or revise its reading if the frame proves wrong, if the scene is under-specified, if a rival reading preserves more of the material, or if the structural mapping begins to shame, rank, or bind persons. Χ is therefore not a decorative operator in this case. It is the discipline that keeps recognition from becoming a compulsory closure demand.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading proceeds in the canonical operator order: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. The order matters because later operators depend on earlier structuring. The analysis does not begin with responsibility, integration, self-binding, or dignity. It begins with minimal difference.

#### Δ — Difference

Δ is active and major. Recognition becomes analyzable only when there is a distinction between what is to be acknowledged and what is not yet acknowledged. The differentiated object may be a person, claim, situation, responsibility, acceptance, denial, delay, or explicit formulation. Without this differentiation, PMS has no entry point.

The case-specific role of Δ is to establish the minimal object of recognition. Recognition cannot be analyzed if everything remains undifferentiated. Something must be picked out as acknowledgment-relevant: a claim, a condition, a contribution, a harm, a responsibility, a refusal, or an omission. This does not yet tell us whether the object is valid, true, owed, or correctly framed. It only makes the object available for analysis.

The calibration issue is that the differentiated object must be named carefully. Is the case about a person being recognized, a claim being accepted, a situation being noticed, a responsibility being named, or a denial being reversed? These are not equivalent. Treating them as interchangeable would make the analysis too broad. A person’s demand for recognition differs from an institution’s receipt of a complaint; a moral acknowledgment differs from a legal admission; an implicit acceptance differs from explicit naming.

The dependency risk at Δ is importation. The analyst may import distinctions from a moral, legal, political, empirical, or therapeutic frame not actually supplied by the case. If that happens, PMS appears to analyze the case, but is really analyzing an imported rival structure. This is why Δ must remain minimal: it differentiates the recognition object without deciding its normative status.

The overclaim would be to treat the presence of difference as proof of capture. Difference makes analysis possible; it does not prove that PMS exhausts recognition. Δ opens the case; it does not close it.

#### ∇ — Impulse

∇ is weak to moderate. The case contains a structural directedness toward acknowledgment: something differentiated may press toward being noticed, accepted, denied, corrected, delayed, or made explicit. This directedness is not subjective desire. It is not longing, resentment, need, shame, avoidance, or bad faith. It is a formal orientation generated by difference within a possible frame.

The case-specific role of ∇ is to show why recognition is not a merely static classification. Once something becomes acknowledgment-relevant, it tends to orient the field toward response. The object may solicit noticing, acceptance, denial, clarification, refusal, or explicitness. This does not mean that anyone consciously wants or owes such response. It means that the structure contains a directional pressure.

The calibration issue is to specify the type of directedness. Is it practical, argumentative, ethical, institutional, procedural, interpersonal, or merely conceptual? In the completed case, it remains mostly conceptual. There is no evidence of an actor’s inner motivation.

The dependency risk is psychologization. Once recognition is framed as directed toward acknowledgment, it is tempting to infer that someone wants acknowledgment, avoids it, withholds it, or resists it. The YAML explicitly blocks this. ∇ supplies structural orientation only.

The overclaim would be to convert weak directedness into motive, desire, guilt, denial, manipulation, or refusal. The case does not authorize that move. ∇ is not a diagnostic window into subjectivity.

#### □ — Frame

□ is active and major. Recognition requires a frame that determines what counts as acknowledgment, denial, delay, acceptance, explicitness, or responsibility. In this case, the frame is conceptual acknowledgment rather than a concrete legal, institutional, political, clinical, or interpersonal scene.

The case-specific role of □ is to establish the criteria under which acknowledgment becomes meaningful. A statement may count as acknowledgment in one frame and not in another. “I hear your concern” may count as receipt in an institutional frame, but not as substantive acknowledgment in an interpersonal moral frame. “This was a team effort” may count as inclusive recognition in one setting and erasure in another. The frame determines what the act is.

The calibration issue is frame specificity. Recognition can belong to many frames: interpersonal recognition, ethical responsiveness, legal admission, institutional notice, political status, epistemic uptake, or procedural acknowledgment. Each frame has different criteria. A legal admission is not the same as emotional validation. Political recognition is not the same as private acceptance. The PMS Core reading is valid only as long as it keeps the frame explicit and does not smuggle one domain into another.

The dependency risk is frame drift. A shift from noticing to accepting, or from implicit awareness to explicit acknowledgment, cannot be treated as a single operation without calibration. The frame must tell us what changed. If the frame changes silently, the analysis may appear coherent while actually switching criteria.

The overclaim would be to treat the conceptual frame as if it were a concrete frame. Since no actual scene is supplied, □ is satisfied at the conceptual level but not operationalized.

#### Λ — Non-Event

Λ is active and major, but carefully bounded. Recognition-as-acknowledgment centrally involves possible non-event: acknowledgment may not occur, may be delayed, may be denied, may remain implicit, or responsibility may remain unnamed. This does not mean that every absence is failure. A non-event is meaningful only within a frame that generates an expectation.

The case-specific role of Λ is to make absence structurally visible. If a claim calls for acknowledgment and no acknowledgment occurs, something may be structurally relevant even though “nothing happened.” If a responsibility should be named and remains unnamed, the absence may matter. If a response is delayed, temporality and non-event interact. But the case is careful: absence is not automatically failure.

The calibration issue is to specify the expected occurrence. Was a reply expected? Was explicit naming expected? Was acceptance expected? Was responsibility-taking expected? Was public acknowledgment expected? Was private acknowledgment enough? Without specifying the expected event, absence cannot be responsibly interpreted.

The dependency risk is moralizing absence. Silence, delay, refusal, or non-explicitness can be structurally relevant, but may also be legitimate boundary-setting, prudence, incapacity, uncertainty, non-involvement, or rival framing. The YAML preserves this non-capture zone.

The overclaim would be to translate absence into incomplete integration, failed maturity, denial, evasion, or wrongdoing. Λ marks structured absence; it does not decide its legitimacy.

#### Α — Attractor

Α is weak. The abstract material mentions denial, delay, and explicitness, but does not show recurrence, stabilization, routine, path dependence, or role-script formation. Recognition cases can become attractor cases if patterns of acknowledgment, refusal, delay, or reframing repeat and stabilize. This case does not yet show that.

The case-specific role of Α is mostly cautionary. It asks whether recognition dynamics are episodic or patterned. A single delayed acknowledgment differs from a recurring structure in which acknowledgment is always deferred. A one-time omission differs from an institutional routine of generic recognition language. The present case does not supply recurrence.

The calibration issue is repetition. A single conceptual possibility is not an attractor. Repeated denial, repeated delay, stabilized omission, institutional routine, or recurring role-script would be needed.

The dependency risk is drift inflation. If Α is treated as active too cheaply, the analysis can begin to infer patterns where only conceptual possibilities exist. This is especially dangerous in recognition cases because a reader may generalize from one absence into a characterological or institutional pattern.

The overclaim would be to use Α to support drift-pattern findings or person-level tendencies. The YAML keeps attractor status weak and conditional.

#### Ω — Asymmetry

Ω is weak-active only in a conditional sense. Recognition may involve asymmetry where one position controls acknowledgment, timing, frame, publicness, language, responsibility naming, or exposure. Responsibility, vulnerability, authority, capacity, or obligation may also create asymmetry. But the supplied material does not specify a concrete asymmetry.

The case-specific role of Ω is to keep open the possibility that recognition is not evenly distributed. In a concrete scene, one role might have the power to name, delay, accept, ignore, or institutionalize acknowledgment, while another bears the cost of waiting, explaining, or remaining unrecognized. This possibility is structurally important. But possibility is not evidence.

The calibration issue is direction and kind. Who can acknowledge? Who depends on acknowledgment? Who bears responsibility? Who is exposed? Who has timing control? Who has language access? Who can exit? Who must wait? The case cannot answer these questions concretely.

The dependency risk is moralizing asymmetry. Structural imbalance is not culpability. It is not diagnosis, liability, guilt, domination, or exploitation by default. Ω must remain a structural operator.

The overclaim would be to treat possible asymmetry as established real Ω. This becomes especially important for EDEN, because EDEN requires real Ω before false-coordination claims can be responsibly made.

#### Θ — Temporality

Θ is major. Recognition is temporally structured. Something may be noticed later, accepted later, denied first and acknowledged later, remain implicit before becoming explicit, or be carried as responsibility across time. Delay and explicitness make Θ central to the case.

The case-specific role of Θ is to prevent recognition from being treated as a purely static status. Acknowledgment may have a before and after. A delay may change the meaning of the response. An eventual acknowledgment may or may not repair the earlier non-event. A refusal may remain stable or be revised. Responsibility may be named too early, too late, or not at all.

The calibration issue is to distinguish sequence, delay, persistence, anticipation, and later explicitness. A static distinction between recognized and unrecognized is insufficient. The case becomes more precise when it asks whether acknowledgment is delayed, deferred, transformed, or integrated over time.

The dependency risk is temporal overextension. Not every recognition relation is temporally complex. If recognition is treated only as instantaneous status, Θ should not be inflated. Here Θ is justified because delay, denial, explicitness, and responsibility across time are structurally central, but still not concretely evidenced.

The overclaim would be to use temporality to infer development, maturation, deterioration, continuity, or responsibility. Θ supplies sequence and temporal structure, not a moral arc.

#### Φ — Recontextualization

Φ is weak to major. It appears where an object moves from unnoticed to noticed, implicit to explicit, denied to acknowledged, responsibility-unclear to responsibility-named, or claim-unframed to claim-framed. Recognition often involves precisely such reframing.

The case-specific role of Φ is to mark shifts in how the recognition object becomes understandable. A contribution may be reframed as “team effort.” A complaint may be reframed as “concern.” A harm may be reframed as “experience.” A refusal may be reframed as “restraint.” Recontextualization can clarify, but it can also reduce, redirect, or absorb.

The calibration issue is source and target frame. What was the old frame? What is the new one? Private concern to public statement? Denial to acceptance? Ambiguous responsibility to named responsibility? Claim of harm to procedural concern? Without this calibration, Φ becomes too broad.

The dependency risk is over-assimilation. If every rupture, resistance, silence, disagreement, or refusal is treated as recontextualization, PMS absorbs non-fit instead of preserving it. The YAML explicitly warns against using Φ to swallow rival frames.

The overclaim would be to treat recontextualization as clarification by default. A reframing can clarify, but it can also distort, coerce, assimilate, sanitize, or prematurely close.

#### Χ — Distance

Χ is active and major as application discipline. It requires reflective distance, suspension of verdict, reversibility, non-tribunal posture, and refusal to convert acknowledgment into person-ranking, diagnosis, enforcement, or moral judgment.

The case-specific role of Χ is to prevent the recognition frame from becoming compulsory. Recognition often invites the thought: if something is real, it must be acknowledged; if it is not acknowledged, someone is failing. Χ slows this transition. It requires the analysis to preserve rival readings, question frame fit, and refuse premature application.

The calibration issue is to distinguish distance from avoidance. Χ is not empty neutrality, refusal of responsibility, or detachment from the case. It is the discipline that keeps the analysis from becoming coercive. It allows structure to be mapped without forcing closure.

The dependency risk is that later operators, especially Σ and Ψ, may create a false sense of completion unless Χ remains active. Integration and self-binding can appear attractive in recognition cases because they seem to promise resolution. Χ prevents that promise from becoming an analytic shortcut.

The overclaim would be to suspend Χ in the name of truth, clarity, urgency, dignity, responsibility, or repair. If Χ is suspended to force a verdict, the PMS application becomes invalid.

#### Σ — Integration

Σ is weak. Acknowledgment may integrate claim, frame, expectation, responsibility, and response into coherent praxis, but the material does not show actual integration. The case can imagine integration; it cannot demonstrate it.

The case-specific role of Σ is to ask whether acknowledgment becomes part of a coherent practice. Does a response actually integrate the claim? Does responsibility enter conduct? Does an institution change procedure? Does a relation become newly coordinated? Does a statement bind future action? None of this is evidenced in the abstract case.

The calibration issue is discriminative coherence. Integration is not mere wording, tidiness, consensus, or narrative smoothness. A coherent account can still be false, over-assimilative, or coercive. Σ must show uptake into praxis, not just a pleasing structural fit.

The dependency risk is treating coherence as proof. Because PMS can generate elegant chains, it may appear to validate the reading by internal coherence alone. The YAML blocks that move. Coherence does not prove truth, empirical adequacy, or full capture.

The overclaim would be to say that recognition is integrated because it has been described coherently. Description is not integration.

#### Ψ — Self-Binding

Ψ is weak. Acknowledged responsibility could become self-binding if a person, institution, or role commits to integrated obligations over time. But no concrete self-binding commitment is supplied. The case does not show durable responsibility, stable normativity, or binding self-relation.

The case-specific role of Ψ is to mark the difference between acknowledgment as statement and acknowledgment as binding practice. A person may say “I acknowledge this,” but whether that statement becomes self-binding depends on later integration, continuity, and responsibility. An institution may receive a concern, but whether it binds itself to review or change remains another matter.

The calibration issue is to apply Ψ only where self-binding is explicitly in scope. One cannot infer selfhood, maturity, sincerity, guilt, accountability, or durable commitment from an abstract recognition structure.

The dependency risk is premature completion. Ψ depends on supported Σ, Θ, and Χ. If integration is weak, temporality is not concretely evidenced, and distance is required, Ψ cannot be used as if self-binding has been established.

The overclaim would be to treat SELF_FIXPOINT or responsibility-as-self-binding as a metaphysical proof of subjectivity. In this case, Ψ remains conditional and analytic.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The PMS dependency discipline is one of the most important parts of the completed case. Later operators cannot be used cheaply because they depend on earlier operators and on calibrated scene structure. Responsibility cannot be inferred simply because recognition language appears. Integration cannot be inferred because the analysis is coherent. Self-binding cannot be inferred because acknowledgment is conceptually possible. Dignity-in-Practice cannot be invoked to rank, shame, or morally elevate any position.

The dependency order protects the analysis from closure by elegance. Recognition cases often invite elegant chains: something is differentiated, framed, left unacknowledged, later recontextualized, and then supposedly integrated or self-bound. But PMS requires that each step be supported. Δ must identify an object. ∇ must remain structural unless subjective evidence appears. □ must specify the frame. Λ must identify a non-event relative to that frame. Α requires recurrence. Ω requires real asymmetry. Θ requires meaningful temporal structure. Φ requires a calibrated shift in frame. Χ requires distance before application. Σ requires integration into praxis. Ψ requires self-binding across time.

Σ remains conditional because the case does not show coherent uptake into praxis. It shows that acknowledgment could integrate claim, frame, expectation, responsibility, and response. But there is no concrete response, no procedure, no repair act, no institutional uptake, no mutual recognition event, and no durable practice. Therefore integration remains possible but unproven.

Ψ remains conditional because no self-binding commitment is supplied. Responsibility is named as a possible recognition object, but naming responsibility is not the same as a subject or institution binding itself to that responsibility over time. PMS requires more than conceptual availability.

Ω remains conditional because no concrete asymmetry is mapped. The case can say that recognition may involve timing control, language access, recognition control, publicness, exposure, or explanation burden. It cannot say who actually had those powers or how they structured options and costs.

Θ is active but not decisive. Temporality is central, but temporal sequence does not itself imply development, deterioration, avoidance, responsibility, or maturation. Delay matters only under a frame. Later acknowledgment matters only if its relation to the earlier non-event is specified.

Λ is active but not moralized. Absence can matter. But absence is not automatically denial, and denial is not automatically failure. PMS dependency discipline prevents non-event from becoming verdict.

Coherence is not proof. The PMS formula can produce a structurally elegant reading: recognition involves temporally framed differentiation, possible non-event, possible asymmetry, possible recontextualization, distance, and conditional integration or self-binding. But the question marks matter. Ω, Σ, Ψ, R, and E remain conditional. The formula states a PMS-internal projection, not empirical validation.

Non-capture is not failure. A refusal to acknowledge may preserve truth. A delay may preserve procedural fairness. Silence may be a boundary. Disagreement may be a rival frame. Non-integration may prevent false integration. The YAML insists that rupture, resistance, absence, and non-fit are not automatically PMS drift or maturity failure.

PMS must not become a closure machine because its power lies in reversible structural mapping, not final authority. If every recognition problem were redescribed as differentiation, non-event, recontextualization, weak integration, or missing self-binding, PMS would erase the very rival readings it is required to keep open. The application discipline therefore protects both the case and PMS itself.

Invalid dependency shortcuts include using Λ to prove failure, using Ω to prove culpability, using Θ to prove developmental trajectory, using Φ to absorb all disagreement, using Σ to prove truth, using Ψ to prove selfhood, maturity, or responsibility, using Dignity-in-Practice to rank persons, and using PMS coherence to validate PMS Base. Each shortcut would turn a conceptual projection into an unauthorized conclusion.

### Derived structures

The derived structures are formula-derived, not primitive. They are not independent modules that can be activated at will. Each depends on PMS operators and must inherit the calibration limits of those operators.

#### Awareness_A

Awareness_A is active as structural awareness. Its formula is Θ, □, and Δ: sustained, framed differentiation across time. Recognition requires this kind of awareness because something must be differentiated, framed as acknowledgment-relevant, and tracked across time as noticed, delayed, denied, accepted, or made explicit.

This is not phenomenological awareness. The case does not claim that anyone experiences recognition, feels unseen, consciously avoids acknowledgment, or inwardly knows responsibility. Awareness_A means that the structure of the case allows recognition to be modeled as temporally framed differentiation.

The status is active, but only at the structural level. It becomes overclaim if treated as subjective consciousness, moral insight, emotional validation, testimonial uptake, or empirical knowledge. It also becomes overclaim if it is used to assign maturity. The fact that a recognition structure can be made aware within the model does not mean that a person in a concrete scene had awareness.

Awareness_A therefore contributes analytic visibility. It shows that recognition-as-acknowledgment can be structured as an object moving through time within a frame. It does not establish subjective recognition.

#### Coherence_C

Coherence_C is weak-active. Its formula is Θ, Λ, □, and ∇: temporally stabilized structuring of impulse and expectation within a frame. Recognition can become coherent where a differentiated object, directedness toward acknowledgment, an expected event, a non-event, and temporal structure form an acknowledgment trajectory.

The weakness is important. The abstract case does not prove truth, validity, integration, or sufficient discrimination. It can show that recognition-as-acknowledgment has a coherent PMS structure, but not that this structure is the correct, complete, or final account.

Coherence_C becomes overclaim if coherence is treated as truth, legal validity, moral adequacy, forced integration, narrative smoothing, or PMS Base validation. A coherent account may still be under-discriminating. It may fit the model too well because rival frames have been excluded too early. This is why the case preserves rival readings and falsifiers.

The derived coherence is therefore useful but limited. It shows that the recognition structure is not random. It does not show that the recognition reading is exhaustive.

#### Responsibility_R

Responsibility_R is weak. Its formula is Ψ, Φ, Θ, and Ω: self-binding orientation toward asymmetry across time and recontextualization. Although responsibility is named in the case material, the derived PMS structure requires more than naming. It requires asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, and self-binding.

Those conditions are not concretely evidenced. Ω is weak. Φ is possible but not operationalized. Θ is structurally central but not empirically specified. Ψ is weak. Therefore Responsibility_R cannot become an active finding.

Responsibility_R remains structural, not moralistic, legal, forensic, or diagnostic. It becomes overclaim if the analysis assigns blame, guilt, liability, avoidance, obligation, person-level responsibility, or institutional accountability without a concrete scene. It also becomes overclaim if responsibility is inferred from the mere presence of an acknowledgment request.

The responsible move in this case is to keep R conditional. The analysis can ask where responsibility would be located if real asymmetry, recontextualization, and self-binding were shown. It cannot locate it now.

#### Action_E

Action_E is under-specified. Its formula is Σ, Θ, and ∇: integrated realization of directedness across time. Acceptance, explicit acknowledgment, response, or responsibility-taking could become action if directedness were temporally structured and integrated. But the case supplies no concrete enactment.

The analysis must distinguish action from mere noticing, wording, claim-status, conceptual structure, or unverified acknowledgment. Saying “acknowledgment could occur” is not the same as showing action. Saying “recognition becomes explicit” is not the same as documenting enacted integration.

Action_E remains under-specified because Σ is weak and no concrete practice is supplied. It would be overclaim to treat a conceptual possibility of acknowledgment as actual action, or to treat non-action as failure without concrete □/Λ/Ω/Θ conditions.

Action_E is therefore a threshold rather than a finding. It tells us what stronger evidence would need to show: not only that acknowledgment was possible, but that directedness became integrated praxis over time.

#### Dignity_in_Practice_D

Dignity_in_Practice_D is active only as an application constraint. Its formula is Ψ, Χ, and Ω: self-bound reflective restraint and protection in asymmetrical relations. In this case, D requires the analysis to handle possible asymmetry without ranking, shaming, humiliating, diagnosing, or ontologically evaluating any person or group.

This is not a dignity score. It is not a claim that one role has more dignity, less dignity, violated dignity, mature dignity, or failed dignity. It is a constraint on how the analysis speaks.

D becomes overclaim if it is used to elevate, shame, protect one side from all responsibility, or expose another side as degraded. The MIP layer later keeps the D-module off for precisely this reason. Dignity appears as non-degradation discipline, not as person-status evaluation.

Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological, not ontological. D₀ is untouched. No model layer can rank human worth. In this case, D protects the analysis from becoming humiliating or coercive.

#### SELF_FIXPOINT

SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only. Its formula sequence is Ψ ∘ Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ. It represents self-binding as the result of integrated, reflexively modulated, temporally extended, asymmetry-aware patterns of praxis being bound into a relatively stable self-relation.

The case does not establish SELF_FIXPOINT as its primary structure. A full self-binding chain would require concrete evidence of integrated, distanced, temporally extended, asymmetry-aware praxis bound into a stable self-relation. That evidence is absent.

SELF_FIXPOINT therefore remains a formal analytic possibility within PMS, not a metaphysical proof of subjectivity, identity, agency, or personhood. It must not be applied literally to abstract concepts, institutions, or role-positions unless explicitly scoped as structural or analytic self-binding.

Its status in this case is analytic-only and conditional. It is useful because it shows what a full PMS closure would require. It is also useful because it shows why such closure is not available here.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core case does not produce a drift-pattern finding. It identifies drift risks that would become relevant if the conceptual recognition structure were treated as more complete than it is. This distinction is central: a drift risk is not a diagnosis, not a score, not a verdict, and not evidence that a person or role has failed. The case’s drift-pattern analysis is a calibration device.

The first relevant drift risk is **AD_A>>E**, the risk that awareness becomes much stronger than enactment. Recognition cases are especially vulnerable to this because it is easy to describe, perceive, or conceptually understand the need for acknowledgment without showing any integrated action. One may know that something is acknowledgment-relevant, one may frame it accurately, and one may even name its temporality, while still lacking any evidence of enacted acknowledgment, responsibility uptake, repair, or self-binding. In the completed case, Awareness_A is structurally active, but Action_E is under-specified. This asymmetry creates an A>>E risk.

However, the case does not claim that A>>E has occurred in a concrete actor. It does not say that someone is merely aware but fails to act. It does not say that recognition has remained theoretical because of avoidance or immaturity. It says only that the abstract case material supports awareness more strongly than enactment. The correct reading is therefore: the YAML can model recognition-as-awareness better than recognition-as-action because action evidence is absent.

The second relevant drift risk is **AD_Sigma_low**, the risk that integration remains weak. Recognition-as-acknowledgment can become coherent in language without becoming integrated in praxis. A case may have a differentiated claim, a frame, a temporal structure, and a language of acknowledgment while still lacking actual uptake, repair, responsibility distribution, or durable practice. The completed PMS Core reading marks Σ as weak for this reason.

Again, low Σ is not a failure finding. It does not mean that the scene failed to integrate. It means that the supplied material does not evidence integration. Under-specification is not a low score. The absence of integration evidence is not itself evidence of non-integration. The YAML distinguishes epistemic non-availability from structural failure.

A related risk is **coherence over-discrimination or under-discrimination**. Because recognition language often produces elegant narratives, there is a temptation to equate coherence with adequate discrimination. If a recognition case can be narrated as “something was unseen, then became seen,” the narrative feels complete. PMS resists this by requiring operator calibration. What exactly was differentiated? What frame made it acknowledgment-relevant? What non-event mattered? What asymmetry was real? What temporal sequence was evidenced? What recontextualization occurred? What was integrated? What became self-binding? If these questions remain unanswered, coherence remains only partial.

There is also an **under-assignment risk**. Because the case carefully avoids concrete findings, a reader might mistakenly interpret non-assignment as a weak or negative judgment. That would be wrong. The case does not assign low maturity, low integration, low responsibility, or failed acknowledgment. It withholds assignment because the evidence basis is insufficient. Non-assignment preserves analytic cleanliness.

The drift-pattern section therefore has a double function. It warns against premature closure, and it warns against misreading restraint as deficiency. The case is not saying: recognition failed. It is saying: the conditions that would allow a recognition-failure finding are not present in the completed YAML material.

#### PMS-EDEN as overlay/application profile

The PMS-EDEN overlay is applied strictly as an overlay/application profile over the completed PMS Core case. It is not a PMS Base layer, not a new operator system, not a replacement for PMS Core, and not a correction of the Core pass. PMS.yaml remains the only PMS Base reference. PMS-EDEN supplies domain-specific visibility for adult reciprocity, false-coordinated real asymmetry, responsibility distribution, Action-Power, non-action/non-binding thresholds, cost distribution, Σ/Ψ blockage, counter-scene discipline, and application boundaries.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer here because recognition-as-acknowledgment has a specific danger that PMS Core can show but does not isolate as EDEN does: acknowledgment language may appear symmetrical, respectful, or mutually intelligible while concealing a real asymmetry in who can define the frame, delay response, allocate recognition, shift explanation burden, or externalize the cost of non-integration. PMS Core can mark Ω, Λ, Φ, Σ, and Ψ as weak or conditional. EDEN asks a more targeted question: does the scene show false-coordinated real asymmetry?

The EDEN overlay begins with a positive baseline rather than a failure narrative. Its benchmark is **Adult Reciprocity**, understood as truthful coordination of real asymmetry. This is not a PMS operator and not a new PMS axis. It is an EDEN normative benchmark. In a mature reciprocal recognition scene, real differences in capacity, vulnerability, authority, exposure, obligation, timing, language, or responsibility would be acknowledged without being erased, moralized, inflated, or weaponized. Asymmetry itself is not failure. False coordination of asymmetry is the concern.

The primary EDEN mechanism, **False-Coordinated Real Asymmetry**, is not established in this case. The overlay states the threshold clearly: real Ω plus a false coordination frame plus displaced responsibility plus blocked, weakened, simulated, avoided, or externalized Σ/Ψ would be required. The completed Core case does not supply concrete real Ω. It does not show displaced responsibility. It does not show actual cost distribution. It does not show blocked integration or shifted self-binding load. Therefore the EDEN mechanism remains suspended.

This suspension is not a failure of EDEN. It is EDEN functioning properly. PMS-EDEN does not authorize “EDEN-by-vibe.” It cannot infer false coordination merely because recognition, delay, refusal, responsibility, or asymmetry language appears. It needs a concrete scene packet.

The EDEN overlay makes several reduced signatures visible, but most remain weak or inactive. Real Ω mapping remains indeterminate. Responsibility and Action-Power distribution remain conditional. Σ/Ψ status is mixed or indeterminate. Cost distribution cannot be assigned. Non-action and non-binding are recognized as possible EDEN-relevant questions, but not classified as passive praxis or avoidance. Immunization, overexposure, and AEP are not supported. PFO and Reciprocity Loss are not claimed. The overlay’s main active construct is the **boundary condition** itself: EDEN becomes relevant only if a concrete scene can satisfy its thresholds.

Several EDEN constructs function as overlay handles rather than PMS operators. Adult Reciprocity, False-Coordinated Real Asymmetry, Action-Power, PFO, AEP, non-action/non-binding thresholds, immunization, overexposure, Reciprocity Loss, counter-scene protocol, and description-to-action firewall are not added to PMS Base. They organize and sharpen the application of existing PMS operators. They do not alter the PMS operator spine, derived structures, dependencies, or Core case findings.

The EDEN overlay also identifies drift risks, but treats them as risks rather than findings. A recognition frame could drift into false coordination if it presents delay, denial, or refusal as neutral while shifting costs to the affected role. It could drift into immunization if one role’s vulnerability or protected status removes all bounded responsibility. It could drift into overexposure if another role receives responsibility without legitimate language, protection, or critique access. It could drift into AEP if high exposure, low legitimate language, critique-to-threat switching, and responsibility overload appear together. None of these is established here.

The overlay’s counter-scene discipline is strong. The strongest counter-scene is that this remains a PMS-Core-only recognition case, or a speech-act, ethical, legal, procedural, interpersonal-validation, or non-assimilative refusal case. Denial, delay, silence, refusal, or non-binding may be legitimate. They may protect accuracy. They may preserve non-capture. They may reflect uncertainty, incapacity, lack of standing, or procedural constraints. EDEN must not convert disagreement into proof.

The EDEN final reading is therefore narrow: PMS-EDEN makes visible what would be required for a false-coordinated recognition-asymmetry claim, but those requirements are not met. The overlay remains a boundary/non-capture layer over the completed Core pass.

The AHP result confirms the core quality-control judgment: the analyses are structurally valid as layered working-note artefacts, but their very polish creates reuse risk. The correct hardening response is not stronger conclusion, but stronger boundary presentation.

### Example 1 — The Unanswered Acknowledgment Request

**Type:** micro
**Recommended insertion point:** After “Scene and frame structure,” before the PMS Core operator reading.

A contributor sends a short message after a shared project: “I want to make sure my role in drafting the proposal is acknowledged before the team presents it.” The project lead reads the message but does not answer before the presentation. In the meeting, the lead says, “This was a team effort,” without naming individual contributions. The contributor later feels that something relevant was left unacknowledged, while the lead may believe that the general team formulation was adequate or that individual naming was unnecessary in that context.

This vignette demonstrates why recognition-as-acknowledgment is structurally difficult. Something has been differentiated: a contribution, a request, and a possible responsibility to acknowledge. A frame is present: the question is not simply whether work occurred, but whether that work should be explicitly acknowledged. A non-event is also present: the requested acknowledgment did not occur in the requested form and time. Temporality matters because the message was sent before the presentation, the presentation occurred, and the opportunity for that particular acknowledgment passed.

The PMS Core hooks are clear but limited. Δ appears in the differentiation of the contribution and the acknowledgment request. □ appears in the frame of public or semi-public team recognition. Λ appears in the unanswered message and the missing explicit naming. Θ appears in the timing before and during the presentation. Ω may be relevant if the lead controlled the presentation frame, but this is not yet enough to establish consequential asymmetry. Φ could occur if the situation is later reframed as “team language” rather than “individual omission.” Χ is required because the analyst must not rush to decide whether the omission was wrongful. Σ and Ψ remain conditional because no repair, integration, or durable self-binding is shown.

The EDEN overlay would ask whether the general “team effort” language falsely coordinated a real asymmetry by shifting cost, visibility, or responsibility. But the vignette is too thin for that finding. Perhaps the lead erased a contribution; perhaps the team norm was genuinely collective; perhaps there were confidentiality, hierarchy, or presentation constraints; perhaps the request itself was unclear or late. EDEN may mark the threshold, but it cannot cross it without more scene data. The example therefore demonstrates EDEN’s restraint: the overlay becomes relevant because recognition, timing, visibility, and possible role power are present, but it remains suspended because real Ω, displaced R, cost distribution, and blocked Σ/Ψ are not established.

MIP would treat this as a working-note example only. No maturity score is assigned. The analysis may notice that the example requires awareness of role, timing, publicness, and power-to-act, but it cannot infer immaturity, avoidance, entitlement, or bad faith. The relevant MIP question is not “who acted maturely?” but “what would need to be known before maturity, responsibility, or asymmetry could be responsibly assessed?” AHP would flag the main risk: the example is readable enough that readers may supply motives not present in the vignette. The QC implication is that even a simple micro-example must preserve the same non-decision boundary as the full case.

Forbidden claims include: “the lead denied recognition,” “the contributor was entitled to public credit,” “the lead acted immaturely,” “the contributor is oversensitive,” “this proves false coordination,” or “this shows a duty to repair.” None of those follows from the bounded vignette alone.

The non-capture note is essential: a missing acknowledgment may be a failure, but it may also be a legitimate use of a different frame. The article takeaway is that recognition analysis begins where an acknowledgment-relevant difference becomes visible, but it remains disciplined only if silence, delay, and omission are not converted into verdicts too quickly.

### Example 2 — The Delayed Apology That May or May Not Be Avoidance

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** After “PMS dependency and calibration discipline,” before “Derived structures,” or later as a worked example after “Non-capture and rival readings.”

Two colleagues, Mira and Jonas, disagree after a tense planning meeting. Mira says afterward, “When you dismissed my concern in front of everyone, I need you to acknowledge that it put me in a difficult position.” Jonas replies, “I hear that the meeting felt bad. I need to think about what actually happened before I say more.” Three days pass. Mira experiences the delay as a second injury: first the public dismissal, then the absence of acknowledgment. Jonas experiences the same delay as restraint: he does not want to apologize for something he does not yet understand or accept. A third colleague says the meeting was rushed and that several people interrupted one another.

This example demonstrates the difference between acknowledgment as a structural frame and acknowledgment as a concluded obligation. The case contains many recognition elements, but it does not by itself decide whether Jonas owes an apology, whether Mira’s request is accurate, or whether the delay is avoidance. The vignette is concrete enough to show PMS hooks, but still bounded enough to preserve rival readings.

The PMS Core reading begins with Δ. A differentiated event is named: the meeting interruption or dismissal. A differentiated claim is also named: Mira’s request that Jonas acknowledge the effect of his conduct. ∇ appears as directedness toward response: Mira’s statement presses toward acknowledgment, while Jonas’s reply directs the scene toward delay, review, or caution. □ is the acknowledgment frame: the relevant question is not merely whether the meeting was tense, but whether Jonas should explicitly acknowledge a particular effect on Mira.

Λ is central. The apology or acknowledgment does not occur immediately. The delay becomes structurally meaningful because Mira’s request creates an expectation of response. Yet Λ must be calibrated carefully. The non-event may be evasion, but it may also be legitimate restraint. Jonas’s “I need to think” is not automatically passive praxis, refusal, manipulation, or responsibility-shift. Α is not established unless the delay becomes a recurring pattern or stabilized role-script.

Ω is possible but not settled. Jonas may have had public speaking power in the meeting; Mira may have borne exposure; the public setting may have amplified cost. But the example does not yet provide enough evidence about hierarchy, authority, meeting norms, consequences, or alternatives. Θ is strong because the sequence matters: meeting, request, delayed reply, three-day interval, later interpretation. Φ is possible because the situation can be reframed in several ways: “dismissal,” “tense meeting,” “rushed exchange,” “public harm,” “careful review,” or “avoidance.”

Χ is the key calibration operator. Without Χ, the example would collapse into a quick verdict. One reader may identify with Mira and treat the delay as obvious non-recognition. Another may identify with Jonas and treat the request as premature moral pressure. PMS does not resolve that by authority. It holds the frame open while asking what evidence would support each reading.

Σ remains conditional. No integrated resolution is shown. Jonas has not yet acknowledged, refused, repaired, or explained in a way that integrates the event into a shared practice. Mira’s experience is also not integrated into a mutually accepted account. Ψ remains conditional because no one is shown to take durable responsibility or self-bind to a future practice.

The EDEN overlay becomes relevant but suspended. EDEN asks whether Jonas’s delay falsely coordinates real asymmetry. If Jonas had clear public power, knew the effect, had available alternatives, and used “I need to think” to externalize the emotional or explanatory burden onto Mira, EDEN could become more active. If the delay shifted waiting cost, explanation burden, and self-binding load to Mira while preserving Jonas’s appearance of reasonableness, false-coordinated real asymmetry might become a candidate. But the vignette does not yet show that. It also contains counter-scene evidence: the meeting was rushed, several people interrupted one another, and Jonas may be preserving accuracy rather than avoiding responsibility.

MIP would treat this as a maturity-in-practice scene only if more evidence were supplied. It could ask what each role could realistically know, what alternatives each had, what consequences were predictable, and whether there was a serious integration attempt. It would not assign an M-score from the vignette alone. It would also keep the D-module off unless a safe, concrete, time-bound dignity analysis were necessary and protective. AHP would warn that the example is emotionally legible enough to invite premature labeling: “avoidant,” “demanding,” “immature,” “defensive,” or “overexposed.” Those labels are prohibited.

Forbidden claims include: Jonas is avoiding responsibility; Mira is demanding false acknowledgment; the delay is passive aggression; the apology is owed; EDEN false coordination is established; public dignity was violated; the scene proves maturity or immaturity. The example supports none of these as findings.

A stronger reading would require additional scene data: the meeting record, role hierarchy, what Jonas could realistically know, what alternatives he had, whether Mira bore concrete waiting or exposure costs, whether Jonas later clarified or repaired, and whether the delay became a repeated pattern. The reading would weaken if the delay preserved accuracy, if the public interaction was genuinely ambiguous, if Jonas lacked authority to acknowledge the claim, if a procedural review was needed, or if Mira’s requested frame did not fit the event. These weakening conditions are not side-notes; they are part of what makes the example compatible with the completed YAML discipline.

The non-capture note is that delayed acknowledgment has at least two structurally serious interpretations. It can be a way of refusing responsibility while appearing careful, but it can also be the only responsible alternative to false closure. The article takeaway is that PMS and EDEN together do not ask whether delay “feels bad” or “looks reasonable” in isolation. They ask what the delay does within a frame, under what asymmetries, across what time, with what costs, and with what openness to integration or correction.

### Example 3 — The Institutional “We Acknowledge Your Concern” Reply

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** After “Layer interaction,” before “Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions.”

A student writes to a university office after a seminar incident: “I want the department to acknowledge that the way my question was handled discouraged me from participating further.” The office replies: “We acknowledge your concern and take student experience seriously. We will share your feedback with the relevant staff.” The student reads the reply as formulaic and non-committal. The office reads it as appropriate because it avoids prejudging the incident before review. No further information is available about the seminar, the staff member, the department’s procedures, or any later follow-up.

This example demonstrates why acknowledgment language itself is not enough. The sentence “We acknowledge your concern” appears to perform recognition, but its structural meaning remains open. It may be a genuine first step in a procedural process. It may be a bureaucratic formula that recognizes the existence of a complaint while avoiding the substance. It may preserve fairness by not making premature findings. It may also shift the burden of waiting and explanation back to the student. The example is therefore useful precisely because the word “acknowledge” appears while the case remains non-closed.

The PMS Core hooks begin with Δ: a seminar incident, a student’s participation concern, an institutional response, and a distinction between acknowledging a concern and acknowledging the claimed event. ∇ appears in the student’s request for recognition and in the office’s directed response. □ is the institutional acknowledgment frame. The frame matters because institutional acknowledgment is not the same as interpersonal apology. It may be governed by procedure, confidentiality, review obligations, fairness to staff, and risk management.

Λ appears in what the office does not say. It does not say the incident happened as described. It does not say the student was wronged. It does not say the staff member acted improperly. It does not state a remedy. But those absences cannot be interpreted without the frame. In an institutional context, such non-events may be protective, evasive, procedural, or all three in different respects.

Α is not established. One formulaic reply does not prove a bureaucratic attractor or institutional pattern. A repeated practice of generic acknowledgments without review, repair, or feedback could later create an attractor, but that is not supplied. Ω is possible because the institution may control process, language, timing, and access to review. The student may carry waiting cost or reduced participation. Still, real Ω cannot be fully mapped without information about procedure, timelines, appeal routes, staff obligations, and the student’s actual exposure.

Θ is highly relevant. The reply may be an early-stage procedural acknowledgment rather than a final response. Its meaning changes depending on what follows. A formulaic first reply followed by serious review differs from a formulaic reply followed by silence. Φ is also relevant: the student’s claim is reframed as “your concern” and “student experience.” This could be a legitimate procedural narrowing, or it could reduce the claim’s substance. PMS cannot decide without more evidence.

Χ is indispensable. The analyst must not treat institutional language as bad faith merely because it is generic. Nor may the analyst treat procedure as innocence. The reply may be both procedurally justified and experientially insufficient. Χ preserves that tension.

Σ remains conditional. The office’s reply does not show whether the concern is integrated into review, policy, staff feedback, repair, or future practice. Ψ remains conditional because the institution has not clearly self-bound to any responsibility beyond sharing feedback. Even that phrase is vague.

The EDEN overlay is especially relevant as a threshold tool. The office’s reply may coordinate asymmetry truthfully if it accurately reflects procedural limits while keeping the student’s concern reviewable. It may falsely coordinate asymmetry if it uses recognition language to appear responsive while shifting all cost of waiting, uncertainty, and renewed explanation to the student. The key EDEN question is not whether the reply sounds bureaucratic. It is whether real institutional Ω is coordinated truthfully or falsely.

Action-Power is visible as a possible overlay handle. The office may have power over timing, process, language, recognition status, and access to remedy. The student may have limited power beyond submitting the concern and waiting. But EDEN cannot conclude false coordination without concrete evidence of displaced responsibility, blocked Σ, externalized Ψ-load, or cost distribution. The counter-scene is strong: procedural neutrality may be ethically and legally necessary before review.

MIP would treat this example as a transmission and IA-risk scene. Institutional replies often carry asymmetry because one side controls the official record and timing. But MIP would not assign maturity to the student, staff member, or institution from this vignette. It would ask what the office could know, what it was permitted to say, what alternatives existed, what consequences were predictable, and whether a serious integration attempt followed. The D-module would remain off unless a safe and specific dignity analysis were needed. AHP would flag the danger that polished institutional language and polished analytical language can both look more complete than they are.

For operationalization, the example would need a procedural timeline, the office’s mandate, what “share your feedback” actually means, whether the student can contest the frame, whether any review or follow-up occurs, and whether the reply changes the student’s exposure, options, or participation conditions. Without those details, the example remains a worked demonstration of thresholds rather than an institutional finding. The QC status therefore mirrors the full article: structurally useful, non-decisional, non-forensic, and not mature for application beyond the bounded illustrative purpose.

Forbidden claims include: the institution gaslit the student; the student’s claim was validated; the staff member acted wrongly; the reply is legally inadequate; the office acted maturely or immaturely; the student’s dignity was violated; EDEN PFO is present; Reciprocity Loss is established. None of these follows from the bounded material.

The non-capture note is that institutional acknowledgment often performs two incompatible-looking functions at once: it may recognize receipt while withholding substantive judgment. That withholding can be legitimate, evasive, protective, or harmful depending on scene data. The article takeaway is that the analytical stack is useful precisely because it can separate acknowledgment wording from acknowledgment substance, procedural restraint from avoidance, and possible asymmetry from proven false coordination.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The YAML analyses preserve several rival readings. These rival readings are not defects. They are necessary conditions of responsible PMS application. A rival reading preserves something that the PMS or overlay structure might otherwise assimilate too quickly.

An **epistemological rival reading** treats the central issue as knowledge or evidentiary uptake rather than acknowledgment. Perhaps the recognition object is not yet sufficiently known. Perhaps delay reflects incomplete evidence rather than refusal. Perhaps non-acknowledgment is epistemic caution. Perhaps the correct act is not acknowledgment but verification. PMS can map the structure of differentiation and frame, but it must not collapse knowledge conditions into recognition failure.

A **phenomenological rival reading** treats recognition as lived experience, felt visibility, affective uptake, or interpersonal resonance. PMS can model structural awareness and acknowledgment, but it cannot claim access to phenomenological experience without testimony or scene evidence. What it calls recognition-as-acknowledgment may be too formal for some lived recognition contexts. A person may feel recognized without explicit acknowledgment; another may feel unrecognized despite formal acknowledgment. The PMS frame does not erase that.

A **language-practice rival reading** treats acknowledgment as a speech act or discourse practice. The decisive issue may be what words do in a given language game: admit, validate, apologize, notice, register, refuse, preserve ambiguity, or avoid legal exposure. PMS can model frame and non-event, but a language-practice reading may better preserve the local force of actual utterances. Since no utterances are supplied in the Core case, this remains open.

An **ethical rival reading** treats acknowledgment as moral responsiveness. It asks whether one owes acknowledgment, apology, repair, or accountability. PMS can model responsibility structurally, but it does not itself supply the moral norm. The case therefore cannot conclude that acknowledgment is ethically required. A future ethical reading might outrank PMS if it supplies concrete norms and scene evidence.

A **procedural or legal rival reading** treats acknowledgment as governed by rules, rights, duties, timing, evidence, or due process. In some contexts, delay or refusal may be required. Acknowledgment may have consequences that justify caution. PMS must not redescribe procedural restraint as recognition failure without domain-specific authority.

An **anti-capture rival reading** treats refusal, silence, non-binding, or non-integration as a legitimate way of preserving truth. Not every claim should be acknowledged. Not every frame should be accepted. Not every demand for recognition is valid. The YAML stack explicitly protects this rival reading.

An **institutional rival reading** may treat acknowledgment as a procedural step rather than a substantive act. “We acknowledge your concern” may be weak, but it may also be exactly what a procedure allows at an early stage. PMS and EDEN must not convert institutional caution into evasion unless the scene shows displaced responsibility, cost shifting, or blocked correction.

An **interpersonal rival reading** may treat acknowledgment as relational repair rather than structural recognition. It may ask whether a person felt seen, whether trust was restored, or whether the response carried adequate warmth. PMS can make this structurally visible only if the relevant scene data exist.

The EDEN overlay adds its own counter-scene: the case may simply not be an EDEN case. It may not contain real Ω, displaced R, cost shift, blocked Σ/Ψ, or correctability erosion. It may be better understood as PMS-Core-only, speech-act, ethical-procedural, or non-assimilative refusal.

MIP adds a further rival reading at the artefact level: the case may demonstrate analysis maturity mainly by refusing to score. The key outcome may not be a maturity finding but a transmission discipline finding.

AHP adds one more rival reading: the most important issue may be not the case content but artefact-risk. The analysis may be precise yet dangerous if reused without its working-note boundary.

None of these rival readings defeats PMS. They define the conditions under which PMS remains honest. Non-capture is not a weakness of the model. It is a requirement for responsible use. The model must be able to say, “This may not be a PMS closure case,” “This may not be an EDEN mechanism case,” “This may not be a MIP scoring case,” and “This may not be mature for transmission.”

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would be weakened if the recognition frame proved wrong or too narrow. If a concrete case showed that the decisive issue was legal notice, ritual status, institutional procedure, therapeutic containment, political recognition, evidentiary standard, or linguistic convention, the PMS recognition-as-acknowledgment frame would need revision.

The PMS reading would also be weakened if Δ was mis-specified. If the differentiated object was not a recognition claim but a liability question, a boundary request, a factual dispute, or a category error, the Core case would require recalibration.

The reading would be weakened if Λ became an over-assimilative non-event category. If silence, refusal, delay, or non-explicitness were legitimate, irrelevant, procedurally required, or outside the frame, treating them as recognition non-events would be under-discriminating.

The PMS reading would become under-discriminating if it treated all acknowledgment cases alike. Interpersonal recognition, legal acknowledgment, moral apology, empirical acceptance, public recognition, and institutional admission require different calibration. A one-size PMS reading would erase this.

The PMS reading would become over-assimilative if Φ absorbed disagreement. If every refusal became recontextualized as delayed acknowledgment, PMS would become a closure machine. Rival frames must be able to change the reading.

Stronger PMS claims would require concrete scene data: actors or role-positions, what was differentiated, what frame governed the situation, what acknowledgment was expected, what non-event occurred, what asymmetry existed, what sequence unfolded, what recontextualization happened, what integration was or was not achieved, and whether any self-binding occurred.

The EDEN reading would be weakened or defeated if no real Ω structures the scene. It would also be defeated if responsibility follows actual structuring effect, if costs are not shifted, if Σ remains open, if Ψ remains self-bound, if delay or refusal is legitimate, or if a rival explanation accounts for the decisive evidence.

The EDEN reading would become over-assimilative if it began from PFO, false coordination, sex category, role suspicion, or vibe. It would become invalid if it treated non-action as praxis by default, non-binding as avoidance by default, protection as immunity, responsibility as permission to degrade, or disagreement as confirmation.

A stronger EDEN claim would require a concrete scene packet showing real Ω, false coordination frame, displaced R, blocked/weakened/simulated/avoided/externalized Σ/Ψ, cost distribution, Action-Power vectors, counter-scene exposure, falsifiers, affected-role contestation, and independent application authority. Those requirements remain unfulfilled.

The MIP reading would be weakened if the analysis artefact were transmitted as a decision basis despite its working-note status. It would also be weakened if A or M bands were used comparatively, if D were activated without a safe scene, if IA risk were treated as verdict, or if maturity language became person-labeling.

A stronger MIP claim would require a concrete scene with role mandates, predictability, alternatives, knowledge, leverage, serious integration attempts, publicness, consequences, and reversibility conditions. The current case does not supply these.

The AHP reading would be weakened if its hardening points were treated as criticism of persons or as changes to the case result. AHP operates only on the analysis artefact. Its falsifier would be an artefact that already contains the recommended evidence register, transmission banner, score boundaries, D-boundary, IA review criteria, and rival-reading adjudication in a visually unavoidable way.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled across the stack. No layer has sufficient basis for action. The stack can guide future evidence collection, scene-packet construction, review design, and transmission hygiene. It cannot operationalize a recognition judgment.

The minimal operationalization packet for any future stronger reading would need at least the following: a bounded scene; role positions; timeline; relevant utterances or actions; expected acknowledgment; actual response or non-response; publicness; costs and exposure; available alternatives; power or authority distribution; affected-role contestation; counter-scene; falsifier; review condition; and transmission boundary. Without these, the reading must remain conceptual.

### Misuse boundary

This case has a substantial misuse boundary because recognition language can easily become coercive. The most obvious misuse would be diagnosis. The analysis cannot be used to diagnose denial, avoidance, immaturity, narcissism, pathology, lack of empathy, trauma response, or incapacity. PMS operators are not symptoms. EDEN mechanisms are not person types. MIP scores are not personality measures.

A second misuse is person ranking. The case does not rank role_A and role_B. It does not say who is more mature, more responsible, more dignified, more truthful, more adult, more reciprocal, or more coherent. Role-positions are analytic placeholders.

A third misuse is legal or forensic conclusion. Acknowledgment is not admission. Responsibility_R is not liability. Non-acknowledgment is not legal denial. EDEN false coordination is not evidence of wrongdoing. MIP maturity is not a legal standard.

A fourth misuse is metaphysical proof. PMS does not prove the essence of recognition, selfhood, dignity, reciprocity, responsibility, or subjectivity. SELF_FIXPOINT is not a metaphysical result in this case. It is analytic-only.

A fifth misuse is empirical proof. The analysis does not prove that anyone acknowledged, refused, delayed, denied, shifted costs, blocked integration, externalized self-binding, or damaged correctability. No empirical scene is present.

A sixth misuse is implementation validation. The stack does not validate an intervention, conversation strategy, demand, confrontation, institutional policy, therapeutic move, legal step, public statement, or repair protocol.

A seventh misuse is PMS Base validation. A coherent case application does not prove PMS Base. The case demonstrates how PMS can be applied under constraints; it does not establish PMS as necessary, exhaustive, or complete.

An eighth misuse is add-on-as-operator. EDEN constructs must not be smuggled into PMS Base. Adult Reciprocity, False-Coordinated Real Asymmetry, Action-Power, PFO, AEP, Reciprocity Loss, and counter-scene protocol are overlay handles. They are not PMS operators.

A ninth misuse is drift-as-verdict. A>>E risk, low-Σ risk, over-assimilation risk, under-discrimination risk, IA risk, and AHP attack surfaces are not findings of failure. They are caution markers.

A tenth misuse is closure-by-authority. The stack is technically elaborate, but technical elaboration is not authority to close the case. If anything, the completed analyses show that more structure can increase the duty to preserve non-closure.

The final misuse boundary is humiliation. No analytic clarity justifies shaming. No model term authorizes exposure. No dignity language may be used to degrade. No recognition analysis may become a tool for forcing acknowledgment by pressure.

This is especially important in applied contexts. A person reading the article may identify with the acknowledgment-seeking role and want the analysis to prove that acknowledgment was owed. Another may identify with the delaying or refusing role and want the analysis to prove that refusal was legitimate. The article authorizes neither. It preserves the frame conditions under which those questions could later be asked more responsibly.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of the completed stack is a disciplined structural reading of recognition as acknowledgment under strong non-closure conditions. PMS Core shows that recognition-as-acknowledgment can be mapped through differentiation, directedness, frame, non-event, weak attractor possibility, conditional asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, weak integration, and weak self-binding. It makes the recognition structure legible without treating that legibility as proof.

PMS-EDEN adds a sharper threshold question: does the recognition frame falsely coordinate real asymmetry? The answer remains suspended. The case does not establish real Ω, displaced responsibility, cost distribution, blocked/weakened/simulated/avoided/externalized Σ/Ψ, PFO, AEP, or Reciprocity Loss. EDEN therefore contributes a boundary discipline rather than a mechanism finding. It tells us what would have to be shown before a stronger asymmetry claim could responsibly be made.

MIP adds a maturity-in-practice reading of the analysis artefact and its possible use. It does not score persons. It does not assign M to role positions. It keeps the D-module off. It identifies the output as a working note and treats the main responsibility as transmission hygiene: avoid overclaiming, avoid person-labeling, avoid score inflation, avoid dignity misuse, and avoid converting structural clarity into action authority.

AHP adds second-order hardening. It recognizes that the analysis is precise, but that precision itself creates a risk. A formally complete article can look empirically complete. A polished YAML-derived chapter can look more decisive than the case allows. AHP therefore recommends visible evidence boundaries, transmission banners, score-hygiene notes, D-boundaries, IA review criteria, and rival-reading adjudication. It hardens the analysis without rescoring it.

The three examples contribute concrete readability without replacing the analysis. The unanswered acknowledgment request shows how Δ, □, Λ, and Θ can become visible in a small scene while Ω, Σ, Ψ, EDEN, and MIP claims remain under-supported. The delayed apology example shows how delay can be either avoidance or restraint, and why Χ must remain active when acknowledgment feels morally urgent. The institutional “We acknowledge your concern” example shows why acknowledgment language itself is not enough: procedural recognition, substantive acknowledgment, bureaucratic containment, and false coordination must be distinguished rather than collapsed.

The examples are not evidence for the original case. They do not prove that recognition failed. They do not prove that EDEN applies. They do not establish maturity or immaturity. They demonstrate how the architecture should behave when confronted with readable but bounded scenes. Their function is pedagogical and methodological: they show how the analysis resists overclaim even when the vignette invites it.

What remains unclaimed is extensive and essential. The case does not claim empirical proof. It does not diagnose anyone. It does not rank persons. It does not make legal or forensic conclusions. It does not prove metaphysical claims about recognition, selfhood, dignity, or responsibility. It does not validate PMS Base. It does not validate EDEN as a mechanism finding. It does not operationalize action. It does not activate the MIP D-module. It does not upgrade the working note into a decision basis. It does not treat risk as finding, coherence as proof, or non-assignment as low score.

The boundary discipline is the real result. Recognition is made analyzable, but not captured. Acknowledgment is made structurally visible, but not compelled. Absence is made visible, but not moralized. Asymmetry is made possible, but not presumed. Integration is named, but not invented. Self-binding is modeled, but not metaphysically asserted. Dignity constrains the analysis, but does not become a score. Add-on terms sharpen thresholds, but do not become PMS operators.

The final methodological takeaway is that a layered analytical stack can be most powerful when it refuses to become louder than its evidence. PMS, EDEN, MIP, and AHP together do not produce a stronger verdict; they produce a stronger discipline of non-verdictive analysis. The case demonstrates how to render recognition-as-acknowledgment with high structural resolution while preserving rival readings, falsifiers, operational limits, and dignity-protective restraint. This is not a failure to conclude. It is the conclusion the material warrants: a chapter-ready map of recognition under conditions where closure would be premature.
