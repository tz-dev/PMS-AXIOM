### Why this case matters

Structural injustice matters because it places pressure on one of the most persistent closure habits in moral, political, legal, and social analysis: the demand to locate harm in a single agent, intention, wrongful act, or discrete culpable event. The case asks what happens when harms, disadvantages, responsibilities, or constraints arise from arrangements rather than from one isolated wrong. Such arrangements may be social, institutional, historical, procedural, distributional, recognitional, or infrastructural. Their effects may be real without being easily reducible to a single actor’s intention. Their persistence may be visible without yielding a simple assignment of guilt. Their repair may be necessary without a clean tribunal-style origin story.

This is philosophically difficult because it pulls against several familiar closure demands at once. One closure demand asks for agency: who did it? Another asks for intention: who meant it? Another asks for legality: who violated a duty? Another asks for metaphysics: what kind of entity is a structure, and can it be responsible? Another asks for moral psychology: who is blameworthy? Another asks for governance: what should be done? Structural injustice becomes hard to handle because these demands do not disappear, but none of them can be allowed to dominate the analysis prematurely.

The case belongs in AXIOM because AXIOM is a cartographic and indexing paper for closure-demands, not a new base theory and not a verdict machine. Structural injustice is exemplary for AXIOM because the case is not merely asking for an answer. It is asking which kind of closure is being demanded, which frame makes the demand intelligible, which residues remain, which operators carry the analysis, which layer is allowed to add precision, and where misuse begins. The case therefore tests AXIOM’s ability to hold several things together without collapsing them: structural legibility without metaphysical totalization; responsibility without blame inflation; asymmetry without person-ranking; non-capture without nihilism; and conflict-risk visibility without conflict diagnosis.

The closure-demand is especially strong because “structural injustice” often appears where ordinary responsibility language feels both necessary and inadequate. If analysis remains too agent-centered, it may miss path-dependence, institutional routines, inherited exclusions, distributional patterns, recognition failures, delayed effects, and unequal capacities to contest. If analysis becomes too structural, it may dissolve agency, responsibility, and repair into vague background conditions. If analysis becomes too legal, it may ignore harms that are not readily actionable under legal doctrine. If analysis becomes too moralized, it may turn structural position into shame, guilt, or moral identity. If analysis becomes too empirical too quickly, it may overclaim evidence that the case material does not provide. If analysis becomes too metaphysical, it may ask for a final theory of collective agency or structural responsibility where the present artefact only warrants a structural mapping.

For that reason, the completed YAML analyses do not “solve” structural injustice. They make it readable as a disciplined structural category. PMS Core identifies the operator chain through which the category becomes legible. PMS-CONFLICT shows where the category can become conflict-relevant without treating conflict as already established. MIP checks whether the analysis can be used responsibly as a structural working note. AHP tests the analysis artefact itself for precision, attack surfaces, and hardening needs. Each layer adds visibility, but each layer also narrows what may be claimed.

The case is not being solved metaphysically because the material does not establish what structures ultimately are, whether collectives have metaphysical agency, or whether responsibility can be grounded in a final theory of social ontology. It is not being solved empirically because no data are supplied about actual arrangements, harms, affected groups, institutions, or causal mechanisms. It is not being solved clinically or diagnostically because no person, personality, pathology, or clinical presentation is under analysis. It is not being solved legally or forensically because no jurisdiction, duty, standard of causation, evidentiary record, liability rule, or remedy structure is supplied. It is not being solved implementationally because no policy environment, institutional authority, procedural target, or operational design requirement is given. The YAMLs therefore preserve the case as a bounded conceptual-domain projection.

The case matters precisely because it shows how a category can be structurally powerful without becoming authoritative. Structural injustice is not reduced to “somebody is guilty,” but neither is it emptied into “nobody is responsible.” It becomes a disciplined space of analysis in which frames, asymmetries, temporal patterns, non-events, recontextualizations, distance constraints, weak integration, and conditional self-binding must be kept visible.

### Claim status and scope discipline

The PMS Core pass types the case as a philosophical and conceptual-domain projection. The claim is structural: it concerns how the category of structural injustice can be modeled within PMS_1.3. It is not a concrete scene claim. It does not say that a given institution has committed structural injustice, that a specific group is harmed, that a legal duty has been breached, that any individual is blameworthy, or that any empirical pattern has been demonstrated.

The claim status is therefore bounded and analytic. PMS may describe the category as one in which arrangements can frame, stabilize, distribute, and temporally extend harms, disadvantages, responsibilities, or constraints. It may mark the need to distinguish isolated acts from patterned arrangements. It may identify the role of asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, and residual non-closure. It may show where responsibility becomes structurally possible but not yet assigned. It may show why later operators such as Σ and Ψ remain expensive. It may not claim that PMS has validated itself by handling the case.

The concrete-scene boundary is central. Because the case is not a concrete scene, the analysis cannot specify actual roles, actual event sequences, actual harms, actual evidence, actual institutions, actual decision points, actual public exposure, or actual responsibility bearers. This does not make the analysis empty. It means that the analysis remains at the level of case category, operator grammar, and boundary discipline. The YAMLs treat this as a strength rather than a defect: the absence of scene material prevents overclaim and forces the reading to state what would be needed for stronger claims.

The empirical boundary is strict. The case material does not provide empirical evidence that any structural injustice has occurred. It does not provide data about distributional outcomes, institutional histories, social mechanisms, affected groups, policy effects, or causal pathways. Therefore no empirical finding is made. The analysis may say what empirical work would need to examine: recurrence, stabilization, asymmetry, temporal path-dependence, institutional routines, unequal exposure, blocked remedies, missing recognition, delayed repair, or responsibility diffusion. But those requirements are marked as unfulfilled.

The metaphysical boundary is equally strict. The analysis does not decide whether structures are metaphysical agents, whether collective responsibility has an ontological basis, whether historical arrangements have agency, whether injustice has an ultimate moral property, or whether dignity is metaphysically grounded. PMS does not turn structural legibility into metaphysical proof. MIP likewise preserves D₀ as untouched and non-rankable; it does not derive ontological dignity from case analysis. The case therefore remains non-metaphysical in its claim mode.

The diagnostic and clinical boundaries are non-negotiable. No person is described as immature, deficient, disordered, guilty, pathological, unaware, irresponsible, or dignity-impaired. Drift patterns are not diagnoses. MIP fields are not person scores. D-language is protective only. Even where the case concerns harm, disadvantage, or responsibility, the analysis does not infer inner states, motives, character traits, psychological failure, or clinical conditions.

The legal and forensic boundary prevents the structural reading from becoming liability analysis. Structural responsibility is not legal responsibility. PMS Responsibility_R is not guilt. MIP M-score is not assigned and would not be guilt even if assigned in a concrete case. No legal duty, causation test, evidentiary threshold, remedy, jurisdiction, procedural standing, or forensic attribution is supplied. The analysis may say that legal-forensic readings remain rival or downstream frameworks; it may not substitute for them.

The implementation boundary is also active. The YAMLs do not recommend a policy, intervention, governance step, institutional redesign, algorithmic tool, administrative action, or enforcement procedure. MIP classifies the current artefact as a working note. AHP explicitly states that hardening the analysis does not make it public-ready or implementable. Any move into governance, legal evaluation, policy design, public classification, clinical use, computational deployment, or institutional decision-making would be a mode shift requiring new evidence and domain validation.

The PMS Base validation boundary is explicit. A successful PMS mapping does not prove PMS. A coherent operator chain does not validate PMS_1.3. A useful CONFLICT overlay does not validate PMS-CONFLICT. A high AHP Precision Heuristic does not validate MIP or the underlying case. The analyses are artefact applications, not model proofs. The correct conclusion is not “PMS solves structural injustice,” but “within the declared limits, PMS makes the closure-demand and its residues structurally legible.”

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. Stronger claims would require concrete scene data: identified arrangements, affected positions, institutional roles, evidence of recurrence, temporal sequence, actual asymmetry, publicness conditions, responsibility bearers, available alternatives, contest channels, and domain-specific standards. Without those, no A-score, M-score, IA classification, CONFLICT terminality, binding collision, exposure capture, or downstream docking can be established.

### Scene and frame structure

The case has a conceptual scene rather than a concrete one. The scene is the philosophical and structural question: how should structural injustice be understood when harms, disadvantages, responsibilities, or constraints arise from arrangements rather than from a single agent, intention, or isolated wrongful act? This scene is not located in a courtroom, workplace, institution, policy process, public controversy, clinical encounter, or empirical dataset. It is a category scene: a frame for asking what kinds of structures are needed to make arrangement-mediated injustice legible.

The frame □ is the category frame of structural injustice. Inside the frame are several elements: social arrangements, institutional arrangements, historical arrangements, harms, disadvantages, responsibilities, constraints, recurrence or stabilization, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, and the refusal to reduce the category to a single intentional act. The frame also includes the methodological demand to distinguish structural explanation from exculpation and structural responsibility from guilt. It holds open the possibility that responsibility may be distributed, role-bound, temporally extended, or institutionally mediated, but it does not assign such responsibility without further specification.

Outside the frame are metaphysical theories of ultimate justice, legal doctrines of liability, empirical causal findings, psychological diagnoses, clinical categories, person evaluations, direct governance interventions, and implementation designs. Also outside the frame are any claims that would require concrete scene evidence: who acted, who knew, who was harmed, what institution did what, what rule applied, what evidence exists, what remedy is warranted, or what public exposure occurred.

The protected constraints are boundary discipline, reversibility, distance, and dignity-in-practice. The frame protects against several collapses. It prevents structural injustice from collapsing into isolated wrongdoing. It prevents arrangement-mediated analysis from collapsing into “nobody is responsible.” It prevents asymmetry from becoming person-ranking. It prevents responsibility from becoming guilt. It prevents non-capture from becoming analytical failure. It prevents conflict-risk language from becoming conflict verdict. It prevents MIP fields from becoming person scores. It prevents AHP hardening from becoming transmission approval.

Roles are present only as possible structural positions, not as concrete agents. The MIP analysis names possible affected positions, possible structural or institutional role-bearers, and the analyst or observer. Affected positions would be those bearing harms, disadvantages, constraints, vulnerability, exclusion, or reduced options within arrangements, but none are concretely identified. Structural or institutional role-bearers might include institutions, offices, collectives, policymakers, administrators, publics, or participants whose actions reproduce, stabilize, mitigate, or contest arrangements, but none are specified. The analyst role is the only concrete role within the artefact: an external, non-adjudicative structural analyst with interpretive power over the representation of the case and with responsibility to maintain boundaries.

Temporality Θ is central because structural injustice is not usually legible as a single moment. It involves historical arrangement, persistence, accumulation, inheritance, delayed repair, path-dependence, repeated routines, or durable distribution of costs. In the abstract case, however, no concrete timeline is supplied. Θ is therefore conceptually active but empirically unscaled. The analysis can say that structural injustice requires temporal attention; it cannot say what the timeline is, how long a pattern persisted, whether a window for revision closed, or whether lock-in occurred.

The reversibility window belongs first to the analysis itself. Because the case is abstract, all readings remain revisable. PMS Core remains a structural reading, PMS-CONFLICT remains weak or risk-level, MIP remains a working note, and AHP hardens the artefact without upgrading it. In a future concrete case, reversibility would require attention to correction paths, appeal channels, review points, publicness, archival effects, role-specific exit options, and the possibility that structural labels harden into durable records. But those are requirements, not findings.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are category-level. The case explicitly concerns harms, disadvantages, responsibilities, and constraints, which makes Ω central. Yet the actual exposure gradient is not established. We do not know who bears harm, who controls framing, who has authority, who can contest, who benefits, who is delayed, who is ignored, or who pays the cost of revision. PMS can mark the structural relevance of asymmetry; it cannot assign the gradient.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core analysis proceeds through the operator inventory in order: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. The order matters because the later operators cannot be used as cheap closure. Structural injustice is precisely the kind of case where premature movement to integration, self-binding, responsibility, or final judgement would produce overclaim.

**Δ, Difference, is active.** The basic difference is the distinction between harm or disadvantage reducible to a single agent, intention, or isolated wrongful act and harm or disadvantage arising from arrangements. This difference is not merely topical. It changes the kind of closure being demanded. If an isolated wrong is at issue, one may ask who acted, with what intention, in which moment. If a structural arrangement is at issue, the analysis must ask how frames, routines, histories, positions, institutions, and asymmetries distribute effects. Δ is calibrated by the case material itself, which explicitly contrasts single-agent reduction with social, institutional, or historical arrangements. Overclaim would occur if Δ were treated as evidence that structural injustice has in fact occurred in a concrete setting. At this level, Δ only makes the category distinction legible.

**∇, Impulse, is weak but present as closure pressure.** The case generates pressure to explain injustice structurally. It also generates pressure to close responsibility: if harms arise from arrangements, how should responsibility be understood? Yet no concrete demand, actor, affect, institutional decision, or practical urgency is supplied. ∇ therefore marks the directional pressure of the philosophical problem rather than a live scene impulse. Pushing ∇ too far would turn the conceptual question into an urgent demand for judgement or action. The YAMLs avoid that by treating ∇ as category-level pressure only.

**□, Frame, is active and central.** The case is framed around structural injustice as an arrangement-mediated category. The frame determines what counts as relevant: social, institutional, and historical arrangements; harms, disadvantages, responsibilities, and constraints; non-reduction to single intention or isolated act. The frame is not a metaphysical totality. It is a declared analytic boundary. Calibration is satisfied because the case material itself gives the frame language. But the frame remains abstract: it does not specify which institution, history, rule, social practice, or normative order is at issue. Overclaim would occur if the frame were treated as a concrete institution or if structural injustice were assumed wherever inequality or harm appears.

**Λ, Non-event or residue, is partial and important.** Structural injustice often involves missing events: absent recognition, absent protection, absent remedy, absent access, absent correction, absent handover, absent contest channel, absent explicit responsibility, absent institutional response. The category is strongly compatible with Λ because structural harms can arise not only from what is done but also from what fails to occur within a frame where it would be expected. Yet the case supplies no specific expected event whose absence is established. Therefore Λ remains partial. It marks possible structured absence, not proven omission. Overclaim would occur if every missing piece of evidence, every disagreement, every rival frame, or every absence were automatically translated into structural injustice. The YAMLs explicitly block that move.

**Α, Attractor, is partial.** Structural injustice often requires recurrence, stabilization, path-dependence, or role-script reproduction. Arrangements become structurally unjust when patterns persist, not merely when one bad event occurs. PMS Core therefore finds Α conceptually relevant. But no repeated scene, empirical recurrence, institutional routine, or stabilized script is supplied. Α can mark the kind of evidence a stronger analysis would need; it cannot claim actual attractor formation. Overclaim would occur if the mere seriousness of the topic were treated as proof of recurrence.

**Ω, Asymmetry, is active and central.** The case concerns harms, disadvantages, responsibilities, and constraints. These are asymmetry-laden terms. Structural injustice becomes intelligible where exposure, capacity, vulnerability, obligation, access, recognition, contestability, or authority are unevenly distributed. Ω is calibrated by the case material itself. But Ω must remain structural. It cannot become moral ranking, humiliation, guilt assignment, victim-status adjudication, authority claim, or dignity hierarchy. A stronger Ω analysis would need to identify who bears which costs, who controls which frame, who can exit, who can contest, who is heard, who is delayed, and who benefits from the arrangement. Without this, Ω remains category-active but not empirically assigned.

**Θ, Temporality, is active and central.** Structural injustice is difficult precisely because it often unfolds over time: historical arrangements, inherited constraints, accumulated disadvantage, delayed recognition, path dependence, long-term exposure, and slow institutional routines. Θ is calibrated by the reference to historical arrangements and by the category’s dependence on persistence. Yet no concrete chronology is supplied. We do not know when the arrangement began, how it persisted, how it changed, when remedies were missed, or whether revision windows closed. Overclaim would occur if temporality were used rhetorically to imply historical guilt or irreversible lock-in without evidence.

**Φ, Recontextualization, is active.** The case itself performs a recontextualization: instead of treating injustice through isolated agent-intention or isolated wrongful act, it shifts the analysis toward social, institutional, or historical arrangements. This does not prove the structural reading superior in every context. It marks the analytic move that makes the category visible. Φ is well calibrated because the case material explicitly asks for this shift. Its risk is over-assimilation: rival readings might be treated as merely incomplete versions of the PMS frame. The YAMLs prevent that by keeping rival moral, legal, empirical, political, phenomenological, and metaphysical readings open.

**Χ, Distance, is active as application discipline.** Χ prevents the analysis from fusing structural injustice with verdict, blame, enforcement, diagnosis, legal conclusion, or public label. It keeps the reading reversible and frame-bound. Χ is especially important here because structural injustice language can easily become morally charged. The completed analyses repeatedly use Χ to block closure-by-authority: no empirical proof, no metaphysical proof, no diagnosis, no legal-forensic conclusion, no implementation claim, no PMS Base validation, no person ranking. Calibration is strong because the output consistently maintains claim boundaries. Overclaim would occur if the analysis used its own coherence to bypass distance.

**Σ, Integration, is weak.** The case does not provide a full theory integrating agency, structure, causation, harm, responsibility, frame, non-event, and temporal trajectory. PMS can produce a coherent structural reading, but that coherence is not the same as full integration of the phenomenon. Σ remains weak because too many pieces are under-specified: concrete roles, evidence, legal criteria, affected perspectives, causal mechanisms, responsibility bearers, and rival frameworks. Overclaim would occur if the PMS reading were treated as complete because it is elegant. The YAMLs explicitly mark coherence risk: the reading may become too smooth or under-discriminating.

**Ψ, Self-binding, is weak and conditional.** Responsibility is named in the case material, but no self-binding bearer is supplied. PMS Responsibility_R requires Ψ together with Φ, Θ, and Ω. Without a bearer—person, institution, office, collective, role, or procedure—self-binding cannot be established. The analysis may say that structural injustice raises possible responsibility-structures; it may not say who is bound to what. Overclaim would occur if responsibility vocabulary were inflated into guilt, legal liability, collective personhood, institutional selfhood, or binding collision. The YAMLs block that move.

The Core reading therefore produces a precise but limited formula: structural injustice is readable as a frame-bound category in which arrangement-mediated patterns may stabilize asymmetries across time, while recontextualization shifts analysis away from isolated agent-intention models and distance prevents conversion into verdict. Integration and self-binding remain conditional because no concrete scene, bearer, or evidence is supplied.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The dependency discipline is not a technical afterthought; it is the main safeguard of the case. Structural injustice creates strong pressure to move quickly from harm to responsibility, from responsibility to blame, from blame to verdict, and from verdict to action. PMS prevents that shortcut by requiring the operator chain to remain calibrated.

Later operators cannot be used cheaply because they carry more closure. Σ and Ψ are especially expensive. Σ requires more than narrative coherence; it requires integration under Χ and Φ. Ψ requires more than the word “responsibility”; it requires a self-binding bearer and a commitment structure across time. In this case, Σ is weak and Ψ is under-specified. The analysis is therefore not allowed to conclude that responsibility has been assigned, that a structure has bound itself, that a conflict has terminally stabilized, or that action follows.

The most obvious invalid shortcut would be Δ/∇ → Ψ: “there is harm or disadvantage; therefore someone must be bound as responsible.” The YAMLs reject this. A second invalid shortcut would be Ω → verdict: “there is asymmetry; therefore blame, guilt, or authority is established.” This is also rejected. A third shortcut would be Θ → historical guilt: “there is historical arrangement; therefore responsibility is settled.” That too is invalid. A fourth shortcut would be Φ → superiority: “the structural frame is more advanced than agent-centered readings.” The analysis refuses that by preserving rival formalizations. A fifth shortcut would be Χ as avoidance: “distance means no responsibility.” The analysis does not do that; Χ preserves boundedness but does not erase possible responsibility.

Coherence is not proof. The PMS reading fits the case well because structural injustice is already structurally formulated. That creates a danger: the model may appear to capture the phenomenon too smoothly. The YAMLs call this a coherence risk and under-discrimination risk. A coherent operator chain must still distinguish structural injustice from isolated wrongdoing, accidental aggregation, ordinary inequality, lawful but non-injurious asymmetry, vague social pattern, legal liability, moral blame, and metaphysical theories of justice. If it cannot do that, it becomes too general.

Non-capture is not failure. PMS does not need to absorb every rival account. Normative theories of justice may require principles outside PMS. Empirical social science may require data and causal identification. Legal-forensic accounts may require doctrine-specific standards. Phenomenology may preserve lived experience that structural grammar does not capture. Political theories may treat rupture, refusal, antagonism, or domination as central rather than as integration problems. Metaphysical accounts may ask about agency, collectivity, or dignity in ways PMS does not settle. The YAMLs insist that these remain genuine rival readings, not merely PMS residues.

PMS must not become a closure machine. The case is about structural injustice, but the analysis is not permitted to close by structural vocabulary alone. It must keep Λ visible, keep Χ active, keep Σ and Ψ conditional, keep operationalization unfulfilled, and keep MIP/AHP transmission boundaries intact. The discipline is not only that PMS should map closure-demands; it must also show where closure is not available.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS drift-pattern analysis remains deliberately restrained. The completed YAMLs do not classify drift as an active finding. They mark drift-patterns as conditional structural risks under under-specification. This distinction matters: a drift marker is not a diagnosis, not a score, not a verdict, not a person-type, and not proof that the structure has already failed. It names what would become structurally risky if certain dependencies were skipped, suppressed, or over-hardened.

The first PMS drift-pattern is **AD_A>>E**. In the completed case, this pattern is not established as an active drift. The case is not a concrete action scene, and there is no actor whose awareness expands while action remains chronically suppressed or delayed. Still, the abstract category of structural injustice can invite an AD_A>>E-like risk. One can become highly aware of structures, harms, historical arrangements, disadvantages, constraints, and responsibility problems while no actionable pathway becomes specified. In such a situation, awareness may expand faster than action capacity. But the YAMLs avoid converting that risk into a finding. The case supplies no concrete actor, no institutional role, no available option-space, no blocked action, no delayed repair, and no decision point. Therefore the action axis remains under-specified rather than suppressed.

For AD_A>>E, the awareness axis is best rendered as over-expanded risk / under-specified in concrete terms. The analysis has strong conceptual awareness: it identifies frame, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, non-capture, and misuse risks. But this is awareness in the artefact, not awareness in an actor. The action axis remains under-specified, not chronically delayed. The important boundary is that non-action here is not failure. Because no action scene is supplied, no one can be accused of inaction, avoidance, paralysis, immaturity, or refusal. A case can be structurally aware and still legitimately non-actionable at the current layer.

The second PMS drift-pattern is **AD_Sigma_low**. This is more directly relevant, but again as a risk rather than as a finding. Σ is weak in the PMS Core analysis because the case does not provide enough material to integrate agency, arrangement, harm, responsibility, causality, historical trajectory, and repair into a coherent concrete account. Weak Σ does not mean incoherence, failure, immaturity, or contradiction. It means that the analysis remains intentionally open because stronger integration would require evidence and scene data. The coherence axis is therefore mixed / under-specified, not simply volatile. The responsibility axis is under-specified, not inconsistent. Responsibility is present as a conceptual topic, but no responsibility bearer is specified.

The danger of AD_Sigma_low would be to treat weak integration as a defect to be repaired by forcing closure. In structural injustice cases, this temptation is strong. One might say: if the structure is unjust, someone must be responsible; if someone is responsible, a duty must be assigned; if a duty is assigned, an action must follow. PMS blocks this chain unless the dependencies are fulfilled. Weak Σ is the correct result when the material does not yet support stronger integration. The analysis is not failing by refusing closure; it is preserving the distinction between structural category, concrete evidence, responsibility calibration, and applied use.

The coherence risk is therefore two-sided. On one side, the PMS reading may be too smooth: structural injustice fits frame, asymmetry, temporality, and non-reduction so well that the model may seem to have captured the phenomenon completely. On the other side, the reading may remain too weak if it never differentiates structural injustice from ordinary inequality, accidental aggregation, vague background conditions, lawful but non-injurious asymmetry, empirical pattern, legal liability, or moral blame. The YAMLs mark this as an under-discrimination risk. A successful reading must do both: avoid premature closure and still discriminate the category.

The most important drift boundary is that **under-specification is not failure**. No A-score is low because no A-score is assigned. No M-score is low because no M-score is assigned. IA is unclear because the material lacks concrete arrangement data, not because the analysis failed to decide. D is protection-only, not because dignity is weak, but because no D-profile is warranted. Σ is weak not because the case is incoherent, but because the case is abstract. Ψ is weak not because responsibility is absent in principle, but because no self-binding bearer is specified. These non-assignments protect the analysis from false precision.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The add-on layer used in the completed analysis is **PMS-CONFLICT**. PMS-CONFLICT is explicitly treated as an overlay/application profile only. It does not redefine PMS operators, change PMS dependencies, introduce new PMS primitives, or rewrite the PMS Core pass. Its role is to add conflict-specific visibility to a case that PMS Core already makes structurally legible.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer here because structural injustice is not only an arrangement-mediated category; it can also become conflict-relevant. The Core reading shows how structural injustice involves frame, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, weak integration, and conditional responsibility. PMS-CONFLICT asks a more specific question: when do such structures become stabilized incompatibility under binding, time, cost, and exposure? That question is not needed for every PMS case, but it is justified here because structural injustice often involves incompatible frames of agency, responsibility, harm, institutional history, recognition, and distribution. These frames may remain locally coherent while resisting shared integration.

The add-on does not claim that such a conflict has actually occurred. It makes visible thresholds and risks. The completed CONFLICT overlay classifies the case as **DESCRIPTIVE**, **PRIVATE**, **MG_LOW**, with **red-zone absent**, **downstream docking not triggered**, and **terminal regime not applicable**. This classification is essential. The overlay is not public, not institutional, not legal, not person-near, not sanctioning, and not governance-oriented. It is a private descriptive conceptual-domain projection.

The overlay’s reduced signatures remain shorthand only. They are not proof chains. They do not replace PMS dependencies. They do not allow Χ, Σ, or Ψ to be used without their PMS prerequisites. A reduced signature that contains Χ still inherits the PMS dependency in which Χ depends on Φ, Θ, and □. A reduced signature that contains Σ still depends on Χ and Φ. A reduced signature that contains Ψ still depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. This matters because conflict language can otherwise become a shortcut to severity, terminality, or action.

The first reduced signature, **CONFLICT_terminal_core**, is **not applicable**. Terminal conflict would require Σ under sustained load with Ψ, Ω, Θ, and costly or asymmetric Χ. The case does not meet this threshold. Ψ is not specified, Σ is weak rather than failed under concrete load, and Χ is maintained as analytic distance rather than shown as priced, blocked, or asymmetric exit. Ω and Θ are conceptually central, but that is insufficient for terminal conflict. Terminality is therefore not a finding.

The second reduced signature, **CONFLICT_stabilized_pattern**, is **weak**. PMS Core gives Α, Ω, and Θ conceptual weight: structural injustice can involve recurrence, asymmetry, and historical trajectory. But conflict-specific stabilized incompatibility is not established. A stabilized conflict pattern would require recurrence of the incompatibility itself, not merely recurrence of structural arrangements. The case does not show repeated conflict events, hardened positions, failed recontextualizations, or binding persistence. The signature therefore remains weak and under-specified.

**DRIFT_binding_collision** is **not applicable**. Binding collision requires two or more Ψ-bindings that collide under a shared frame and temporal trajectory. The case mentions responsibilities, but no self-binding agents, institutions, offices, collectives, roles, or commitments are specified. Seriousness is not Ψ-collision. Political or moral contestation is not enough. The overlay therefore rejects binding collision as a case finding.

**DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in** is marked as **risk only**. Structural injustice may involve unequal exit, delay, exposure, credibility, procedural access, revision costs, or burdens of proof. That makes lock-in conceptually relevant. But the abstract case does not identify actual role positions, exit routes, delay costs, exposure burdens, or revision asymmetries. A stronger claim would require showing that one position bears disproportionate costs for distance, refusal, waiting, correction, reframing, or exit. Since no such scene is supplied, lock-in remains a risk marker.

**DRIFT_exposure_capture** is also **risk only**. Exposure capture would occur if the descriptive reading travels into public, institutional, administrative, legal, media, or person-near contexts and becomes record-like or enforcement-like. Within the current bounded application, interpretation remains private and reversible. The overlay therefore marks exposure capture as a transfer risk, not as an active drift. It warns that Φ can clarify but can also harden under publicness.

**DRIFT_dignity_collapse** is a guarded misuse risk only. Dignity collapse would occur if conflict or structural-injustice language became shaming, ranking, humiliation, diagnosis, coercive transfer, person-near verdict, or sanction logic. The overlay explicitly blocks this. Dignity-in-Practice remains active as a constraint, not as a finding that dignity has collapsed.

Several add-on constructs are explicitly non-operator handles. **Publicness Overlay P** is not an operator. It amplifies Ω, Θ, Α, and Φ only under public or institutional circulation. In this case, P is inactive internally because no public scene is supplied. It functions as a transfer boundary. **Misuse Gradient MG** is also not an operator. It tracks transmission risk, especially where structural descriptions travel into public, institutional, or person-near environments. In the current case, MG is low, but it would increase if the analysis were published, archived, attached to persons, used institutionally, or converted into classification.

Other non-operator constructs include conflict attractor, binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, dignity collapse, terminal regime, downstream docking, scale matrices, and reduced signatures. They are overlay handles for organizing visibility, not PMS primitives. They do not expand PMS Base. They cannot create new findings by being named.

The overlay also applies several scale matrices, mostly to mark non-applicability. Θ-history scaling is weak but unscaled: historical temporality is conceptually central, but no concrete lock-in speed, recurrence interval, hardening rate, deadline structure, or reversibility window is supplied. Young Α-field dynamics are not applicable because no recently intensified volatile attractor field is described. Φ-frequency cannot be scaled because the case contains one analytic recontextualization, not repeated frame churn. Institutionalized Χ scaling is not applicable because no institution or distance regime is supplied. Application-boundary scaling is active because it concerns the status of the overlay itself: DESCRIPTIVE, PRIVATE, MG_LOW, red-zone absent. Terminal positioning is not applicable.

The CONFLICT overlay therefore adds precision without adding authority. It shows that structural injustice can become conflict-legible when arrangement-mediated harm, responsibility attribution, historical trajectory, recognition, distribution, and asymmetrical exposure stabilize into incompatible frames. But in this case, that remains weak and conceptual. No terminal regime, no tragic legibility in the strong PMS-CONFLICT sense, no binding collision, no active exposure capture, no downstream docking, and no conflict-attractor finding are established.

### Layer interaction

The PMS Core reading remains the base. It supplies the operator grammar, order, dependencies, derived structures, claim boundaries, and non-capture discipline. The Core pass defines the case as a conceptual structural reading of arrangement-mediated injustice. Everything later inherits that status. This inheritance relation is essential: the later layers may sharpen visibility, but they cannot make the case more concrete than the Core pass allows.

PMS-CONFLICT sharpens conflict-relevant thresholds. It does not add a new theory of injustice. It does not change Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, or Ψ. It does not make Ω more severe or Σ more failed by naming conflict. It asks whether the Core configuration crosses conflict-specific thresholds: stabilized incompatibility, binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, dignity collapse, terminality, or downstream docking. The answer is mostly no, or risk only. The overlay therefore functions mainly as a threshold discipline: it prevents the article from using conflict language too early, while still making visible how structural injustice can become conflict-sensitive under publicness, asymmetry, time, and responsibility pressure.

MIP adds a different kind of layer. It does not add PMS operators and does not revise PMS-CONFLICT. It reviews the completed structural artefact through A–C–R–P–D, IA-box, score hygiene, role mapping, publicness, transmission status, and guardrails. Its function is downstream governance/guardrail reading, not person evaluation. In the completed MIP analysis, the case remains a working note. A-score is not assigned. M-score is not assigned. IA-box is unclear. D is activated only as protection signal. Transmission is not approved for unrestricted use. The point is not that the case has “low” awareness, responsibility, action, or dignity; the point is that the analysis does not possess the concrete scene data required to score or classify them.

AHP then hardens the MIP analysis artefact. It is second-order. It does not analyze structural injustice directly as a new first-order layer. It analyzes the quality, attackability, precision, and transmission risks of the completed MIP output. It gives the artefact a high Precision Heuristic because the analysis defines terms, states scope boundaries, names conditions and thresholds, links evidence and inference, preserves reversibility, and maintains language hygiene. But high PH does not mean the case is more true, more empirical, more public-ready, or more actionable. It means the artefact is better bounded and easier to attack, revise, and harden.

The interaction among layers is therefore additive but non-mixing. PMS Core makes the category structurally legible. PMS-CONFLICT marks conflict-specific risk thresholds. MIP checks the artefact’s use conditions and guardrails. AHP identifies vulnerabilities in the analysis artefact and proposes hardening actions. No layer is allowed to redefine a previous layer. No layer converts another layer’s boundary into a license. The stack increases inspectability, not authority. It increases the number of explicit constraints, not the strength of empirical or legal claim.

Overlay language could overreach in several ways. “Conflict” could make a weak conceptual risk sound like an active conflict. “Terminal” could be misread as finality or severity. “Dignity collapse” could be mistaken for a dignity finding. “Maturity” could be mistaken for person evaluation. “High PH” could be mistaken for public readiness. “Structural injustice” could be mistaken for legal liability or moral guilt. The completed YAMLs repeatedly block these readings.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. PMS preserves Λ and weak Σ/Ψ. CONFLICT preserves non-terminality and risk-only drift status. MIP preserves working-note status, no scores, unclear IA, and protection-only D. AHP preserves attack surfaces rather than hiding them. The result is layered legibility without closure-by-authority.

### Example 1 — The admissions rule that never names anyone

A university department uses an admissions procedure that appears formally neutral. Applicants are ranked by prior institutional affiliation, recommendation letters, uninterrupted academic trajectory, and evidence of early access to research networks. No member of the admissions committee intends to exclude anyone. No rule explicitly names class background, disability, migration history, care obligations, or regional inequality. Each reviewer can plausibly say that they assessed only the documents before them.

Over several years, however, the same pattern recurs. Applicants from less well-connected institutions are rarely admitted. Applicants with interrupted trajectories are treated as “less competitive” even where the interruption reflects care work, illness, displacement, or lack of early access to academic mentoring. Recommendation letters from prestigious networks carry implicit credibility that other letters do not. The result is not easily reducible to one malicious decision, one explicit discriminatory rule, or one isolated wrongful act. Yet the arrangement may still produce patterned disadvantage.

This vignette demonstrates the central category problem of structural injustice. The question is not whether one reviewer secretly intended exclusion. Nor is it whether a legal violation has already been established. The question is how an arrangement can distribute opportunity, credibility, risk, and burden without a single decisive wrongdoer. The example makes visible why the case cannot be handled only through the agent-intention frame. If the analysis asks only, “Who intended to exclude?”, it may miss the way the frame itself filters relevance.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. Δ distinguishes isolated wrongful act from arrangement-mediated disadvantage. □ is the admissions frame: the rules, credibility markers, institutional expectations, and role-space in which applications are read. Λ appears as possible structured absence: missing recognition of interrupted trajectories, missing access to recommendation networks, missing review of whether prestige is being used as proxy for merit. Α appears if the pattern recurs over cycles and stabilizes into routine. Ω appears in unequal credibility, unequal access to preparatory resources, unequal ability to absorb interruption, and unequal burden of proof. Θ appears because the disadvantage is cumulative: early access, institutional prestige, and uninterrupted trajectory compound over time. Φ appears when the case is recontextualized from “neutral individual assessment” to “arrangement-mediated opportunity sorting.” Χ is required so that the analysis does not become a tribunal against individual reviewers. Σ remains conditional because a full integrated account would require data, institutional history, comparison groups, and procedural analysis. Ψ remains conditional because no self-binding responsibility bearer is yet specified: it may later be a committee, a faculty, an institution, a policy office, or no single bearer in the strong PMS sense.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant only as risk visibility. The example could become conflict-relevant if merit, fairness, institutional autonomy, historical exclusion, and responsibility attribution stabilize into mutually incompatible frames. But the vignette does not establish terminal conflict. There is no binding collision finding, no priced exit finding, and no active exposure capture. If the example were used publicly to shame named committee members, that would be a misuse of the overlay, not a valid CONFLICT application.

MIP would treat this as a possible future concrete case only if additional information were supplied. No A-score or M-score follows from the vignette. The IA-box cannot be completed without evidence about transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility of the admissions rule. Dignity-in-Practice would matter as a protection constraint: applicants must not be represented as deficient persons, and reviewers must not be converted into global blame objects without role-specific calibration. AHP would likely flag provenance and transmission risk if this example were inserted into a public article: the example must remain illustrative unless data are provided.

The forbidden claims are important. This example does not prove unlawful discrimination. It does not prove that any reviewer is biased, immoral, negligent, or diagnostically deficient. It does not prove that the admissions system is empirically unjust. It does not prove that prestige should never matter. It does not establish legal liability, metaphysical responsibility, or policy remedy. It shows only how structural injustice becomes intelligible when formally neutral procedures distribute opportunity through recurrent frames, asymmetries, and temporal accumulation.

The non-capture note is equally important. A legal theorist might ask whether protected-category discrimination can be shown. A sociologist might demand longitudinal data. A political theorist might focus on class reproduction or institutional legitimacy. A phenomenological account might focus on how rejected applicants experience invisibility or humiliation. PMS does not defeat those readings. It prepares the structural map on which their questions can be located.

The article takeaway is that structural injustice becomes visible when the analysis shifts from intention alone to frame, recurrence, asymmetry, and time — but that shift still does not authorize blame, liability, diagnosis, scoring, or closure.

### Example 2 — The complaint channel that formally exists

A public institution has a complaint procedure for residents who believe they were treated unfairly. The procedure is documented on the website, the form is available, and a response deadline exists. Formally, therefore, the institution can say that remedy is available. No one is barred from filing a complaint. No official explicitly refuses recognition. No policy states that some residents matter less than others.

In practice, the procedure is difficult to use. The form requires technical language. Supporting documents must be uploaded in a format that many residents cannot easily produce. Responses are brief and cite internal categories that are not explained. Appeals are possible but must be filed within a short deadline, while the institution itself may take months to answer. Residents who have stable time, language skills, administrative experience, and legal support can navigate the channel. Others can formally enter the procedure but cannot realistically make it work.

This vignette demonstrates why Λ, structured absence, is crucial but dangerous. The complaint channel exists; therefore the injustice, if any, is not simply that there is no procedure. The relevant absence is more subtle: missing intelligibility, missing practical access, missing explanation, missing time symmetry, missing support, missing effective reversibility. PMS allows such absences to be analyzed only if they are anchored in a frame. Here the frame is the institution’s own promise of review. The absence matters because the procedure creates an expectation: complaints should be not only formally receivable but practically contestable.

The PMS Core hooks unfold in order. Δ distinguishes formal availability from real accessibility. ∇ appears as the pressure to contest a decision or seek review. □ is the complaint procedure as institutional frame. Λ appears where expected explanation, access, or remedy fails to materialize in practice. Α would be established only if this pattern recurs across cases rather than appearing once. Ω appears in the asymmetry between institutional time and resident time, institutional language and resident language, institutional record control and resident evidence burdens. Θ appears in delay, appeal deadlines, accumulated fatigue, and the way procedural burdens compound. Φ recontextualizes the issue from “a remedy exists” to “the remedy may be structurally unavailable to some positions.” Χ prevents the analysis from immediately declaring bad faith or legal violation. Σ remains conditional because an integrated account would need records, user experiences, procedural data, and comparison with administrative constraints. Ψ remains conditional because responsibility requires specifying whether the institution, office, administrators, designers, policymakers, or another role is self-bound to remedy access.

This example also shows why dependency shortcuts would be invalid. It would be too quick to move from “some residents struggle” to “the institution is guilty.” It would also be too quick to move from “the procedure exists” to “the institution is fair.” PMS forces the analysis to stay between these closures. The structural question is whether the frame’s own promise generates expectations that are not practically fulfilled, whether this failure recurs, whether it distributes burdens asymmetrically, and whether it persists over time.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant as a risk layer. Conflict may arise if institutional actors frame the procedure as adequate because it exists, while residents frame it as inaccessible because it cannot be effectively used. These frames can become locally coherent yet mutually non-integrable. Still, the example does not establish CONFLICT_terminal_core. It does not show failed Σ under binding, costly Χ, and sustained Ψ collision. DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in is a risk only: if residents bear high costs for contesting while the institution bears low costs for delay, lock-in may become relevant. DRIFT_exposure_capture is also only a transfer risk: if complaints become public records or residents are exposed through the process, the analysis would require a stronger publicness and misuse-gradient review.

MIP would treat the example as requiring careful IA-box work. Transparency may be partial: the procedure is published, but its categories may be opaque. Justification may be unclear: the institution may have reasons for document requirements, but those reasons must be tested against access burdens. Time-boundedness is asymmetrical if resident deadlines are short while institutional delays are long. Reversibility may be formal rather than real if appeals are too hard to use. Yet no IA verdict follows from the vignette alone. The example supplies a structure, not evidence. No A-score or M-score should be assigned. Dignity-in-Practice remains a guardrail: residents should not be portrayed as incompetent, and officials should not be portrayed as malicious without further evidence.

AHP would likely recommend hardening if this example were used in the article. The article should clearly distinguish illustrative structure from empirical finding. It should state that “formal availability versus practical accessibility” is the analytic distinction, not a conclusion that this particular institution is unjust. It should also prevent high coherence from becoming authority: the example is readable, but readability is not proof.

Forbidden claims include legal invalidity, administrative misconduct, intentional exclusion, clinical stress claims, moral inferiority, dignity loss, or policy prescription. The example does not prove that the complaint channel is unlawful or unjust in a given real-world setting. It does not show that any official intended harm. It does not establish that all procedural complexity is injustice. It does not prescribe reform. It shows what a structural analysis would need to ask.

The non-capture note should remain visible. Administrative law might frame the issue through due process, standing, deadlines, and reason-giving. Empirical research might measure who uses the procedure and with what outcomes. A phenomenological account might describe exhaustion, humiliation, or distrust. A political account might treat the procedure as legitimacy management. PMS maps the structural conditions but does not absorb these rival analyses.

The article takeaway is that structural injustice may appear not where remedy is absent in name, but where the practical conditions of remedy are asymmetrically distributed. Even then, the correct output is not verdict but calibrated inquiry.

### Example 3 — The public label that hardens faster than the analysis

A research group prepares an internal structural analysis of a city housing process. The draft says that certain residents may face arrangement-mediated disadvantage because eligibility categories, documentation requirements, waiting lists, and appeal procedures interact over time. The draft is careful. It states that no legal conclusion is being made, no person is being blamed, no empirical finding is final, and further data are required.

A short excerpt is then shared publicly without those boundaries. The excerpt says: “The housing office reproduces structural injustice.” In public discussion, this becomes a label. Some readers treat it as proof of wrongdoing by specific staff. Others treat it as an accusation that the whole institution is morally corrupt. Staff respond defensively. Residents who were supposed to be protected by the analysis become visible as examples of harm. The structural question hardens into a public conflict before the evidence, roles, and responsibility pathways have been calibrated.

This vignette demonstrates why the later layers of the completed analysis matter. PMS Core can make structural injustice legible, but legibility is not harmless. Once a structural reading travels into publicness, the analysis may change function. What was a reversible working note can become a durable public label. What was a conceptual frame can become an accusation. What was a cautious Φ recontextualization can become exposure capture. This is why the completed CONFLICT, MIP, and AHP analyses repeatedly emphasize transmission boundaries.

The PMS Core hooks begin with Δ: internal working note versus public label; structural description versus verdict; arrangement-mediated disadvantage versus staff blame. □ shifts from research frame to public discourse frame. Λ appears if the excerpt omits the original caveats, review conditions, and uncertainty markers. Α may form if the public label repeats and stabilizes. Ω appears in unequal exposure: residents, staff, and institutions do not bear the same costs of being named or framed. Θ appears as the label persists in records, search results, institutional memory, or public debate. Φ is central because the analysis is recontextualized from a bounded research note to public accusation. Χ is endangered because public circulation reduces reflective distance. Σ may become harder because positions polarize. Ψ remains conditional: no binding collision is established merely because public conflict begins.

The PMS-CONFLICT hooks are especially important. This is a clean illustration of exposure capture as risk, not as automatic finding. In the original internal draft, publicness P is low and MG is low. After public excerpting, P rises. The misuse gradient rises because the language becomes person-near, institution-near, and potentially record-like. DRIFT_exposure_capture becomes a serious risk if interpretation hardens into enforcement affordance or reputational pressure before calibration. DRIFT_dignity_collapse becomes a guarded misuse risk if staff, residents, or groups are shamed, ranked, humiliated, or converted into moral symbols. DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in may become relevant if one side bears higher costs of correction or response. Still, terminal conflict is not established. The vignette shows a transmission danger, not a completed terminal regime.

MIP clarifies the use-status problem. The original analysis should have remained a working note until concrete evidence, publicness, role mapping, and IA conditions were reviewed. No A-score or M-score should be assigned from the public excerpt. The IA-box would require transparent criteria, justified use, time-boundedness, and reversibility. If the excerpt removes reversibility, the IA risk increases. Dignity-in-Practice becomes active as protection: residents must not be exposed as case material, staff must not be converted into blame objects, and public discourse must not turn structural analysis into humiliation.

AHP would identify several attack surfaces. First, the excerpt creates a model-stack authority effect: the phrase “structural injustice” sounds like a completed finding even if the original analysis was cautious. Second, it creates transmission-status ambiguity: readers no longer know that the analysis was a working note. Third, it creates D-language risk if harm and dignity language are used publicly without guardrails. Fourth, it creates a provenance problem: the public cannot see which claims came from data, which from structural theory, and which were hypotheses. The hardening response would be to require an excerpt preface: this is a private structural working note; it contains no person finding, no score, no legal conclusion, and no transmission upgrade.

Forbidden claims are extensive. The example does not show that the housing office is legally liable. It does not show that staff are blameworthy. It does not prove that residents were harmed in the described way. It does not establish that public criticism is always wrong. It does not establish exposure capture merely because a text becomes public. It does not show that structural-injustice language should be avoided. It shows only that public transfer changes the structural status of an analysis and can create risks that were not present in private review.

The non-capture note matters here because public conflict may also be politically legitimate. A political theorist might argue that public naming is necessary where institutions repeatedly avoid accountability. A legal advocate might argue that public pressure is part of remedy. A phenomenological account might argue that silence itself preserves humiliation. PMS-CONFLICT and MIP do not defeat those readings. They mark the additional burden: if one moves into publicness, one must own the change in exposure, reversibility, dignity risk, and misuse gradient.

The article takeaway is that structural legibility is not the same as safe transmission. A careful analysis can become a harmful or overreaching object if its boundaries are stripped away. For this case, that is not a side issue; it is part of the structural problem.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The completed analyses preserve rival readings as legitimate alternatives, not as defects. This is central to the case. Structural injustice is too philosophically dense to be exhausted by PMS, PMS-CONFLICT, MIP, or AHP.

An **agent-centered moral reading** preserves the importance of intention, negligence, culpable action, individual responsibility, and moral agency. PMS may make arrangement-mediated structures legible, but an agent-centered reading can insist that some harms still require careful analysis of who acted, with what knowledge, under what options, and with what moral relation to the outcome. The PMS reading must not erase individual agency where it matters.

An **institutional reading** preserves rules, procedures, offices, access structures, role allocation, decision rights, and organizational pathways. PMS can describe frames and asymmetries, but institutional analysis may require concrete knowledge of how a rule works, who administers it, what review mechanisms exist, and how institutional routines distribute burdens. This cannot be derived from the abstract case alone.

A **historical-causal reading** preserves inheritance, path-dependence, accumulation, delayed effects, and historical sequencing. PMS marks Θ as central, but historical-causal work requires actual chronology and evidence. It may show that some structural patterns are much stronger, weaker, older, newer, more contingent, or more reversible than the conceptual frame suggests.

A **distributional reading** preserves the allocation of goods, burdens, risks, opportunities, protections, and vulnerabilities. PMS can mark Ω, but distributional analysis may require measurement, comparison, baselines, population-level data, and normative criteria for fairness. Structural asymmetry in PMS is not itself a distributive justice theory.

A **recognition-based reading** preserves invisibility, misrecognition, exclusion, standing, voice, and symbolic harm. PMS and MIP can mark dignity-protection and exposure risks, but recognition theory may capture lived standing and social meaning in ways the operator grammar does not fully render. This is especially important where harm is not only material but also relational or symbolic.

A **legal-forensic reading** preserves duty, causation, liability, evidence, remedy, jurisdiction, procedure, and standards of proof. PMS may describe structural responsibility as possible, but legal responsibility requires doctrine-specific criteria. A legal reading may conclude that a structural injustice is morally or politically important but legally non-actionable, or conversely that liability attaches in ways PMS alone does not decide.

An **empirical social-scientific reading** preserves data, causal identification, measurement, counterfactual comparison, mechanism tracing, and methodological validity. PMS can name what would need to be studied, but it cannot replace empirical work. It cannot infer recurrence, pattern stabilization, or causal structure from conceptual fit.

A **phenomenological reading** preserves lived harm, experience, embodiment, affect, recognition, and the way structural conditions are encountered from within. PMS is structural and praxeological; it does not claim to capture the inner experience of injustice. A phenomenological account may show why some absences, humiliations, delays, or exclusions matter in ways a structural map can only indicate indirectly.

A **political-theory reading** may treat antagonism, domination, refusal, rupture, resistance, or contestation as central. PMS must not automatically redescribe rupture as failed integration, resistance as incomplete calibration, disagreement as drift, or refusal as immaturity. Some political grammars preserve non-integration as necessary rather than deficient.

A **metaphysical reading** may ask about collective agency, structural causation, social ontology, personhood, or dignity in ways PMS does not answer. PMS does not prove or disprove such theories. It avoids metaphysical closure rather than defeating metaphysics.

An **anti-capture reading** preserves the possibility that structural injustice resists formalization. It may argue that any operator grammar risks sanitizing injustice, softening domination, or turning political struggle into analytic management. The completed YAMLs do not dismiss this. They preserve non-capture as a live boundary.

MIP adds its own rival openness. A critic may argue that MIP softens structural injustice by translating it into maturity-in-practice fields, potentially underplaying domination, law, material distribution, or political struggle. An affected perspective may reject abstract reversibility if the lived arrangement is urgent, harmful, historically entrenched, or materially coercive. An external observer may see the analysis as too cautious. The YAMLs treat these as relevant counter-perspectives, not as defects.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

Several conditions would weaken the PMS reading.

First, the PMS reading is weakened if no relevant social, institutional, historical, or normative frame can be specified beyond vague background. Structural injustice cannot be claimed merely because harm exists. The analysis requires a frame in which arrangement-mediated effects can be described. Without that, □ remains too weak.

Second, the reading is under-discriminating if it cannot distinguish structural injustice from isolated wrongdoing, accidental aggregation, ordinary inequality, lawful but non-injurious asymmetry, empirical social pattern, legal liability, moral blame, or metaphysical accounts of justice. A structural reading that captures everything captures too little. The completed YAMLs therefore require discrimination tests.

Third, the reading over-assimilates if resistance, rupture, disagreement, rival framing, unresolved absence, or missing evidence is automatically translated into PMS drift, failed integration, incomplete calibration, or recontextualization. PMS must not treat every non-fit as a PMS-readable defect. Some non-fit is genuine rivalry or non-capture.

Fourth, the reading is overextended if Ω is used to infer blame, guilt, legal liability, moral inferiority, moral superiority, or person-ranking without independent grounds. Asymmetry is necessary to the category, but it is not a tribunal.

Fifth, the reading becomes invalid as a PMS application if Χ, reversibility, or Dignity-in-Practice is suspended to force closure, enforce a verdict, suppress rival readings, or transform conceptual projection into a tribunal.

Several conditions would weaken the CONFLICT overlay. It would be weakened if no stabilized incompatibility can be shown. It would be overextended if conflict language is applied simply because the topic is morally serious or politically contested. It would fail if weak Σ were treated as Σ collapse, if weak Ψ were treated as binding collision, if conceptual asymmetry were treated as lock-in, or if terminality were used as dramatic severity language. Stronger CONFLICT claims would require concrete competing frames or bindings, temporal persistence, integration failure, asymmetric or priced distance, and evidence that attempts at repair or recontextualization reproduce the incompatibility.

The MIP reading would be weakened if it began to score abstract roles. It would also be weakened if D-language produced more shame than clarity, if M-score logic were used as guilt, if IA-box unclear status were treated as indecision rather than correct boundary, or if working-note status were ignored. Stronger MIP claims would require concrete roles, time windows, publicness, evidence, affected perspectives, levers, alternatives, predictability, and role-specific responsibility calibration.

The AHP overlay identifies attack surfaces in the MIP analysis artefact. The first is a model-stack authority effect: PMS Core, PMS-CONFLICT, MIP, and AHP together may look more authoritative than the material warrants. The hardening action is to state explicitly that multi-model coherence increases auditability, not evidentiary force or domain authority. The second is that IA-box unclear status may be read as weakness rather than correct boundary. The hardening action is to clarify that IA remains unclear because concrete arrangement data are missing. The third is that D-module activation may be misread as D-profile escalation. The hardening action is to clarify that D activation means protection-only activation, not dignity finding. Further attack points concern provenance mapping, M-ceiling operationalization, public excerpt risk, and the danger that high PH may be misread as public readiness.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled across all layers. For empirical operationalization, one would need data about actual harms, distributions, patterns, mechanisms, recurrence, affected populations, and temporal trajectories. For legal operationalization, one would need jurisdiction, duty, causation, liability, evidence, standing, and remedy. For governance operationalization, one would need authority, mandate, procedure, stakeholder participation, publicness, review conditions, and accountability. For MIP scoring, one would need concrete roles, knowledge, power/control, predictability, alternatives, and serious integration attempts. For IA-box classification, one would need a specific asymmetry and evidence about transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. For CONFLICT terminality, one would need concrete failed integration under binding, asymmetry, time, and costly distance. None of these is supplied.

The final weakening condition is misuse. If the analysis is used as a person label, score, public shame device, legal conclusion, clinical inference, governance warrant, or proof of PMS/MIP completeness, the artefact should be revised or withdrawn from that use.

### Misuse boundary

The misuse boundary is substantial because this case is unusually vulnerable to moral, political, legal, and public overreach.

There is **no diagnosis**. Structural injustice is not a clinical category in this analysis. No person, group, institution, or role is diagnosed. No one is described as pathological, immature, unaware, deficient, or disordered.

There is **no person ranking**. The analysis does not rank dignity, maturity, responsibility, moral worth, epistemic virtue, or social standing. MIP’s A-score and M-score are not assigned. D is not a score. D₀ remains untouched. Dignity-in-Practice is a constraint on the analysis, not a measurement of persons.

There is **no legal or forensic conclusion**. The analysis does not establish liability, causation, duty, remedy, admissibility, culpability, negligence, intent, or institutional fault. Structural responsibility is not legal responsibility. CONFLICT risk is not forensic evidence.

There is **no metaphysical proof**. The case does not prove what structures are, whether collectives are agents, whether injustice has an ultimate essence, whether responsibility is metaphysically grounded, or whether dignity is ontologically derivable.

There is **no empirical proof**. No data are supplied. The analysis does not prove that any harm, disadvantage, pattern, recurrence, asymmetry, or historical mechanism occurred. It states what such evidence would need to show.

There is **no implementation validation**. The analysis is not a policy, intervention, governance protocol, audit tool, decision engine, or institutional design. MIP transmission status remains working note. AHP hardening does not upgrade transmission.

There is **no PMS Base validation**. Successful mapping does not prove PMS. The fact that PMS can structure the case does not validate PMS_1.3, PMS-CONFLICT, MIP, or AHP. The artefact is an application, not a proof of the model.

There is **no add-on-as-operator**. PMS-CONFLICT constructs such as P, MG, conflict attractor, binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, dignity collapse, terminal regime, downstream docking, and scale matrices are not PMS operators. They do not change PMS dependencies and do not create findings by themselves.

There is **no drift-as-verdict**. Drift risks are conditional structural risks, not accusations. AD_A>>E, AD_Sigma_low, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, dignity collapse, and binding collision are not labels for persons or groups. Risk-only status must remain risk-only.

There is **no closure-by-authority**. Neither PMS coherence, CONFLICT vocabulary, MIP structure, nor AHP PH may be used to force closure. High precision does not mean public readiness. Working note means working note. Weak Ψ means no responsibility bearer is established. Unclear IA means the necessary concrete data are absent. Non-capture remains legitimate.

A specific misuse risk is **blame transfer**. Structural injustice language can become a way to shift blame onto vague structures, or conversely to assign guilt to anyone associated with a structure. Both are invalid. Structure must neither exculpate everything nor accuse everyone.

Another misuse risk is **public exposure**. If the analysis is excerpted into public or institutional settings, structural language may harden into record-like interpretation. That could increase reputational, procedural, or dignity risks. Any such transfer requires renewed mode, publicness, misuse-gradient, and red-zone review.

Another misuse risk is **dignity inflation**. Because the case concerns injustice, dignity language may become morally charged. The YAMLs prevent this by keeping D as protection-only and D₀ untouched. If D-language begins to shame, rank, or expose, it should be dialed down.

A final misuse risk is **model-stack authority**. The presence of PMS Core, PMS-CONFLICT, MIP, and AHP can make the article look stronger than the case material. The correct interpretation is the opposite: the stack increases the number of explicit checks, not the authority of the claim. It makes the artefact more attackable, not more final.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is that structural injustice can be rendered as a disciplined, multi-layer closure-demand without being turned into a verdict. PMS Core shows why the category cannot be reduced to one agent, one intention, or one isolated wrongful act: the relevant structure involves frame, possible structured absence, recurrence, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, weak integration, and conditional self-binding. PMS-CONFLICT then shows that such a category can become conflict-relevant when frames of agency, responsibility, harm, institutional history, recognition, and distribution resist shared integration under asymmetry and time. But the overlay does not establish terminal conflict, binding collision, active exposure capture, or dignity collapse. MIP further checks the use-status of the artefact and keeps it as a working note: no A-score, no M-score, no IA verdict, and D only as protection signal. AHP finally hardens the analysis as an artefact by making its attack surfaces explicit, without rescoring, transmitting, validating, or closing the case.

The three examples contribute by making the abstract architecture readable without replacing it. The admissions example shows how formally neutral procedures may distribute opportunity through recurrent frames, asymmetries, and temporal accumulation without immediately proving intention, illegality, or culpability. The complaint-channel example shows how formal availability and practical accessibility can diverge, and why Λ must be calibrated through a frame rather than inferred from mere absence. The public-label example shows why structural legibility changes when transmitted: a careful internal analysis can become public exposure if its boundaries are stripped away. Together, the examples demonstrate the article’s central discipline: structural injustice becomes visible through arrangements, but visibility is not proof, and proof is not supplied by formal elegance.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is shown. The analysis does not prove that any concrete structural injustice occurred. It does not identify liable agents, guilty persons, defective institutions, harmed groups, or valid remedies. It does not diagnose anyone, rank anyone, or evaluate dignity. It does not establish legal liability, empirical causation, clinical relevance, metaphysical truth, implementation readiness, PMS Base validity, or add-on authority. It does not assign A-score, M-score, IA result, D-profile, CONFLICT terminality, or binding collision. It does not treat rival readings as failed PMS readings. It does not turn non-capture into weakness.

The boundary discipline is therefore not secondary. It is the result. Structural injustice is precisely the kind of case in which the pressure for closure can become analytically dangerous: moral closure wants blame, legal closure wants liability, empirical closure wants data, political closure wants action, metaphysical closure wants ultimate grounding, and institutional closure wants procedure. The completed stack does not deny these demands. It sorts them. PMS Core maps the structural grammar; PMS-CONFLICT marks conflict thresholds; MIP governs responsible use; AHP hardens the artefact against overreading. Each layer adds visibility only by accepting limits.

The final methodological takeaway is that AXIOM can make structural injustice legible as a frame-bound, temporally extended, asymmetry-bearing and arrangement-mediated category while preserving the Λ-residue that prevents false closure. The case shows where closure is possible: the category can be structurally mapped, its operator dependencies can be named, its drift risks can be bounded, its misuse channels can be guarded. It also shows where closure must remain withheld: empirical fact, legal responsibility, metaphysical grounding, person evaluation, implementation, and final integration all require materials not present here. The appropriate output is therefore not a verdict on structural injustice, but a disciplined map of what must remain visible before any stronger claim may responsibly begin.
