### Why this case matters

Causality matters for AXIOM because it is one of the classical philosophical problem-fields in which closure pressure is especially strong. The word “cause” often promises more than a frame has supplied. It can mean relation, sequence, dependence, explanation, production, regularity, necessity, intervention, agency, responsibility, or event-structure. Because these meanings are close enough to overlap but different enough to generate disputes, causality becomes a high-value test case for structural cartography.

The closure-demand is not merely “what happened before what?” It is the demand to know what makes an event dependent on another event, what licenses explanation, what distinguishes causal dependence from correlation, whether repeated regularity is sufficient, whether necessity is required, whether agency changes the structure, and whether a counterfactual non-event is part of the causal claim. A causal statement often asks to stabilize a relation that is at once temporal, explanatory, inferential, and sometimes practical. That is why the case belongs in AXIOM: it shows how a classical philosophical problem can be mapped as a bundle of frame-bound closure pressures rather than solved by metaphysical assertion.

Structurally, the case is difficult because causality invites false compression. Temporal order can be mistaken for causation. Repeated conjunction can be mistaken for necessity. Explanatory usefulness can be mistaken for metaphysical production. Agency language can be imported into non-agentic event-structure. Institutional or legal causation can be confused with physical causation. Counterfactual dependence can be treated as if it settled the entire ontology of causation. These are not merely conceptual confusions; they are closure shortcuts.

AXIOM’s task is not to decide among all causal theories. It does not claim that causality is only regularity, only counterfactual dependence, only intervention, only agency, only explanation, or only event-relation. It maps the structural places where those rival readings draw their force. This is why the analysis must remain reversible. The presence of a coherent PMS formula does not establish metaphysical adequacy. It only shows how PMS can organize the problem-field without pretending to terminate it.

The case is also important because causal claims often become powerful outside philosophy. They can support blame, repair, prediction, institutional assignment, model choice, policy, and responsibility. Yet the completed YAML does not provide a concrete scene that would justify any of those applied claims. The article therefore keeps the applied risks visible without activating them as findings. No person is evaluated. No institution is judged. No causal claim is treated as proven. No legal or forensic attribution follows. The case remains a philosophical conceptual projection with boundary and taxonomy outputs.

### PMS Core structural reading

#### Δ — Difference

Δ is active and foundational. Causality cannot be structurally analyzed without difference: cause and effect, prior and later, correlation and causation, regularity and necessity, explanation and event-order, agency and non-agentic sequence. Δ makes the field legible by marking the contrasts that the causal problem asks to stabilize.

The calibration point is that distinction is not proof. To distinguish cause from effect is not to establish causal dependence. To distinguish correlation from causation is not to decide which relation is present. Δ opens the problem; it does not close it. Overclaim occurs if the mere presence of a before/after or condition/outcome distinction is treated as causal structure.

#### ∇ — Impulse

∇ is weak and conditional. It appears as the pressure toward causal closure: the desire to say what produced what, what explains what, or which condition drives a consequence. In agency readings, ∇ may also mark directed production or intervention. In impersonal event-structure, however, ∇ must not be psychologized.

The dependency risk is personification. Generic causal direction is not desire, will, motive, or practical impulse. PMS may model explanatory directedness structurally, but it must not turn impersonal causation into proto-agency. ∇ becomes stronger only where the causal reading explicitly includes production, tendency, intervention, or agency.

#### □ — Frame

□ is active and major. Causality changes meaning across frames. In a scientific frame, the question may concern mechanism, regularity, experiment, or intervention. In a legal frame, it may concern liability and evidentiary sufficiency. In an engineering frame, it may concern root-cause analysis and repair. In metaphysics, it may concern necessity or causal powers. In ordinary explanation, it may concern intelligibility.

The calibration point is that no causal claim is frame-free in this analysis. The frame determines what counts as relevant evidence, acceptable uncertainty, admissible dependence, and sufficient explanation. Overclaim occurs when one frame’s closure standard is silently universalized: for example, when legal causation is treated as metaphysical causation, or when predictive regularity is treated as necessary connection.

#### Λ — Non-Event

Λ is active and important. Causality often depends on what did not happen, what would not have happened, what failed to appear, or what remains absent as a decisive warrant. Counterfactual dependence is the clearest case: would the effect have occurred without the cause? Λ also appears in prevention, omission, missing evidence, failed intervention, and absent necessity.

The calibration point is that structured absence is not metaphysical proof. A non-event can be meaningful within a frame without settling the ontology of causation. Λ preserves counterfactual and underdetermination residues. Overclaim occurs if the presence of a counterfactual contrast is treated as final causal theory, or if missing evidence is erased in order to force closure.

#### Α — Attractor

Α is active. Causal discourse is strongly shaped by repeated patterns, stable conjunctions, regularities, routines, and expectations. Regularity theories of causation rely heavily on this structure. More broadly, repeated success in explanation or prediction stabilizes causal habits: “this kind of event leads to that kind of outcome.”

The calibration problem is the gap between regularity and necessity. A pattern may be stable without being necessary. Repetition may justify expectation within a frame without proving metaphysical causation. Α therefore explains why causal claims become attractive and stable, but it does not eliminate underdetermination. Overclaim occurs when repeated conjunction is treated as sufficient causal proof.

#### Ω — Asymmetry

Ω is weak and conditional. It is tempting to treat cause/effect direction as asymmetry, but PMS Ω is not generic directionality. Ω concerns structural imbalance in power, exposure, capacity, obligation, vulnerability, or authority. A causal relation may be directional without being an Ω relation in the PMS sense.

Ω becomes relevant if the case is reframed as agency, intervention, responsibility, legal attribution, institutional explanation, or power-laden causal closure. For example, an institution may impose a causal story while exposed roles bear costs. But that is not present in the abstract material. Overclaim occurs if causal priority is automatically translated into PMS asymmetry.

#### Θ — Temporality

Θ is central. Causality is often structured through sequence, delay, trajectory, persistence, accumulation, and event-order. Many causal questions ask how something unfolds over time, whether one event precedes another, whether a condition persists long enough to matter, or whether an effect appears only after a lag.

The calibration point is strict: temporality is not causality by itself. Θ allows causal analysis to track sequence and trajectory, but causal dependence requires further frame-specific criteria. Overclaim occurs when “after” is treated as “because,” or when a temporal narrative is taken as proof of causal relation.

#### Φ — Recontextualization

Φ is active as an analytic operator. Causality is highly recontextualizable. The same relation may be read as regularity, mechanism, counterfactual dependence, intervention, legal responsibility, moral agency, probabilistic shift, or explanation. Frame shifts change what the causal claim is asking for.

The calibration point is that Φ must preserve rival readings, not absorb them. Recontextualization is legitimate when it clarifies what has changed between frames. It becomes over-assimilative when every rival theory is redescribed as an incomplete PMS formulation. PMS must not turn recontextualization into conquest.

#### Χ — Distance

Χ is major. Causality invites premature closure, and Χ is the operator that prevents the analysis from collapsing into verdict. It suspends metaphysical overreach, empirical overclaim, diagnostic inference, legal attribution, and PMS self-validation. It keeps the analysis reversible and bounded.

Χ is not avoidance. It is disciplined restraint. It allows PMS to say: this is structurally legible, but not proven; this is frame-bound, not universal; this is coherent, but not exhaustive. Overclaim occurs if Χ is suspended to force a final causal theory or to treat PMS mapping as sufficient.

#### Σ — Integration

Σ is weak-to-conditional. A causal analysis may integrate sequence, regularity, absence, explanation, frame, and dependence into a coherent account. But integration is dangerous here because causality is broad enough that almost every theory can be made to look PMS-coherent.

The calibration point is discriminative force. A Σ integration is useful only if it distinguishes causal dependence from correlation, temporal order from explanation, regularity from necessity, agency from impersonal event-structure, and coherence from proof. Overclaim occurs when the analysis becomes smooth but under-discriminating.

#### Ψ — Self-Binding

Ψ is not applicable to causality in general. Causality is not self-binding. The abstract problem-field has no self-relation, commitment, autobiographical continuity, or responsibility-as-self-commitment. Ψ becomes relevant only if the case is reframed as agency-responsibility, institutional binding, legal precedent, or application-level self-binding of the analysis itself.

The calibration point is non-personification. Generic causal structure must not be treated as a subject. PMS may bind an analysis to restraint, but causality itself is not thereby a self. Overclaim occurs if Ψ is activated without agency, responsibility, stable normativity, or explicit analytic self-binding.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The dependency discipline is crucial because causality is exactly the kind of case where later operators can be used too cheaply. The analysis begins with Δ and □ because the field first requires distinctions and frames. Λ and Α then become relevant because causal discourse often depends on absence, counterfactuals, recurrence, and regularity. Θ becomes central because causal relations are frequently temporal. Φ and Χ become necessary because causality is multiply recontextualizable and high-risk for closure. Σ remains limited. Ψ remains inactive except in explicitly scoped subcases.

The invalid shortcut is Δ/∇ → Ψ: a difference appears, closure pressure arises, and the analysis jumps directly to binding blame, doctrine, standard, or final theory. In causal discourse, this shortcut can take many forms: “something changed, therefore this caused it”; “the pattern repeated, therefore necessity is proven”; “the model works, therefore the causal story is true”; “this explanation is coherent, therefore it is final.” PMS rejects these shortcuts as dependency failures.

Σ cannot be used cheaply because integration requires Χ and Φ. Without reflective distance and explicit frame tracking, integration becomes mere smoothing. In this case, the risk is high because causality can be integrated too easily: relation, sequence, dependence, explanation, regularity, and agency can all be made to fit into a single broad story. That broadness is not a victory unless it discriminates.

Ψ remains conditional because self-binding requires Σ, Θ, and Χ. It cannot be applied to causality as such. It can only apply where an agent, institution, policy, legal system, or analysis artefact binds itself to a causal standard or responsibility structure. The present case does not supply that material.

Coherence is not proof. A PMS-coherent causal mapping shows internal structural legibility. It does not prove causal truth, metaphysical adequacy, empirical validity, legal admissibility, or implementation reliability. This is one of the most important outputs of the case: PMS works here only if it refuses to treat its own coherence as sufficient.

Non-capture is not failure. If causal powers, physical causation, counterfactual semantics, formal causal logic, interventionism, or metaphysical necessity exceed PMS capture, that does not mean the case has failed or that the rival theory is immature. It may mean that PMS is providing a cartographic boundary rather than an exhaustive theory.

PMS must therefore not become a closure machine. It is permitted to map the pressure toward causal closure. It is not permitted to satisfy that pressure by authority.

For that reason, the bounded primary formula remains deliberately restrained: Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ Δ. This formula does not deny that ∇, Ω, Σ, or Ψ can matter in causal subcases. It only refuses to place them in the primary structure of generic causality. ∇ becomes relevant where causal discourse is framed as directed production or agency; Ω where power, exposure, capacity, vulnerability, obligation, or authority enter; Σ where an explicit integrative account is being built; and Ψ only where a causal account becomes bound into responsibility, standard, precedent, policy, or analytic self-commitment.

### Example 1 — Correlation, recurrence, and the demand for “the cause”

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after the discussion of Α and Θ in the PMS Core structural reading.

A research team observes that a certain operational failure repeatedly occurs shortly after a software update is deployed. In several cycles, the pattern looks stable: update first, failure later. The team begins to describe the update as “the cause” of the failure. The claim is practical and understandable. A difference has become visible: ordinary operation versus failure. A temporal sequence has appeared: update before failure. A recurrence has stabilized: the pattern repeats often enough to become salient. The organization now wants causal closure because repair, planning, and accountability depend on knowing what to change.

The example demonstrates why PMS treats causal closure as structurally tempting but not self-validating. Δ makes the contrast visible: before and after, normal and failed, update and disruption. Α stabilizes the pattern through repetition. Θ orders the events across time. Yet none of these operators proves causality by itself. The observed recurrence may indicate a causal pathway, but it may also reflect a hidden dependency, a shared environmental condition, a monitoring artifact, a scheduling pattern, or an unobserved third factor. The PMS reading therefore distinguishes recurrence from necessity and temporal order from causal dependence.

The key PMS Core hook is that Α and Θ are active but insufficient. The repeated pattern is not dismissed; it is structurally meaningful. But Χ must prevent the pattern from being hardened too quickly into a final causal claim. □ also matters because the frame must be declared: is the team doing engineering root-cause analysis, statistical correlation review, incident triage, or legal accountability? Each frame changes what counts as sufficient causal closure. Λ remains active because missing logs, untested conditions, unobserved dependencies, and counterfactual uncertainty remain part of the case. Σ may later integrate multiple factors into a usable explanation, but only if the analysis remains explicit about uncertainty and does not collapse coherence into proof.

There is no add-on activation in this example. PMS Core is sufficient to show the difference between pattern stabilization and causal closure. A LOGIC overlay might become relevant if the example were reframed as a justification problem about whether repeated success warrants a general law. MIP or AHP might become relevant if the causal account were converted into a governance artefact, such as a policy assigning responsibility or mandating enforcement. QC or QC-EXT would require a specific quality-control or audit-trace context. None of those layers is warranted merely by the vignette.

The forbidden claims are important. The example does not prove that the software update caused the failure. It does not prove that the team’s initial hypothesis is wrong. It does not assign blame to the developer, manager, or operator. It does not establish negligence, liability, or technical implementation validity. It also does not validate PMS as a causal inference model. PMS is not replacing testing, debugging, controlled comparison, or statistical analysis.

The non-capture note is that empirical causality may require methods PMS does not supply here: experiments, logs, counterfactual testing, dependency analysis, causal graphs, or statistical controls. PMS can map why the update becomes causally salient; it cannot decide the empirical causal question from structural salience alone.

The article takeaway is that causality often begins with a legitimate pattern: difference, recurrence, and temporal order. PMS preserves that pattern without allowing it to become necessity by rhetorical acceleration. The analysis shows why “it keeps happening after X” is a serious causal prompt, but not yet causal closure.

### Example 2 — Counterfactual absence and the missing decisive event

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after the discussion of Λ in the PMS Core structural reading.

A safety team reviews a near-miss. A machine stopped unexpectedly, and no injury occurred. One group argues that the emergency stop prevented harm. Another argues that harm would not have occurred anyway, because the operator had already stepped back. The central question becomes counterfactual: without the emergency stop, would the harmful outcome have happened?

This vignette demonstrates why Λ is central to causality. The causal claim depends not only on what happened, but on what did not happen and on what would have happened under an unrealized alternative. The injury did not occur. The alternative sequence did not unfold. The decisive comparison cannot be directly observed. The missing event is not irrelevant; it is the very structure around which the causal dispute turns.

PMS Core makes this legible without pretending to settle it. Δ distinguishes the actual sequence from possible alternative sequences. □ defines the frame: safety review, technical incident analysis, training review, or institutional reporting. Θ orders the available events: machine instability, operator movement, emergency stop, non-injury. Λ marks the structured absence: the unrealized harmful event and the missing counterfactual pathway. Α may enter if similar near-misses recur and stabilize a pattern. Χ is crucial because the analysis must not convert one preferred counterfactual narrative into certainty merely because a decision is needed. Σ may later integrate sensor records, witness statements, timing data, and operational assumptions into a bounded account, but the integration must carry the Λ residue rather than erase it.

The example also shows why Ω remains conditional. If the safety review stays technical and exploratory, Ω may be weak. If an institution uses the causal story to assign blame, deny compensation, or impose sanctions, Ω becomes stronger because authority and exposure gradients enter the scene. But that would be a different layer of analysis. The vignette alone does not justify responsibility assignment.

There are no add-on hooks activated by default. PMS Core is enough to show how counterfactual absence structures causal reasoning. A LOGIC overlay could be added if the focus shifted to warrant and justification: what would count as sufficient reason to accept one counterfactual over another? MIP or AHP would be relevant only if the analysis became an institutional artefact with governance consequences. QC might become relevant if the case were built into an audit trace or procedural review. In the present example, those layers remain inactive.

The forbidden claims are strict. The example does not prove that the emergency stop caused the non-injury. It does not prove that the operator would have been harmed. It does not prove the opposite either. It does not assign fault, negligence, competence, or praise. It does not create a legal or forensic conclusion. It does not justify enforcement or discipline.

The non-capture note is that counterfactual causality may require formal causal models, physical reconstruction, timing data, simulation, or domain expertise. PMS can identify the structured absence and preserve it as Λ. It cannot, by itself, determine the counterfactual truth.

The article takeaway is that causal reasoning often depends on absent events. PMS treats these absences neither as empty gaps nor as automatic proof. Λ marks the place where the demand for closure is strongest and where overclaim is most tempting.

### Example 3 — Frame-shift from explanation to responsibility

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after the discussion of Φ and Χ, or in the later section on non-capture and rival readings.

A project misses a deadline. In one meeting, the delay is explained through coordination factors: dependency changes, unclear handovers, late feedback, and shifting requirements. In a later meeting, the same delay is discussed as a responsibility question: who failed, who must answer, and who should carry the consequences? The word “cause” appears in both meetings, but it does not do the same work in each frame.

This example demonstrates the importance of Φ. A causal account can move from explanation to accountability without anyone explicitly noticing that the frame has changed. In the coordination frame, the question is how the delay emerged. In the responsibility frame, the question is who is answerable. These are related but not identical. If the shift is not marked, a multi-factor explanation may be compressed into a person-level verdict.

PMS Core helps keep the shift visible. Δ distinguishes the missed deadline from the expected timeline, and also distinguishes explanation from responsibility. ∇ appears as closure pressure: the group wants to settle the matter, restore control, and prevent recurrence. □ is decisive because “cause” means something different in each frame. Λ remains active because missing handovers, absent updates, unclear authority, or unrecorded decisions may prevent clean closure. Α may stabilize familiar scripts: “requirements always change,” “the same team always delays,” or “management always blames delivery.” Ω becomes conditionally active when authority enters: some roles can assign responsibility while others bear reputational or workload costs. Θ matters because deadlines, delays, lock-in, and accumulated commitments unfold over time. Φ marks the recontextualization from coordination to accountability. Χ prevents the analysis from sliding into blame by default. Σ may integrate distributed conditions into a responsibility map only if the frame justifies it. Ψ remains inactive unless a legitimate responsibility-binding structure is explicitly established.

The add-on status remains inactive in the base example. PMS Core is sufficient to show the frame shift and its risks. A LOGIC overlay could become relevant if the group argues that the explanation logically entails a responsibility conclusion. MIP could become relevant if the resulting analysis is used for institutional accountability or policy. AHP could harden the artefact if claims, counterclaims, and failure conditions need explicit review. QC could apply if the case becomes an audit trace of process quality. But none of these should be silently activated.

The forbidden claims define the example’s boundary. The vignette does not identify who caused the delay. It does not diagnose avoidance, incompetence, negligence, or bad faith. It does not justify sanctions, performance judgments, or legal claims. It does not claim that responsibility is impossible. It only shows that explanation and responsibility require different frames and cannot be collapsed without additional warrant.

The non-capture note is that actual responsibility analysis may require records, role definitions, communication traces, authority maps, contractual obligations, and institutional standards. PMS can show where the frame shift occurs and where overclaim becomes likely. It cannot decide responsibility from the vignette alone.

The article takeaway is that causal language often travels between frames. PMS does not forbid that movement, but it requires Φ to be explicit and Χ to remain active. A causal explanation may become relevant to responsibility, but it is not already a responsibility verdict.

### Non-capture and rival readings

Several rival readings remain open, and the analysis treats them as legitimate non-capture or partial-capture zones rather than defects.

A **regularity reading** treats causality as stable repetition or constant conjunction. PMS can map this through Α and Θ, but it does not prove that regularity is sufficient for causation. The rival reading preserves the importance of recurrence, pattern, and expectation. Example 1 illustrates this pressure: repeated post-update failure is structurally serious, but repetition alone does not become necessity.

A **correlation reading** highlights the risk of confusing co-variation with causal dependence. PMS can distinguish this through Δ, □, Λ, and Χ, but empirical separation of correlation and causation requires methods not supplied here. The rival reading preserves statistical and evidentiary caution. PMS can map the closure pressure created by correlation; it cannot convert correlation into a finding.

A **counterfactual reading** treats causality as dependence on what would not have happened otherwise. PMS can make this visible through Λ, □, and Θ. But PMS does not settle counterfactual semantics. Example 2 shows this clearly: the missing harmful event structures the causal dispute, but the structured absence does not decide which counterfactual is true.

A **necessity reading** treats causality as necessary connection. PMS can mark where the demand for necessity arises, but it cannot validate metaphysical necessity. This remains a strong non-capture zone. If a causal theory requires necessity in a metaphysical sense, PMS may only map the closure-demand around that requirement.

An **agency reading** treats causality as production, doing, intervention, or action. PMS can conditionally activate ∇, Ω, Action_E, Responsibility_R, and possibly Ψ in such subcases. But the general case does not warrant importing agency into causality as a whole. Example 3 shows why: explanation can become responsibility-relevant, but responsibility does not follow unless the frame and dependencies support it.

An **event-structure reading** treats causality as a relation among events. PMS can map difference, frame, and temporality, but it does not replace event ontology. It can say that event-order and frame matter; it cannot claim to have settled the nature of events themselves.

An **explanatory-pragmatic reading** treats causal claims as tools for making events intelligible within practical frames. PMS is especially compatible with this reading, but compatibility is not proof. The rival reading preserves the pragmatic function of explanation and helps prevent the analysis from pretending to be metaphysical.

A **probabilistic reading** treats causality as probability-shift under conditions. PMS can mark recurrence, frame, and expectation, but statistical or mathematical validation remains outside the case. Any probability-based causal claim would need methods and data not provided here.

These rival readings matter because they prevent PMS from becoming too smooth. The task is not to defeat them but to locate what each preserves and where PMS mapping remains formal rather than exhaustive.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would be weakened if the case required a metaphysical proof of causality. PMS Core is not designed to provide one here. It would also be weakened if causality were defined in a way that did not require difference, frame, absence, recurrence, sequence, explanation, or dependence, because then the PMS operator mapping would lose its entry points.

The reading would become under-discriminating if the same PMS formula fit all causal theories without distinguishing them. That is a serious risk in this case. A strong PMS analysis must show where regularity, counterfactual dependence, intervention, agency, necessity, and event-structure differ, not simply translate all of them into broad operator language.

The reading would become over-assimilative if rival theories, disagreement, missing evidence, metaphysical residue, or non-fit were automatically redescribed as PMS drift, incomplete integration, or calibration failure. Non-capture must remain a possible result.

The reading would be overextended if it claimed empirical causal inference without data, legal causation without evidence and standards, agency without an agent, responsibility without obligation and asymmetry, or implementation validity without architecture and testing.

The reading would fail dependency discipline if Ω were treated as active merely because causal direction exists, or if Ψ were treated as active merely because causal claims can become binding in some other context. It would also fail if Σ coherence were treated as causal truth.

For stronger claims, operationalization would require case-specific material. An empirical use would need observations, variables, methods, interventions, causal inference rules, and validation criteria. A legal use would need a jurisdictional frame, evidence, standards of proof, liability rules, and procedural safeguards. An implementation use would need a causal model, architecture, interface, test hooks, and failure criteria. A metaphysical use would need explicit metaphysical premises and argument. A comparative philosophical use would need criteria for evaluating rival causal theories.

None of these requirements is fulfilled in the present case. They are listed as conditions for escalation, not as hidden achievements.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It does not identify a person’s reasoning style, pathology, maturity level, intention, competence, or psychological tendency.

It must not be used for person ranking. A causal theory, causal claim, or causal uncertainty cannot be translated into moral superiority, inferiority, dignity ranking, intelligence ranking, or worth.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusion. No liability, guilt, intent, negligence, evidentiary sufficiency, or causal responsibility follows from this analysis.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. PMS does not settle whether causality is necessity, causal power, regularity, intervention, counterfactual dependence, or event-production.

It must not be used as empirical proof. No causal finding follows without data, method, and validation.

It must not be used as implementation validation. YAML representation, operator mapping, or formula coherence does not implement causal inference or validate a causal system.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. A coherent case rendering does not prove PMS complete, uniquely minimal, or superior to all rival formalisms.

It must not turn add-ons into operators. No add-on is active here, and no overlay vocabulary may be silently imported as base grammar.

It must not treat drift as verdict. Drift patterns are not findings in this case. They are only possible risks in later concrete applications.

It must not close by authority. PMS language cannot be used to force causal closure where Λ-residue remains structurally visible.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of the case is that causality can be rendered as a PMS-readable closure-demand without being solved as a metaphysical, empirical, legal, diagnostic, or implementation problem. PMS Core makes visible the structural components that causal discourse repeatedly mobilizes: Δ marks causal contrasts, □ frames what counts as admissible relation or explanation, Λ preserves counterfactual and evidentiary absence, Α stabilizes regularity and recurrent expectation, Θ orders trajectories, Φ tracks frame-shifts, Χ prevents closure inflation, and Σ remains limited to bounded integration. ∇ and Ω remain conditional, while Ψ is inactive for generic causality except in explicitly scoped responsibility, agency, institutional, or analysis-binding contexts.

The examples contribute by showing how the abstract analysis becomes readable without becoming evidentiary. The software-update vignette demonstrates how recurrence and temporal order create a serious causal prompt without proving causation. The near-miss vignette demonstrates how absent events and unrealized alternatives can structure causal reasoning without settling the counterfactual. The deadline vignette demonstrates how causal language can travel from explanation to responsibility and why Φ and Χ are required to keep that movement from becoming an unlicensed verdict. None of these examples replaces the analysis; each illustrates a boundary already present in the PMS rendering.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is claimed. The article does not identify the nature of causation. It does not decide between regularity, counterfactual, interventionist, agency, event-ontological, probabilistic, explanatory-pragmatic, causal-power, or necessity-based theories. It does not prove any empirical causal relation. It does not assign responsibility, fault, competence, or liability. It does not validate PMS Base. It does not convert causal uncertainty into drift, immaturity, or failed integration.

The boundary discipline is therefore the main achievement. PMS is allowed to map the structure of causal closure, but it is not allowed to satisfy closure by authority. Coherence is not proof. Recurrence is not necessity. Sequence is not causation. Counterfactual absence is not automatic warrant. Explanation is not responsibility. Frame-shift is not permission to overclaim. Non-capture remains possible and must remain visible.

The final methodological takeaway is that, within this AXIOM/PMS Core rendering, causality is best handled as a cartographic case: PMS Core shows where closure is demanded, which operators make that demand legible, where dependency shortcuts would distort the analysis, and where Λ-residue must remain explicit. The examples support this methodological point by illustrating pattern-pressure, counterfactual absence, and frame-shift; they do not function as evidence for a causal theory. The result is a boundary-and-taxonomy output, not a causal verdict.
