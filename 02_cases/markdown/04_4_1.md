### Why this case matters

Personal identity over time is one of the classical closure-demands of philosophy. The question appears simple: how can something change and still be the same person? But the apparent simplicity hides several competing demands. One demand asks for numerical sameness: is the later person strictly identical with the earlier person? Another asks for psychological continuity: do memory, intention, character, or consciousness preserve identity? Another asks for bodily or biological continuity: does organismic persistence carry the case? Another asks for narrative or social continuity: is identity stabilized by stories, recognitions, roles, and commitments that persist across time? Still another asks for responsibility: how can a later self bear consequences for earlier acts?

AXIOM includes this case because it is a strong test of whether PMS can map a classical problem without converting the map into a verdict. The identity problem pressures a model toward closure. It tempts the analysis to say: identity is memory, identity is body, identity is narrative, identity is responsibility, identity is soul, identity is legal continuity, or identity is self-binding. The PMS reading refuses that move. It does not replace one metaphysical criterion with another. It treats the closure-demand itself as the object of structural analysis.

The case is structurally difficult because identity over time is not merely a temporal sequence. A sequence of states is not yet a self-relation. A body persisting through time is not yet a personhood criterion inside every frame. A memory relation is not automatically sufficient for responsibility. A narrative can stabilize continuity while smoothing over rupture. Legal identity can bind records without settling phenomenological persistence. PMS therefore has to remain discriminating: it must show why Δ, Θ, Σ, and Ψ matter without making them too cheap.

The case also matters because it is a high-coherence-risk case for PMS. Diachronic identity maps smoothly onto temporality, integration, and self-binding. That smoothness is useful, but dangerous. A model that explains too much too easily may become under-discriminating. PMS must not absorb every rival account into its own language and then treat that absorption as victory. The article therefore keeps rival readings open and treats non-capture as a serious boundary, not as a defect.

The YAML treats the case as a **conceptual domain projection**, not as a concrete scene. There is no single event, institution, person, dispute, diagnostic setting, legal case, or empirical dataset. The analysis therefore cannot yield findings about a person or class of persons. It cannot infer memory, pathology, competence, responsibility, or legal continuity. It can only render the conceptual architecture by which identity over time becomes legible as a structural problem.

The case is not being solved metaphysically. PMS does not claim to identify the final bearer of identity. It does not prove a persisting substance, soul, subject, organism, consciousness, or narrative essence. It is not being solved empirically, because no observations, tests, interviews, records, measurements, or behavioral evidence are supplied. It is not being solved clinically, because no diagnostic frame is active. It is not being solved legally, because no jurisdiction, status rule, contract, capacity standard, or forensic criterion is in play. It is not being solved diagnostically, because the analysis does not evaluate persons, traits, memory states, competence, intent, or pathology.

The value of the case lies in another register: it shows how the closure-demand itself can be made structurally explicit. Personal identity over time becomes a field in which framed differences, recurring continuity criteria, temporal trajectories, recontextualizations, asymmetries, reflective distance, integrations, and self-bindings interact. PMS can map that field without declaring that one criterion has finally won.

### Scene and frame structure

Because this is not a concrete scene, the “scene” is a conceptual scene: the philosophical problem-space in which continuity, change, rupture, self-relation, memory, embodiment, role persistence, responsibility, and recognition are placed under pressure by the question of identity over time.

The primary frame, □, is a philosophical-conceptual inquiry into diachronic personal identity. Inside this frame are distinctions among numerical sameness, bodily continuity, psychological continuity, narrative continuity, social recognition, role persistence, responsibility, and structural self-binding. These are not collapsed into one criterion. They are treated as competing or overlapping identity attractors within the conceptual field.

Secondary frames remain visible but not authoritative. A practical accountability frame asks how earlier acts can bind a later self. A memory frame asks how recollection supports continuity. A bodily frame asks how organismic persistence matters. A narrative frame asks how coherence is maintained across time. A social-recognition frame asks how others participate in stabilizing identity. A legal frame could bind records and status, but it remains outside the present analysis unless separately supplied. A phenomenological frame could ask about first-person persistence, but PMS does not claim access to that interiority.

Outside the frame are metaphysical proof, clinical diagnosis, legal adjudication, empirical validation, implementation, and person-ranking. Also outside the frame is any attempt to declare that PMS self-binding is the true essence of personal identity. PMS is used as a structural grammar, not as a replacement metaphysics.

The protected constraints are Distance, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. Distance means the analysis must not fuse itself with a verdict. Reversibility means every structural reading remains revisable. Dignity-in-Practice means that continuity, discontinuity, memory loss, transformation, non-fit, or rival framing cannot be used to shame, rank, humiliate, or ontologically devalue persons or groups.

Roles are conceptual rather than scene-specific. One role is the earlier phase of life: the phase that acted, promised, suffered, changed, remembered, or was recorded. A second role is the later phase: the phase that interprets, bears consequences, remembers, forgets, revises, or refuses continuity. A third role is the external recognizer: the person, institution, community, or record-system that attributes sameness or difference. A fourth role is the conceptual analyst, who must keep frames separate rather than importing metaphysical, diagnostic, legal, or moral conclusions.

Temporality, Θ, is central. The case exists only because identity is diachronic. Self-relation, embodiment, memory, consequence, commitment, and recognition persist, shift, or rupture across time. A static comparison of two moments would not be enough. What matters is trajectory: continuity through change, change through continuity, and the possibility that some ruptures are not integrable.

The reversibility window is broad at the conceptual level. Identity readings remain revisable under recontextualization. Earlier actions, roles, memories, or self-descriptions can be placed into later frames. But reversibility does not mean that all rupture is harmless or that all reinterpretation is valid. It means that PMS must not harden a structural projection into a final identity verdict.

Exposure and asymmetry are moderate, not absent. Earlier actions may expose later phases to responsibility or consequence. Earlier vulnerability may be interpreted by later frames. External recognizers may have power to stabilize or deny identity. However, because no concrete relation of power is supplied, Ω cannot be treated as an active finding about domination, blame, or hierarchy. It remains a structural feature that must be calibrated carefully.

The absence of a concrete scene has consequences. It lowers the warrant for active drift assignment. It weakens responsibility claims. It prevents empirical calibration. It keeps Action_E under-specified. It also prevents the case from being used as an applied determination. The scene is enough for conceptual mapping, but not enough for diagnosis, governance, legal conclusion, or empirical validation.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading begins with **Δ, Difference**. Personal identity over time becomes analyzable only because differences are drawn: sameness versus change, continuity versus rupture, earlier versus later, memory versus forgetting, bodily persistence versus transformation, narrative coherence versus discontinuity, legal identity versus lived self-relation, and PMS self-binding versus metaphysical persistence. Δ is major because without these distinctions the case would dissolve into an undifferentiated question of “same or not same.” But Δ must not be pushed too far. Distinguishing identity criteria does not prove that PMS captures the full identity problem. It only creates the analytic field in which the problem can be structured.

**∇, Impulse**, appears as criterion-pressure. Once temporal difference is legible, the problem demands orientation: how can something change and still count as the same person, agent, or accountable self? This pressure is argumentative and structural, not psychological. The analysis does not infer desire, anxiety, fear, or existential need. ∇ remains moderate because the case is conceptual: it names the directionality of the problem, not an inner state. If ∇ were pushed too far, the analysis would become a story about human anxiety over identity rather than a structural account of closure-demand.

**□, Frame**, is major. The active frame is philosophical inquiry into diachronic personal identity. This frame determines which distinctions matter and which conclusions are out of scope. It also prevents frame-slippage. A metaphysical frame, a legal frame, a narrative frame, a biological frame, a clinical frame, and a PMS-structural frame do not license the same claims. The main calibration demand is to mark frame shifts rather than merging them. If no identity-over-time frame were specified, the analysis would become too abstract to distinguish personal identity from any temporal process.

**Λ, Non-Event**, is active in a specific sense. The case is structured by an expected but absent decisive identity criterion. A metaphysical proof is sought but not supplied. A concrete scene is also absent. These absences matter within the identity-over-time frame. Λ is not failure by itself. The absence of final proof may mark a real limit of the problem, a limit of PMS, or a boundary between structural mapping and metaphysical closure. The risk is to redescribe every absence as incomplete integration. PMS must preserve the possibility that some absences are non-capture zones.

**Α, Attractor**, is major. Across the history of the problem, certain identity criteria recur: memory continuity, bodily persistence, narrative coherence, role continuity, responsibility across time, and social recognition. PMS reads these not as settled truths but as stabilized patterns in the conceptual domain. They attract repeated attempts at closure. Α must be calibrated by showing recurrence rather than merely naming sequence. A list of life-events is not yet an attractor. A stable criterion, role script, commitment pattern, or recognition loop is.

**Ω, Asymmetry**, is moderate. Asymmetry appears between earlier and later phases of a life, between the remembered and the forgotten, between the agent who acted and the later agent who bears consequences, and between external recognizers and the self-relation being recognized. These asymmetries are directional: past acts can burden later selves; later interpretations can overwrite past vulnerability; institutions can recognize or deny continuity. But Ω must not be moralized. It is not blame, superiority, or person-ranking. Because no concrete scene is supplied, Ω remains structurally relevant but not strongly activated as a finding.

**Θ, Temporality**, is central and major. Identity over time is not identity at an instant. It concerns persistence, sequence, delay, accumulation, transformation, rupture, and development. Memory, embodiment, commitment, role, consequence, and recognition become identity-relevant because they endure or change over time. The calibration point is to distinguish diachronic trajectory from static comparison. If temporal extension were not required, the case would no longer be personal identity over time.

**Φ, Recontextualization**, is major. Earlier actions, memories, self-descriptions, roles, and vulnerabilities can be placed into later frames. The same event can become evidence of continuity, transformation, rupture, growth, denial, repair, or reinterpretation. Φ is essential because identity is not only persistence but also the later re-reading of earlier structures. Yet Φ carries an over-assimilation risk. If every rupture, forgetting, transformation, trauma, refusal, or disagreement is absorbed as recontextualization, PMS becomes too smooth. Some discontinuities may resist integration and must remain possible non-capture.

**Χ, Distance**, is major and application-validating. Distance prevents the analysis from declaring that personal identity simply is memory, body, soul, narrative, law, social recognition, or PMS self-binding. It suspends premature closure and preserves reversibility among rival readings. Distance is not avoidance. It does not refuse the identity question; it prevents the structural projection from becoming a verdict. If Χ is suspended to force metaphysical or practical closure, the PMS application becomes invalid.

**Σ, Integration**, is major but conditional. Integration appears as the attempt to coordinate bodily, psychological, narrative, relational, and normative continuities into a coherent account of diachronic identity. PMS can show how such coordination works structurally. But coherence is not proof. A smooth integration may hide rupture, forgetting, trauma, social conflict, or rival metaphysical commitments. Σ must discriminate among continuity types rather than merging them into vague sameness. If coherence is treated as proof of identity, Σ is overclaimed.

**Ψ, Self-Binding**, is the main PMS projection. Diachronic identity can be modeled as commitment to integrated trajectories across time. A self is not treated as a primitive substance but as a relatively stable self-relation produced by structured praxis. However, Ψ must remain structural. It does not prove that a metaphysical subject persists. It does not prove consciousness. It does not settle numerical identity. It depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. If Ψ is applied without integration, temporality, and distance, or if it is treated as subject-substance, the reading becomes overextended.

The operator chain is therefore not a ladder to final identity. It is a discipline for preserving the structural conditions under which identity-over-time claims become intelligible without being illegitimately closed.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The operator chain is dependency-respecting. Δ enables distinctions. ∇ arises from those distinctions as criterion-pressure. □ frames the pressure as a personal-identity problem. Λ marks the absence of decisive proof or concrete scene. Α stabilizes recurring identity criteria. Ω introduces directional exposure, obligation, and recognition. Θ temporalizes the configuration. Φ recontextualizes earlier structures within later frames. Χ suspends premature verdict. Σ integrates multiple continuity criteria. Ψ binds the integrated trajectory into a structural self-relation.

Later operators cannot be used cheaply. Ψ cannot be invoked merely because a person persists through time. It requires Σ, Θ, and Χ. Σ cannot be invoked merely because a narrative sounds coherent. It requires distance and recontextualization, and it must preserve difference rather than erase it. Χ cannot be invoked as empty neutrality; it must actively prevent closure-by-verdict while keeping the problem intelligible. Φ cannot become a universal solvent for rupture.

The most important dependency risk is **Δ/∇ → Ψ collapse**: a contrast plus pressure for closure becomes a final identity claim without frame discipline, non-event preservation, attractor analysis, asymmetry calibration, temporality, recontextualization, distance, or integration. In this case, the collapse would look like: “Because continuity is demanded, self-binding proves identity.” That is invalid. PMS must not become a closure machine.

Coherence is not proof. A PMS reading can integrate memory, embodiment, role, narrative, and responsibility into a plausible self-binding model. That does not show that PMS has solved the metaphysical problem. It shows that PMS can render a structural projection of diachronic self-relation. The difference is decisive.

Non-capture is not failure. Metaphysical numerical identity, first-person persistence, biological organism continuity, legal identity criteria, cultural identity practices, and hard rupture cases may remain outside or only partially captured by PMS. These zones must not automatically be redescribed as drift, failed integration, structured absence, or immaturity. They may be genuine limits of the model’s scope.

This dependency discipline also constrains calibration. Ω cannot be inflated into domination without a concrete asymmetry scene. Responsibility_R cannot be treated as active merely because identity and time are present. Action_E cannot be fulfilled without concrete enactment. SELF_FIXPOINT cannot be read as proof of metaphysical identity. Dignity-in-Practice cannot be smuggled into ontological dignity. Derived structures remain formula-derived, not primitive.

The same discipline can also be read through a set of intermediate PMS chains. At the minimal-praxis level, `□ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ` describes how the identity-over-time frame constrains the criterion-seeking impulse arising from the difference between persistence and change. At the pattern-formation level, `Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ` describes how recurring identity criteria stabilize around the absence of one decisive proof. At the asymmetry-emergence level, `Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ` marks how stabilized identity patterns create moderate directional relations between past action and later accountability, past vulnerability and present interpretation, or self-relation and external recognition.

The developmental-trajectory chain, `Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ`, makes the case specifically diachronic: identity over time becomes a trajectory of patterned continuities, asymmetries, and revisions rather than a static relation between isolated moments. The reflexive-praxis chain, `Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ`, shows how multiple continuity criteria may be recontextualized, reflectively distanced, and integrated without being treated as proof. The self-binding chain, `Ψ ∘ Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ`, is therefore primary for this case, but only as a PMS projection of structural self-relation.

PMS remains valid here precisely by not completing the closure the philosophical problem demands. It renders the structure of the demand, marks the dependencies that would be required for stronger claims, and preserves Λ where no final criterion is warranted.

### Example 1 — The promise carried across a changed life

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “PMS Core structural reading,” especially after the discussion of Θ, Φ, Σ, and Ψ.

A person makes a serious promise at one stage of life: to care for a sibling after a parent’s death, to maintain a shared project, or to preserve access to an inherited home. Years later, the person’s values, relationships, economic situation, and self-understanding have changed substantially. The later person no longer identifies with many assumptions of the earlier life-phase. Nevertheless, the promise remains part of the practical field. Others refer to it. Records preserve it. The later person may feel some pull of continuity, but also some distance from the earlier self who made the commitment.

The vignette is bounded. It is not a legal case, not a clinical case, and not a moral verdict about whether the later person “must” keep the promise. The example only asks how a continuity claim becomes structurally legible when a past self-binding appears inside a later frame.

The example demonstrates why personal identity over time cannot be reduced to static sameness. The later person is not simply identical in all respects with the earlier person. Δ marks the difference: changed circumstances, changed values, changed capacities, and changed self-description. ∇ appears as the pressure to decide whether the earlier commitment still binds. □ sets the operative frame: this may be a relational promise-frame, not a metaphysical, legal, or diagnostic frame. Λ remains visible because no decisive event appears that proves metaphysical sameness between the earlier and later person. The promise persists, but persistence is not proof of essence.

Α appears where repeated recognition stabilizes the promise as part of the person’s practical identity: family conversations, documents, rituals, shared expectations, and prior acts of care may form attractors. Ω appears because not all parties are equally exposed. The sibling may depend on the promise; the later person may bear costs; other relatives may have narrative or emotional power to pressure closure. Θ is central because the promise matters only as a trajectory: it was made earlier, remembered or recorded later, and may shape future action.

Φ becomes important because the later self can recontextualize the earlier commitment. What was once a promise of direct care may now be read as a commitment to ensure support, to maintain contact, to preserve dignity, or to renegotiate responsibility. Χ is required so that the analysis does not immediately collapse into either “the promise proves the same person” or “change cancels all continuity.” Σ may integrate memory, role, capacity, dependence, and changed circumstances into a workable account. Ψ is conditional: a later self may renew, revise, or refuse the earlier binding, but PMS cannot decide that renewal by authority.

There is no add-on hook here. PMS Core is sufficient because the example concerns diachronic self-binding under change. PMS-LOGIC would only be relevant if the example were used to analyze justificatory claims about why the promise must count as binding. MIP or AHP would only become relevant if an institution used the analysis for governance, policy, audit, or responsibility allocation. They are not activated here.

The forbidden claims are substantial. The example does not prove that the later person is metaphysically identical with the earlier person. It does not show that a promise is morally binding. It does not establish legal enforceability. It does not diagnose avoidance, immaturity, guilt, selfishness, loyalty, or sincerity. It does not rank the earlier or later self. It does not validate PMS Base.

The non-capture note is equally important. A psychological reading may preserve questions about memory and emotional continuity. A legal reading may treat the promise differently if it was formalized. An ethical reading may ask what is owed to the sibling. A phenomenological reading may ask whether the later person experiences the earlier promise as “mine.” PMS can map these as frames and structural pressures, but it does not replace them.

The article takeaway is that personal identity over time becomes visible here as a structured continuity problem, not as an essence claim. A past commitment can remain structurally active without proving metaphysical sameness, and later revision can be structurally legitimate without erasing all continuity.

### Example 2 — Memory loss and the pressure to preserve sameness

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Derived structures,” especially after Awareness_A, Coherence_C, Responsibility_R, and SELF_FIXPOINT.

A person loses access to parts of their autobiographical memory after illness, injury, aging, or another life event. Friends and family still recognize them. Official records remain continuous. Some habits and preferences persist, while other patterns change. The person may not remember important shared events or earlier commitments. Others feel pressure to say either “this is still the same person” or “the person we knew is gone.”

The vignette must remain abstract and guardrail-bound. It is not a clinical description, not a neurological claim, and not a diagnostic scene. No real person is being assessed. The example concerns the structural pressure generated when memory continuity weakens but other forms of continuity remain.

The example demonstrates why PMS must not equate personal identity with any single continuity criterion. Δ marks differences between remembered and forgotten, recognized and unrecognized, bodily continuity and autobiographical rupture, external record and first-person access. ∇ appears as the pressure to close the ambiguity: relatives, institutions, or the person themselves may seek a stable answer. □ is the frame that determines what question is being asked. A medical frame, a relational frame, a legal frame, a narrative frame, and a PMS-structural frame do not authorize the same claims.

Λ is especially important. The missing memory is not merely a gap to be patched by narrative. It may remain a genuine non-capture zone. The expected event — remembered continuity — does not arrive. ### Example 3 — A public record, a changed self, and institutional recognition

### Non-capture and rival readings

Rival readings remain open. PMS translation does not defeat them.

A **psychological-continuity reading** preserves questions about memory, intention, character, belief, and psychological connectedness that PMS can structurally map but not empirically validate. PMS may model memory as an identity attractor or continuity pattern, but it does not prove that psychological continuity is necessary or sufficient for identity.

A **bodily-continuity or animalist reading** preserves organismic persistence. PMS can place embodiment within a structural frame, but it does not replace biological continuity with self-binding. If personal identity requires organismic conditions that cannot be represented as praxis, self-relation, or structural trajectory, then PMS would be weakened as a full account.

A **narrative-identity reading** preserves the role of autobiographical coherence and meaning over time. PMS can model narrative coherence through Α, Θ, Φ, Σ, and Ψ. But PMS must not reduce narrative identity to smooth integration. Narratives can conceal rupture, enforce social scripts, or fail to capture lived discontinuity.

A **memory-criterion reading** preserves the special role of recollection and experiential linkage. PMS can treat memory continuity as an attractor, but it does not infer phenomenological access. Memory loss, false memory, trauma, or non-linear recollection may resist neat integration.

A **social-recognition account** preserves the fact that identity is partly stabilized by others: family, institutions, communities, documents, and recognition practices. PMS can model this through Ω and Α, but recognition has cultural, legal, and political dimensions not exhausted by PMS Core.

A **bundle-theoretic or no-self account** preserves the possibility that there is no deep persisting self beyond aggregates, processes, or constructed relations. PMS can read this as a rival frame rather than as failure. It must not simply redescribe no-self as incomplete self-binding.

A **legal-personhood account** preserves procedural continuity: records, responsibility regimes, contracts, identity documents, and institutional recognition. PMS can map these as framed and temporally stabilized, but it cannot establish legal criteria without legal analysis.

A **phenomenological reading** preserves first-person persistence, mineness, temporal experience, and subjectivity. PMS explicitly does not claim phenomenological completeness. Structural self-binding is not lived subjectivity.

An **ethical reading** preserves questions of care, obligation, respect, responsibility, and harm. PMS may show how responsibility can become structurally weak, conditional, or asymmetrically distributed, but it does not determine what should be done.

An **anti-capture reading** preserves the possibility that the very attempt to map identity through PMS may smooth over rupture, transformation, trauma, refusal, or discontinuity. This is especially important here because the PMS reading fits well. The better it fits, the more important it becomes to preserve the possibility that fit is assimilation rather than capture.

The examples illustrate these non-capture zones without resolving them. The promise example leaves ethical and legal questions open. The memory-loss example leaves clinical, phenomenological, and care questions open. The public-record example leaves legal, social, and dignity-sensitive governance questions open. In all three, PMS clarifies closure-pressure but does not replace domain-specific standards.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would be weakened if personal identity over time were shown to require metaphysical persistence conditions that cannot be represented as praxis, self-binding, temporal trajectory, or structural relation. PMS can model identity as self-binding across integrated trajectories, but if numerical identity depends on a non-praxeological condition, PMS would not capture that condition.

The reading would be under-discriminating if it produced the same formula for memory continuity, bodily continuity, narrative identity, legal identity, and no-self accounts without preserving their differences. A useful PMS reading must distinguish these rival theories. Coverage alone is not enough.

The reading would be over-assimilative if discontinuity, rupture, disagreement, forgetting, refusal, or rival framing were automatically redescribed as incomplete integration, recontextualization, or drift. PMS must allow some non-fit to remain non-fit.

The reading would also be weakened if Θ were not truly central. If the case could be handled as a static comparison with no diachronic structure, it would no longer be the personal-identity-over-time problem in the PMS sense. Similarly, if Ψ were applied without evidence of self-binding, or Σ without actual discriminating integration, those operators would be overextended.

Stronger claims would require scene data. An applied case would need observable distinctions among identity, change, continuity, rupture, and criteria; evidence of criterion-pressure; a declared operative frame; identifiable structured absences; recurrent patterns of memory, embodiment, role, or recognition; directional asymmetries of obligation or exposure; temporal sequence and persistence; source and target frames for recontextualization; reflective distance; non-forced integration; and actual self-binding commitments.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. No empirical validation, comparative validation, computational validation, legal validation, clinical validation, or applied validation is provided. Comparative use would require explicit criteria for why PMS discriminates better than rival theories rather than merely assimilating them.

The examples do not remove this limitation. They demonstrate how the structural reading can be used in bounded vignettes. They do not supply empirical proof, legal conclusion, clinical evidence, metaphysical proof, or implementation validation. They are illustrative, not evidentiary.

A stronger applied reading of the promise example would require details about the promise frame, actual commitments, authority conditions, relational context, capacities, revision opportunities, and consent. A stronger applied reading of the memory-loss example would require clinical and phenomenological data, but that would move outside this article’s warrant. A stronger applied reading of the public-record example would require institutional rules, legal context, exposure risks, and governance safeguards. None of those stronger readings is claimed here.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It does not identify mental states, memory disorders, dissociation, identity disturbance, pathology, competence, or risk.

It must not be used for person-ranking. No person, temporal phase, identity pattern, continuity form, or discontinuity form is more mature, real, dignified, or valuable because of this analysis.

It must not be used legally or forensically. It does not establish legal identity, criminal responsibility, contractual capacity, personhood status, forensic accountability, or record-handling requirements.

It must not be used metaphysically. It does not prove numerical identity, soul persistence, substance continuity, subject persistence, or the final nature of personal identity.

It must not be used empirically unless the missing evidentiary and calibration conditions are supplied. A conceptual PMS projection is not an empirical finding.

It must not be used as implementation validation. YAML representation is not implementation of praxis, agency, selfhood, memory, or consciousness. Self-binding in a formal model is not subjective experience.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. The case applies PMS; it does not prove PMS completeness, uniqueness, necessity, maturity, or finality.

It must not treat derived structures as primitives. Awareness, coherence, responsibility, action, Dignity-in-Practice, SELF_FIXPOINT, and drift patterns are formula-derived PMS constructs.

It must not treat drift as verdict. AD_A>>E and AD_Sigma_low are possible risks only. They are not active findings, diagnoses, scores, or character assessments.

It must not produce closure by authority. PMS terminology does not authorize final identity claims. Distance, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice remain mandatory for valid application.

It must not treat Dignity-in-Practice as ontological dignity. Dignity-in-Practice is a praxeological constraint on how asymmetry and self-binding are handled. It prevents coercive or ranking uses of the analysis, but it does not derive metaphysical dignity.

It must not treat non-fit as drift. Resistance, refusal, discontinuity, rupture, absence, disagreement, memory loss, changed self-description, or non-integration may be structurally important without being failures. The misuse boundary protects these zones against forced closure.
