### Why this case matters

The question of reversible planning is philosophically important because planning naturally produces a closure-demand. To plan is not merely to imagine possible futures. It is to orient action, attention, preparation, and commitment toward some future configuration rather than another. Even a light plan distinguishes relevant from irrelevant possibilities, likely from unlikely trajectories, preparatory action from hesitation, and provisional orientation from binding decision. Planning therefore presses toward closure even when the future remains open.

The difficulty is that planning cannot simply refuse closure. A wholly open field is not yet a plan. It may be attention, imagination, waiting, speculation, or sensitivity to possibility, but planning requires at least some provisional integration. It must select a frame, differentiate possible futures, retain a temporal horizon, and coordinate practical orientation. Yet if planning closes too soon, it becomes lock-in: interpretation hardens into verdict, preparation becomes commitment, possibility becomes expectation, and expectation becomes authority. The philosophical problem is therefore not whether closure is good or bad. The problem is how a future-oriented structure can become sufficiently coherent to guide action without treating that coherence as final.

This makes the case structurally difficult in AXIOM terms. It is a closure-demand that must not be answered by closure alone. The case asks for orientation without premature fixation, commitment without irreversible self-binding, coherence without totalization, anticipation without prognosis, and future-directed action-readiness without action compulsion. Its central tension is not a lack of structure; it is too much structurally tempting structure. The more successful planning becomes at organizing uncertainty, the easier it is for the plan to appear as the correct path rather than one revisable orientation among several.

The case belongs in AXIOM because AXIOM is concerned with mapping classical and practical closure-demands as explicit structural artefacts rather than solving them through metaphysical or authoritative assertion. Reversible planning is not a classical metaphysical puzzle in the same form as personal identity, causality, or truth, but it displays the same closure-pressure in a praxis-oriented form. It asks where closure is necessary, where it becomes overreach, and where Λ-residue must remain visible. It is therefore a strong AXIOM case because it shows the method operating on a live structural problem: how to preserve future openness without dissolving practical orientation.

The case is not being solved metaphysically. PMS does not claim that the future has a determinate or indeterminate ontology, nor that possible futures exist in any metaphysical sense. The analysis does not decide whether time is open, branching, block-like, probabilistic, or phenomenologically constituted. The future appears only as a structural horizon of planning under Θ and Λ.

The case is not being solved empirically. No empirical evidence is supplied about how people, institutions, planners, teams, or artificial systems actually plan. There is no study of decision-making, no behavioral data, no organizational trace, no experimental comparison between reversible and irreversible planning, and no evidence that a given planning style succeeds or fails in practice.

The case is not clinical or diagnostic. A refusal to lock in is not treated as avoidance, indecision, anxiety, immaturity, or dysfunction. A readiness to commit is not treated as courage, rigidity, pathology, or competence. PMS explicitly prevents such person-level translation. The case is structural: it concerns the relation among frames, possible futures, non-events, attractor risks, temporal horizons, distance, provisional integration, and self-binding.

The case is not legal or forensic. It does not determine liability for failed planning, negligence, breach of duty, responsibility for foreseeable harms, or evidentiary standards for planning decisions. Legal relevance would require a concrete scene, explicit role duties, jurisdictional criteria, evidence, and an accountability frame. None is present here.

The case is also not an implementation claim. The YAML rendering is not an algorithm, not a governance protocol, not a planning system, not an AI-agent architecture, and not a decision procedure. It can support later operationalization, but it does not itself implement reversible planning.

Finally, the case does not validate PMS. A good PMS reading of reversible planning shows that PMS can render the case structurally legible under the supplied artefacts. It does not prove that PMS is complete, uniquely ordered, empirically adequate, or superior to all rival formalisms. The analysis remains open to decision-theoretic, pragmatist, phenomenological, systems-theoretic, design-theoretic, and ethical-political rival readings.

### Scene and frame structure

Because this is not a concrete praxis scene, the “scene” is a conceptual scene. Its boundary is the philosophical question of planning without lock-in: how a present orientation toward possible futures can remain reversible when planning itself tends to select, stabilize, and bind. The scene is therefore not “a planner deciding what to do,” but “the structure of planning as a closure-prone future-oriented configuration.”

The frame □ is the planning frame under reversibility constraints. Inside this frame are possible futures, provisional interpretations, alternative action paths, the difference between orientation and commitment, the possibility of delayed decision, and the risk that a plan becomes a fixed path. The frame selects what counts as relevant for the case: future-orientation, non-event preservation, reflective distance, recontextualization, provisional integration, and conditional self-binding.

Outside the frame are empirical findings about planning behavior, metaphysical claims about the future, clinical interpretations of hesitation or commitment, legal judgments about planning duties, and implementation rules for how to plan. Also outside the frame are global normative claims such as “one should always keep plans reversible” or “decisive planning is always better.” The case does not decide the ethics of planning in general. It maps the structure of reversibility under closure pressure.

The protected constraints are central. The case must preserve Χ, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice as application constraints. It must not convert planning into authority, anticipation into prognosis, probability into authorization, or coherence into proof. It must keep rival formalizations open. It must not translate non-fit, delay, absence, resistance, or unresolved possibility into PMS drift by default.

No concrete roles are present in the case material. The Core YAML therefore does not identify an actual planner, authority, affected party, implementer, or exposed role. It can only describe possible roles that would become relevant in a later operationalization: a planning agent, a frame-setter, affected parties, authority-bearing institutions, downstream users, or roles exposed to lock-in costs. Since none of these are supplied, Ω remains weak and conditional.

Temporality Θ is central. The case exists because planning is temporally extended. It concerns possible futures, anticipation, delay, trajectories, timing of commitment, and the danger that present closure creates durable future consequences. Θ is not a background condition; it is one of the main structural carriers of the case.

The reversibility window is conceptual rather than scene-specific. It refers to the interval or condition under which interpretation, commitment, and action remain revisable. In a concrete scene, this window might be defined by deadlines, costs, legal obligations, technical lock-ins, irreversible harm, or resource commitments. In the current abstract case, the window is defined structurally: a plan remains reversible while Φ can recontextualize it, Χ can suspend premature closure, Λ can remain visible, and Σ has not hardened into final Ψ-binding.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are not established. The case may later involve asymmetry if planning affects others, distributes risks unevenly, or creates obligations across roles. But the completed YAML correctly refuses to force Ω into the case. ### PMS Core structural reading

#### Δ — Difference

Δ is active and foundational. Reversible planning begins with distinctions: possible futures differ from one another; provisional interpretation differs from final interpretation; orientation differs from commitment; commitment differs from action; reversible paths differ from irreversible paths; planning differs from lock-in. Without these distinctions, there is no case to analyze.

The calibration of Δ is strong because the case explicitly turns on such contrasts. The central contrast is not simply “future A versus future B,” but “planning as reversible orientation versus planning as premature closure.” Δ makes it possible to ask whether a plan is still an orienting structure or has already become a fixed path.

The dependency risk is that Δ may be treated too cheaply. If every difference is simply listed without frame discipline, the analysis becomes a catalogue of possibilities rather than a planning structure. Conversely, if Δ is collapsed too early, the case loses the very distinctions that make reversibility possible. A false closure would treat “planning” and “commitment” as the same, or “orientation” and “action” as the same. PMS prevents that by requiring Δ to remain explicit before later operators integrate or bind the field.

Overclaim would occur if Δ were treated as proof that PMS captures the whole planning problem. Difference is the entry condition for legibility, not a complete theory of planning.

#### ∇ — Impulse

∇ is active but partially calibrated. Planning is not pure contemplation. It carries directional pressure: the need or pull to orient toward possible futures, prepare, decide, or act. In the YAML, this is treated structurally rather than psychologically. There is no claim that a planner feels anxiety, desire, ambition, fear, or urgency. ∇ names the directional tension that arises once possible futures matter for praxis.

The calibration is partial because no concrete motive, urgency, agent, need, or decision pressure is supplied. The case says there is planning pressure, not what its source is. This distinction is important. If ∇ were read psychologically, the case would drift into diagnosis. If it were read normatively, the case would become a prescription to act. The correct PMS reading keeps ∇ as structural directedness.

The dependency risk is Θ without calibrated ∇: future-orientation could become abstract speculation without practical orientation. Conversely, ∇ without Χ can become urgency that closes the field prematurely. In reversible planning, ∇ must be strong enough to explain why planning occurs, but not so strong that it overrides reversibility.

Overclaim would occur if the analysis inferred actual intention or subjective motivation from the existence of planning pressure.

#### □ — Frame

□ is active and major. Reversible planning is not orientation toward “the future” in general. It occurs within a frame that selects which futures matter, which constraints count, what kind of commitment is at stake, what counts as lock-in, and what counts as reversibility. The frame is therefore the site where the case becomes structurally bounded.

The calibration of □ is strong at the conceptual level. The operative frame is the philosophical case-type: planning under uncertainty with reversibility as a protected constraint. Inside this frame, a plan is not judged by empirical success, moral virtue, or legal duty. It is judged by whether it can remain future-oriented without prematurely closing interpretation, commitment, or action.

The frame itself must remain revisable. This is crucial. In many planning failures, lock-in does not begin with action but with framing. Once the frame silently decides what counts as relevant, what alternatives are admissible, or what future is “realistic,” later openness may be merely apparent. The Core YAML therefore treats frame revisability as part of the case, not as an external feature.

The dependency risk is that □ can be overconstrained. A frame that is too narrow can manufacture apparent necessity. A frame that is too loose can prevent planning from becoming coherent. Reversible planning requires a frame that can orient without pretending to be exhaustive.

Overclaim would occur if the chosen frame were treated as the natural or final frame of planning. PMS requires the frame to be declared and contestable.

#### Λ — Non-Event

Λ is active and highly important. Reversible planning is saturated with meaningful non-events: the future has not occurred; some alternatives remain unrealized; a final decision has not been made; a commitment is delayed; expected information may not arrive; an action path remains withheld. These absences are not merely empty. They structure the planning field.

The calibration of Λ is one of the strongest parts of the case. The expected occurrence is eventual orientation or action, but the case is about how the delay or non-occurrence of final commitment can remain meaningful without being treated as failure. The not-yet is part of planning, not simply a defect in planning.

This is a major non-capture safeguard. The YAML explicitly warns that absence, delay, non-decision, or unrealized alternatives must not automatically be translated into avoidance, drift, incomplete integration, or immaturity. In reversible planning, non-finality may be the very condition of structural validity.

The dependency risk is that Λ may be closed too early. If the absence of final decision is treated as a problem to be eliminated, planning becomes lock-in. If Λ is romanticized and never integrated, planning may fail to orient action. The case therefore requires Λ to be carried, not erased, and later coordinated with Χ, Φ, and provisional Σ.

Overclaim would occur if the analysis treated every non-event as meaningful in the PMS sense. Λ requires an expected occurrence within a frame. In this case, that expectation is future orientation, possible commitment, or action. Without that expectation, absence would be under-specified.

#### Α — Attractor

Α is active mainly as risk. Lock-in is structurally readable as attractor stabilization: a plan, frame, assumption, or projected path begins to repeat, self-reinforce, and become the path of least resistance. However, the case material does not show actual recurrence. It names the concern of lock-in, but it does not provide evidence of repeated patterning.

The calibration of Α is therefore weak-to-moderate. It is legitimate to monitor attractor risk because planning can create path dependence. Once a future is repeatedly used to organize interpretation, resources, and expectations, it may become increasingly difficult to revise. But the YAML correctly refuses to claim that such a pattern has already formed.

The dependency risk is premature Α attribution. If any provisional plan is called an attractor, PMS loses discrimination. A plan may stabilize enough to be useful without becoming lock-in. Conversely, if actual recurrence were later shown, ignoring Α would miss a key mechanism by which reversible planning fails.

Overclaim would occur if lock-in risk were treated as established lock-in. In the completed YAML, Α remains monitored rather than asserted.

#### Ω — Asymmetry

Ω is weak and conditional. This is one of the most important scope decisions in the case. Planning often affects others. It often distributes risk, exposure, authority, and cost. In many concrete contexts, Ω would become central. But the supplied case material does not identify affected parties, power gradients, vulnerable roles, capacity differences, obligation asymmetries, or authority relations.

The calibration of Ω is therefore weak by design. The analysis may say that Ω would become relevant if planning created responsibility gradients or exposed others to lock-in costs. It may not say that such asymmetry is already present.

This weak status protects the case from moralization. If Ω were forced into the analysis, the case might begin to imply responsibility, vulnerability, coercion, or authority where none has been supplied. PMS discipline requires asymmetry to be mapped, not assumed.

The dependency risk is two-sided. If Ω is ignored in a concrete scene where planning affects others, the analysis would be under-calibrated and potentially unsafe. If Ω is imposed in an abstract scene without evidence, the analysis overreaches. The completed YAML chooses the correct middle: Ω is part of the dependency chain and a conditional guardrail, but not a strong finding.

Overclaim would occur if reversible planning were treated as ethically valid simply because Ω is not specified. Absence of supplied asymmetry is not proof that no asymmetry exists in possible applications. It only limits the present case.

#### Θ — Temporality

Θ is central. The case is unintelligible without temporal structure. Planning orients the present toward possible futures; it involves anticipation, delay, sequencing, commitment timing, reversibility windows, possible path-dependence, and durable consequences. Θ is therefore one of the core carriers of the analysis.

The calibration of Θ is strong. The case explicitly concerns possible futures and the danger of premature lock-in over time. It is not about a static preference for openness. Reversibility must be sustained across time, where windows may close, commitments may harden, and costs may accumulate.

Θ also prevents a simplistic reading of reversible planning as indefinite suspension. A plan must eventually relate to temporal pressure: deadlines, changing information, closing opportunities, or the increasing cost of revision. Even though no concrete timeline is supplied, the case-type itself is temporal.

The dependency risk is future-orientation without Χ or Λ. Θ can become projection if possible futures are treated as already settled. It can become urgency if closing windows are invoked without distance. It can become lock-in if temporal trajectory is mistaken for necessity.

Overclaim would occur if the analysis inferred a specific time horizon, deadline, or action sequence. The current case supports Θ as structural temporality, not an empirical timeline.

#### Φ — Recontextualization

Φ is active and central. Reversible planning depends on the ability to recontextualize a plan when assumptions, constraints, meanings, or possible futures shift. Without Φ, a plan can only remain what it first was. Reversibility would then be superficial: the plan might be delayed, but not genuinely reframable.

The calibration of Φ is strong. Source frames include the current interpretation, initial planning assumptions, projected futures, and current relevance criteria. Target frames include revised constraints, new information, altered futures, rival formalizations, or changed purposes. Φ is what allows a plan to be placed into a new frame without treating the prior frame as final.

But Φ has its own risk. Recontextualization can become endless reframing. If every pressure toward integration is dissolved by another frame shift, planning may never stabilize enough to guide action. The YAML therefore pairs Φ with provisional Σ and Χ rather than allowing it to dominate alone.

Another risk is over-assimilation. PMS must not use Φ to absorb every disagreement, rupture, resistance, non-fit, or rival formalization as merely incomplete recontextualization. Rival frames may genuinely preserve something PMS does not capture. The completed YAML explicitly protects this boundary.

Overclaim would occur if Φ were treated as universal repair. Recontextualization can revise planning, but it does not guarantee adequacy.

#### Χ — Distance

Χ is central and strongly calibrated. It is the main anti-lock-in operator. Reversible planning requires reflective distance from urgency, projection, fixed interpretation, premature commitment, and immediate action. Χ allows planning to remain oriented without fusing with its own projected future.

The case also self-applies Χ. The analysis does not convert PMS coherence into truth. It does not convert the ANTICIPATION overlay into authority. It does not claim empirical success, diagnosis, legal validity, or implementation readiness. This self-restraint is not stylistic; it is structurally required.

Χ must be distinguished from avoidance. This is a major calibration point. Distance is not passivity, paralysis, empty neutrality, superiority, or refusal to decide. It is reflective withdrawal sufficient to prevent premature closure. A concrete case would need evidence to decide whether distance is productive or excessive. In this abstract case, no such finding is possible.

The dependency risk is Θ and ∇ without Χ. Future pressure can become action compulsion. Planning can become urgency. Coherence can become authority. Χ interrupts these conversions.

Overclaim would occur if the analysis praised distance as always superior to commitment. PMS does not do that. Χ is necessary for valid application, but action may still be required in some concrete scenes. This case does not decide when.

#### Σ — Integration

Σ is active but bounded. Planning requires integration: alternatives, frames, uncertainties, constraints, and temporal horizons must be coordinated enough to guide orientation. Without Σ, reversible planning could dissolve into scattered possibility.

But Σ is also dangerous. It is the operator most likely to become false closure in this case. A coherent plan can feel like the plan. A well-integrated narrative of future action can make rival futures appear irrelevant. This is why the YAML marks Σ as provisional. It must be enough to orient, not enough to totalize.

The calibration of Σ is partial. The case needs some integration but does not specify what counts as adequate integration in a concrete planning scene. No criteria are supplied for sufficiency, trade-off resolution, or action-readiness. Stronger Σ claims would require such criteria.

The dependency risk is Σ without Χ and Φ. Integration without distance becomes forced coherence. Integration without recontextualization becomes rigid. Integration without visible Λ erases the not-yet. Integration under unexamined Ω may become coercive if others are affected.

Overclaim would occur if PMS coherence were treated as proof that the analysis is correct or that the plan is adequate. The YAML explicitly rejects this. Coherence is not sufficiency.

#### Ψ — Self-Binding

Ψ is weak-to-conditional. Planning may involve commitments across time, but this case is specifically about avoiding premature lock-in. Therefore Ψ cannot be treated as fully active in the sense of stable self-binding or full SELF_FIXPOINT. It appears as a conditional discipline of reversible commitment.

The calibration of Ψ is weak because no agent, commitment, promise, duty, policy, or identity-binding is supplied. The case permits discussion of reversible self-binding: commitments that can orient future action while remaining revisable under defined conditions. But it does not establish actual self-relation or responsibility.

Ψ is important precisely because it is dangerous here. Planning often becomes lock-in when provisional orientation is bound as identity, promise, doctrine, policy, or irreversible action path. The case therefore treats Ψ as something to be constrained, not maximized.

The dependency risk is Ψ without provisional Σ, Θ, and Χ. If self-binding occurs without adequate integration, temporal awareness, and distance, it becomes premature commitment. In the ANTICIPATION overlay, Ψ must bind the anticipator to restraint rather than binding others to the anticipated path. That overlay point builds directly on the Core caution.

Overclaim would occur if the case asserted SELF_FIXPOINT, responsibility, or identity commitment. The completed YAML does not do this.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The PMS dependency discipline is crucial because reversible planning tempts shortcutting. It is easy to move from Δ and ∇ directly to commitment: a difference appears, pressure arises, a plan is selected, and the selected plan is treated as binding. PMS blocks this shortcut by requiring the intervening structure to remain visible: frame declaration, non-event preservation, pattern monitoring, asymmetry checking, temporality, recontextualization, distance, and only then integration and possible self-binding.

Later operators cannot be used cheaply. Σ requires Χ and Φ. It cannot be invoked merely because a coherent plan has been written. Ψ requires Σ, Θ, and Χ. It cannot be invoked merely because a future-oriented statement has been made. Responsibility_R requires Ψ, Φ, Θ, and Ω. It cannot be claimed without concrete asymmetry and self-binding. Dignity-in-Practice_D requires Ψ, Χ, and Ω as a derived structure, though in this case it functions primarily as an application constraint.

Σ remains conditional because integration must be provisional. In reversible planning, a plan needs coherence, but coherence must remain open to later frame shifts and residual uncertainty. The analysis therefore does not praise low integration. It says that integration must not become closure by stealth.

Ψ remains conditional because the case is about pre-lock-in planning. Self-binding may eventually become necessary, but the YAML does not supply the conditions under which it becomes legitimate. In a concrete scene, one would need to know who is bound, what is bound, for how long, under what authority, with what revision conditions, and with what exposure distribution.

Coherence is not proof. This is one of the central quality-control points. A PMS reading can become too smooth because reversible planning fits PMS vocabulary well. Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Λ, and Ψ map naturally onto the problem. That fit is useful, but it also creates danger. A coherent PMS derivation may under-discriminate between reversible planning, avoidance, indecision, adaptive optionality, democratic openness, technical rollback, and ethical restraint. Therefore coherence must be tested by discrimination, falsifiers, rival readings, and operationalization requirements.

Non-capture is not failure. Some aspects of reversible planning may be better captured by other frameworks. Decision theory may treat the issue as option value under uncertainty. Pragmatism may treat it as experimental action. Phenomenology may treat it as lived openness to futurity. Systems theory may treat it as adaptive feedback. Design theory may treat it as modularity and rollback. Ethical-political theory may treat reversibility as resistance to imposed futures. PMS can render a structural reading without owning the phenomenon.

PMS must not become a closure machine. The whole point of the case is to preserve visible Λ-residue and reversibility. If PMS were used to declare that the correct formula has solved reversible planning, the analysis would contradict its own application discipline. PMS maps closure-demand; it does not solve by authority.

Invalid dependency shortcuts would include moving from possible future to action mandate, from coherent plan to truth, from delayed commitment to failure, from recontextualization to maturity, from absence to drift, from planning pressure to responsibility, from lock-in risk to established attractor, or from weak Ω to ethical authority. The completed YAML avoids these shortcuts by marking major uncertain elements as weak, conditional, under-specified, or analytic-only.

### Derived structures

Derived structures are formula-derived PMS constructs, not independent primitives. They do not float above the operators and they do not supply separate authority. Their status in this case depends on whether the relevant operator formula is sufficiently activated by the case material.

#### Awareness_A

Awareness_A is active. Its formula is Θ / □ / Δ: sustained, framed differentiation across time. The case clearly supports this. Reversible planning requires the capacity to distinguish possible futures, interpretations, commitments, and action paths within a frame across a temporal horizon.

This is structural awareness, not consciousness. It does not claim phenomenological experience, subjective insight, mindfulness, or psychological awareness. It means that the planning field keeps relevant distinctions available over time rather than collapsing them prematurely.

Awareness_A matters because reversible planning fails if the field loses differentiation. If future alternatives are no longer distinguishable, if provisional and final commitments blur, or if interpretation and action fuse, reversibility is already compromised. Awareness_A is therefore one of the strongest derived structures in the Core case.

#### Coherence_C

Coherence_C is active but provisional. Its formula is Θ / Λ / □ / ∇: temporally stabilized structuring of directional pressure and expectation within a frame, including meaningful non-events. The case supports this because planning requires orientation over time while carrying not-yet structures and delayed commitments.

The provisional character is essential. Coherence_C does not mean full integration. It means the planning orientation is stable enough to remain intelligible: there is a frame, a future horizon, an orienting pressure, and meaningful absence. But this coherence must not become proof, closure, or final Σ.

Coherence_C is especially important because the case is not a defense of incoherence. Reversibility does not mean refusing structure. It means preserving a kind of coherence that can carry Λ without erasing it.

#### Responsibility_R

Responsibility_R is weak. Its formula is Ψ / Φ / Θ / Ω. The case strongly supports Θ and Φ, but it does not establish Ω or Ψ concretely. #### Action_E

Action_E is under-specified. Its formula is Σ / Θ / ∇: integrated realization of directedness across time. The case concerns orientation toward possible action, but no action is taken, no plan is enacted, and no sequence of implementation is described.

This under-specification is not a failure. It is exactly what the case requires. The problem is planning before lock-in, not enacted planning after decision. Action_E may appear downstream if a concrete plan becomes implemented, but the current case cannot claim it.

Action_E also marks one of the main risks: reversible planning could drift into too much awareness and too little enactment. But because no concrete enactment is supplied, the analysis cannot classify that drift as present. It can only identify what would be needed to assess it.

#### Dignity_in_Practice_D

Dignity_in_Practice_D functions as an application constraint. Its formula is Ψ / Χ / Ω: self-bound reflective restraint and protection under asymmetry. In this case, Ω and Ψ are not concretely established, so D cannot be claimed as a full derived scene structure. But D is active as a rule governing the analysis itself.

This means the case must not rank persons, shame planners, diagnose hesitation, humiliate those who delay, or treat commitment style as moral worth. It must not convert asymmetry into authority if a later scene introduces affected parties. It must not use planning language to coerce.

D is praxeological, not ontological. The case does not prove dignity. It does not rank dignity. It does not claim moral superiority for reversible planners. It constrains how the analysis may handle asymmetry, especially if planning affects others.

#### SELF_FIXPOINT

SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only and not established. Its formula is the full derived self-binding chain: Ψ ∘ Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ. The case does not support this as an achieved structure because it explicitly resists premature self-binding and lacks a concrete agent or self-relation.

The correct reading is that SELF_FIXPOINT appears as a boundary condition, not a result. Reversible planning asks how self-binding might remain revisable if it later occurs. It does not show that a stable self-relation has been formed.

This distinction is central. If the analysis claimed SELF_FIXPOINT, it would turn planning without lock-in into a completed self-binding structure. That would contradict the case. The YAML correctly keeps SELF_FIXPOINT analytic-only: available for dependency inspection, not activated as a finding.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The completed PMS Core YAML treats drift-patterns as **possible structural risks**, not as findings. This distinction is decisive. The case is abstract, not a concrete praxis scene, and therefore it cannot establish chronic delay, under-enactment, fragmented integration, avoidance, coercion, low responsibility, or any other applied drift condition. It can only identify where reversible planning might drift if a later scene supplied evidence.

The two PMS drift-patterns considered are **AD_A>>E** and **AD_Sigma_low**. Both are relevant because reversible planning sits precisely at the boundary between sustained awareness and eventual enactment, and between repeated recontextualization and provisional integration. But neither pattern is classified as present.

#### AD_A>>E — Excessive distance between Awareness and Enactment

**AD_A>>E** is treated as **possible but not entailed**. The risk is clear: reversible planning could over-expand awareness, scenario-work, interpretation, evaluation, and reflective distance while suppressing enactment. A planner, institution, or analysis could keep generating possible futures, alternative frames, and non-event residues without ever moving toward Action_E. In such a case, Awareness_A would expand while integrated action remained chronically delayed.

However, the current case does not warrant that finding. No concrete actor is supplied. No timeline is given. No missed action window is named. No repeated postponement is evidenced. No affected party is exposed to delay. No action was required and then withheld. Therefore the axis effects remain **under-specified**: the awareness axis is under-specified, and the action axis is under-specified.

This matters because reversible planning must not be automatically pathologized as avoidance or paralysis. A refusal to lock in may be structurally valid. Keeping Λ open may be necessary. Sustaining Χ may be the condition that prevents planning from becoming coercive, premature, or false. The drift-pattern becomes relevant only if later evidence shows that awareness, reflection, or scenario expansion has become recurrently detached from any possible enactment under conditions where action was structurally required.

In this case, AD_A>>E functions as a calibration warning: do not let reversibility become a license for endless distance. But it is not a verdict.

#### AD_Sigma_low — Fragmented integration

**AD_Sigma_low** is also **possible but not entailed**. The risk is that Φ may outpace Σ. Reversible planning depends on recontextualization: assumptions shift, futures change, frames are revised, and plans are re-read under new constraints. But if reframing becomes too dominant, provisional integration may never stabilize. The planning field may become volatile, with each new possible future dissolving the previous orientation before it can guide action.

Again, the current case does not establish that this has occurred. No concrete sequence of repeated reframing is supplied. No failed integration is documented. No responsibility axis is activated. No coherence collapse is shown. The YAML therefore marks the coherence axis as under-specified and the responsibility axis as under-specified.

This is not a low score. Under-specification is not failure. It is a claim-boundary. In an abstract philosophical case, PMS cannot infer that Σ is too low merely because the case values reversibility. Non-lock-in is not fragmentation. Openness to rival frames is not weak integration. Unresolved absence is not incoherence. A plan may remain reversible precisely because Σ is provisional rather than final.

AD_Sigma_low therefore functions as a later test: if a concrete scene shows that recontextualization repeatedly prevents even provisional orientation, or that no temporary coherence can survive the appearance of rival futures, then under-integration may become a live drift-pattern. In the present case, it remains a risk condition only.

#### Coherence risk and under-discrimination risk

The broader Core YAML identifies a high **coherence risk**. Reversible planning fits PMS vocabulary unusually well. Θ, Λ, Φ, Χ, provisional Σ, and conditional Ψ all map cleanly onto the case. That fit is useful, but it is also dangerous. A smooth PMS rendering may create the impression that the problem has been solved because it has been elegantly organized.

The YAML explicitly resists this. PMS coherence is not proof. A coherent operator chain must still discriminate. It must distinguish reversible planning from avoidance, indecision, adaptive optionality, democratic openness, technical rollback, ethical restraint, strategic flexibility, and pragmatic experimentation. If PMS cannot make those distinctions, the reading becomes too broad.

The under-discrimination risk is therefore central. PMS must not turn “planning without lock-in” into a universal success formula. It must show where reversibility is structurally meaningful, where it becomes excessive delay, where it requires action constraints, where it creates responsibility gradients, and where rival formalisms capture more than PMS does. In the completed YAML, this risk is mitigated by weak markings, non-capture zones, rival readings, falsifier conditions, and unfulfilled operationalization requirements.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant add-on is **PMS-ANTICIPATION**. It is applied strictly as an **overlay/application profile**, not as PMS Base. PMS.yaml remains the only PMS Base reference. ANTICIPATION does not redefine Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, or Ψ. It does not change operator order or dependency logic. Its reduced signatures are overlay shorthand only.

The add-on is justified because PMS Core makes the planning structure legible, but it does not isolate anticipation-specific risks with the same precision. Core PMS can already show future-orientation, non-events, frame structure, distance, provisional integration, conditional self-binding, and lock-in risk. ANTICIPATION adds a more specific lens for future-oriented praxis: it distinguishes anticipation from prognosis, carries possible future events and non-events under Λ and Θ, identifies probability-action compulsion, preventive escalation, post-confirmation drift, and the specific danger that anticipation becomes authority.

In other words, PMS Core explains how reversible planning can be structurally mapped. PMS-ANTICIPATION sharpens the question of how planning can remain anticipatory rather than prognostic or coercive.

#### Overlay status

The overlay is subordinate to the completed Core pass. It adds an anticipation-specific reading of the already typed case without changing the PMS Core case result. Its claims are overlay claims only. It does not validate PMS, does not correct the Core reading, does not produce a second PMS Base, and does not convert the case into governance, diagnosis, or implementation.

This subordination is not cosmetic. It prevents operator mixing. ANTICIPATION constructs such as `ANT_min`, `ANT_viable_full`, `ANT_to_planning_constraint`, prognosis drift, post-confirmation discipline, or probability-action compulsion are not new PMS operators. They are handles for reading the PMS operators under an anticipation profile.

#### What ANTICIPATION makes visible

The overlay defines anticipation as a **structurally disciplined present stance under Λ and Θ in the presence of possible Ω, sustained by Χ, Σ, and Ψ, without closure claims**. Applied to this case, it makes visible that reversible planning is not merely planning with optionality. It is a present stance toward possible futures that must preserve meaningful non-occurrence, delayed commitment, and future uncertainty while still orienting planning.

The key ANTICIPATION distinction is between anticipation and prognosis. Prognosis is a future-truth posture: it treats a specific future state as sufficiently settled to coordinate around it. Anticipation, by contrast, carries uncertainty without closure. It may prepare, orient, or constrain planning, but it does not claim to know the future and does not authorize action by itself.

This distinction is crucial for reversible planning. A plan can subtly become prognosis when a possible future is treated as the future. The overlay blocks that slide. A possible future may be salient, plausible, worrying, attractive, or strategically important, but none of that licenses closure while Λ persists.

#### ANT_min

`ANT_min` is active. Its reduced signature is **Δ, □, Λ, Θ, Χ**. This means a bounded possibility is held within a frame, remains structurally open as Λ, is temporally extended under Θ, and is restrained by Χ.

The case meets this minimal signature strongly. A bounded future possibility is differentiated. It is held within a planning frame. The future may not occur, may remain undecided, or may require reframing. Θ is central through future-orientation, delay, and possible irreversibility. Χ preserves distance and prevents future-orientation from becoming action-authorization.

But `ANT_min` is overlay shorthand only. It does not replace PMS dependencies. It does not mean that ∇, Α, Ω, Φ, Σ, or Ψ are absent from PMS Base. It means that, for ANTICIPATION purposes, the minimal anticipatory stance is visible through this reduced configuration.

#### ANT_viable_full

`ANT_viable_full` is weak. Its full configuration includes all PMS operators: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. Mature anticipation would discipline impulse, carry openness, recognize possible pattern stabilization, handle asymmetry across time, adapt through recontextualization, preserve distance, integrate without closure, and bind the anticipator to restraint.

The abstract case does not support that full configuration. Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Θ, Φ, Χ, and provisional Σ are active. But Α is only a monitored lock-in risk. Ω is weak and conditional because no exposure or obligation gradient is specified. Ψ is only bounded self-binding discipline, not established full anticipatory self-relation. Action_E is also under-specified.

Therefore `ANT_viable_full` functions as a calibration horizon, not an achieved structure. It tells us what a fuller anticipatory praxis would require, but it does not let us claim that the case has already met those conditions.

#### ANT_to_planning_constraint

`ANT_to_planning_constraint` is active, but it is explicitly **not an operator**. It is an overlay construct stating how anticipation constrains downstream planning or preparedness. It requires Χ, D, and reversibility.

This is one of the most important overlay contributions. The case explicitly concerns planning, so ANTICIPATION can say that planning may emerge downstream from anticipatory stance, but it must not convert probability, scenario salience, concern, urgency, or coherence into compulsion while Λ persists under Θ.

This construct also clarifies why the case is not action-prescriptive. Anticipation can support planning constraints without mandating readiness, execution, or intervention. It binds the anticipatory stance to restraint rather than giving it authority.

#### Viability criteria

The overlay defines viability independently of being right. An anticipatory configuration is viable if it remains coherent under Λ and responsibility-bearing under Ω and Θ without collapsing Χ. In this case, viability is satisfied minimally but not fully.

Λ is carried. Possible futures are not resolved into certainty. Postponed commitment and unrealized alternatives remain meaningful.

Θ is respected. The analysis recognizes future-orientation, delay, commitment timing, possible irreversibility, and the danger that early lock-in creates durable consequences.

Χ is active. The overlay refuses future-truth claims, action mandates, probability authorization, and premature closure.

Σ integrates openness provisionally. Uncertainty is coordinated enough to orient planning, but not converted into certainty.

Ψ is weak. The anticipatory stance binds itself only to restraint and consequence-handling. It does not bind unspecified others, and it does not become identity or authority.

Ω remains weak. Since no concrete asymmetry is supplied, the overlay cannot claim responsibility-bearing anticipation under actual exposure gradients. It can only state that if Ω appears later, it must increase restraint, not authority.

#### Formal invalidity markers

The overlay identifies several formal invalidity markers. In this case they function mostly as risks.

The first is replacing Λ with certainty. This would occur if the plan treated a possible future as settled. The current reading avoids this, but lock-in would involve precisely such a collapse.

The second is actionism neutralizing Θ. This would occur if the future horizon were compressed into an immediate must-act command, ignoring irreversibility and trajectory weight. The current overlay avoids action compulsion.

The third is Χ suspension through assumed superior knowledge. If the planner, analyst, or institution claimed privileged future access, distance would collapse into authority. The current reading prevents this by refusing prognosis posture.

The fourth is Ω instrumentalization. This is absent in the current case because no concrete asymmetry is specified. It would appear if an anticipatory stance used power gradients to enforce compliance.

The fifth is Ψ externalization. This is also absent. The overlay does not bind others. It binds only the anticipatory stance to restraint.

#### Anticipation versus prognosis

The case remains anticipation rather than prognosis. It contains no forecast, no probability estimate, no evidence-based prediction, no determinate future claim, and no assertion that one future is sufficiently settled to coordinate action by authority.

Prognosis appears only as contrast risk. Reversible planning becomes prognosis-like when a possible future is treated as settled enough to close interpretation, commitment, or action. It also becomes prognosis-like when planning coherence is taken as evidence that the projected path is correct.

The overlay permits prognoses as descriptive inputs in later scenes, but only if they are reprocessed through □, Λ, Θ, Χ, D, and reversibility. Prognosis may supply content; it must not supply authority.

#### ANTICIPATION drift risks

The overlay identifies several drift risks, all treated as risks rather than findings.

**Probability-induced action compulsion** is a risk. Even without numerical probability in the case, the structural danger is relevant: a possible future may be treated as likely enough to demand action. The overlay blocks this by insisting that probability does not authorize compulsion while Λ persists.

**Preventive escalation** is a risk. Planning may become preemptive commitment before uncertainty is resolved. In a concrete scene, this could mean irreversible preparation, exclusion of alternatives, or coercive intervention justified by possible future harm. The current case marks this risk without claiming it is present.

**AD_A≫E** appears again under the overlay as over-reflection and under-enactment. Here it means that sustained anticipatory awareness may delay action. Again, this is not a finding because no concrete action window is supplied.

**Post-confirmation Χ erosion** is a risk. If a projected future later occurs, the anticipator may treat correctness as authority. The overlay insists that confirmation should reopen distance, not remove it. Being right does not retroactively erase Λ, justify earlier closure, or authorize future dominance.

**Moralization of uncertainty** is not active. The case does not treat uncertainty as guilt, virtue, failure, or obligation.

**Preemptive authoritarian drift** is not active. No authority relation or enforcement mechanism is specified.

#### Post-confirmation discipline

Post-confirmation discipline is weak but important as a guardrail. Since no anticipated event is confirmed in the abstract case, the overlay cannot apply a concrete post-confirmation reading. But it can state what would be required if a future projection later turned out correct.

Confirmation must not become entitlement. It must not bind identity to being right. It must not produce Ω-inflation at the epistemic level. It must not erode Χ. It must not rewrite prior uncertainty as inevitability. In PMS-ANTICIPATION terms, confirmation calls for renewed distance rather than accelerated authority.

This is especially relevant to reversible planning because planning success can be retrospectively misread as proof that the plan had been correct all along. The overlay blocks that retrospective closure. A plan may succeed without having been certain. A future may occur without having been inevitable. A good anticipatory stance remains disciplined even after apparent confirmation.

### Layer interaction

The PMS Core reading and the ANTICIPATION overlay interact without mixing.

The PMS Core reading remains responsible for the base structural grammar. It supplies Δ through Ψ, operator dependencies, derived structures, entry conditions, and application guardrails. It determines that the case is a conceptual projection, not a concrete scene. It marks Θ, Φ, Χ, Λ, □, Δ, and provisional Σ as central; ∇ as structurally active but partially calibrated; Α as monitored risk; Ω and Ψ as weak or conditional; Responsibility_R as weak; Action_E as under-specified; SELF_FIXPOINT as analytic-only.

The ANTICIPATION overlay sharpens one domain-specific aspect: the case’s future-oriented stance under non-event conditions. It makes visible that reversible planning is not only a Core PMS problem of distance and provisional integration. It is also an anticipation problem: the plan must hold possible future events and non-events under Θ without becoming prognosis, action compulsion, preventive escalation, or externalized self-binding.

What remains PMS Core is the operator grammar. The overlay cannot redefine Λ as merely “future uncertainty,” cannot redefine Θ as prediction horizon, cannot redefine Χ as caution, cannot redefine Σ as planning quality, and cannot redefine Ψ as obligation to act. Those would be overlay overreaches. The overlay can only read these PMS operators under the anticipation profile.

What the overlay sharpens is the contrast between present stance and future-truth claim. PMS Core already says that Λ must remain visible. ANTICIPATION specifies what happens when Λ is carried as possible future non-occurrence. PMS Core already says Χ prevents premature closure. ANTICIPATION specifies how Χ blocks probability-action compulsion, prognosis authority, and post-confirmation entitlement. PMS Core already treats Ψ as conditional. ANTICIPATION specifies that Ψ must bind the anticipator to restraint, not bind others to the anticipated path.

Overlay language could overreach if `ANT_min` were treated as a new operator chain replacing PMS dependencies, if `ANT_viable_full` were claimed despite weak Ω and Ψ, if planning constraints were treated as prescriptions, if prognosis contrast were used to dismiss all forecasting, or if post-confirmation discipline were used to imply that being right never matters. The completed overlay avoids these moves.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. Core PMS preserves Λ as meaningful not-yet. ANTICIPATION preserves possible non-occurrence and blocks future-truth authority. The final result is not “reversible planning is solved.” It is: reversible planning is structurally intelligible when Θ-centered future-orientation is held under Λ, Φ, Χ, provisional Σ, and conditional Ψ, with ANTICIPATION clarifying the risks of prognosis drift and action authorization.

### Example 1 — The refundable route

A small research group plans a trip to a conference in another city. The date is fixed, but the group has not yet decided whether to travel by train the evening before, take an early train on the day itself, or book a refundable hotel near the venue. Weather conditions, strike announcements, and the final conference schedule are still uncertain. The group therefore does not treat any one itinerary as final. Instead, it holds three options open, marks the latest reasonable booking point, and agrees to review the frame once the schedule and travel conditions become clearer.

The example demonstrates reversible planning in a low-stakes, readable form. The group is genuinely planning: it is not merely waiting. It distinguishes possible futures, compares constraints, and prepares for action. But it does not treat the currently most convenient route as destiny. The plan remains a structured orientation toward possible futures rather than a locked path.

The PMS Core hooks are straightforward. **Δ** distinguishes travel options, booking conditions, cost exposure, and timing. **∇** appears as the practical pressure to orient before prices rise or seats disappear. **□** is the planning frame: conference travel under uncertainty, not a general theory of mobility or risk. **Λ** is central: the strike may not happen, the schedule may change, the early train may remain feasible, and final confirmation has not yet arrived. **Θ** appears through booking deadlines, travel timing, and the closing of the reversibility window. **Φ** is active because new information can recontextualize the route. **Χ** is the restraint that prevents the group from treating the first plausible option as the final path. **Σ** appears only provisionally: the group integrates enough information to remain oriented, but not enough to claim final closure. **Ψ** is weak: the group has a review commitment, not an irreversible self-binding.

The ANTICIPATION overlay sharpens what PMS Core already shows. **ANT_min** is active: a bounded future possibility is held inside a frame while Λ remains open under Θ and Χ. **ANT_to_planning_constraint** is also active: planning may proceed, but the possibility of a strike does not itself authorize panic, overbooking, or command. **ANT_viable_full** remains too strong because no substantial Ω structure, responsibility gradient, or durable self-binding is established.

MIP, AHP, and QC are not activated. The example is not a governance artefact, not a risk audit, and not an analysis-hardening case. If the planning affected many dependent participants or imposed costs on others, MIP might become relevant downstream, but not here.

Forbidden claims are important. The example does not show that reversible planning is always better, that the group is more mature, that decisive booking would be irrational, or that delay is virtuous. It also does not diagnose hesitation or praise flexibility as a personality trait.

The non-capture note is that a decision-theoretic reading could model the same example as option value under uncertainty. A design reading could model it as keeping rollback capacity. PMS-ANTICIPATION does not defeat those readings; it shows how the case remains structurally legible as planning with Λ preserved.

The article takeaway is simple: reversible planning is not the absence of planning. It is planning in which the not-yet remains structurally visible until the frame warrants stronger commitment.

### Example 2 — The modular syllabus

A lecturer designs a twelve-week seminar. The topic is fixed, but the class composition is not yet known. The students may be advanced, mixed, or mostly new to the field. Instead of writing a rigid week-by-week plan that assumes one level of prior knowledge, the lecturer prepares a modular syllabus: three core sessions are fixed, several readings are marked as optional extensions, and two review points are built into the schedule. The lecturer also states in the syllabus that later sessions may be reweighted after the first discussions reveal what the group can reasonably carry.

This is a strong example of planning without lock-in because it shows that reversibility can coexist with real structure. The lecturer has not refused commitment. There is a topic, a frame, a sequence, a minimum reading path, and a temporal horizon. Yet the plan does not pretend to know in advance which interpretive route will fit the actual group. It preserves revision without abandoning orientation.

The PMS Core structure is layered. **Δ** distinguishes fixed core content from adjustable extensions, expected from actual student preparation, and planning from enactment. **∇** is the pressure to produce a syllabus before the course begins. **□** is the educational frame: seminar planning under uncertain group conditions. **Λ** appears in the absence of present knowledge about the group’s background and in the possibility that expected comprehension will not occur. **Α** is only a risk: an initial syllabus structure may become an attractor if the lecturer follows it merely because it exists. **Ω** is weak but more visible than in the travel example, because the lecturer has role authority and students may bear the cost of a bad plan. Still, the example does not justify strong Ω claims without more scene detail. **Θ** is central because the course unfolds over weeks and revision windows matter. **Φ** allows the plan to be recontextualized after early sessions. **Χ** prevents the syllabus from becoming an authority object that overrides emerging evidence. **Σ** is active but provisional: the syllabus integrates aims, readings, sequence, and uncertainty, yet does not claim final fit. **Ψ** appears only as bounded commitment to a revisable teaching structure.

The ANTICIPATION overlay adds precision. **ANT_min** is active because a bounded possible future—the expected learning trajectory—is held open under Λ and Θ with Χ. **ANT_to_planning_constraint** is active because the lecturer may prepare, but preparation must not become prognosis. The plan should not say, in effect, “the class will be this kind of class.” **ANT_viable_full** remains weak because responsibility, asymmetry, and self-binding are present only in a limited pedagogical sense and are not analyzed through a governance layer.

MIP and AHP remain inactive in the article example. If the syllabus were part of an institutional policy, a high-stakes qualification process, or a formal accessibility obligation, a governance or quality-control layer might become relevant. In this bounded example, it remains a pedagogical planning vignette, not an audit.

The forbidden claims are that the lecturer is not diagnosed as flexible, cautious, or responsible; the students are not evaluated; and the example does not prove an educational method. It also does not claim empirical superiority for modular syllabi.

The non-capture note is that educational theory might describe the same case through scaffolding, adaptive pedagogy, curriculum design, or constructivist learning. Those readings may preserve pedagogical details PMS does not attempt to capture.

The article takeaway is that Σ can be strong enough to orient a plan without becoming closure. A reversible plan may be more structured, not less, precisely because it marks where revision is legitimate.

### Example 3 — The contingency memo that does not command

A small cultural venue is preparing for an outdoor evening event. Several things may happen: the weather may remain mild, heavy rain may arrive, public transport may be disrupted, or the event may need to move indoors. The organizing team writes a contingency memo. The memo does not announce that rain will happen. It does not instruct everyone to cancel. It does not treat a forecast as authority. Instead, it states the relevant possibilities, the decision points, the latest safe time for switching indoors, the communication channels, and the conditions under which the plan should be reviewed.

The example demonstrates the central difference between anticipation and prognosis. A prognosis-like version of the memo would say: “Bad weather is likely; therefore we must move indoors now.” A more disciplined anticipatory version says: “Bad weather is possible; this possibility matters under time pressure; here are the reversible preparations and review conditions that keep later action possible without forcing closure now.” The second version is still practical. It prepares. But it does not convert possibility into command.

The PMS Core hooks are substantial. **Δ** distinguishes outdoor continuation, indoor relocation, delay, and cancellation. **∇** appears as planning pressure because the event cannot remain unplanned until the last minute. **□** is the event-planning frame: safety, accessibility, communication, and feasibility for this particular event, not universal event policy. **Λ** is central: rain may not occur; transport disruption may not occur; the indoor option may not be needed; final information is absent. **Α** appears as a monitored risk: once the team writes a memo, one pathway may become administratively easier and start functioning as a default. **Ω** is conditional but relevant: organizers have more control over planning decisions than attendees, volunteers, or performers; some roles may bear more cost if communication is late. This does not authorize moral judgment, but it makes asymmetry visible. **Θ** is decisive because decision windows close: at some point, setup, communication, and access constraints make revision harder. **Φ** allows the memo to be reframed when weather information, transport updates, or venue constraints change. **Χ** prevents urgency from becoming premature action. **Σ** integrates weather uncertainty, logistics, communication, and timing into a usable but revisable structure. **Ψ** remains bounded: the team binds itself to review conditions and communication discipline, not to a fixed future.

The ANTICIPATION overlay is highly relevant. **ANT_min** is active because bounded future possibilities are carried inside a planning frame while Λ remains open under Θ and Χ. **ANT_to_planning_constraint** is active because the memo is downstream planning: it prepares possible action without authorizing action merely from possibility. **ANT_viable_full** remains weak-to-partial rather than fully active. The example has some Ω and some Ψ, but it still lacks full evidence, formal responsibility allocation, and a complete governance artefact. It should therefore not be treated as a complete anticipatory responsibility model.

The overlay also clarifies drift risks. Probability-action compulsion is a risk if “rain is possible” becomes “we must cancel now.” Preventive escalation is a risk if the team imposes an irreversible decision before Λ warrants it. **AD_A≫E** is also a possible risk in the opposite direction: the team might keep reviewing scenarios until action windows close. None of these is a finding. The example only shows how the risks can be made visible.

MIP, AHP, and QC are not active in this article example. If the venue had formal safety duties, public authority obligations, insurance constraints, or accessibility compliance requirements, then a downstream governance layer could be justified. AHP might become relevant if the memo itself were used as an analysis artefact requiring claim hardening. But in this bounded vignette, no governance validation is claimed.

Forbidden claims must remain explicit. The example does not determine what the venue should do. It does not judge the organizers as responsible or irresponsible. It does not create legal safety guidance. It does not validate a planning procedure. It does not convert a weather possibility into authority.

The non-capture note is significant. Risk management, event operations, accessibility planning, and legal compliance may all capture dimensions PMS-ANTICIPATION does not. A meteorological model may supply better forecast content. A venue safety protocol may supply binding operational rules. PMS-ANTICIPATION does not replace those frameworks; it clarifies the structural boundary between anticipatory preparation and closure-by-prognosis.

The article takeaway is that anticipation becomes structurally valid when it increases restraint and consequence-awareness before action. It becomes invalid when it uses the future as authority.

Across the three examples, the point is demonstrative rather than evidential. The travel route shows Λ-preserving orientation in a low-stakes setting; the modular syllabus shows provisional Σ without final fit-claims; the contingency memo shows how anticipation can structure preparedness without becoming prognosis or command. None of the examples proves that reversible planning works better, diagnoses any agent, assigns responsibility, or validates PMS. They are article-ready illustrations of the completed artefacts, not independent grounds for stronger claims.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The completed YAMLs preserve multiple rival readings. These are not defects. They are necessary to prevent PMS and ANTICIPATION from becoming closure machines.

A **decision-theoretic reading** would treat reversible planning as preservation of option value under uncertainty. This reading may capture formal trade-offs, expected value, information value, irreversibility, and timing in ways PMS does not quantify. It preserves the economic and strategic logic of waiting, staging, and maintaining alternatives. PMS can map the structure of option-preservation, but it does not calculate option value.

A **pragmatist reading** would treat planning as experimental action. On this view, reversibility is not primarily a structural operator issue but a mode of inquiry: act provisionally, observe consequences, revise. This preserves the role of feedback, learning, and situated correction. PMS can represent Φ and provisional Σ, but pragmatism may better capture the lived and practical logic of experimentation.

A **phenomenological reading** would treat anticipation as lived futurity, openness, projection, or temporal experience. This lies outside PMS by design. PMS does not analyze subjective time-consciousness, affective orientation, anxiety, hope, dread, or lived openness. The phenomenological rival preserves dimensions of experience that the PMS case intentionally does not claim.

A **systems-theory reading** would treat reversible planning as adaptive feedback, resilience, and non-lock-in within dynamic systems. This may better capture feedback loops, environmental coupling, homeostasis, path dependence, and adaptive control. PMS can describe attractor risk and recontextualization, but systems theory may provide sharper formal tools for feedback behavior.

A **design or engineering reading** would treat reversibility as modularity, rollback, versioning, staged deployment, option architecture, or recoverability. This preserves concrete implementation mechanisms that PMS does not supply. In a software or systems-design scene, the design reading may be more operationally useful than PMS.

An **ethical-political reading** would treat reversibility as protection against domination, imposed futures, irreversible institutional decisions, or exclusion of affected voices. This reading preserves plurality, democratic contestability, and power critique. PMS can map Ω and D constraints when a concrete scene supplies them, but the current case does not activate those structures strongly.

A **strategic foresight reading** would treat reversible planning as scenario planning, horizon scanning, preparedness, or portfolio orientation. This may preserve professional methods and institutional planning practices that ANTICIPATION only structurally constrains.

An **anti-capture reading** would insist that the very desire to formalize reversible planning risks turning openness into another managed object. This rival reading is especially important. It reminds AXIOM that mapping non-closure can itself become a closure practice if handled without Χ.

Each rival reading preserves something PMS or PMS-ANTICIPATION may not fully capture. The PMS reading is therefore strong only as a structural cartography, not as a total theory of planning.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The Core PMS reading would be weakened if the planning problem were specified as one where irreversible commitment is required from the outset. In emergency, triage, wartime command, or rapidly escalating safety contexts, closure may be structurally necessary even while tragic. Such cases would not automatically refute PMS, but they would weaken this specific reversible-planning reading unless the frame could show a genuine reversibility window.

The PMS reading would also be weakened if the problem were purely technical and fully specified, with no meaningful interpretive openness. If reversibility were simply a procedural rollback property, and all relevant states, costs, and transitions were formally defined, the PMS analysis might add little beyond design or engineering vocabulary.

The reading would be under-discriminating if it could not distinguish reversible planning from avoidance, indecision, endless reframing, technical rollback, decision-theoretic option preservation, ethical restraint, or democratic openness. A PMS formula that covers all of these too easily would be structurally elegant but analytically weak.

The reading would be over-assimilative if it treated resistance, rival framing, rupture, disagreement, unresolved absence, or missing evidence as PMS drift or incomplete calibration by default. This is a major falsifier for the spirit of the analysis. If PMS absorbs all non-fit as its own residue, it becomes a closure machine.

The Core reading would also weaken if Ω were later shown to be central but remained unmapped. For example, if a planning scene involved vulnerable affected parties, unequal exposure, authority over irreversible consequences, or role-based obligations, Ω could no longer remain weak. Responsibility_R and Dignity-in-Practice_D would need stronger treatment, and possibly MIP or AHP might become relevant.

The ANTICIPATION overlay would be weakened if the case were not genuinely anticipatory. If the planning problem did not involve possible future events or non-events, delayed commitment, future uncertainty, or irreversibility under Θ, the overlay would be unnecessary. PMS Core would suffice.

The overlay would also be weakened if anticipation were explicitly replaced by prognosis. If a later artefact supplied a statistically grounded forecast and the task was to evaluate evidentiary adequacy, predictive validity, or decision thresholds, ANTICIPATION would need to be subordinated to epistemic, statistical, or decision-theoretic analysis. Prognosis may inform anticipation, but it is not the same.

The overlay would be invalid if it authorized action by itself. ANTICIPATION explicitly rejects the conversion of probability into compulsion, prognosis into command, or possible future into authority. Any reading that used ANTICIPATION to say “therefore act now” would violate the overlay.

The overlay would also be invalid if Ψ were externalized. Anticipation binds the anticipator to restraint and consequence-handling. It does not bind others to comply with the anticipated future. If the overlay were used to impose obligations on unspecified agents, it would breach its own discipline.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. To move from conceptual case to applied analysis, one would need at least the following: a concrete planning scene; identified roles; affected parties; decision horizon; possible futures; criteria for lock-in; criteria for reversibility; evidence of planning behavior; timing of commitments; action windows; exposure and asymmetry mapping; data about costs of delay and costs of premature commitment; and explicit accountability procedures if the analysis informs practice.

For empirical validation, one would need observable indicators for operator activation: differentiated alternatives for Δ; directional planning pressure for ∇; declared planning frame for □; meaningful delayed commitments or non-events for Λ; recurrence or path-dependence evidence for Α; mapped asymmetry for Ω; time horizon and revision windows for Θ; source and target frames for Φ; reflective distance indicators for Χ; provisional integration criteria for Σ; and actual reversible commitments for Ψ.

For comparative validation, PMS would need to be compared against rival models using explicit criteria. Does PMS discriminate better than decision theory, systems theory, pragmatism, design theory, or phenomenology? Does ANTICIPATION add clarity beyond standard foresight methods? Without such comparison, superiority cannot be claimed.

For implementation, the YAML would need to be translated into an architecture, protocol, or decision process with testable constraints. The current artefact is not that. It is a structured analysis, not a working system.

MIP, AHP, QC, or QC-EXT would require a further escalation step that is not present in this case. MIP would require downstream governance relevance, responsibility allocation, institutional use, or guardrail-facing application. AHP would require second-order analysis-artifact hardening, such as explicit claim surfaces, attack points, counterfactual hooks, and revision mechanics; it would not rescore the case or activate person findings. QC or QC-EXT would require quality-control or extended review conditions around the artefact or its use-context, not merely a philosophically coherent PMS reading. None of these triggers is supplied by the current case.

For legal or clinical use, entirely different evidentiary and ethical requirements would apply. None are satisfied here.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It does not identify avoidant planners, indecisive agents, rigid actors, immature subjects, anxious decision-makers, or responsible persons. The presence of delay, distance, or non-closure is not evidence of pathology. The presence of commitment is not evidence of maturity.

It must not be used for person ranking. No person, group, institution, or planning style receives a dignity score, maturity score, rationality score, or competence ranking. Dignity-in-Practice is a constraint on analysis under asymmetry, not a measure of worth.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusion. The analysis does not determine negligence, foreseeability, liability, breach of duty, culpability, or evidentiary sufficiency. Reversible planning may matter in legal contexts, but this case is not a legal instrument.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. PMS does not prove that futures are open, that agency is free, that possibility has ontological status, or that self-binding constitutes identity. ANTICIPATION does not prove access to future truth.

It must not be used as empirical proof. The case does not show that reversible planning is effective, optimal, safer, more ethical, or more rational in real-world planning. Such claims require evidence.

It must not be used as implementation validation. A YAML artefact does not implement a planning system. The analysis does not provide a software specification, AI safety protocol, governance process, or organizational method.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. A coherent case rendering does not prove PMS. It only shows a bounded application.

It must not treat add-on constructs as PMS operators. `ANT_min`, `ANT_viable_full`, `ANT_to_planning_constraint`, prognosis contrast, and post-confirmation discipline are overlay handles. They do not revise Δ–Ψ.

It must not treat drift as verdict. AD_A>>E, AD_Sigma_low, preventive escalation, probability-action compulsion, and post-confirmation Χ erosion are risks. They are not findings in this case.

It must not produce closure by authority. Neither PMS Core nor ANTICIPATION may be used to declare that one plan, frame, future, or commitment is correct. The analysis preserves the conditions under which closure must remain provisional.

### Case conclusion

The integrated result is that reversible planning can be rendered as a **Θ-centered structural problem** in which future-orientation must remain intelligible without becoming final. PMS Core shows the base configuration: planning differentiates possible futures through Δ, takes on directional pressure through ∇, operates inside a declared frame □, carries meaningful not-yet structures as Λ, monitors lock-in as possible Α, treats Ω only as weak and conditional unless a concrete scene supplies asymmetry, depends centrally on Θ, remains revisable through Φ, preserves reflective distance through Χ, integrates only provisionally through Σ, and treats Ψ as bounded or conditional rather than achieved full self-binding. The Core reading therefore does not solve planning by closure. It shows which closures are structurally tempting, which must remain provisional, and where Λ-residue must stay visible.

The ANTICIPATION overlay adds a narrower and necessary clarification. It shows that reversible planning is not merely the preservation of options, but a present anticipatory stance that must carry possible future events and non-events under Θ without becoming prognosis. `ANT_min` is active because the case holds bounded future possibilities inside a frame while preserving Λ and Χ. `ANT_to_planning_constraint` is active because planning may proceed downstream from anticipation, but must not convert possibility, probability, concern, or coherence into command. `ANT_viable_full` remains weak because the abstract case does not establish full Ω, Α, Ψ, or Action_E. The overlay’s strongest contribution is therefore boundary discipline: anticipation must increase restraint and consequence-awareness before action, not provide authority for action.

The three examples contribute readability without functioning as evidence. The refundable route shows that reversible planning is not mere waiting: the not-yet can remain visible while practical orientation is maintained. The modular syllabus shows that provisional Σ can be strong enough to structure a plan without locking the future interpretation of the scene. The contingency memo shows the add-on distinction between anticipation and prognosis: a future possibility can guide preparation without becoming a command. Together, the examples also show why the article requires both PMS Core and the ANTICIPATION overlay: Core makes the operator structure legible, while the overlay prevents future-orientation from becoming prognosis, command, or action authorization. Each example demonstrates the analysis, but none proves empirical superiority, diagnoses an agent, assigns responsibility, activates MIP/AHP/QC, or validates PMS.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is shown. The article does not claim that reversible planning is always preferable, that commitment is suspicious, that delay is mature, or that action is premature. It does not establish a decision rule for when to act. It does not produce a legal, clinical, empirical, metaphysical, implementation, or governance result. It does not show that PMS captures all relevant aspects of planning. Decision theory, pragmatism, phenomenology, systems theory, design theory, strategic foresight, and ethical-political accounts may all preserve dimensions that PMS and ANTICIPATION do not fully capture.

The boundary discipline is therefore constitutive of the result. Risk is not finding. Weak Ω is not denial of possible asymmetry. Under-specified Action_E is not a low score. Non-assignment is not failure. Coherence is not proof. Drift-patterns are not verdicts. Dignity-in-Practice remains a praxeological application constraint, not an ontological ranking. ANTICIPATION terms remain overlay handles, not PMS operators. The analysis remains reversible because it refuses to treat its own structural coherence as final closure.

The final methodological takeaway is that planning without lock-in is not a plan with no commitments. It is a plan whose commitments remain explicitly staged, revisable, and non-authoritative while the relevant Λ-residue persists. AXIOM’s contribution is not to tell a planner what to do. It is to map where the closure-demand arises, where PMS Core can discipline it, where the ANTICIPATION overlay sharpens the future-oriented risks, and where the analysis must stop so that non-capture remains visible.
