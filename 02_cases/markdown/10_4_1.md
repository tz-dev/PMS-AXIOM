### Why this case matters

Consent under asymmetry is structurally difficult because it sits exactly where ordinary closure language becomes tempting and unsafe. Consent seems, at first glance, to demand a yes-or-no decision: consent was given or not given; refusal was clear or not clear; pressure was present or not present; asymmetry mattered or did not matter. The philosophical closure-demand is therefore powerful. The case asks whether agreement can count as consent when the conditions around agreement include pressure, dependency, vulnerability, unequal capacity, or unequal access to refusal and exit.

That demand is not merely semantic. It affects how agency, autonomy, responsibility, vulnerability, and legitimacy are interpreted. A surface agreement may be visible, but the conditions under which it was produced may remain under-specified. A refusal may be spoken, delayed, muted, constrained, punished, or unavailable. A person may formally have options while practically lacking safe exit. A dependency may be relevant without automatically eliminating agency. Vulnerability may matter without turning into a diagnosis. Unequal capacity may call for restraint without producing an immediate legal, clinical, metaphysical, or moral conclusion. The difficulty is that each of these distinctions has to remain alive at the same time.

The case belongs in AXIOM because AXIOM maps closure-demands rather than solving them by authority. Consent under asymmetry is a paradigmatic closure-demand: it asks for settlement under conditions where settlement itself can become coercive, reductive, or misleading. The case therefore tests whether PMS can hold a complex field open without dissolving it into vagueness. It must make the consent structure legible while not turning structural legibility into verdict.

The completed YAML analyses treat the case as a conceptual domain projection. That matters. No concrete scene is supplied. There is no named participant, no statement, no chronology, no institution, no evidentiary record, no medical or legal file, no sexual practice, no public dispute, and no documented cost distribution. The case material names a problem family: consent under asymmetry. It does not provide the kind of fact pattern that would allow empirical, legal, clinical, forensic, maturity-related, or implementation conclusions.

The philosophical value lies precisely there. This is not a case about whether a particular consent event was valid. It is a case about what must be structurally visible before such a judgment could even become responsible. PMS Core maps the consent-field. PMS-SEX shows which domain-specific questions would become relevant if the case were concretized as sexual praxis. MIP shows why maturity and responsibility language must remain working-note language only. AHP shows how even the analysis itself can become over-authoritative unless its attack surface is made explicit.

The case is not solved metaphysically because PMS does not decide what autonomy, will, agency, or personhood ultimately are. It is not solved empirically because no evidence is supplied. It is not solved clinically because no capacity assessment, trauma record, diagnosis, or therapeutic frame is present. It is not solved legally because no jurisdiction, doctrine, procedural setting, evidentiary threshold, or legal question is specified. It is not solved diagnostically because PMS, PMS-SEX, MIP, and AHP are not person-classification tools. It is not solved by AXIOM because AXIOM is a cartographic and indexing paper: it shows where structural closure is possible and where residue must remain visible.

Consent under asymmetry therefore matters because it is a test case for disciplined non-closure. It asks whether a model can be strong enough to discriminate and restrained enough not to adjudicate. It asks whether asymmetry can be made visible without becoming accusation; whether agreement can be distinguished from meaningful consent without denying agency; whether vulnerability can matter without becoming pathology; whether responsibility can be named without becoming authority; and whether a structured analysis can remain reversible under pressure to decide.

The case also matters because it tests the boundary between structural explanation and normative temptation. A weak model may collapse consent into words: if a person said yes, the analysis stops. A different weak model may collapse asymmetry into invalidity: if pressure or dependency is visible, the analysis stops. AXIOM refuses both collapses. It asks how agreement, refusal, asymmetry, temporality, frame, recontextualization, distance, integration, and self-binding relate, and where the available material does not support further closure.

That refusal is not indecision. It is the method. The case demonstrates that a structural model can be analytically substantial while remaining non-tribunal. It can name risks without turning them into findings. It can name missing evidence without treating absence as guilt or innocence. It can name asymmetry without transforming it into pathology. It can name non-capture without treating non-capture as failure. In this sense, the case is not only about consent. It is about whether an analytical system can preserve the dignity of unresolved structure.

The frame allows PMS to ask how these distinctions become structurally organized. It does not itself decide which frame would govern a real case.

Inside the frame are consent-relevant structural questions. What counts as agreement? What counts as refusal? Is refusal practically available? Does pressure affect the meaning of agreement? ### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading proceeds through all eleven operators in order. This order matters because later operators cannot be used cheaply. A consent analysis that jumps directly from visible agreement to self-binding, or from asymmetry to guilt, or from later contestation to invalidity, would bypass the dependency discipline that keeps the case reversible.

**Δ — Difference.**
Δ is active and carries major analytic weight. The case begins with distinctions: agreement versus consent, refusal versus silence, pressure versus coercion, dependency versus incapacity, vulnerability versus lack of agency, unequal capacity versus ordinary difference, given consent versus withheld or contested consent. Without Δ, the case collapses into vague concern about consent. With Δ, the case becomes discriminable.

The calibration issue is that these distinctions must not be collapsed. Agreement is not automatically meaningful consent. Pressure is not automatically coercion. Dependency is not automatically incapacity. Vulnerability is not automatically absence of agency. Later contestation is not automatically proof that original consent was invalid. Δ enables the distinctions but does not decide them.

The overclaim would be to treat the presence of a distinction as evidence for one side of it. Naming pressure does not establish coercion. Naming dependency does not establish non-consent. Naming contestation does not establish invalidity. Δ is the entry point, not the verdict.

**∇ — Impulse.**
∇ is active because consent under asymmetry contains directional tensions. There may be movement toward agreement, refusal, compliance, withdrawal, pressure-response, clarification, or contestation. The field is not static. Agreement may be sought; refusal may be difficult; dependency may orient action; vulnerability may intensify caution; pressure may redirect behavior.

The calibration issue is that ∇ is structural directedness, not inner desire. The analysis cannot infer motive, fear, intention, authentic preference, trauma, or subjective willingness from the abstract case. ∇ shows that the consent-field contains directional tension, but it does not reveal psychology.

The overclaim would be to read compliance as desire, refusal as resistance in a psychological sense, agreement as intention, or contestation as retrospective motive. PMS keeps ∇ structural.

**□ — Frame.**
□ is active and central. Consent is intelligible only within a frame that defines what counts as agreement, refusal, pressure, capacity, vulnerability, dependency, and contestation. The abstract frame here is consent under asymmetry. A later concrete case might specify legal, relational, sexual, institutional, clinical, contractual, communicative, or ethical frames. Those frames are not interchangeable.

The calibration issue is frame separation. Legal validity, ethical legitimacy, clinical capacity, institutional procedure, lived experience, and structural PMS analysis are different frames. A claim may be coherent in one and insufficient in another. PMS must not silently move between them.

The overclaim would be to treat the abstract consent frame as if it already contained legal doctrine, clinical assessment, empirical facts, or sexual scene details. □ makes the case legible, but it also limits what can be claimed.

**Λ — Non-Event.**
Λ is weak and conditional. It would become relevant if a concrete scene included meaningful silence, delayed refusal, absent opportunity to withdraw, missing confirmation, omitted safeguard, unresolved expectation, absent review, or unavailable exit. Such absences can matter deeply in consent cases. But the provided material does not establish any specific non-event.

The calibration issue is expectation. Λ requires an expected occurrence within a frame. Silence is not automatically consent, refusal, coercion, or non-event. Missing evidence is not automatically structured absence. A delayed response matters only if the frame establishes that a response was expected and that its absence changes the structure.

The overclaim would be to translate every absence, ambiguity, silence, or missing fact into Λ drift, incomplete integration, or hidden refusal. PMS preserves possible non-capture: sometimes absence means the model lacks evidence.

**Α — Attractor.**
Α is weak and conditional. It would mark recurrent patterning if pressure, dependency, vulnerability, compliance, refusal difficulty, frame-control, or contestation stabilized over time. In many real consent settings, recurrence and role-scripts matter. But this case gives no repetition or stabilized pattern.

The calibration issue is recurrence. A single abstract case-type is not evidence of attractor formation. PMS cannot infer a pattern from a possible pattern.

The overclaim would be to treat consent under asymmetry as already a habitual script, role fixation, coercive pattern, or stabilized dynamic. Α remains weak until a concrete sequence appears.

**Ω — Asymmetry.**
Ω is active and major. The case explicitly concerns asymmetry: power, dependency, vulnerability, capacity, obligation, exposure, information, role authority, or exit access may affect consent. This is the structural center of the case. A consent claim under asymmetry cannot be treated as if all role-positions bear equal cost, equal power, equal visibility, and equal exit capacity.

The calibration issue is direction and kind. What kind of asymmetry is at stake? Power? Dependency? Vulnerability? Unequal capacity? Information? Obligation? Public exposure? Frame control? The case does not specify. Ω is therefore active as a conceptual operator, but its concrete profile is under-specified.

The dependency risk is important: in PMS, Ω depends on Α. Since Α is weak, Ω is conceptually active from the case material but dependency-weak as a fully developed chain. This does not invalidate the reading; it marks that the abstract case names asymmetry without establishing stabilized asymmetrical patterning.

The overclaim would be to moralize Ω. Asymmetry is not automatically coercion, abuse, invalidity, guilt, or blame. It is a structural imbalance that requires calibration.

**Θ — Temporality.**
Θ is structurally important and under-specified. Consent unfolds over time. It may be given, withheld, delayed, revised, withdrawn, pressured, repeated, contested, or recontextualized. Dependency and vulnerability may accumulate. Exit may become more costly. A later contestation may alter the frame in which earlier agreement is read.

The calibration issue is chronology. PMS cannot infer temporal sequence without scene evidence. It cannot know whether pressure preceded agreement, whether dependency accumulated, whether refusal was delayed, whether withdrawal was available, or whether contestation came after changed conditions.

The overclaim would be to treat the case as a temporal trajectory merely because consent can be temporal. Θ is necessary as a calibration point, not established as a concrete timeline.

**Φ — Recontextualization.**
Φ is active. The same surface occurrence may be read differently under different frames: agreement may be meaningful consent, compliance, pressure-response, dependency-preserving action, constrained refusal, ambiguity, or later-contested enactment. Recontextualization is unavoidable because consent under asymmetry is not exhausted by one frame.

The calibration issue is source and target frame. What is being recontextualized into what? From verbal agreement to meaningful consent? From refusal to constrained refusal? From private interaction to legal evaluation? From autonomy to vulnerability? PMS must name the frame shift rather than simply multiply interpretations.

The overclaim would be to absorb every resistance, rupture, rival reading, or missing fact into Φ. If every disagreement becomes recontextualization, PMS becomes too smooth and loses discriminative force.

**Χ — Distance.**
Χ is active as both operator and application constraint. It is the main guard against closure. Χ requires the analysis to pause before verdict, diagnosis, legal conclusion, person-ranking, sexualization, maturity judgment, public accusation, or coercive interpretation.

The calibration issue is that distance is not empty neutrality. Χ does not mean refusing to distinguish. It means distinguishing without premature closure. It allows the analysis to say that asymmetry matters while refusing to decide what asymmetry proves.

The overclaim or failure would occur if Χ were suspended to force a consent verdict, moral ranking, legal finding, diagnostic attribution, or adultness judgment. Without Χ, the analysis would no longer be a valid PMS application.

**Σ — Integration.**
Σ is weak and conditional. It would be relevant if agreement, refusal, pressure, dependency, vulnerability, capacity, frame, temporality, and asymmetry were integrated into a coherent enactment. The abstract case does not establish such integration.

The calibration issue is that coherence is not proof. A PMS analysis can make the case structurally coherent, but that does not mean consent was coherent, valid, invalid, responsible, mature, or integrated. The analyst’s coherence must not be mistaken for the scene’s integration.

The overclaim would be forced coherence: smoothing disagreement, ambiguity, absence, rival framing, or lack of evidence into one elegant PMS story. Σ must remain weak unless scene evidence shows integrated enactment.

**Ψ — Self-Binding.**
Ψ is weak for the scene and active only as analytic self-binding discipline. In a concrete consent case, Ψ would require evidence that consent becomes a self-binding commitment across time, or that responsibility is carried as stable self-commitment under asymmetry. The case supplies no such evidence.

The calibration issue is dependency: Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. Since Σ and Θ are under-specified at scene level, Ψ cannot be claimed for participants. Agreement alone is not self-binding. Silence is not self-binding. Abstract consent language is not self-binding.

The overclaim would be to infer authentic will, identity commitment, maturity, responsibility, or person-level selfhood from agreement. PMS treats Ψ as structurally expensive. Here it binds the analysis to restraint; it does not bind any participant.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The dependency discipline is one of the most important outcomes of the Core YAML. PMS prevents the analysis from using later operators as shortcuts. Consent under asymmetry is especially vulnerable to shortcut reasoning: agreement becomes consent; asymmetry becomes coercion; dependency becomes incapacity; vulnerability becomes diagnosis; refusal difficulty becomes proof of harm; later contestation becomes proof of invalidity; responsibility becomes blame; coherence becomes truth; self-binding becomes authentic will.

PMS blocks these shortcuts by requiring operator dependencies. Ω cannot be fully developed without attention to patterning. Θ cannot be asserted without temporal sequence. Φ must identify frame shifts rather than absorb all non-fit. Χ must remain active before integration. Σ requires distance and recontextualization; it cannot be declared merely because the analyst produced a coherent reading. Ψ requires Σ, Θ, and Χ; it cannot be inferred from surface agreement or abstract consent language.

This matters most for Σ and Ψ. Σ remains conditional because no integrated enactment is shown. The analysis can integrate the conceptual problem, but that does not establish integrated consent. Ψ remains conditional because no self-binding trajectory is shown. Consent may sometimes involve self-binding, but this case does not prove it.

Coherence is not proof. The YAML explicitly marks coherence risk as high because the case fits PMS very well. Consent under asymmetry naturally activates Δ, ∇, □, Ω, Θ, Φ, and Χ. That fit is useful, but it is also dangerous. If PMS makes everything appear structurally composable, the reading can become too smooth. Every ambiguity could become Φ, every absence Λ, every instability weak Σ, every later responsibility question Ψ, every rival interpretation incomplete calibration. That would be over-assimilation.

Non-capture is not failure. PMS does not need to capture subjective experience, legal validity, clinical capacity, trauma, cultural power, political domination, institutional procedure, or evidentiary sufficiency in order to be useful. Those may remain outside PMS or require rival frameworks. A responsible PMS reading preserves such non-capture. It does not redescribe everything that resists PMS as drift, immaturity, incomplete integration, or calibration residue.

PMS must not become a closure machine. Its strength here lies in showing which closures are possible, which are conditional, and which would overreach. It does not solve consent under asymmetry by declaring an answer. It maps the structures that any responsible answer would have to respect.

Invalid dependency shortcuts would include: using Ω to infer coercion without scene evidence; using Θ to infer accumulated pressure without chronology; using Φ to absorb all rival readings; using Χ as an excuse for non-judgment when concrete protection is needed; using Σ to declare consent coherent because the analysis is coherent; using Ψ to infer self-binding from agreement; using Dignity-in-Practice to rank persons; using drift-patterns as verdicts; or treating the PMS chain as validation of PMS Base.

The calibration discipline is therefore not merely technical. It is ethical in the limited praxeological sense that the analysis must not burden the case with claims the material cannot carry. It must not use operator names to decorate assumptions. It must not give the appearance of formal proof where only structural mapping has been performed. The article itself must remain bound by the same rule: if the YAMLs did not establish a concrete scene, the prose cannot smuggle one in.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core analysis identifies drift-pattern relevance without turning drift into a finding. This is crucial. Consent under asymmetry is a case-type in which drift language is tempting: one may want to say that unequal capacity automatically suppresses action, that vulnerability automatically lowers coherence, that resistance automatically marks maturity failure, or that disagreement automatically indicates incomplete integration. The completed YAMLs explicitly resist this. Drift is handled as conditional structural risk, not as diagnosis, verdict, score, or hidden person classification.

The first PMS asymmetry drift-pattern is **AD_A>>E**. In broad terms, this pattern concerns a relation in which awareness or reflective comprehension becomes over-expanded while effective action remains suppressed, delayed, or structurally unavailable. For consent under asymmetry, this is relevant because a role-position may understand the situation, recognize pressure, or know the formal consent frame while still lacking realistic power to refuse, pause, withdraw, contest, or exit. However, the abstract case does not establish this pattern as a finding. It does not show that any role-position had high awareness and low action capacity. It does not show chronic delay, suppression, or incapacity to enact refusal. Therefore the AD_A>>E axis effects remain under-specified: awareness may be over-expanded in some concrete consent-asymmetry scenes, and action may be suppressed or chronically delayed in some concrete scenes, but neither is established here.

The second PMS asymmetry drift-pattern is **AD_Sigma_low**. This pattern concerns low or unstable integration, where coherence and responsibility do not stabilize into reliable action or self-binding. It is relevant because consent under asymmetry often raises questions about whether the frame, roles, refusal conditions, capacity conditions, and responsibility distribution are integrated enough for a consent claim to be meaningful. Still, the completed YAMLs do not treat weak Σ as a failure finding. Σ is weak because the case is abstract. No integrated enactment is supplied. No role-position, speech act, cost distribution, review process, or self-binding trajectory is described. The AD_Sigma_low axis effects therefore remain conditional: coherence could be volatile and responsibility could be inconsistent in a concrete scene, but the current case does not prove either.

The coherence risk is explicitly marked as high because the case fits PMS too well. Consent under asymmetry naturally invites Δ distinctions, ∇ directional tensions, □ framing, Ω asymmetry, Θ temporality, Φ recontextualization, and Χ restraint. This fit can become analytically productive, but it can also become over-smooth. A bad PMS reading would make every ambiguity into Λ, every frame shift into Φ, every absence into non-event, every under-specified integration into Σ failure, every contested consent claim into maturity weakness, and every asymmetry into drift. The YAMLs prevent that by keeping the distinction between risk and finding visible.

Under-discrimination is the main danger. If the analysis cannot distinguish agreement from meaningful consent, pressure from coercion, dependency from incapacity, vulnerability from absence of agency, silence from refusal, later contestation from original invalidity, or structural asymmetry from legal conclusion, then it becomes too broad to be useful. A formally coherent PMS chain would not save it. The reading must discriminate, not merely absorb.

Non-assignment is not a low score. When the YAMLs do not assign a drift, derived structure, A-score, M-score, D-profile, or IA verdict, they are not saying that the case scores low. They are saying that the necessary evidence is absent. This is a major discipline point across all layers. Under-specification is not failure. It is the responsible result when the case is conceptual rather than concrete.

The drift-pattern result can therefore be stated precisely: the case is drift-sensitive but not drift-diagnosed. PMS can say which drift risks would have to be watched in a later concrete scene, but it cannot say that such drift is present. This preserves both analytical force and boundary discipline.

### Layer interaction

The layers interact by division of labor, not by escalation into a verdict. Each layer adds a different kind of visibility, and each layer also adds a different kind of restraint. The stack is therefore not cumulative in the sense of moving from weak claim to strong claim. It is cumulative in the sense of making more boundary conditions visible.

PMS Core remains the base structural reading. It supplies the canonical Δ–Ψ grammar, operator order, dependency discipline, derived structures, drift-pattern caution, non-capture, and boundary language. The Core finding remains: the case is an Ω-active, □-dependent, Φ-contestable, Χ-governed, Θ-under-specified consent structure, with Δ and ∇ active, Λ/Α/Σ/Ψ conditional, derived structures not independently established, and non-capture preserved. This is the only layer that carries PMS Base grammar. All later layers must remain subordinate to it.

PMS-SEX sharpens what PMS Core alone does not isolate. It adds the conditional domain question: if this consent-asymmetry structure is later specified as sexual praxis, what additional cost, exposure, publicness, body, exit, binding, and responsibility conditions must be reconstructed? It makes sexual cost topology, body remainder, publicness overlay, asymmetry-switch drift, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, and adult sexual reciprocity visible as possible calibration requirements. It does not decide that the case is sexual, and it does not redefine PMS operators. Its strongest contribution is not a conclusion but a warning against false equivalence: formal parity, mutual language, or private internal framing may not be sufficient where embodied, reputational, publicness-related, exit-related, or responsibility-related costs distribute asymmetrically.

MIP adds downstream working-note governance. It asks whether maturity-in-practice, responsibility, IA, and D language can be responsibly used on the basis of the available artefact. Its answer is mostly negative: no A-score, no M-score, no IA verdict, no D-profile, no adultness verdict, no maturity ranking, no responsibility finding, and no transmission as decision basis. This is not an omission. It is the correct result of the case’s abstract status. MIP contributes by showing what would have to be known before maturity language could become responsible: role-position, knowledge, power-to-act, predictability, alternatives, serious integration attempts, transparency, justification, time-boundedness, reversibility, publicness, and safe conditions for any D-module activation.

AHP adds second-order artefact hardening. It does not analyze the case or rescore anything. It examines the MIP output’s precision and vulnerabilities. It identifies where the analysis could be misread, over-transmitted, or converted into authority. Its high precision result applies to the analysis artefact, not to the evidentiary strength of the underlying case. AHP therefore protects the article from a subtle misuse: the more complete and formal the analysis looks, the more easily it can be mistaken for a decision-ready assessment. AHP hardens that boundary without changing the MIP result.

What remains PMS Core is the operator chain, dependencies, derived structures, and structural claim status. What PMS-SEX sharpens is sexuality-specific visibility under a conditional overlay. What MIP sharpens is responsibility, score hygiene, D restraint, IA non-verdict status, and transmission discipline. What AHP sharpens is artefact-level attack surface: precision is not evidence, working-note status is not transmission authority, and formal completeness is not decision-readiness.

Overlay language could overreach at several points. PMS-SEX could over-sexualize the case. Sexual cost topology could be misread as a finding rather than a calibration interface. Adult sexual reciprocity could be misread as an adultness verdict. Parity-to-exit and responsibility-to-authority could be misread as accusations. MIP could be misread as a maturity assessment. AHP precision could be misread as evidentiary strength. Each layer therefore has to be read with its own firewall: PMS Core is structural grammar; PMS-SEX is overlay visibility; MIP is guarded working-note governance; AHP is analysis-artifact hardening.

Non-closure remains visible because no layer fills the missing scene. The stack does not culminate in judgment. It culminates in disciplined non-transmission: the analysis is structurally rich, but the case remains abstract. This is not a weak ending. It is the central methodological result of the layered reading.

### Example 1 — Formal agreement in a dependency frame

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “PMS Core structural reading,” before “PMS dependency and calibration discipline.”

A junior researcher is asked by the director of a small institute to present preliminary findings at a public event. The request is framed as an opportunity: the director says it would be “good exposure” and adds that “of course, it is your choice.” The researcher says yes. No threat is made. No explicit penalty is mentioned. The director does not say that funding, recommendation letters, future contracts, or access to data depend on acceptance. Still, the researcher is on a short contract, depends on the director’s recommendation, and knows that declining visible requests has previously made others seem “not committed.” Later, when the presentation receives criticism, the researcher says the agreement did not feel meaningfully free.

This example demonstrates why the case cannot be closed by the presence of agreement alone. The “yes” is structurally relevant, but it is not sufficient by itself to settle whether consent was meaningfully given. At the same time, the dependency structure does not automatically prove coercion, invalidity, exploitation, or blame. The point is not that the director acted wrongly, nor that the researcher lacked agency. The point is that consent under asymmetry requires more discrimination than the surface contrast between yes and no.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ** distinguishes agreement from meaningful consent, opportunity from pressure, dependency from incapacity, and criticism after the fact from proof that the original agreement was invalid. **∇** appears as directional tension: the director’s request, the researcher’s wish to remain in good standing, the pull of career opportunity, and the possible impulse to avoid refusal. **□** is the institutional research frame: contracts, recommendations, reputation, visibility, and implicit expectations shape what the agreement means. **Λ** remains conditional. If the institute had an expected refusal channel and none was offered, or if silence around consequences carried meaning, Λ could become relevant. But absence of an explicit threat is not automatically a PMS Non-Event. **Α** is also conditional: prior cases of people being treated as “not committed” may suggest a possible attractor, but the vignette does not give enough evidence to establish a stabilized pattern.

**Ω** is active because the relation includes dependency, role authority, and possible future costs. Yet Ω must be calibrated. The asymmetry may be real without being coercive in a legal sense. **Θ** is relevant because the agreement cannot be read only at the instant of saying yes: prior institute culture, contract duration, future recommendations, public criticism, and later contestation all matter. But the example still does not provide enough chronology for a full temporal trajectory. **Φ** is active because the same event can be recontextualized: as opportunity, institutional expectation, pressure-response, professional development, or contested consent. **Χ** is required to prevent the analysis from becoming accusation or exoneration. **Σ** remains conditional: we do not yet know whether the request, dependency, refusal options, and later criticism were integrated into a coherent institutional practice. **Ψ** cannot be inferred from the researcher’s yes. Saying yes to the presentation is not automatically a self-binding commitment to absorb all future consequences.

No add-on hook is necessary for PMS-SEX here unless the example is later moved into a domain involving sexual praxis, bodily exposure, sexual cost topology, or public intimacy. As written, it is deliberately non-sexual. This helps the article show that consent under asymmetry is structurally broader than sexual consent while still preparing the reader for why PMS-SEX may later become relevant in domain-specific contexts.

MIP status remains working-note only. The vignette does not warrant an A-score, M-score, IA verdict, D-profile, adultness judgment, or maturity ranking. The IA-box would ask whether the asymmetry was transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible: did the researcher know the real stakes, was the request justified by a legitimate institutional purpose, was refusal reviewable, and were exit or repair options available? But these questions are not yet answered. The AHP relevance is indirect: a formal write-up of this vignette could easily sound more decisive than the evidence allows, so any analysis artefact would need to mark what is known, inferred, and unknown.

The forbidden claims are clear. This example does not prove coercion. It does not prove valid consent. It does not diagnose dependency, fear, ambition, immaturity, or manipulation. It does not make a legal or HR finding. It does not rank the director or researcher. It does not show that asymmetry always invalidates agreement. It also does not show that formal agreement is enough.

The non-capture note is important. The researcher’s lived experience may not be captured by the structural map. Legal standards may treat the case differently. Institutional ethics may require additional inquiry. A labor-rights frame, a relational autonomy frame, or an organizational culture analysis may preserve aspects that PMS only marks as requiring calibration.

The article takeaway is that PMS Core makes the consent field more legible without closing it. The example shows why Δ, Ω, Θ, Φ, and Χ carry major weight, and why Σ and Ψ must remain conditional. The analysis becomes stronger by refusing the two easy closures: “they said yes, so consent is settled,” and “there was dependency, so consent is impossible.”

### Example 2 — Conditional sexual cost topology without a sexual finding

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Add-on / overlay reading,” especially after the discussion of sexual cost topology and PMS-SEX reduced signatures.

Two adults are in a private relationship. One suggests making a private intimate exchange more visible within a small social circle, not by sharing explicit material, but by allowing others to know that the relationship has a non-standard dynamic. The other agrees at the time. Both describe the decision as mutual. Later, one partner says that the agreement carried different consequences for each of them: one could treat the disclosure as playful and reversible, while the other faced reputational exposure, family pressure, and a much higher cost of withdrawal. The original discussion did not name those different costs.

This example is intentionally bounded. It does not describe explicit sexual content, does not provide arousal-oriented detail, and does not instruct anyone how to manage such a situation. It is included only to show why PMS-SEX may be relevant as an overlay when consent under asymmetry becomes entangled with intimacy, publicness, role visibility, and unequal consequence distribution. The example does not turn the article’s abstract case into a sexual case; rather, it demonstrates what would have to be reconstructed if a concrete sexual-praxis scene were supplied.

The PMS Core hooks remain primary. **Δ** distinguishes mutual agreement from equal cost, private meaning from public readability, and disclosure from consent to all future consequences. **∇** appears in the pull toward visibility, recognition, play, intimacy, or shared identity, as well as the later pull toward withdrawal or contestation. **□** is the relationship frame, but it begins to extend toward a publicness frame once others become aware. **Λ** may become relevant if certain expected safeguards were absent: no explicit discussion of reputational cost, no later review point, no withdrawal pathway, no clarification of what “mutual” covers. But these absences must be established as expectations within the frame; they cannot simply be assumed. **Α** would require recurrence: repeated disclosures, repeated pressure, or a stabilized role-script. The vignette gives a possible pattern but not enough to assert one.

**Ω** is active because consequences may distribute asymmetrically. One role-position may bear higher reputational cost, family cost, exit cost, or publicness cost. **Θ** is active because the effects of disclosure may sediment over time: what seemed reversible in private may become difficult to undo once others know. **Φ** is central: the same agreement may be recontextualized from “mutual playful disclosure” to “unequal public exposure” or “unmapped cost topology.” **Χ** is necessary to avoid accusation, moral panic, or dismissal. **Σ** remains conditional: the question is whether internal meaning, outer legibility, publicness, and cost distribution were integrated. **Ψ** cannot be inferred from the initial agreement. Consent to one disclosure does not automatically bind a person to all later public interpretations.

The PMS-SEX hooks are the reason for the example. The overlay does not add new PMS operators. It sharpens the question of **sexual cost topology**: which costs attach where, which are reversible, which sediment under Θ, which are publicness-related, which are reputational, which are exit-related, and which can be externalized through mutuality language. The example also illustrates **inner / outer / public layer separation**. Internally, the disclosure may have been framed as trust, play, intimacy, or mutual recognition. Externally, it may become readable as a marked relationship form. Publicly, even a small social circle may create reputational sedimentation. These layers overlap but must not be collapsed.

The overlay also shows why **parity-to-exit** is a risk lens, not a finding. If one partner says “we both agreed, so no one owes anything,” parity language could potentially function as an exit from responsibility under unequal cost topology. But the example does not establish that this happened. The phrase “mutual” may be sincere, partially accurate, or insufficiently calibrated. Similarly, **responsibility-to-authority** is only a risk: if one partner later says, “because I was protecting you, I get to decide how this is interpreted,” responsibility may drift toward frame control. But that is not stated in the vignette. **Asymmetry-switch drift** also remains a risk only: it would require unmarked, repeatable asymmetry activation without Σ/Ψ re-binding.

MIP status remains non-scoring. No A-band or M-band should be assigned. The example does not show what either person knew, could predict, could refuse, could revise, or could realistically exit. The IA-box would ask whether the publicness asymmetry was transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. Were the reputational costs named? Was the disclosure bounded? Was later withdrawal possible? Was there a review point? These are necessary questions, not answered findings. D-module activation remains inappropriate unless the scene is safely bounded and D-language would clarify rather than shame.

AHP status is relevant because the example is high-risk for over-reading. A polished analysis could easily sound like it is judging the relationship. AHP would require a warning: this example is an analysis of structural conditions, not evidence of wrongdoing, immaturity, coercion, or dignity violation.

Forbidden claims must be stated plainly. The example does not say the disclosure was wrong. It does not deny that consent may have been real. It does not say that non-standard intimacy is immature, pathological, deviant, or undignified. It does not classify identities, orientations, or practices. It does not make a legal or clinical claim. It does not treat publicness as public punishment license. It does not turn sexual cost topology into a score.

The non-capture note is substantial. The partners’ lived meanings may exceed the structural map. A queer-theoretical, relational, legal, cultural, or phenomenological account might interpret the disclosure differently. Internal framing may be real even where it is not total. Publicness may matter without being the origin of the scene. The overlay should not erase erotic meaning, relational trust, cultural context, or agency.

The article takeaway is that PMS-SEX is useful precisely when it remains conditional. It does not decide the scene. It shows what must be visible before consent under sexualized asymmetry can be responsibly interpreted: not only agreement, but cost topology, exit realism, publicness, temporal sedimentation, frame control, and non-externalization of unequal burdens.

### Example 3 — When a careful analysis artefact becomes too authoritative

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “MIP-AHP / analysis-artifact hardening,” before “Layer interaction” or as the final example before the article’s later conclusion.

A research group prepares a structured internal note about a disputed consent situation. The note is careful: it says no legal conclusion is reached, no diagnosis is made, no A-score or M-score is assigned, and the D-module is not activated. It repeatedly states that the material is under-specified. It marks the output as a working note. Nevertheless, because the note is long, formally organized, and written in technical language, a manager later forwards it as if it were an expert assessment. The receiving committee reads the structure as authority. Even without explicit scores, the presence of headings such as asymmetry, responsibility, dignity protection, and drift risks creates the impression that the situation has already been evaluated.

This example demonstrates why the AHP layer matters. It is not about the original consent case. It is about the analysis artefact itself. Even a well-guarded MIP working note can become risky when its form is mistaken for decision-readiness. Formal precision can create authority-pressure. A structured document can be misused even when its content says “do not use this as a verdict.”

The PMS Core hooks are indirect but important. **Δ** distinguishes analysis precision from evidentiary strength, working note from decision basis, structural risk from finding, and under-specification from low score. **∇** appears in the institutional pull toward closure: the committee wants a usable assessment, the manager wants a document that organizes uncertainty, and the technical form seems to move the case toward decision. **□** is the transmission frame: the same note means one thing inside an internal analytic workspace and something else when forwarded to a committee. **Λ** may become relevant if the note lacks a prominent non-transmission banner or if review conditions are expected but absent. **Α** could become relevant if such notes are repeatedly reused as quasi-verdicts. **Ω** is active because document authority, expertise, managerial power, and affected-party vulnerability may be unevenly distributed. **Θ** matters because the note’s effects may sediment: once forwarded, it may influence records, reputations, or decisions. **Φ** is central because the artefact is recontextualized from working note to institutional evidence. **Χ** is the needed restraint: pause before transmission, preserve context, and prevent the document from becoming a closure machine. **Σ** would require integration of evidence status, use boundary, review process, and institutional decision rules. **Ψ** would require the institution to bind itself to the note’s limits rather than merely cite them.

PMS-SEX may be relevant only if the underlying case involved sexual praxis, but this example does not require that. Its main add-on relevance is negative: if PMS-SEX terms such as sexual cost topology, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, or adult sexual reciprocity appeared in the note, they would need especially strong boundary language. Such terms are rhetorically powerful. A committee could misread them as findings even when marked as risk lenses. The overlay’s guardrails therefore reinforce the AHP concern: technical handles must not become accusation, identity classification, or maturity verdict.

MIP status is the center of the example. The note should remain a working note. It should not assign A-score or M-score. It should not activate D-profile. It should not issue IA verdict. It should not become adultness judgment, responsibility finding, disciplinary recommendation, HR assessment, legal advice, clinical interpretation, or public communication. If transmitted at all, it would require explicit review conditions, scope explanation, and domain separation.

AHP status is fully active in this example, but only as analysis-artifact hardening. AHP would identify attack points: working-note status may be lost in reuse; formal precision may be mistaken for evidence; unknown may be mistaken for known absent; technical risk handles may be over-read as findings; D-language may invite dignity misreadings; and the document’s completeness may create institutional authority-pressure. The hardening actions would include a top-level non-transmission banner, a distinction between analysis precision and evidentiary strength, explicit “risk lens not finding” notices, staged evidence requirements before any IA or MIP assessment, and a rule that the document cannot be used as a decision basis without further review.

The forbidden claims are the lesson. The example does not prove that structured analysis is harmful. It does not say institutions should avoid formal notes. It does not say that careful models are manipulative. It does not diagnose managers or committees. It does not infer bad faith. It does not rank the analyst, manager, or committee. It does not make any legal claim about process. It shows only that an analysis artefact has its own transmission risks.

The non-capture note is also important. Institutional practice may have legal requirements, procedural duties, risk-management obligations, or safeguarding responsibilities that PMS, MIP, and AHP do not replace. A committee may need to act under uncertainty. AHP does not prohibit action; it prevents a working note from being misrepresented as stronger than it is. Other frameworks may govern what must happen next.

The article takeaway is that precision is not innocence. A formally precise analysis can still become dangerous if transmitted outside its scope. The AHP layer therefore protects the case, the affected parties, and the model itself: it keeps structural analysis from becoming hidden adjudication, and it keeps under-specification visible rather than allowing the document’s form to close what its content leaves open.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The completed YAMLs preserve non-capture across several domains. PMS and PMS-SEX do not claim to exhaust the phenomenon of consent under asymmetry. MIP and AHP do not convert non-capture into weakness. Rival readings are not defects; they may preserve aspects of the case that the PMS stack does not capture.

An **epistemological rival reading** might focus on what can be known about consent, refusal, pressure, dependency, vulnerability, or capacity. It would ask about evidence, testimony, credibility, information access, and uncertainty. PMS can map structural under-specification, but it does not supply epistemic warrant criteria for real cases. An epistemological framework may preserve evidentiary nuance that PMS leaves as operationalization requirement.

A **phenomenological rival reading** might focus on lived experience: what it is like to agree under pressure, to hesitate, to feel unable to refuse, to experience dependency, to later reinterpret an event, or to inhabit ambiguous agency. PMS does not claim access to subjective experience. PMS-SEX explicitly refuses to infer desire, fear, authenticity, erotic meaning, or inner framing from the abstract case. Phenomenology may preserve the first-person texture that structural mapping cannot capture.

A **language-practice or ordinary-language reading** might focus on how words like “consent,” “agreement,” “pressure,” “refusal,” “capacity,” and “coercion” function in actual communicative practices. It may attend to speech acts, uptake, conversational norms, silence, implication, and pragmatic context. PMS identifies frame-dependence and recontextualization, but it does not replace a detailed account of language use.

An **ethical rival reading** might focus on autonomy, care, vulnerability, exploitation, trust, responsibility, or respect. PMS can map asymmetry, burden distribution, and Dignity-in-Practice constraints, but it does not itself decide what ought to be done. Care ethics, relational autonomy, or exploitation theory may preserve normative substance that PMS intentionally does not supply.

A **legal rival reading** might apply doctrines of consent, coercion, undue influence, capacity, evidentiary standards, or procedural rights. PMS cannot substitute for that. Legal analysis may decide questions that PMS leaves outside scope, provided a concrete case and jurisdiction are supplied.

A **clinical or capacity-oriented rival reading** might address impairment, trauma, developmental capacity, coercive control, or psychological constraint. PMS and PMS-SEX do not diagnose. Such a reading would require appropriate expertise and evidence.

A **political or domination-analysis reading** might focus on structural power, gender, class, institution, race, disability, economic dependency, or social hierarchy. PMS can model Ω asymmetry but does not supply the sociological or historical content of domination. Political analysis may preserve macro-structures that the abstract case does not specify.

An **anti-capture reading** might insist that consent under asymmetry cannot be adequately captured by formal operator grammar at all, because the relevant phenomenon is lived, historical, affective, legal, or political in a way that resists structural translation. The completed YAMLs leave that possibility open. Refusal of PMS terms is not treated as drift, immaturity, or denial.

The three examples also preserve non-capture. The institutional research example leaves labor law, organizational ethics, and lived experience open. The sexual cost topology example leaves erotic meaning, relational trust, cultural context, and legal analysis open. The AHP example leaves institutional procedure and safeguarding obligations open. None of the examples becomes evidence for the case. They demonstrate structural distinctions without replacing the case analysis.

This is important because rival readings do not weaken AXIOM’s purpose. AXIOM is not trying to win a jurisdictional battle against law, phenomenology, ethics, sociology, or clinical expertise. It is mapping where a PMS reading can clarify structure and where other frameworks may be necessary. The presence of rivals is therefore a sign of boundary discipline, not a defect.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

Several conditions would weaken the PMS Core reading. The reading would weaken if a concrete case showed that asymmetry was irrelevant to the consent frame, that agreement and consent were not being contested, that refusal and withdrawal were fully available and not structurally burdened, or that a rival framework captured the relevant distinctions more discriminately. It would also weaken if PMS failed to distinguish agreement, consent, compliance, coercion, refusal, silence, capacity, vulnerability, and contestation in practice.

The PMS reading would become under-discriminating if it treated all asymmetry as the same. Pressure, dependency, vulnerability, unequal capacity, authority, exposure, publicness, and exit cost are not identical. A reading that collapses them into a generic Ω marker would not be strong enough.

It would become over-assimilative if every disagreement, rupture, non-fit, missing fact, rival framing, or unresolved absence were translated into PMS drift, Λ, Φ, weak Σ, or incomplete calibration. Non-capture must remain possible.

The add-on reading would weaken if the case were clearly non-sexual and there were no reason to prepare sexual-praxis calibration questions. It would also weaken if PMS-SEX language began to dominate the Core reading, turning a general consent-asymmetry case into a sexual scene without evidence. The overlay is justified only as conditional visibility, not as a finding.

The PMS-SEX reading would overreach if sexual cost topology were mapped without sexual role-positions, if body remainder were asserted without bodily consequences, if publicness were asserted without documentation or circulation, if adult sexual reciprocity were treated as adultness, or if parity-to-exit and responsibility-to-authority were treated as accusations.

The MIP reading would weaken if it assigned scores without evidence. It would also weaken if it treated the working note as decision basis. Its strength depends on refusing A/M bands, D activation, IA verdict, and transmission status until scene data exist.

The AHP reading would weaken if its high Precision Heuristic were interpreted as high case evidentiary strength. AHP hardens the analysis artefact. It does not make the underlying case more decidable.

The full stack would be falsified or require major revision if concrete evidence showed that the abstract consent-asymmetry frame was the wrong frame, that no meaningful asymmetry was involved, that consent was not the relevant issue, that the central dispute was legal capacity, clinical impairment, institutional procedure, or political domination in a way PMS did not capture, or that a rival framework delivered greater discriminative force without PMS’s operator structure.

Stronger claims would require substantial operationalization. A concrete scene would need actors or role-positions, words or acts, chronology, evidence, dependency facts, vulnerability facts, capacity facts, pressure indicators, refusal or withdrawal conditions, exit channels, publicness conditions, institutional context, legal frame if relevant, clinical frame if relevant, and rival-framework comparison.

For PMS-SEX, stronger claims would require sexual scene specification, internal framing, outer legibility, embodied consequences, sexual cost topology, publicness channels, exit costs, binding conditions, asymmetry activation, parity rhetoric, responsibility handling, Σ coordination, Ψ rebinding, and D-preserving accountability.

For MIP, stronger claims would require role-specific knowledge, power/control, predictability, access to alternatives, serious integration attempts, IA transparency, justification, time-boundedness, reversibility, and safe conditions for any D-module activation. None of these are fulfilled.

For AHP, further hardening would require preserving working-note status, distinguishing analysis precision from evidence strength, separating not-used domains from known-absent domains, adding IA minimum evidence thresholds, staging predictability and alternatives for future M-score work, aligning D₀ and D₁/D₂ notation, and marking PMS-SEX terms as risk lenses only.

The examples clarify the same weakening logic. The researcher example would become stronger only if actual institutional patterns, refusal channels, dependency facts, and consequences were documented. The publicness example would become stronger only if actual disclosure, role-position, cost topology, and exit conditions were supplied. The analysis-artefact example would become stronger only if there were a real transmission event and documented institutional uptake. Without such evidence, the examples remain demonstrations, not findings.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It does not diagnose capacity, trauma, pathology, vulnerability, immaturity, coercive personality, desire, fear, or psychological state.

It must not be used for person ranking. No role-position, participant, group, identity, orientation, practice, vulnerability, or capacity condition is ranked. Dignity is not scored. Maturity is not scored. Adultness is not judged.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusion. It does not determine consent validity, coercion, undue influence, liability, credibility, evidence sufficiency, capacity, or legal responsibility.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. It does not prove what autonomy, will, agency, dignity, or consent ultimately are.

It must not be used as empirical proof. It contains no evidence of an actual event. It does not establish what happened.

It must not be used as implementation validation. It is not a consent tool, risk assessment tool, governance engine, clinical instrument, legal classifier, or computational validator.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. A coherent case application does not prove PMS complete, final, or uniquely correct.

It must not use PMS-SEX as an operator system. PMS-SEX terms remain overlay handles: sexual cost topology, body remainder, publicness, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, asymmetry-switch drift, and adult sexual reciprocity are not PMS operators.

It must not use drift as verdict. Drift markers are risks, not findings. Asymmetry-switch drift, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, AD_A>>E, and AD_Sigma_low do not classify persons or decide scenes.

It must not use MIP as a maturity tribunal. No A-score, M-score, D-profile, adultness judgment, or maturity verdict is assigned.

It must not use AHP as a credibility upgrade. High analysis precision does not produce evidence, authority, transmissibility, or decision-readiness.

It must not create closure by authority. The stack’s purpose is to preserve the difference between structural legibility and warranted conclusion. The correct outcome of this multi-layer analysis is not “therefore consent is valid” or “therefore consent is invalid.” The correct outcome is a disciplined map of what must be visible before stronger claims become responsible.

The examples must also remain inside this misuse boundary. The junior researcher is not evidence of coercion or valid consent. The intimate-publicness vignette is not evidence of sexual wrongdoing, deviance, or adultness failure. The analysis-artefact vignette is not evidence that structured analysis is harmful or that institutions act in bad faith. Each example illustrates a structural risk of overclosure. None supplies a verdict.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is that `Consent Under Asymmetry` is best held as a reversible, Ω-active, □-dependent, Φ-contestable, Χ-governed, Θ-under-specified consent structure. PMS Core makes the main structure visible: agreement, refusal, pressure, dependency, vulnerability, unequal capacity, and contestation require careful differentiation; asymmetry matters; temporality matters; recontextualization is unavoidable; distance is mandatory; integration and self-binding remain conditional. The Core result is not a consent verdict. It is a disciplined structural map.

PMS-SEX adds a conditional overlay. It shows that if the consent-under-asymmetry structure is later specified as sexual praxis, additional questions become necessary: sexual cost topology, embodied consequence, publicness, exit realism, role-position burden, asymmetry activation, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, adult sexual reciprocity, and Dignity-in-Practice under domain-specific pressure. But the overlay does not sexualize the case. It supplies visibility requirements, not findings.

MIP adds working-note discipline. It shows that maturity, responsibility, IA, and D-language cannot responsibly be activated as scoring or verdict language here. No A-score, M-score, IA verdict, D-profile, adultness judgment, or maturity ranking is warranted. The case is too abstract and lacks the concrete role-position, knowledge, power-to-act, predictability, alternatives, chronology, publicness, and evidence required for stronger MIP claims.

AHP adds second-order hardening. It shows that the analysis artefact itself has an attack surface. A formally precise working note can be misused as authority. Therefore high precision must not be mistaken for evidence, transmission-readiness, D activation, M-score support, IA verdict, or PMS-SEX finding. AHP protects the analysis by making its own misuse risks visible.

The three examples contribute by demonstrating the architecture without replacing it. The researcher example shows why formal agreement under dependency cannot be closed either as simple consent or automatic coercion. The sexual cost topology example shows why PMS-SEX must remain conditional: it becomes useful only when concrete sexual-praxis cost channels are supplied. The analysis-artefact example shows why AHP is necessary: even careful structural work can become over-authoritative when transmitted outside its boundary. None of the examples is evidence for the case. Each is a controlled demonstration of a structural distinction.

What remains unclaimed is extensive and important. The article does not claim that any actual consent was valid or invalid. It does not claim coercion, incapacity, exploitation, trauma, diagnosis, adultness failure, moral blame, legal liability, clinical impairment, empirical prevalence, metaphysical truth, or implementation readiness. It does not claim that PMS captures all relevant phenomena. It does not claim that PMS-SEX is necessary for every consent case. It does not claim that MIP or AHP can decide what happened. It does not claim PMS Base validation.

The boundary discipline is therefore the conclusion’s core result. Risk is not finding. Coherence is not proof. Non-assignment is not low score. Non-capture is not failure. Resistance, refusal, non-fit, disagreement, rupture, absence, and rival framing are not automatically drift. Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological, not ontological. Add-on terms remain overlay handles, not PMS operators. AHP hardens the analysis but does not rescore it.

The final methodological takeaway is that consent under asymmetry cannot be responsibly handled by either surface closure or suspicion closure. Surface closure says: agreement settles the case. Suspicion closure says: asymmetry settles the case. AXIOM preserves a third path: structural legibility without premature adjudication. The case shows where PMS can map the consent field, where PMS-SEX can mark domain-specific calibration requirements, where MIP must refuse maturity scoring, and where AHP must protect the artefact from becoming authority. The result is not final closure; it is a disciplined account of what any responsible closure would have to earn: concrete scene evidence, calibrated asymmetry, temporal reconstruction, real exit analysis, rival-framework comparison, and a transmission boundary strong enough to keep analysis from becoming verdict.
