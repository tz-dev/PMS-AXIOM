### Why this case matters

Pseudo-symmetry matters because it addresses a recurring philosophical closure-demand: the demand to decide whether declared equality is enough when lived, institutional, or practical distributions remain unequal. The case concerns situations in which parity is verbally asserted, but outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations are not evenly distributed. The difficulty is not merely that there is a contradiction between words and deeds. The deeper difficulty is that the very language of symmetry may prevent the asymmetry from becoming visible as an asymmetry. A statement such as “both sides are equally responsible,” “everyone is treated the same,” or “the rules apply equally” may function in different ways. It may be a truthful formal claim. It may express an aspiration. It may simplify a complex role structure. It may name a legal or administrative parity while practical burdens remain differentiated. Or it may operate as a surface of equality that obscures unequal exposure, cost, or responsibility.

This makes the case structurally difficult. The analyst must not leap from unevenness to guilt, from asymmetry to injustice, or from parity-language to deception. At the same time, the analyst must not allow parity-language to close the case prematurely. Pseudo-symmetry is precisely the field in which a closure-demand appears on both sides. One closure says: “parity has been asserted, therefore the structure is equal.” Another closure says: “unevenness remains, therefore the parity claim is false, ideological, or culpable.” AXIOM cannot accept either closure without further structural discrimination. The case belongs in AXIOM because it maps the pressure between these closures without becoming a tribunal.

The case also matters because it tests the difference between symmetry and reciprocity. Mature relational or institutional praxis need not require identical burdens. Some asymmetries are functional, role-bound, transparent, justified, time-limited, reversible, and coordinated. Others are hidden, denied, externalized, permanentized, or rendered unspeakable by parity rhetoric. The case therefore asks how to distinguish coordinated asymmetry from pseudo-symmetry without making PMS or EDEN into a verdict machine.

Pseudo-symmetry is also an important AXIOM case because it reveals how closure can be produced by apparently anti-hierarchical language. Many closure problems arise through explicit hierarchy: command, rank, exclusion, authority, sanction, or domination. Pseudo-symmetry is subtler. Closure may occur through the assertion that hierarchy or asymmetry has already been overcome. The phrase “we are all equal here” may open a shared space; it may also prevent questions about who pays more for participating in that space. The phrase “everyone is responsible” may support shared ownership; it may also flatten responsibility where access, knowledge, power, and consequence are uneven. The phrase “the same rules apply to all” may be procedurally important; it may also hide different costs of compliance, contestation, or exposure. This makes the case philosophically rich: its problem is not asymmetry alone, but asymmetry under a parity surface.

This is why the case is not solved metaphysically. The analysis does not ask what equality “really is” in an ultimate ontological sense. It does not decide whether persons are equal in essence, dignity, consciousness, rationality, or moral worth. PMS explicitly does not operate at that level. Dignity-in-Practice, where relevant, is not an ontological ranking but a praxeological constraint on how asymmetry is handled. The analysis therefore refuses to convert parity into metaphysical equality or asymmetry into metaphysical inferiority. It asks only how a parity claim and a distributional structure relate inside a praxis frame.

Nor is the case solved empirically. No dataset, field report, institutional record, interview, complaint file, ethnographic observation, or measured outcome distribution is supplied. The case material gives a structural motif, not evidence. PMS can map the motif, but it cannot certify that any actual institution or relationship exhibits pseudo-symmetry. A concrete application would require scene material: who said what, under which rules, who bore which cost, who had access to review, what happened over time, and which rival explanation fits the pattern.

Nor is the case solved clinically, diagnostically, legally, or forensically. The analysis does not infer motives, manipulation, denial, immaturity, pathology, guilt, liability, discrimination, or intent. It does not rank persons or groups by moral worth. It does not decide whether any legal threshold has been met. It does not prescribe sanctions, governance interventions, therapeutic action, or institutional reform. It provides a structural cartography of a philosophical problem: how parity-language and uneven distribution can coexist, and what must remain visible if that coexistence is to be analyzed responsibly.

### Scene and frame structure

The conceptual scene is defined by a relational or institutional frame in which parity is asserted while distribution remains uneven. The frame, in PMS terms, is □. It is not a neutral container. It determines what counts as parity, what counts as obligation, what counts as exposure, what counts as outcome, and what kind of unevenness is relevant. In one frame, parity might mean equal formal standing. In another, equal burden. In another, reciprocal voice. In another, equal procedural access. In another, coordinated asymmetry under different roles. The case cannot be understood until the relevant frame is specified.

Inside the frame are at least three abstract elements. First, there is a parity claim: some verbal or symbolic assertion of equality, symmetry, mutuality, equivalence, or shared responsibility. Second, there is an uneven distribution: outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations remain asymmetrically allocated. Third, there is a relation between the two: the parity claim may describe, obscure, normalize, justify, aspire beyond, or administratively simplify the distributional structure.

Outside the frame are all claims that would require evidence not supplied. The analysis cannot determine motives. It cannot determine whether the parity claim is sincere, manipulative, mistaken, aspirational, juridical, strategic, or symbolic. It cannot determine whether the uneven distribution is justified, harmful, reversible, unavoidable, functional, accepted, contested, or imposed. It cannot determine whether the structure recurs. The protected constraints are those imposed by PMS application discipline: distance, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. Distance requires that the analyst not collapse the case into a verdict. Reversibility requires that the reading remain revisable if new frame information appears. Temporality, Θ, is weak but relevant. The case material says that outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations “remain” unevenly distributed. Exposure and asymmetry conditions are central but under-specified. Ω is named by the case itself: distribution is uneven. But the direction of the unevenness is unknown. ### PMS Core structural reading

PMS Core provides the base reading of the case through the full operator inventory: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. The YAML does not merely list these operators. It calibrates them, marks their status, and warns against using later operators too cheaply.

**Δ — Difference** is active and foundational. The case begins with a structural distinction: parity is verbally asserted, but outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations remain unevenly distributed. This difference is not yet a moral conclusion. It is the condition of intelligibility. Without Δ, the case would collapse into either pure symmetry or pure asymmetry. Δ makes visible that the parity claim and the distributional structure may not coincide.

The calibration issue for Δ is inflation. If every difference between language and distribution is automatically treated as pseudo-symmetry, the reading becomes too broad. Some differences may be harmless, functional, formal, transitional, or explicitly acknowledged. Δ opens the case; it does not decide it.

**∇ — Impulse** is weak and conditional. The mismatch between asserted parity and uneven distribution may generate structural pressure: pressure to justify, correct, deny, contest, stabilize, or reinterpret the mismatch. But ∇ must not be psychologized. The YAML does not infer resentment, desire, avoidance, manipulation, or motive. The impulse is structural directedness, not inner drive.

The overclaim risk is to treat the mere presence of mismatch as evidence of subjective tension or agency. In a concrete case, one would need to show that the mismatch actually generates action pressure, argument pressure, institutional pressure, or interpretive pressure. Here that remains weak.

**□ — Frame** is active and indispensable. Pseudo-symmetry only appears inside a frame that makes parity and uneven distribution mutually relevant. A relational or institutional setting is presupposed. The frame determines whether parity means equal status, equal burden, equal voice, equal responsibility, equal rule application, or something else.

The calibration issue for □ is frame confusion. A statement may be true in one frame and misleading in another. “Equal before the rule” may coexist with unequal exposure to the rule. “Equal responsibility” may coexist with unequal capacity to act. “Equal access” may coexist with unequal cost of access. PMS must therefore identify the relevant frame before drawing any stronger conclusion.

**Λ — Non-Event** is weak and conditional. A pseudo-symmetry case may involve a structured absence: missing reciprocity, missing acknowledgment of asymmetry, missing redistribution, missing repair, or missing interface work under named Ω. But the YAML correctly refuses to assert Λ strongly. The expected event is not specified. We do not know whether equalization was expected, promised, required, deferred, impossible, or irrelevant.

The overclaim risk is serious. Analysts often turn absence into failure too quickly. But absence is only a PMS Λ when it is meaningful within a frame. If no expectation of reciprocity, acknowledgment, or adjustment exists in the relevant frame, Λ cannot carry strong analytic weight.

**Α — Attractor** is weak and conditional. Pseudo-symmetry becomes structurally stronger if parity-language and uneven distribution recur, stabilize, or become a role-script. The phrase “situations where” suggests a pattern-type, but the case material does not supply recurrence evidence. PMS therefore cannot claim a stabilized attractor.

The dependency risk is that Ω in PMS depends on Α. Strong asymmetry in the PMS chain is not simply any observed unevenness; it is asymmetry emerging from or linked to stabilized patterning. Since Α is weak, later claims based on stable asymmetry must remain calibrated.

**Ω — Asymmetry** is active and central, but dependency-weak. The case explicitly names uneven distribution. Outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations are not evenly allocated. This is the core material of Ω. Yet Ω must remain structural and non-moralized. Asymmetry may be unjustified, but it may also be functional, transparent, role-bound, temporary, accepted, or necessary.

The calibration issue is directionality. What exactly is uneven? Outcomes? Risk? Recognition? Voice? Obligations? Costs? Burden of proof? Exposure to sanction? Capacity to exit? The case does not say. Therefore Ω is central but not fully mapped. It names the problem field, not a verdict.

**Θ — Temporality** is weak-to-moderate. The word “remain” gives a temporal hint. Uneven distribution is not merely instantaneously present; it may persist. But no trajectory is supplied. There is no sequence, no duration, no accumulation, no delay, no review failure, no permanentization, no before/after.

The overclaim risk is to treat the conceptual word “remain” as evidence of chronicity. A full Θ reading would require data across time: recurrence, repeated parity claims, delayed correction, growing cost, or stabilized expectations.

**Φ — Recontextualization** is active and calibration-sensitive. This is one of the most important PMS operators in the case. Parity-language may recontextualize asymmetry as symmetry. It may place an uneven distribution into a frame of fairness, mutuality, equality, formal sameness, shared responsibility, institutional neutrality, or aspiration. This can clarify, conceal, stabilize, soften, justify, or defer the asymmetry.

The YAML is careful here. Φ must not become an all-consuming explanation. Not every rival framing is recontextualization in the PMS sense. Not every disagreement is drift. Not every appeal to parity is masking. A parity claim may correctly name formal equality while acknowledging practical difference. It may be aspirational rather than descriptive. It may be administrative rather than ideological. Φ is active because reframing is structurally plausible, but its function remains undecided.

**Χ — Distance** is active primarily as an application constraint. Within the case scene itself, Χ is not established because there is no concrete scene. But the analysis must maintain Χ. It must pause before verdict, resist fusion with accusation, and preserve rival readings. Without Χ, PMS would become exactly what it must not become: a closure apparatus.

The calibration issue is that distance is not neutrality-as-erasure. Χ does not deny asymmetry. It holds asymmetry visible without rushing to blame. It also prevents the analyst from using PMS vocabulary as authority-language. If the analysis declares “this is pseudo-symmetry, therefore guilt,” Χ has failed.

**Σ — Integration** is weak and contested. The case may show a verbal attempt at coherence: parity language can make a structure sound coherent. But PMS integration is not verbal smoothness. Σ would require that the uneven distribution be integrated into the frame in a way that can handle its practical consequences. The YAML therefore distinguishes asserted coherence from integrated praxis.

The overclaim risk is to grant Σ too cheaply. A symmetrical sentence is not integrated action. A formal parity claim is not necessarily coherent burden-handling. If PMS treats the case as coherent merely because the operators map smoothly, the analysis becomes under-discriminating.

**Ψ — Self-Binding** is weak and conditional. Ψ would become relevant if parity claims bind actors, institutions, or role-positions across time. A declared commitment to equality, reciprocity, shared responsibility, or formal parity might become Ψ if it creates durable obligation, review, accountability, or self-relation under named asymmetry. The case does not show this.

The overclaim risk is to treat any statement of parity as self-binding. PMS refuses this. A slogan is not Ψ. A policy phrase is not Ψ unless it is bound into practice across time. In this case, Ψ remains mostly an analytic discipline: the analysis itself must be self-bound by its own guardrails.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The PMS YAML is especially clear that later operators cannot be used cheaply. This case makes that rule visible. It is tempting to jump from Δ and Ω directly to Σ failure or Ψ failure: parity and distribution diverge, therefore integration has failed, therefore self-binding is simulated or displaced. That shortcut is invalid. PMS dependencies require attention to the chain.

If Α is weak, stable asymmetry cannot be overclaimed. If Θ is weak, chronic drift cannot be asserted. If Λ is under-specified, missing reciprocity cannot be treated as established failure. If Φ is ambiguous, recontextualization cannot be interpreted as concealment by default. If Χ is not maintained, the analysis becomes accusatory. If Σ is weak, coherence must not be treated as proof. If Ψ is weak, no durable self-binding or responsibility structure can be inferred.

Σ and Ψ therefore remain conditional. Σ would require evidence that the parity/asymmetry mismatch is actually integrated into coherent praxis. Ψ would require evidence that actors or institutions bind themselves across time to that integration. The case supplies neither. It supplies a structural pattern that raises the question.

Coherence is not proof. This is one of the most important lessons of the case. PMS can map pseudo-symmetry elegantly: Δ between claim and distribution, □ frame, Ω asymmetry, Φ reframing, Χ restraint, Σ question, Ψ condition. But elegance is a danger. The YAML explicitly marks coherence risk because a smooth PMS derivation can appear stronger than the evidence. The analysis must show discriminative force: it must distinguish pseudo-symmetry from formal equality, functional asymmetry, transitional reform, symbolic parity, administrative simplification, and contested framing.

Non-capture is not failure. If a rival reading explains a concrete version of the case better, PMS has not “failed” in a humiliating sense. It has found a boundary. PMS is a cartographic grammar, not a sovereign theory. Some cases may be better captured by distributive justice, recognition theory, contract theory, legitimacy theory, legal doctrine, institutional economics, sociology of organizations, or discourse theory.

Invalid dependency shortcuts include: using Ω as moral blame; using Λ without a named expectation; using Α without recurrence; using Θ without temporal evidence; using Φ to absorb all disagreement; using Σ as rhetorical smoothness; using Ψ as institutional slogan; using Dignity-in-Practice as ontological dignity; using drift-patterns as verdicts; and using PMS readability as PMS validation.

The calibration discipline therefore preserves the central AXIOM rule: PMS shows where closure is possible and where Λ-residue or non-capture must remain visible. In Pseudo-Symmetry, closure is not yet possible. The case remains structurally readable but evidentially underdetermined.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core YAML treats drift-patterns as conditional structural risks, not as findings. This distinction is essential. Pseudo-Symmetry has a strong intuitive affinity with drift language because it already names a possible mismatch between claimed parity and practical distribution. But PMS does not permit the analyst to convert intuitive fit into drift assignment. Drift requires structural conditions that the case material does not yet supply: recurrence, stabilization, temporal extension, integration failure, self-binding failure, or repeated recontextualization under asymmetry.

The first relevant drift-pattern is **AD_A>>E**, excessive distance between awareness and enactment. In PMS terms, this risk would appear where evaluative or positional asymmetry becomes stabilized and recontextualized while balanced enactment remains suppressed or chronically delayed. The case is adjacent to this pattern because parity may be verbally available while action, redistribution, acknowledgment, or reciprocal burden-bearing remains absent. In ordinary language, one might say that a system “knows” or “says” equality but does not enact it. PMS does not allow that statement to become a finding without evidence.

The YAML therefore marks AD_A>>E as possible but not established. Awareness_A is weak. Action_E is under-specified. There is no concrete action sequence, no evidence of chronic delay, no documented correction failure, and no stabilized pattern of knowing without doing. Even if the concept of pseudo-symmetry points toward a gap between awareness and enactment, the actual PMS drift pattern requires more than a conceptual gap. It requires a patterned relation between available awareness and blocked or delayed action. That relation is not yet shown.

The second relevant drift-pattern is **AD_Sigma_low**, fragmented or insufficient integration. This risk is especially close to the case. Pseudo-symmetry may display apparent coherence: a parity claim can make a structure look balanced, reciprocal, or fair while the distribution remains uneven. If recontextualization repeatedly outpaces integration, the structure may appear coherent without being integrated. EDEN later sharpens this as possible Σ-simulation. PMS Core already sees the risk: verbal symmetry can be mistaken for integrated praxis.

Yet AD_Sigma_low is also not established. The YAML marks it as possible but not entailed. To activate it, one would need evidence that the frame repeatedly recontextualizes uneven distribution as parity while failing to integrate the asymmetry into coherent practice. A single conceptual description is not enough. Nor is disagreement enough. Nor is non-fit enough. Nor is the mere absence of equal distribution enough. PMS must not read every unresolved difference as low integration.

The coherence risk is therefore twofold. First, the case itself may involve false coherence: parity language may create the appearance of integration. Second, the analysis may generate false coherence: PMS may map the case so smoothly that the reading seems proven. The YAML explicitly guards against this. A coherent PMS derivation is not sufficient by itself. It must show discriminative force. It must distinguish pseudo-symmetry from justified role differentiation, formal equality with material inequality, aspirational parity, temporary transition, administrative simplification, symbolic parity, and contested frame selection.

Under-discrimination is the main danger. If every mismatch between equality-language and uneven distribution becomes pseudo-symmetry, the category loses usefulness. If every pseudo-symmetry becomes AD_A>>E or AD_Sigma_low, drift language becomes a verdict engine. The PMS result is more careful: the case opens drift questions but does not assign drift. Risk is not finding. Under-specification is not failure. Non-assignment is not a low score. It is the correct result under the available material.

Therefore EDEN can name the pseudo-symmetry signature, but it cannot claim a full pseudo-symmetry regime.

The **asymmetry switch** is marked as a risk, not a finding. EDEN uses this paper-internal handle for a shift from parity claim to asymmetry activation under real or invoked Ω/Θ pressure. The case creates the possibility of such a switch because parity is asserted while uneven distribution remains. In such a configuration, asymmetry might become selectively activatable: invoked when useful, denied when costly, hidden when accountability appears, or activated as exception under parity surface. But the case does not show actual activation. It does not show who activates asymmetry, when, under what pressure, whether it is marked as exceptional, whether it is bounded by Χ, or whether it is re-bound into Σ/Ψ. Therefore AS remains an overlay risk handle only.

The **Permanentized Exception Switch** is also only a downstream risk. EDEN reserves this for cases where asymmetry activation ceases to be emergency-bound and becomes a standing lever while parity remains rhetorically preserved. The current case does not show a standing lever, repeatable exception protocol, or permanentized regime. It only supplies abstract ingredients that could later matter if a concrete scene showed repeated parity surface plus unmarked asymmetry activation.

**Parity-to-exit** is marked as a risk because the case explicitly concerns parity assertions and uneven responsibilities or obligations. EDEN uses parity-to-exit for drift in which equality-language becomes an exit from re-internalizing unequal costs. This is a highly relevant risk. A parity claim can say, in effect: because we are equal, no additional responsibility is owed, even if one role-position bears more exposure or consequence. But the case does not show that such exit is actually occurring. There is no concrete refusal, no cost-shifting event, no denied repair, no review failure. PTE remains a calibration question.

**Responsibility-to-authority** is not applicable. That EDEN drift profile concerns cases where required self-binding under asymmetry is converted into control, frame authority, protection entitlement, or governance over another role-position. The case names uneven responsibilities and obligations, but it does not show conversion of responsibility into authority. This non-assignment matters. EDEN must not infer authority drift merely from asymmetrical responsibility.

**PFO** is weak and too strong as a primary label. EDEN defines PFO as a paper-internal regime label involving comparison governance, Ω-legibility management or suppression, permanentized exception-switch availability, high Φ repair, persistent Λ, protocolized Α, low or suppressed Σ, and displaced or misbound Ψ across Θ. The case does not establish these conditions. It supports local PS and possible switch-risk, but not a full regime. PFO remains a guarded downstream comparison point: it shows what would have to be added before the case could be read as regime drift.

**Reciprocity loss** is a risk, not a finding. EDEN’s major contribution is to clarify that reciprocity is not symmetry. Reciprocity is coordinated asymmetry: Ω legible as a functional gradient, Σ active, Ψ anchored, Χ present, and D constraining Ω-handling. Pseudo-symmetry may erode reciprocity if formal parity replaces this coordination. But the current case does not show erosion. It does not show chronic Λ, stabilized Α, Θ accumulation, Σ/Ψ breakdown, or enacted cost externalization. It only shows why reciprocity could be at risk if parity surface substitutes for coordinated asymmetry.

Several EDEN constructs are not applicable. Eden ground-state is not active because the case does not begin from stable praxis without deficit pressure. Threshold explicitness is not active because no selectable breach option becomes visible. Breach as chain interruption is not active because no event or realization under ∇ is supplied. NRK is not active because there is no option-set, realization, or explicit Σ/Ψ failure sequence. Dissatisfaction as system cost is not active because no complaint loop, comparison loop, adjustment cycle, or accumulated cost experience appears. Devaluation and humiliation are not applicable because no standing reduction, status-control move, humiliation script, or residual stabilization is described.

This restraint is one of the strongest parts of the EDEN overlay. It does not simply import the whole EDEN pipeline. It locates the case at the PS node and holds the rest as weak, risk-only, not applicable, or downstream. The master drift trace therefore applies only partially: EDEN → threshold → NRK → comparison → PS → asymmetry switch → PFO → parity-to-exit or responsibility-to-authority → devaluation/humiliation → reciprocity loss is not present as a full chain. The case sits primarily at PS, with possible AS and PTE risk.

EDEN-MAP gives a partial mapping. The scene packet is incomplete. The frame anchor is a relational or institutional parity frame under uneven distribution. Roles are abstract placeholders: a parity-claiming position, an uneven-cost-bearing position, and possibly a frame-maintaining position. Λ candidates include missing reciprocity, missing acknowledgment of asymmetry, missing redistribution, missing re-binding of unequal obligation, or missing distinction between formal parity and practical cost-bearing. Realizations are absent. Action_E is not present. Alpha stabilizers are not evidenced. Ω is conceptually central but not concretely mapped. Ψ is weak because declared parity is not self-binding. Switch drift is unclear.

EDEN’s final overlay signature can therefore be rendered as follows: PS core signature active and bounded; □ parity surface plus Ω real or asserted uneven distribution plus Φ possible recontextualization as symmetry; Σ weak or possibly simulated but not established; Ψ weak or unestablished; Λ weak as possible missing reciprocity; Α and Θ under-specified; AS and PTE risk unclear; PFO weak and not warranted; reciprocity loss risk but not finding; all governed by Χ, reversibility, and D.

### Layer interaction

The PMS Core and EDEN layers interact by division of labor. PMS Core remains the canonical operator grammar. It defines Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ and their dependencies. It determines that derived structures such as A, C, R, E, D, and SELF_FIXPOINT are formula-derived rather than primitive. It supplies the basic structural map: difference between parity claim and uneven distribution inside a frame, with asymmetry central and recontextualization possible.

EDEN sharpens the case-specific visibility of pseudo-symmetry. PMS Core can say that Φ may recontextualize Ω inside □. EDEN can say that, in this case, the recontextualization may take the specific form of parity surface under uneven distribution. PMS Core can say that Σ is weak or contested. EDEN can add that parity-language may simulate Σ by displaying coherence without integrating cost. PMS Core can say Ψ is weak. EDEN can ask whether declared parity is misbound to appearance-management or whether genuine self-binding under named Ω is present. PMS Core can say reciprocity is not established. EDEN can specify that reciprocity is coordinated asymmetry, not formal sameness.

But EDEN must not be allowed to redefine PMS. EDEN terms such as PS, PFO, NRK, AS, PES, PTE, RTA, reciprocity loss, devaluation, and humiliation are overlay handles. They are not PMS operators. They do not alter PMS dependencies. They do not allow the analyst to skip from Δ and Ω to Σ or Ψ failure. They do not convert risk into diagnosis. They do not assign maturity, dignity, guilt, or legal status.

Overlay language could overreach in several ways. It could label any parity/asymmetry mismatch as PS regime. It could infer PFO from local pseudo-symmetry. It could treat AS as a person trait. It could read PTE into any appeal to equality. It could treat reciprocity loss as established where only unequal distribution is named. It could import devaluation or humiliation without evidence. The YAML prevents this by marking most EDEN constructs weak, risk-only, or not applicable.

MIP and AHP remain downstream, not explanatory base layers. MIP can ask whether the case would support maturity-in-practice analysis if concrete scene data were supplied. It can ask about awareness, coherence, responsibility, power-to-act, asymmetry legitimacy, and Dignity-in-Practice protection. In this case, however, MIP remains deliberately non-scoring. It can identify the downstream questions — especially whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible — but it cannot answer them without a concrete role map, time window, publicness condition, cost topology, and evidence of available levers. AHP can then harden the analysis artefact by checking evidence linkage, scope boundaries, reversibility clauses, language hygiene, and transmission status. Its function is second-order: it improves the safety and precision of the analysis, but it does not rescore MIP, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or convert the article into a decision instrument.

Non-closure remains visible across the layers. PMS Core does not close the case because key operators remain weak or conditional. EDEN does not close the case because PS is active only at conceptual signature level. MIP does not close the case because scoring and IA classification require concrete evidence. AHP does not close the case because it hardens analysis quality rather than deciding the case. The layers together therefore produce a disciplined map, not a final verdict.

### Example 1 — Equal Voice, Unequal Exposure

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Scene and frame structure” or after the PMS Core operator reading.

A small committee adopts a rule that “all members have equal voice.” In meetings, each person is formally permitted to speak, propose revisions, object to drafts, and request clarification. The rule is written neutrally and applies to everyone. Yet one member occupies a precarious role: they are junior, their contract renewal depends partly on the committee chair, and their objections are more likely to be remembered as “difficult” or “not collegial.” Senior members can disagree without comparable exposure. The committee therefore has a parity surface — equal voice — while the cost of using that voice is unevenly distributed.

This example demonstrates the core difficulty of pseudo-symmetry. The parity claim may be formally true. Nobody is explicitly forbidden from speaking. The rule may even be well-intentioned. The problem is that formal permission and practical exposure do not necessarily coincide. If the committee treats the equal-voice rule as sufficient proof that participation is symmetrical, the unequal exposure attached to speaking may become difficult to name. If, however, the analyst immediately declares the committee hypocritical or abusive, the reading overreaches. The structure requires calibration, not a verdict.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ** appears in the distinction between formal speaking rights and unequal practical exposure. **∇** is weakly visible as pressure around whether the junior member should speak, remain silent, or accept the parity frame. **□** is the committee setting: its rules, hierarchy, norms of collegiality, and evaluation channels. **Λ** may be present if an expected protection is absent — for example, if the committee says all may speak but provides no way to protect low-status speakers from retaliation or reputational cost. This Λ remains conditional unless the expectation of protection is actually part of the frame. **Α** would require recurrence: repeated meetings where equal-voice language coexists with uneven exposure. A single meeting is not enough.

**Ω** is central because exposure is uneven. But it must not be moralized too quickly. Unequal exposure may be built into the role hierarchy; it may be recognized, mitigated, ignored, or denied. **Θ** becomes stronger if the pattern persists across meetings, review cycles, or contract decisions. **Φ** appears if the equal-voice rule recontextualizes unequal exposure as practical symmetry: “everyone had the same opportunity to speak.” **Χ** is required of the analysis: the reading must not collapse into blame or innocence. **Σ** is weak unless the committee integrates the exposure problem into actual practice, such as protected channels, chair restraint, or explicit review. **Ψ** is weak unless the committee binds itself across time to maintain not only formal voice but usable voice.

The EDEN overlay is relevant but must remain bounded. The example fits the **PS** signature: parity surface under uneven exposure. It also raises a possible **parity-to-exit** risk: if senior members say, “You had the same chance to speak, so the outcome is equally owned,” parity language may become an exit from responsibility for unequal risk. But this is a risk, not a finding. There is no full **PFO** unless the committee develops a stable regime in which asymmetry is unspeakable, exception-switches are permanentized, and Σ/Ψ coordination is blocked. There is no **humiliation** finding merely because the junior member is exposed. There is no **NRK** unless a concrete realization sequence with Σ/Ψ failure is supplied.

The MIP status would be non-scoring at this stage. The example opens questions about awareness, coherence, responsibility, and real power-to-act, but it does not by itself assign an A-score, M-score, IA result, or D-profile. MIP would ask whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. Does the committee recognize that equal formal voice does not mean equal practical cost? Is the exposure justified by role? Is there a review point? Can the junior member object without disproportionate penalty? AHP or QC would harden the analysis by warning against mistaking a clean structural fit for evidence depth.

Forbidden claims are crucial. The example does not prove that the chair is manipulative, that senior members are guilty, that the junior member is harmed, that the institution is legally discriminatory, or that the committee is morally defective. It does not diagnose anyone’s motives. It does not show empirical prevalence. It does not validate PMS or EDEN.

The non-capture note is equally important. A rival reading may show that the equal-voice rule is only one part of a broader structure that already provides private feedback, ombudsperson review, or anonymous objections. Another rival reading may argue that role hierarchy necessarily creates different exposure and that the mature task is not identical risk but coordinated asymmetry. A legal reading may focus on formal procedure. A recognition reading may focus on whether the junior member’s concerns are acknowledged.

The article takeaway is that pseudo-symmetry often appears where equal access is declared but the cost of using access is uneven. PMS Core makes the parity/exposure mismatch visible; EDEN names the PS risk; MIP withholds scoring until the asymmetry’s transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility are known.

### Example 2 — Shared Responsibility, Unequal Obligation

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after the EDEN overlay section, especially where parity-to-exit and reciprocity are discussed.

A small project team misses an internal deadline. In the follow-up meeting, the team lead says, “We all share responsibility for this.” The sentence lowers immediate blame pressure and prevents the discussion from becoming a personal accusation. At that level, it may be constructive. Yet the actual distribution of obligation was not equal. One person had authority to allocate tasks, another had access to the missing information, a third repeatedly asked for clarification but received no answer, and a fourth completed their part on time. The language of shared responsibility creates a parity surface, but the structure of agency, access, and obligation remains uneven.

This example demonstrates why pseudo-symmetry is not simply “false equality.” Shared-responsibility language can be useful. It may reduce shame, preserve group orientation, and keep a team from scapegoating one person. PMS and EDEN must therefore not treat every shared-responsibility statement as drift. The structural question is whether the parity claim reopens coordinated responsibility or closes it. If “we all share responsibility” is followed by a differentiated review of access, authority, timing, and repair, it may support integration. If it prevents that differentiation, it may become pseudo-symmetrical.

The PMS Core hooks begin with **Δ**: shared responsibility is verbally asserted, but actual obligations and capacities differ. **∇** appears as the pressure to resolve the missed deadline without producing a humiliating blame scene. **□** is the project frame: task allocation, authority, access to information, deadlines, and review expectations. **Λ** may be the missing clarification, the unanswered request, the absent escalation path, or the missing review of who could act. But each possible Λ must be named. The fact that the deadline failed does not automatically identify the relevant non-event. **Α** would be active only if the same shared-responsibility language repeatedly prevents differentiation across project cycles.

**Ω** is central because power-to-act, information access, and obligation are unevenly distributed. The team lead may have more authority. The person with missing information may have a bottleneck role. The person who asked for clarification may have had low power to solve the problem. **Θ** matters if the pattern accumulates: repeated missed deadlines, repeated flattening of responsibility, or repeated failure to correct access asymmetry. **Φ** is significant because the team lead’s statement may recontextualize differentiated responsibility as equal responsibility. But Φ is ambiguous. It may be a temporary de-escalation frame, not concealment. **Χ** is required to preserve this ambiguity. **Σ** is active only if the team integrates the differentiated structure into repair: who had what authority, what failed, what changes, who follows up. **Ψ** appears only if the team binds itself across time to a revised responsibility structure.

EDEN adds a sharper reading of the risk. The phrase “we all share responsibility” may become **PS** if it preserves parity surface while unequal authority and obligation remain operative. It may become **parity-to-exit** if the person or role with greater authority uses shared responsibility to avoid re-internalizing the cost of their specific leverage. But the same phrase may also be a temporary dignity-preserving buffer if it prevents shaming and then reopens Σ/Ψ work. EDEN therefore asks: does parity language reopen coordination under named Ω, or does it replace coordination?

The example also clarifies reciprocity. Reciprocity is not everyone carrying identical blame. Mature reciprocity may require that each role carry the responsibility appropriate to its actual access, authority, and consequence profile. If the lead had allocation authority, their responsibility differs from the person who lacked information. If the information holder had a bottleneck, their responsibility differs from the person who completed their work. Coordinated asymmetry is not pseudo-symmetry. Pseudo-symmetry begins where differentiated responsibility is blocked by a parity surface.

The MIP layer would remain careful. No score is warranted from this vignette alone. MIP would ask what each role knew, what each could control, what consequences were predictable, what alternatives were available, and whether serious integration attempts occurred. The M-score cannot be inferred from the fact that a deadline was missed. IA analysis would ask whether the asymmetry of responsibility was transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. AHP would flag an analysis risk: the phrase “shared responsibility” is rhetorically loaded and can be overread as either mature solidarity or manipulative flattening.

Forbidden claims include: the team lead is not diagnosed as evasive; the team is not declared immature; the missed deadline is not treated as legal wrongdoing; the person with more authority is not automatically guilty; the person with less authority is not automatically absolved. The example does not prove that shared-responsibility language is bad. It shows that the language requires structural calibration.

The non-capture note is strong here. A conflict-resolution reading may say the shared-responsibility statement is a necessary first move to prevent scapegoating. A management reading may focus on process design. A legal reading may distinguish formal accountability from internal responsibility. A virtue-ethical reading may ask whether humility and ownership are being cultivated. PMS and EDEN preserve one structural layer but do not exhaust these readings.

The article takeaway is that pseudo-symmetry can appear not only in equality claims but also in responsibility claims. A parity statement may be constructive if it leads into differentiated repair; it becomes structurally risky if it replaces the work of naming who could know, who could act, who bore cost, and who must bind themselves to change.

### Example 3 — Equal Access, Unequal Cost of Accountability

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** before the misuse boundary or in the later section on operationalization and MIP/AHP status.

An organization introduces an accountability channel. The policy says that anyone may report concerns, request review, or ask for clarification about responsibility allocation. On paper, access is equal. The channel is publicized as evidence that the institution is transparent and accountable. In practice, however, using the channel carries different costs for different roles. Senior staff can file concerns without serious reputational danger. Contractors worry that reporting will affect renewal. People in dependent roles fear being marked as difficult. Those already exposed by the issue may have to reveal private information in order to make the asymmetry legible. The institution can truthfully claim equal access while the practical cost of accountability remains uneven.

This example demonstrates the difference between formal access and usable accountability. It is concrete enough to show the structure but bounded enough to avoid a verdict. The organization may be sincerely attempting to create review. The channel may be better than having no channel. But if equal access is treated as proof that accountability is equally available, pseudo-symmetry may arise. The parity claim is “anyone may report.” The uneven distribution is the cost of reporting, exposure, dependence, and possible retaliation or reputational trace.

The PMS Core hooks are layered. **Δ** marks the difference between equal procedural access and unequal practical cost. **∇** is the pressure around whether to use the channel, remain silent, seek informal support, or accept the institutional parity claim. **□** is the accountability frame: policy language, reporting procedures, confidentiality rules, hierarchy, dependency, and review norms. **Λ** may appear if the policy promises protection but does not provide it, or if review exists formally but no safe uptake occurs. The exact non-event must be specified: missing confidentiality, missing anti-retaliation protection, missing follow-up, missing acknowledgment, or missing repair. **Α** would require recurrence: repeated cases where equal-access language coexists with uneven reporting cost.

**Ω** is central. Exposure, dependency, role security, access to protection, and cost of accountability are uneven. **Θ** matters because reporting consequences may unfold across time: renewal cycles, reputational memory, delayed retaliation, archived complaints, or repeated non-response. **Φ** is active if the institution recontextualizes unequal accountability cost as equal accountability access. **Χ** must be maintained by the analyst: the existence of the channel should not be dismissed as mere cover, but neither should it be accepted as proof of practical equality. **Σ** would require integrated accountability design: confidential options, role-sensitive protections, follow-up, review, and burden-aware procedures. **Ψ** would require the institution to bind itself across time to those protections, not merely publish the policy.

The EDEN hooks are significant. This is a strong **PS** example: parity surface under uneven exposure and accountability cost. It may also show **asymmetry-switch risk**. The institution may invoke equality when asked about access — “everyone can report” — but asymmetry becomes operative when vulnerable roles calculate the cost of doing so. **Parity-to-exit** is a risk if the organization uses the existence of the channel to exit from further responsibility: “We provided equal access; if people did not use it, the responsibility is theirs.” That would be a stronger finding only if the organization actually uses the policy in that way. **PFO** remains too strong unless there is evidence of a regime that suppresses Ω legibility, permanentizes exception-switch availability, and substitutes procedure for Σ/Ψ. **Humiliation** and **devaluation** are not to be inferred unless the process actually lowers standing, exposes persons, or stabilizes status regulation.

This example is especially useful for the MIP layer. MIP would not score the organization or any role-holder from the vignette alone. It would ask whether the asymmetry is transparent: does the institution recognize that reporting cost differs by role? Is it justified: are there protected goods that require the design, and are alternatives considered? Is it time-bound: are the risks reviewed, monitored, or reduced over time? Is it reversible: can a reporter withdraw, correct, appeal, or avoid disproportionate exposure? MIP would also ask who knows about the cost asymmetry, who has real power to alter the channel, what consequences are predictable, what alternatives exist, and whether serious integration attempts were made.

AHP or QC would add hardening discipline to the analysis. It would warn that this example can easily be misused. A reader may want to conclude that the organization is hypocritical. Another reader may want to conclude that the policy proves accountability. Both are too fast. AHP would require evidence-linking, scope boundaries, reversibility clauses, and language hygiene. It would also flag publicness: if reporting exposes private information, D-P risk may become relevant, but no D-profile should be activated without a safe, role-bound, time-windowed basis.

Forbidden claims are therefore explicit. The example does not prove that the organization is acting in bad faith. It does not prove legal retaliation, discrimination, or institutional wrongdoing. It does not diagnose managers or reporters. It does not claim empirical frequency. It does not validate PMS, EDEN, MIP, or AHP. It does not authorize intervention. It does not treat non-use of the channel as immaturity or cowardice, because the cost of use may be real.

The non-capture note matters. A governance reading may argue that no accountability channel can eliminate all cost and that the proper comparison is not perfect equality but available safeguards. A legal reading may distinguish formal access from protected-class impact or retaliation standards. A design reading may focus on confidentiality architecture. A recognition reading may ask whether vulnerable roles are believed before they expose themselves. A systems reading may focus on organizational trust. PMS and EDEN clarify the parity/cost structure but do not replace these frameworks.

The article takeaway is that pseudo-symmetry often appears at the boundary between access and usability. Equal access is not meaningless, but it is not automatically equal accountability. The structural question is whether the institution integrates unequal cost into its accountability design or uses the formal parity of access to close the issue before exposure, dependency, and reversibility have been examined.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The YAML preserves non-capture as an active result. Non-capture does not mean the analysis failed. It means the case may contain distinctions that PMS Core or EDEN should not flatten. Several rival readings remain open.

A **formal-equality reading** may hold that the parity claim refers only to formal standing, rule application, or procedural status. Under this reading, uneven outcomes or obligations do not necessarily contradict parity. Formal parity can coexist with material difference. PMS and EDEN must not assume that parity language always claims practical equality.

A **functional-asymmetry reading** may hold that the uneven distribution is role-appropriate. Different roles often carry different obligations, exposures, and responsibilities. Parent and child, mentor and apprentice, regulator and regulated, caregiver and recipient, office-holder and citizen: such relations may be asymmetrical without being pseudo-symmetrical. If the asymmetry is transparent, justified, bounded, and reversible where appropriate, EDEN’s pseudo-symmetry reading may weaken.

An **aspirational-parity reading** may hold that the parity statement names a target rather than a current description. “We are equal” may mean “we are committed to becoming equal in practice” or “we recognize equal standing even though implementation is incomplete.” Such a reading preserves the possibility that parity language is reform-oriented rather than masking.

A **transitional-reform reading** may interpret uneven distribution as temporary. The asymmetry may be known, acknowledged, and under correction. If so, pseudo-symmetry would be too strong unless parity language denies the transition or blocks review.

An **administrative-simplification reading** may treat parity language as a practical shorthand. Institutions often use general equality language while managing differentiated roles. This can become harmful if it obscures cost, but it is not necessarily pseudo-symmetry.

An **ideological-cover reading** is also possible. Here parity-language masks or stabilizes asymmetry by translating unequal exposure into apparent reciprocity. This is the reading closest to EDEN’s PS risk. But it remains a rival reading until evidence shows that parity language actually functions as cover.

A **discourse-conflict reading** may hold that the conflict concerns which frame should govern the case. One role may define parity formally, another materially, another relationally, another procedurally. What appears as pseudo-symmetry from one frame may appear as legitimate distinction from another. PMS can map this frame conflict, but it cannot dissolve it by fiat.

A **distributive-justice reading** may analyze the case through fairness, burden allocation, equality of opportunity, equality of outcome, desert, need, or institutional design. This may discriminate concrete cases more sharply than PMS.

A **recognition-theory reading** may focus on acknowledgment, misrecognition, respect, standing, and social visibility. This may be especially relevant where parity language denies lived exposure or burden.

A **legal or institutional reading** may ask whether formal rules are equal, whether disparate impact exists, whether duties are lawfully differentiated, whether accountability mechanisms exist, or whether procedural safeguards are adequate. PMS does not replace that analysis.

A **systems-theoretic reading** may interpret the parity/asymmetry relation as a function of role differentiation, organizational complexity, or communication codes. Such a reading may preserve distinctions between symbolic equality and functional differentiation that PMS must not collapse.

Each rival reading preserves something important. Formal-equality readings preserve legal or procedural precision. Functional-asymmetry readings preserve role differentiation. Aspirational readings preserve reform language. Ideological-cover readings preserve suspicion toward surface equality. Recognition readings preserve lived acknowledgment. Systems readings preserve functional complexity. PMS and EDEN are strongest when they remain open to these alternatives.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS Core reading would weaken if the parity claim and distributional asymmetry cannot actually be distinguished. If the alleged unevenness disappears once the correct frame is specified, Δ loses force. If no relational or institutional frame makes parity and distribution mutually relevant, □ becomes too abstract. If no meaningful expected event exists, Λ should not be used. If recurrence is absent, Α remains weak. If asymmetry is not directional or relevant, Ω weakens. If no temporal persistence exists, Θ weakens. If parity language does not recontextualize anything, Φ weakens. If the analysis loses distance, Χ fails. If verbal parity is mistaken for integration, Σ is overclaimed. If a slogan is treated as durable commitment, Ψ is overclaimed.

The EDEN reading would weaken if the case turns out to involve transparent functional asymmetry rather than parity surface under hidden or unresolved cost. It would weaken if parity is explicitly formal or aspirational rather than descriptive. It would weaken if uneven distribution is openly named, justified, time-bound, and reversible. It would weaken if asymmetry activation is marked, bounded, and re-bound into Σ/Ψ. It would weaken if there is no comparison-dominant frame, no parity-to-exit, no switch availability, and no reciprocity displacement. It would fail as a PFO reading unless regime conditions appear: Ω-taboo or suppression, permanentized exception-switch availability, high Φ repair, persistent Λ, protocolized Α, low Σ, displaced Ψ, and Θ stabilization.

The reading becomes under-discriminating if it cannot separate pseudo-symmetry from legitimate role differentiation, formal equality, symbolic parity, transitional reform, administrative simplification, or contested frame selection. It becomes over-assimilative if every disagreement, rupture, absence, resistance, missing evidence, or rival framing is redescribed as recontextualization, low integration, drift, maturity failure, or reciprocity loss. It becomes too smooth if PMS coherence is treated as proof. It becomes too strong if EDEN’s PS signature is inflated into PFO, devaluation, humiliation, or full reciprocity collapse.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. For stronger claims, a future analysis would need a concrete scene packet: the exact parity claim, the relevant frame, role positions, the distributional map, who bears what cost, who has voice, who controls review, whether asymmetry is known, whether it is justified, whether it is time-bound, whether it is reversible, whether parity language blocks critique, whether correction is possible, whether recurrence exists, and whether rival interpretations fit better.

For empirical use, evidence would be needed. For comparative use, rival cases and negative cases would be needed. For computational use, operator activation thresholds and calibration procedures would be needed. For legal use, independent legal standards would be needed. For clinical use, the case would need to move into a domain PMS explicitly does not occupy, so clinical claims remain unavailable. For implementation use, governance, testing, accountability, and public interpretability would be required. None of that is supplied here.

The MIP layer, where present, correctly remains non-scoring. Pseudo-Symmetry opens questions about maturity-in-practice, awareness, coherence, responsibility, power-to-act, and inadult asymmetry, but without concrete role data no A-score, M-score, IA result, or D-profile can be assigned. The IA-box criteria — transparency, justification, time-boundedness, reversibility — remain the right downstream questions. They are not answered by the conceptual case alone.

The AHP layer, where present, should be understood as analysis-hardening. It can identify whether the MIP analysis is precise, bounded, and safe from misuse. It can recommend hardening actions such as clarifying evidence depth, transmission boundaries, IA data requirements, and D-language constraints. It cannot rescore MIP, activate D, upgrade transmission status, or convert the case into a finding.

### Misuse boundary

The misuse boundary for this case must be substantial because the topic invites misuse. Pseudo-symmetry is rhetorically powerful. Once named, it can easily be turned into an accusation: “You claim equality while hiding inequality.” AXIOM must prevent that slide.

There is no diagnosis. The case does not diagnose denial, manipulation, narcissism, immaturity, avoidance, ideology, pathology, or bad faith. It does not classify people or institutions as pseudo-symmetrical.

There is no person ranking. PMS and EDEN do not rank persons by maturity, dignity, coherence, responsibility, or moral worth. Dignity-in-Practice is not a dignity score. D0 remains outside analysis. D-language, if used downstream, concerns enactments and guardrails, not human value.

There is no legal or forensic conclusion. The analysis does not establish liability, discrimination, culpability, intent, negligence, evidentiary sufficiency, or institutional wrongdoing. Legal questions require legal standards and evidence.

There is no metaphysical proof. The case does not prove what equality, reciprocity, recognition, or dignity ultimately are. It does not make claims about essence, consciousness, ontology, or moral status.

There is no empirical proof. The analysis does not show that pseudo-symmetry occurs in a real setting. It does not show frequency, cause, harm, outcome, recurrence, or correlation.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is that **Pseudo-Symmetry** is structurally readable, but not closed. PMS Core identifies the base architecture: Δ between asserted parity and uneven distribution inside □, with Ω central, Φ possible, Χ required, and Σ/Ψ conditional. This is already analytically substantial. It shows why the case cannot be reduced to either “the parity claim is valid” or “the parity claim is false.” The relevant question is how parity-language, distribution, frame, asymmetry, temporality, integration, and self-binding interact.

PMS-EDEN then sharpens the reading without replacing PMS. EDEN makes visible that the case fits a **PS core signature**: parity surface under real or alleged uneven distribution. It also identifies adjacent risks: asymmetry-switch availability, parity-to-exit, Σ-simulation, and reciprocity-loss risk. But EDEN does not elevate these risks into findings. PFO remains weak and too strong. NRK, devaluation, humiliation, breach, dissatisfaction, and responsibility-to-authority are not applicable on the supplied material. This restraint is part of the result. The overlay succeeds not by adding more labels, but by refusing labels where the case does not warrant them.

The examples contribute by making the structure readable without converting it into evidence. **Equal Voice, Unequal Exposure** shows how formal participation can coexist with uneven cost of speaking. **Shared Responsibility, Unequal Obligation** shows how parity language can either preserve collective orientation or flatten differentiated responsibility. **Equal Access, Unequal Cost of Accountability** shows how procedural access can be real but still unevenly usable. These examples demonstrate the analysis; they do not prove the case, establish empirical occurrence, or license any finding about real actors. Their function is pedagogical and discriminative: they show how the same formal parity surface can remain legitimate, become pseudo-symmetrical, or require coordinated asymmetry depending on frame, cost topology, temporality, and reversibility. They prepare later conclusion-work by showing where PMS and EDEN can discriminate between formal parity, pseudo-symmetry, coordinated asymmetry, and overclaim.

What remains unclaimed is just as important as what is claimed. The article does not claim empirical occurrence, metaphysical truth, legal fault, clinical diagnosis, moral guilt, person ranking, or implementation validity. It does not claim that all parity-language is pseudo-symmetrical. It does not claim that asymmetry is inherently illegitimate. It does not claim that EDEN’s full drift pipeline is active. It does not assign MIP scores or D-profiles. It does not validate PMS Base. It does not defeat rival readings.

The boundary discipline is therefore the central methodological achievement of the case. PMS Core prevents premature closure by keeping dependencies visible. EDEN prevents under-specification by naming the pseudo-symmetry signature, but also prevents overreach by marking most stronger constructs as weak, risk-only, or not applicable. MIP keeps downstream evaluation non-scoring where evidence is absent. AHP hardens the analysis artefact without rescoring it. Together, the layers show how AXIOM can map a philosophically charged structure without becoming a verdict machine.

The final methodological takeaway is that pseudo-symmetry must be handled as a **calibration problem**, not as a slogan. A parity claim may be valid, aspirational, formal, functional, administrative, strategic, or masking. Uneven distribution may be justified, unjustified, temporary, permanentized, transparent, hidden, accepted, contested, or externally imposed. The task is not to decide in advance which one it is. The task is to preserve enough structural resolution that parity-language does not prematurely erase asymmetry, and asymmetry-language does not prematurely become accusation. This is the AXIOM value of the case: it shows where closure is tempting, where closure is not yet earned, and where non-capture must remain visible.
