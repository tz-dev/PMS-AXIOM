### Why this case matters

Pseudo-Symmetry matters because it names a closure-demand that is philosophically familiar but structurally treacherous: the demand to decide whether a parity claim is real, false, formal, aspirational, transitional, symbolic, justified, or merely rhetorically stabilizing an uneven distribution. The difficulty is not that parity and asymmetry are hard to notice. The difficulty is that they can coexist under many different grammars. A structure may declare equality while distributing exposure unevenly; it may describe mutuality while assigning different obligations; it may speak in the language of shared responsibility while only one side bears the cost of explanation, waiting, repair, or visibility. But the mere presence of unevenness is not yet failure. Some asymmetries are transparent, functional, temporary, role-bound, voluntarily accepted, or justified by a protected good. Others are masked, displaced, routinized, or made unchallengeable by the very vocabulary that claims symmetry.

This is why the case belongs in AXIOM. It is a stress test for a structural theory that must resist two opposite temptations. The first temptation is metaphysical closure: to decide that the parity claim is simply false because material or practical asymmetry exists. The second is formalist closure: to decide that the parity claim is sufficiently valid because equality is asserted at the level of language, rule, role, or institutional form. AXIOM cannot accept either shortcut. A PMS reading must distinguish difference, frame, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, responsibility, and self-binding without collapsing them into verdicts. A PMS-EDEN reading must sharpen false-coordination risk without treating every asymmetry as illegitimate or every parity claim as masking. A MIP reading must ask whether the practice is mature in its awareness, coherence, responsibility, and power-to-act, without converting maturity into a person score. AHP must then ask whether the analysis itself is precise enough not to become the very closure machine it claims to resist.

The case is structurally difficult because pseudo-symmetry is almost designed to fit PMS and EDEN too smoothly. Its surface form already contains a difference between what is said and what is distributed. That makes Δ immediately available. It contains a relational or institutional setting, so □ is immediately relevant. It explicitly names uneven distribution, so Ω is tempting. It involves the possible reframing of asymmetry as parity, so Φ appears central. But precisely because the fit is so clean, the analysis is at risk of over-assimilation. PMS can make the pattern look formally legible before the evidence supports such legibility. EDEN can make the pattern look like false coordination before real Ω, displaced R, and blocked or simulated Σ/Ψ are established. MIP can make the structure look like immaturity before any role-holder’s knowledge, capacity, alternatives, predictability, or action-power are known. AHP therefore becomes important not because the analysis is weak, but because the analysis is strong enough to need hardening.

The philosophical closure-demand is also a demand about responsibility. If parity is asserted while burdens remain uneven, who is responsible for the mismatch? The speaker of parity-language? The institution maintaining the frame? The role that benefits from symmetry-talk? The role that bears the cost? The analyst who interprets the relation? The system of expectations that makes the mismatch invisible? PMS refuses to answer too quickly. It treats responsibility as structurally derived and dependency-sensitive. EDEN refuses to answer by vibe. It requires scene-established real asymmetry, displaced responsibility, and impaired correctability before false coordination can be claimed. MIP refuses to answer by moral intensity. It asks whether knowledge, power/control, predictability, alternatives, and serious integration attempts are present. AHP refuses to let the analysis smuggle a verdict through language that only appears guarded.

This makes Pseudo-Symmetry philosophically important because it brings together three different pressures: the pressure to see asymmetry, the pressure to respect legitimate difference, and the pressure to avoid using formal caution as a cover for inaction. If the analysis closes too quickly, it risks accusing what may be formal equality, functional differentiation, or aspirational reform. If the analysis refuses all structural naming, it risks allowing parity-language to erase real burden. If the analysis treats its own caution as final neutrality, it may fail to preserve affected-role contestability. The case therefore sits at the point where structural intelligence and methodological humility must operate together.

The case is not being solved metaphysically because the completed YAMLs make no claim about essence, consciousness, qualia, personhood, moral worth, ontological dignity, or the nature of persons. The term “symmetry” is not treated as an ontological proposition, and “asymmetry” is not treated as a metaphysical defect. The case is not being solved empirically because no data are present. There are no measured burdens, no observed sequence, no documented recurrence, no testimony, and no comparison set. The case is not being solved clinically because no psyche, pathology, symptom, diagnosis, mental state, personality type, or therapeutic formulation is in scope. It is not being solved legally or forensically because no liability, culpability, intent, discrimination finding, admissibility standard, or evidentiary record is present. It is not being solved diagnostically because the model is explicitly non-person-labeling. It is not being solved by implementation because no computational classifier, policy tool, or operational detector is validated.

The case therefore matters as a disciplined refusal of premature closure. It asks: how can one make the structure legible without turning legibility into capture? How can one name asymmetry without moralizing it by default? How can one notice recontextualization without treating every reframing as concealment? How can one preserve dignity without converting dignity-language into a shaming instrument? How can one build a formal article from YAML analyses without losing the YAMLs’ restraint? The answer is not a single formula. The answer is a layered rendering in which each layer adds visibility while remaining subordinate to its own boundary conditions.

The analysis is allowed to ask how PMS operators can be calibrated to that pattern. It is reversible because every operator marking can be weakened, revised, or defeated by a better frame, a concrete scene packet, or a rival formalization. It is case-bound because it applies only to the formal pattern named Pseudo-Symmetry, not to every case of inequality, difference, hierarchy, role differentiation, formal equality, institutional asymmetry, or rhetorical parity.

The claim status is also layered. PMS Core may claim that the conceptual structure is legible through the PMS operator grammar. It may not claim that a concrete scene has been captured. The metaphysical boundary is equally strong. PMS Core makes no claim about consciousness, qualia, essence, personhood, moral worth, ontological dignity, or metaphysical equality. The fact that PMS can map the pattern smoothly does not prove that PMS is complete, superior, final, uniquely ordered, or exhaustive. Smooth fit is explicitly a risk. ### Scene and frame structure

The scene is not a scene in the ordinary sense. It is a conceptual scene boundary: a relational or institutional configuration in which parity is asserted while outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations remain unevenly distributed. The word “configuration” matters. It prevents the analysis from inventing a dramatic event. There is no speaker, no listener, no transcript, no policy document, no enacted decision, no sanction, no repair attempt, no delay sequence, no institutional file, and no affected-role testimony. The “scene” is therefore a structural placeholder that allows frame questions to be asked while blocking concrete conclusions.

The frame, □, is nevertheless essential. Pseudo-Symmetry only becomes intelligible inside a frame where parity, responsibility, obligation, exposure, or outcome distribution count as relevant. Without such a frame, the difference between asserted parity and uneven distribution would remain too abstract. The frame may be relational: a partnership, family role, collegial relation, care relation, mentorship, conflict, or mutual obligation structure. It may be institutional: a rule system, organization, administrative process, legal form, policy discourse, governance arrangement, or formal equality regime. But the YAML does not specify which. Therefore frame calibration remains partial.

Inside the frame are the elements required for the pattern to be legible: a parity claim, a distributional arrangement, a possible mismatch between the two, and the question of whether the mismatch matters. The parity claim may take forms such as equality, symmetry, mutuality, neutrality, fairness, shared responsibility, reciprocal obligation, or formal sameness. The uneven distribution may concern outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, obligations, power, vulnerability, cost, language access, timing control, frame control, recognition control, or correction access. In the Core case, only four distributional dimensions are explicitly named: outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, and obligations. EDEN later expands the possible mapping dimensions but keeps them candidate and not scene-established. MIP then asks whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible, but suspends the answer.

Outside the frame are all claims that require more than the conceptual pattern provides. Outside are motives, intentions, hidden strategies, subjective experiences, psychological drives, legal wrongs, clinical meanings, moral character, person status, institutional guilt, and empirical recurrence. Also outside are any conclusions about whether the parity claim is false, manipulative, ideological, merely formal, aspirational, transitional, or justified. These may be rival readings, but they are not decided by the conceptual case.

Protected constraints govern the frame. PMS requires Distance, Χ, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice as application conditions. These constraints prevent the analysis from fusing with verdict, urgency, enforcement, or person-level attribution. EDEN adds a description-to-action firewall: mapping possible false coordination does not authorize confrontation, intervention, punishment, binding, obligation, or policy use. MIP adds score hygiene: A-score and M-score describe enactments or shared structural responsibility only where conditions are met; they are not essence, guilt, or dignity scores. AHP adds analysis-quality discipline: attack points describe vulnerabilities in the analysis artefact, not in persons.

The roles are abstract placeholders. One role is the parity-asserting or parity-included position: the position from which parity-language operates or within which parity is claimed. Another is the distributionally exposed or burdened position: the position that may bear uneven outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations. A third is the relational or institutional frame itself, which defines what counts as parity, obligation, cost, review, and justification. A fourth role emerges in the later MIP analysis: the analyst, who has interpretive power and therefore responsibility for guardrails, scope, publicness, and transmission restraint.

Temporality, Θ, is weak. The case uses the idea that uneven distribution “remains,” which suggests persistence. But suggestion is not evidence. A temporal trajectory would require sequence, duration, accumulation, recurrence, delay, correction history, expectation, or path dependence. The Core YAML therefore marks Θ as weak-to-moderate at most. EDEN refuses to infer reciprocity loss without temporal evidence. MIP refuses to plot A(t), M(t), or D(t) as real trajectories. AHP identifies the risk that model-chain coherence could be mistaken for scene evidence. The reversibility window remains open because no concrete event has closed the case.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are candidate, not established. Ω is thematically central because uneven distribution is explicitly named. But strong Ω in PMS depends on stabilized patterning, and stabilization is not evidenced. In EDEN, real Ω must be scene-established before false coordination can be claimed. In MIP, asymmetry must be tested through transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. Therefore the analysis may say that the case is about candidate asymmetry, but not that a concrete asymmetry has been proven unjustified, harmful, humiliating, irresponsible, or false-coordinated.

The consequence of non-concrete-scene status is not silence. It is disciplined conditionality. The article can render the structure in depth, but every layer must preserve the difference between a structural candidate and a finding. The case is meaningful because it shows what must be asked before closure becomes legitimate. It is not meaningful because it already supplies the answer.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading begins with Δ, Difference. In this case, Δ marks the minimal structural distinction between verbally asserted parity and uneven distribution. The distinction is the entry point of the entire analysis. Without it, there is no pseudo-symmetry to read. The case is not simply “there is asymmetry”; it is “there is a parity claim under conditions where distribution remains uneven.” Δ also distinguishes formal or rhetorical symmetry from practical or distributional asymmetry. It is active and major. But Δ alone proves very little. It does not show that the parity claim is false. It does not show that the uneven distribution is unjustified. It does not show concealment, bad faith, immaturity, responsibility, or harm. If the parity claim and the distributional pattern cannot be distinguished, or if the unevenness disappears once the correct frame is specified, the PMS reading loses its entry point. The overclaim would be to treat difference itself as verdict.

∇, Impulse, is weakly active. It names the directional pressure generated by the mismatch. A parity/asymmetry difference may orient actors, observers, or institutions toward correction, justification, denial, exposure, contestation, stabilization, or reinterpretation. But the YAML is careful: ∇ is structural pressure, not subjective desire. No resentment, manipulation, denial, anxiety, motive, or will is inferred. ∇ depends on Δ, and because Δ is active, a weak ∇ may be marked. Yet the absence of a concrete scene prevents stronger activation. If the mismatch produces no practical, argumentative, explanatory, or structural pressure in a relevant frame, ∇ should remain weak or inactive. The overclaim would be to psychologize the case from the mere existence of mismatch.

□, Frame, is active and major. Pseudo-Symmetry only becomes intelligible inside a relational or institutional frame. A parity claim must mean something somewhere. “Equal,” “mutual,” “shared,” “neutral,” or “fair” are not free-floating terms; they require a role-space in which obligations, exposure, outcomes, and responsibilities can be compared. The frame determines whether parity means formal equality, practical equivalence, reciprocal obligation, equal exposure, equal outcome, aspirational fairness, institutional neutrality, or something else. This is why frame shifts must be marked. A distribution may look uneven under one frame and justified under another. Formal equality with material difference is not automatically pseudo-symmetry. Functional asymmetry is not automatically failure. A contested frame may be the central issue. □ is therefore active, but not settled. The overclaim would be to assume the relevant frame instead of calibrating it.

Λ, Non-Event, is weak and conditional. It may name the structured absence of expected reciprocity, equalized exposure, shared obligation, acknowledgment, redistribution, accountability, or correction. Pseudo-Symmetry often invites the question: if parity is claimed, what expected equalization or recognition fails to occur? But the YAML refuses to invent the missing event. No concrete expectation is specified. It is not known whether reciprocity was expected, whether equalization was required, whether acknowledgment was due, or whether correction was possible. Λ depends on □; without a frame, absence cannot be meaningful. Because the frame is partial, Λ remains weak. The overclaim would be to treat any absence of equalization as failed reciprocity, drift, or incomplete integration. Absence may also mark non-capture, rival framing, legitimate openness, or a different norm.

Α, Attractor, is weak and conditional. The phrase “situations where” suggests a pattern-type, and institutional contexts can stabilize pseudo-symmetry into role scripts. But no recurrence is documented. There is no evidence of repetition, routine, path dependence, expectation stabilization, or role-script emergence. In PMS dependency logic, Α depends on Δ, ∇, □, and Λ. Because ∇ and Λ are weak and the scene is abstract, Α cannot be strongly activated. This matters because later operators, especially Ω and Θ, depend on stabilized patterning in the PMS path. The overclaim would be to treat a conceptual type as if it were an observed attractor. Pattern formation is not established by naming a pattern.

Ω, Asymmetry, is central but dependency-sensitive. The case explicitly concerns uneven distribution of outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, or obligations. Thematically, Ω carries the main load. It asks: what is uneven, in what direction, under which frame, with what consequences, and for whom? But PMS treats Ω as dependent on Α. Strong asymmetry in the operator chain requires more than a static claim of unevenness; it requires some stabilization or patterned role differentiation. The YAML therefore marks Ω as major but dependency-weak. It is active as a conceptual theme, not established as a concrete stabilized asymmetry. The calibration issue is crucial: outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, obligations, power, vulnerability, capacity, and role differentiation are not interchangeable. Asymmetry may be unjustified, justified, functional, transitional, accepted, hidden, contested, or irrelevant under a different frame. The overclaim would be to moralize Ω by default.

Θ, Temporality, is weak-to-moderate. The word “remain” implies persistence, but the case lacks sequence, duration, accumulation, delay, anticipation, correction history, or developmental arc. Θ depends on Ω and Α. Since Ω is dependency-weak and Α is weak, Θ cannot become a full trajectory. It can open a temporal question: does the unevenness persist? Does it accumulate? Does the parity claim delay correction? Does the distribution create future commitments or burdens? But it cannot answer those questions. The overclaim would be to infer a diachronic responsibility chain from a static conceptual mismatch. Temporality is relevant only if the unevenness endures, accumulates, anticipates future burden, or reshapes later commitments.

Φ, Recontextualization, is major and calibration-sensitive. This is one of the most important operators in the case. The parity assertion may recontextualize uneven distribution as symmetry, mutuality, fairness, formal equality, neutrality, shared responsibility, or aspirational norm. That is precisely why Pseudo-Symmetry matters. A frame can transform how a distribution appears. But Φ is not automatically concealment. Recontextualization may clarify, mask, stabilize, simplify, formalize, or project a future standard. The source frame might be distributional asymmetry; the target frame might be asserted parity. Yet without evidence, one cannot decide whether the move is ideological masking, administrative normalization, formal equality, transitional reform, symbolic parity, or legitimate reframing. The overclaim would be to absorb every disagreement, rupture, resistance, or rival frame as Φ. Recontextualization must preserve non-capture.

Χ, Distance, is active primarily as application discipline. The case itself does not establish that actors maintain reflective distance. Rather, the analysis must maintain Χ. It must suspend verdict, keep readings reversible, avoid person-level claims, prevent drift into tribunal language, and preserve rival readings. Χ is not neutrality as avoidance. It is disciplined restraint under asymmetry and uncertainty. It allows the mismatch to remain legible without forcing closure. In dependency terms, Χ depends on Φ, Θ, and □. Because the case has a frame and a recontextualization question, Χ is necessary; because Θ is weak, Χ must be especially careful not to pretend a full trajectory exists. The overclaim would be to suspend distance in the name of clarity, truth, moral urgency, or model coherence.

Σ, Integration, is weak and contested. A parity claim may produce verbal coherence. It can make a situation appear symmetrical, mutual, administratively clean, or normatively unified. But verbal coherence is not integrated praxis. Σ requires synthesis of conflicting elements into a more coherent whole. In this case, integration would require the uneven distribution to be handled coherently within the frame, not merely renamed. The YAML explicitly warns that a parity claim is not integrated because it sounds symmetrical. Σ depends on Χ and Φ. Since Φ is candidate and Χ is an application constraint, Σ remains a question rather than a finding. The overclaim would be to treat PMS coherence as proof that the case is adequately captured, or to treat rhetorical parity as integrated practice.

Ψ, Self-Binding, is weak and conditional. It becomes relevant only if parity claims are bound into durable commitments, institutional self-description, role obligations, or stable responsibility-as-self-commitment across time. The case material does not establish such durable binding. Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. Since Σ is weak, Θ is weak, and Χ is primarily analytic, Ψ cannot be strongly activated. It remains relevant mainly as a formal reminder of what a full self-binding reading would require. The overclaim would be to attribute literal selfhood, stable identity, moral maturity, or institutional subjectivity to an abstract conceptual pattern. Ψ must not become a shortcut from “the pattern is coherent” to “a self or institution is responsibly bound.”

The PMS Core reading, taken as a whole, is therefore strong in structural legibility but cautious in activation. Δ, □, Ω as theme, Φ as candidate reframing, and Χ as discipline are central. ∇, Λ, Α, Θ, Σ, and Ψ remain weak, conditional, or analytic. This is not a weak analysis. It is an analysis that knows where its strength ends.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The dependency discipline of PMS is not decorative. It is what prevents the model from becoming a closure machine. Later operators cannot be used cheaply because they presuppose earlier structures. It is easy to say that a case involves self-binding, integration, temporal responsibility, or asymmetry. It is harder to show that the required distinctions, pressures, frames, absences, patterns, temporal trajectories, and reflective distances are actually present. Pseudo-Symmetry is a particularly important test because it tempts the analyst to move too quickly from Δ to Ω to Φ to Σ to Ψ. The YAML blocks that shortcut.

Δ is available because the parity/asymmetry distinction is explicit. ∇ is only weak because structural pressure is plausible but not evidenced. □ is active because a relational or institutional frame is required. Λ is weak because no expected event is specified. Α is weak because no recurrence is shown. Ω is central but dependency-weak because PMS Ω depends on attractor formation. Θ is weak because persistence is suggested but not documented. Φ is major but must be calibrated because recontextualization has multiple possible meanings. Χ is required as application discipline. Σ is weak because integration is not the same as verbal symmetry. Ψ is analytic-only or weak because durable self-binding is not established.

This dependency structure has several consequences. First, strong Ω cannot be claimed merely because uneven distribution is named. The case is about asymmetry, but the model cannot treat every unevenness as stabilized asymmetry. The direction, type, frame, recurrence, and consequence of unevenness must be specified. Second, Θ cannot be inflated from the word “remain.” Persistence is a clue, not a trajectory. Third, Σ cannot be granted because PMS itself produces a coherent reading. Model coherence is not case integration. Fourth, Ψ cannot be inferred from institutional language, parity claims, or conceptual stability. Self-binding requires temporally extended commitment and integration.

Coherence is not proof. This sentence is one of the central lessons of the case. The PMS reading fits because the case is structurally described as a mismatch between assertion and distribution. But fit can be dangerous. If every part of the model finds a place, the model may be over-assimilating. The YAMLs explicitly mark coherence risk as high. A smooth operator chain may signal analytic strength, but it may also signal under-discrimination among rival cases: formal equality with material inequality, justified role differentiation, aspirational parity, temporary transition, administrative simplification, symbolic parity, strategic masking, and genuine pseudo-symmetry.

Non-capture is not failure. PMS is not required to absorb every remainder. Resistance, rupture, disagreement, absence, missing evidence, or rival framing may indicate a limit of PMS capture rather than drift, low integration, immature praxis, or failed calibration. The case preserves zones where formal equality, functional asymmetry, transitional reform, recognition theory, distributive justice, contract theory, institutional analysis, or discourse analysis may capture something PMS does not. Non-capture is therefore a methodological protection. It prevents PMS from redescribing every outside as inside.

PMS must not become a closure machine. A closure machine is a model that treats its own ability to translate as evidence of truth. In this case, PMS could become such a machine if it treated parity-language as Φ, unevenness as Ω, lack of evidence as Λ, recurrence as Α, coherence as Σ, and institutional identity as Ψ without calibration. It would then close the case by operator coverage rather than by disciplined evidence. The YAMLs prevent this by marking weak statuses, falsifier conditions, rival readings, and operationalization gaps.

Invalid dependency shortcuts include: using Ω strongly without Α; using Θ strongly without recurrence or persistence evidence; using Φ to absorb every rival frame; using Χ as empty neutrality or as denial; using Σ to smooth over contradiction; using Ψ to personify an institution or abstract pattern; using derived structures as primitives; using drift patterns as diagnoses; and treating PMS translation as validation. Each shortcut would preserve PMS vocabulary while violating PMS discipline.

The calibration discipline is therefore not an external caution added after analysis. It is the analysis. Pseudo-Symmetry is readable only if its readability remains reversible.

### Derived structures

Derived structures in PMS are not primitive axes. They are formula-derived constructs generated from operator compositions. The completed YAML explicitly treats Awareness_A, Coherence_C, Responsibility_R, Action_E, Dignity_in_Practice_D, and SELF_FIXPOINT as derived, not independent explanatory layers. This matters because a derived structure can be considered without being activated. It can be weak, conditional, under-specified, analytic-only, or an application constraint. The case repeatedly uses that distinction.

Awareness_A is weak. Its formula is Θ, □, and Δ: sustained, framed differentiation across time. The case has Δ because asserted parity and uneven distribution are distinguished. It has □ because a relational or institutional frame is required. But Θ is weak because temporal structure is only suggested by “remain.” Therefore Awareness_A appears only as a weak structural awareness of the distinction, not as phenomenological consciousness, actor awareness, moral insight, or empirical recognition. The analysis can say that the structure makes a distinction available; it cannot say that any actor knows, sees, denies, or understands it.

Coherence_C is weak. Its formula involves Θ, Λ, □, and ∇: temporally stabilized structuring of impulse and expectation within a frame. Pseudo-Symmetry may show a tension between narrative coherence and distributional mismatch. Parity-language may provide formal or rhetorical coherence. Uneven distribution may destabilize that coherence. But the required components are weak: Θ is under-specified, Λ is conditional, ∇ is weak, and □ is partial. Coherence_C therefore cannot be treated as integrated validity. The case demonstrates the risk of verbal coherence, not the achievement of mature coherence.

Responsibility_R is weak and conditional. Its formula is Ψ, Φ, Θ, and Ω: self-binding orientation toward asymmetry extended across time and recontextualization. The case mentions responsibilities and obligations as unevenly distributed, but PMS Responsibility_R is not the same as ordinary blame or legal responsibility. It requires asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, and self-binding. In this case, Ω is candidate and dependency-weak, Θ is weak, Φ is candidate, and Ψ is not established. Responsibility_R therefore remains a question. Uneven obligation alone is not moral blame, legal liability, diagnosis, or proof of failed praxis.

Action_E is under-specified. Its formula is Σ, Θ, and ∇: integrated realization of directedness across time. The conceptual case does not provide action sequence, enactment, intervention, decision, embodied practice, or temporally integrated realization. It provides a pattern of assertion and distribution. Therefore Action_E cannot be activated. This is one of the strongest safeguards against turning the case into a behavioral judgment. A verbal parity claim may be part of action in a concrete scene, but in this conceptual case it remains insufficient for Action_E.

Dignity_in_Practice_D is active only as an application constraint. Its formula is Ψ, Χ, and Ω: self-bound reflective restraint and protection in asymmetrical relations. The case’s scene does not provide enough evidence to judge whether D is preserved or violated there. But the analysis itself must preserve Dignity-in-Practice by handling asymmetry without ranking, humiliating, diagnosing, or ontologically evaluating persons or groups. D is praxeological, not metaphysical. It is not an ontological superiority claim. It does not rank dignity. It requires restraint under asymmetry. In the later MIP layer this becomes even more explicit: D remains a protection signal and communication-hygiene guardrail, not a full D-profile.

SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only and not primary. Its formula sequence is the full chain Ψ ∘ Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ. In PMS, this can represent self-binding as the result of integrated, reflexively modulated, temporally extended, asymmetry-aware praxis. But Pseudo-Symmetry does not warrant a literal selfhood, identity, institutional subjectivity, or stable self-relation claim. SELF_FIXPOINT is useful only as an analytic reminder of what a strong Ψ reading would require. It must not be applied to abstract concepts or institutions as literal subjectivity unless explicitly scoped as structural self-binding.

The derived-structure result is therefore highly disciplined. Awareness is weak, not consciousness. Coherence is weak, not proof. Responsibility is conditional, not blame. Action is under-specified, not enactment. Dignity-in-Practice is an application constraint, not a dignity score. SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only, not subjectivity. The YAML’s architecture is preserved precisely by refusing to let derived terms become primitives.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core YAML treats drift patterns with particular caution. The case makes drift thinkable, but it does not establish drift as a finding. This distinction is essential. A drift pattern in PMS is not a diagnosis, not a person type, not an institutional label, not a clinical category, and not a moral verdict. It is a structurally derived distortion risk that may become relevant only when the required operator conditions are sufficiently specified.

In Pseudo-Symmetry, the two drift patterns considered are **AD_A>>E** and **AD_Sigma_low**. Both are marked as possible but not entailed. This means the case is structurally adjacent to them, but the YAML refuses to activate them as findings.

**AD_A>>E**, or “Excessive Distance between Awareness and Enactment,” would become relevant if evaluative or positional asymmetry became stabilized through attractor formation while recontextualization reinforced evaluation rather than balanced enactment. Its structural basis involves Φ, Α, and Ω: recontextualization, attractor formation, and asymmetry. In the present case, that structure is tempting because parity-language may recontextualize uneven distribution as symmetry, and uneven distribution is conceptually named. But the missing element is stabilization. No recurrence, routine, path dependence, or repeated evaluative structure is documented. There is also no concrete enactment pattern against which “awareness” and “action” could be separated. For that reason, the YAML keeps AD_A>>E as a risk only.

The danger would be to treat the mere gap between parity-language and uneven distribution as already proving excessive distance between awareness and enactment. That would be too strong. It would imply that someone, or some role, “knows” the asymmetry and fails to enact correction. But the case does not establish knowledge, available correction, action-power, or refusal. It only establishes a conceptual mismatch. Therefore AD_A>>E remains a warning about what could happen in a concrete recurrent scene, not a conclusion about the conceptual pattern.

**AD_Sigma_low**, or fragmented integration, is likewise possible but not entailed. This drift pattern concerns cases where recontextualization outpaces integration. Frequent reframing without adequate integration can generate unstable trajectories and weak coherence of praxis, especially under asymmetrically loaded conditions. In Pseudo-Symmetry, the risk is clear: parity-language may recontextualize asymmetry faster than the frame integrates the uneven distribution into coherent practice. A system may repeatedly rename burden as mutuality, exposure as equality, or obligation displacement as shared responsibility. But again, the present YAML does not provide recurrence, chronic reframing, unstable trajectories, inconsistent responsibility, or repeated failure of integration.

The analysis therefore distinguishes conceptual plausibility from structural activation. AD_Sigma_low is a risk if parity-language repeatedly functions as a smoothing device that prevents integration. It is not a finding merely because integration is weak. Weak Σ is not the same as fragmented integration drift. Weak Σ means that integrated handling is not established. Drift would require additional evidence that the relation between recontextualization and integration has become patterned, consequential, and structurally unstable.

This drift-pattern discipline is one of the most important safeguards in the case. Risk is not finding. Under-specification is not failure. Non-assignment is not a low score. If the YAML says that a drift pattern is not active, this is not because the pattern has been disproved. It is because the case lacks the evidentiary and dependency conditions required to assert it. A concrete scene might later support a drift reading. But the conceptual case does not.

The same logic applies to coherence and under-discrimination risks. The Core YAML explicitly marks coherence risk as high because PMS fits the case smoothly. Pseudo-Symmetry is already structurally phrased as a mismatch between assertion and distribution. PMS can map this elegantly: Δ distinguishes the mismatch, □ supplies frame, Ω names unevenness, Φ names parity-reframing, Χ maintains analytic distance, and Σ tests integration. But smoothness is not sufficiency. The smoother the model fit, the greater the danger that the model will under-discriminate among different phenomena.

A justified functional asymmetry could look like pseudo-symmetry if one ignores the relevant role frame. Formal equality with material difference could look like false coordination if one assumes practical equivalence was claimed. Transitional reform could look like hypocrisy if one ignores time-bounded correction. Symbolic parity could look like concealment if one mistakes symbolic recognition for operational distribution. Administrative simplification could look like ideological masking if one does not examine the purpose and limits of the simplification. The PMS drift-pattern section therefore serves as a restraint against interpretive acceleration.

The final PMS drift status is: the case has drift-risk visibility, not drift activation. It shows where PMS should look if a concrete scene were later supplied. It does not diagnose the case, the roles, the frame, or any person.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant add-on in this case is **PMS-EDEN**. It is explicitly treated as an overlay/application profile only. PMS.yaml remains the only PMS Base reference. PMS-EDEN does not redefine PMS operators, does not change PMS dependencies, does not rewrite the completed PMS Core pass, and does not convert EDEN constructs into PMS meta-axioms. Adult Reciprocity, Action-Power, correctability, Reciprocity Loss, AEP, PFO, and false-coordinated real asymmetry are overlay handles, not PMS operators.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer here because the Core reading identifies the structural pattern but does not isolate EDEN’s more specific concern: the possibility that a parity frame may coordinate real asymmetry in a way that weakens responsibility, integration, self-binding, cost visibility, and correctability. PMS Core can say: there is Δ between asserted parity and uneven distribution; there is a □ frame; Ω is central but dependency-weak; Φ may recontextualize asymmetry as parity; Χ must preserve distance; Σ and Ψ remain weak. EDEN asks a further question: when does such a structure become false coordination of real Ω?

The EDEN overlay’s primary construct is **False-Coordinated Real Asymmetry**. The overlay reads Pseudo-Symmetry as a conceptual candidate for that mechanism, but not as an established EDEN finding. The core EDEN formula, in its final corrected form, is approximately: scene-established real Ω + asserted parity frame + displaced R + blocked, weakened, simulated, avoided, or externalized Σ/Ψ + eroded correctability = false-coordinated real Ω. In this case, none of the required concrete conditions is fully available. Real Ω is conceptually visible because uneven outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, and obligations are named. But it is not scene-established because bearer, direction, cost, capacity, alternatives, protection, action-power, and effect chains are absent. Displaced R is not shown. Blocked or simulated Σ/Ψ is not shown. Correctability erosion is not shown. Therefore the result is a suspended EDEN false-coordination question.

What EDEN makes visible beyond PMS Core is the role of coordination. PMS Core can describe recontextualization through Φ. EDEN sharpens the difference between truthful coordination of asymmetry and false coordination of asymmetry. Adult Reciprocity, in EDEN, functions as a positive baseline: real asymmetry can be coordinated maturely if it remains visible, answerable, proportionate, correctable, and dignity-preserving. EDEN therefore avoids the crude claim that asymmetry itself is failure. Its concern is not asymmetry but the coordination of asymmetry under a frame that may obscure or displace responsibility and correction.

The add-on also makes cost distribution more explicit. PMS Core names uneven outcomes, exposure, responsibilities, and obligations. EDEN asks who bears visibility cost, explanation cost, reaction cost, waiting cost, binding cost, protection cost, speech cost, repair cost, dignity cost, legibility cost, and option loss. In the present case all of these remain candidate or indeterminate. The Core material names cost-relevant dimensions but not the bearer or distribution of those costs. EDEN therefore marks cost-distribution questions rather than answering them.

Action-Power is another EDEN handle. It is not a PMS operator. It asks who has the real capacity to structure praxis conditions. In a concrete case, Action-Power could involve timing control, frame control, recognition control, language access, correction access, institutional leverage, or the capacity to impose waiting and explanation burdens. Here, no such vectors are mapped. The overlay therefore marks Action-Power as not mappable.

EDEN also evaluates non-action and non-binding, but only under strict constraints. Non-action is not praxis by default. Silence, delay, refusal, absence, or non-response becomes structurally relevant only when it changes the scene under □, Λ, Ω, and Θ. Similarly, non-binding is not avoidance by default. It may preserve legitimate openness, uncertainty, boundary, incapacity, restraint, or non-involvement. The case contains no concrete non-action or non-binding, so EDEN refuses to classify them.

The overlay considers immunization, overexposure, and AEP, but none is supported. Immunization would require an effect-without-full-responsibility relation. Overexposure would require responsibility-without-full-legibility or protection. AEP would require high exposure, low legitimate language, critique-to-threat switch, and responsibility overload. The conceptual case provides none of these. This is important because PMS-EDEN can be misused if its profile vocabulary is applied by vibe. The YAML expressly rejects sex truths, person-fixed labels, profile findings, or PFO-by-vibe readings.

PFO or regime stabilization is also not claimed. A downstream regime reading would require recurrence, stabilization, pattern formation, and correctability erosion. Since Α is weak in PMS Core and the EDEN scene packet is absent, no PFO finding is possible. Reciprocity Loss is not established either. It would require concrete evidence that shared correctability has eroded. The conceptual case only raises the question of correctability; it does not show its loss.

The reduced signatures in EDEN are treated only as overlay shorthand. They may organize visibility, but they cannot change PMS dependencies. They cannot prove an EDEN finding. They cannot function as alternative derivation paths. They cannot replace the need for scene-established real Ω, responsibility trace, Σ/Ψ status, cost distribution, and counter-scene exposure. The final EDEN signature remains suspended: candidate conceptual real Ω + asserted parity frame + under-specified R + under-specified Σ/Ψ + untested correctability = suspended EDEN false-coordination question.

The overlay remains subordinate to PMS Base. PMS Core keeps the operator grammar. EDEN sharpens one application domain: the possible false coordination of real asymmetry under parity-language. It does not become the base model. It does not retroactively strengthen Core operator statuses. It does not convert weak Ω into established real Ω. It does not convert weak Σ into blocked Σ. It does not convert weak Ψ into externalized Ψ. It does not convert conceptual relevance into a scene finding.

### Layer interaction

The interaction among PMS Core, PMS-EDEN, MIP, and AHP is layered rather than additive in the sense of accumulating verdicts. Each layer adds visibility but also adds constraints. The final article must therefore preserve what remains Core, what the overlay sharpens, what MIP reframes, and what AHP hardens.

PMS Core remains the operator grammar. It defines the case through Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, and Ψ, with derived structures treated as formula-derived rather than primitive. Its main result is a dependency-sensitive structural reading: Pseudo-Symmetry is readable as a Δ between parity-language and uneven distribution within □, with Ω central but dependency-weak, Φ as a candidate recontextualization, Χ as required application discipline, and Σ/Ψ weak or conditional. This remains PMS Core. No later layer changes it.

PMS-EDEN sharpens the false-coordination question. It asks whether asserted parity truthfully coordinates real Ω or instead functions as a candidate false-coordination frame. It adds Adult Reciprocity, Action-Power, correctability, cost distribution, non-action/non-binding discipline, immunization/overexposure checks, AEP non-activation, counter-scene protocol, and description-to-action firewall. But it must not redefine PMS operators. EDEN language could overreach if “candidate real Ω” were read as established real Ω, if “asserted parity” were read as false coordination, if “under-specified R” were read as displaced responsibility, or if “weak Σ/Ψ” were read as blocked or externalized Σ/Ψ. The overlay’s strength lies in refusing those overreaches.

MIP then reframes the completed PMS and EDEN readings through maturity-in-practice. It asks how the case looks through A, C, R, and P: awareness, coherence, responsibility, and power-to-act. It also asks how asymmetry fares under the IA-box: transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. MIP does not rescore PMS or EDEN. It does not decide EDEN false coordination. Instead, it confirms that the case is not scene-scoreable. A-score is suspended. M-score is not assignable. Responsibility is capped or suspended by missing knowledge, control, predictability, alternatives, and serious integration attempts. D remains a protection and communication-hygiene signal only, not a full D-module activation. MIP thereby prevents the conceptual case from sliding into maturity judgment.

AHP then analyses the analysis artefact. It does not analyse the case directly. It checks precision, attack surface, and hardening needs. It gives the completed MIP analysis a high Precision Heuristic, but that score is not a maturity score. It is an analysis-quality measure. It identifies attack points: model-chain evidence may be mistaken for scene evidence; suspended results may be misread as weak confirmation; non-operational A-band references may detach from their boundary; D protection signals may be mistaken for D-module activation; IA review ownership is unspecified; publicness risk is not operationally staged; bridge language is dense. AHP does not change the base results. It improves the reader’s ability to see where misuse might occur.

The layers therefore interact by successive restraint. PMS makes the structure legible. EDEN makes false-coordination risk visible. MIP asks whether the practice can be maturely assessed and concludes that concrete scoring is suspended. AHP asks whether the analysis itself is precise enough and identifies its vulnerabilities. The stack does not converge on a verdict. It converges on a disciplined non-verdict.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. PMS preserves non-capture and rival readings. EDEN preserves counter-scenes and falsifiers. MIP preserves working-note status and transmission limits. AHP preserves attack points and a hardening backlog. The final layered reading is not weaker because it does not close. It is stronger because it knows what closure would require.

### Example 1 — Formal parity with transparent role asymmetry

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Non-capture and rival readings,” before “Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions.”

A small research institute adopts a written policy stating that all project members have “equal standing” in strategic discussion. In weekly meetings, every person may speak, propose agenda items, object to decisions, and request review. At the same time, the principal investigator carries final grant accountability, signs external reports, answers to the funder, and bears the formal risk if deliverables fail. Junior researchers have equal deliberative access inside the meeting, but they do not carry the same external reporting obligations. The policy therefore asserts parity in one frame while responsibilities and exposure remain uneven in another.

The vignette is bounded. It does not describe a whole institution, a whole person, or a moralized hierarchy. It gives only a narrow configuration: equal deliberative standing within a meeting frame, unequal external accountability within a funding frame. This is enough to make Pseudo-Symmetry relevant as a question, but not enough to prove it as a finding.

What the example demonstrates is the difference between **asserted parity** and **false parity**. The parity claim may be real within the meeting frame even while asymmetry remains real in the reporting frame. A careless reading would say: “They claim equality, but one role carries more responsibility, so this is pseudo-symmetry.” The PMS Core reading blocks that shortcut. The first task is not to decide whether the parity claim is false. The first task is to calibrate the frame. What kind of parity is being asserted? Is it equality of voice, equality of formal status, equality of obligation, equality of risk, equality of exposure, or equality of outcome? In this vignette, the claim appears to concern deliberative standing, not grant liability. That distinction matters.

The PMS Core hooks are straightforward but restrained. **Δ** marks the distinction between equal deliberative standing and unequal external responsibility. **□** is decisive because there are at least two frames: internal meeting procedure and external grant accountability. **Ω** is present as role asymmetry, but it is not automatically a failure. The principal investigator’s heightened responsibility may be transparent, role-justified, and institutionally necessary. **Θ** is only relevant if the arrangement persists, becomes burdensome, or prevents correction over time. **Φ** appears if the phrase “equal standing” is used ambiguously across frames. If “equal standing” begins to imply equal responsibility for funder-facing consequences, the frame may be recontextualizing one asymmetry into another. But that is not established merely by the wording. **Χ** requires the analyst not to force a verdict. **Σ** remains conditional: the arrangement may be coherent if the two frames are clearly distinguished, or incoherent if the parity language hides responsibility gradients. **Ψ** is not established unless the institute durably binds itself to a practice that either preserves or obscures the distinction.

The PMS-EDEN overlay is relevant but suspended. The example contains candidate real Ω: responsibility and exposure are not evenly distributed. But false coordination is not established. Adult Reciprocity may even be partially visible if the institute transparently says: “All members have equal deliberative standing; external grant accountability remains role-specific; review is available if role burdens distort voice.” In that version, asymmetry is coordinated rather than hidden. EDEN would ask whether the parity language truthfully coordinates the asymmetry or makes it illegible. It would also ask whether responsibility, cost, and correctability remain available. If junior researchers are later told that they are “equally responsible” for failures they had no power to prevent, the EDEN risk increases. But in the bounded vignette as stated, the asymmetry is not yet false-coordinated.

The MIP status is likewise non-scoring. A-score and M-score should not be assigned. The example is too small and too role-specific for a maturity score. MIP can only show what would need to be checked: Are the frames transparent? Are role responsibilities justified? Are review points available? Can junior members contest responsibility if accountability language expands beyond their actual power? Does the principal investigator carry disproportionate burden without support? The IA-box would ask whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound or reviewed, and reversible where possible. In this vignette, transparency and justification may be plausible, but they are not proven.

The AHP relevance lies in preventing a reader from overusing the example. The example is not evidence that hierarchical responsibility is mature. It is also not evidence that parity language is harmless. It is an illustration of why frame calibration matters. AHP would flag any article wording that treats the vignette as a resolved case rather than a bounded demonstration.

Forbidden claims include: that the principal investigator is more mature, more responsible as a person, or higher in dignity; that junior researchers are less responsible as persons; that the institute is legally compliant or non-compliant; that the parity policy is empirically effective; that the hierarchy is morally justified in all respects; or that PMS validates the policy.

The non-capture note is important. A labor-law analysis, organizational sociology analysis, or distributive justice analysis might treat this vignette differently. It might ask whether formal voice equality is meaningful under grant dependency, whether junior researchers can safely disagree, or whether external accountability indirectly shapes internal speech. PMS and EDEN do not exhaust those questions.

The article takeaway is that Pseudo-Symmetry cannot be identified simply by finding parity-language beside uneven distribution. A mature reading first asks what kind of parity is claimed, which frame governs it, whether the asymmetry is transparent and justified, and whether correction remains possible.

### Example 2 — Shared responsibility language with uneven exposure

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after the PMS-EDEN overlay section, as a bridge into “Layer interaction.”

A community mediation group publishes a statement after a conflict: “Everyone involved shares responsibility for restoring trust.” The statement is meant to reduce blame and invite repair. However, in practice, one subgroup is repeatedly asked to explain itself publicly, attend additional listening sessions, answer emotionally charged questions, and show patience with delayed recognition. Another subgroup is praised for “openness” while carrying little comparable exposure. The language of shared responsibility remains symmetrical; the practical burden of restoration appears uneven.

This vignette is closer to the core danger of Pseudo-Symmetry than the first example, but it still must be bounded. It does not establish intent, manipulation, guilt, legal wrongdoing, or psychological motive. It describes only a possible distributional pattern: symmetrical repair language combined with asymmetrical explanation and exposure burdens. The case remains a demonstration of analytic risk, not a verdict.

The example demonstrates why PMS Core alone is necessary but not sufficient. PMS Core identifies the operator structure: **Δ** appears between shared-responsibility language and uneven repair burden. **∇** appears as pressure toward justification, contestation, or correction. **□** is the mediation frame: restoration, trust, repair, listening, and shared responsibility. **Λ** may be relevant if acknowledgment, reciprocal exposure, or redistribution of repair burden is expected but absent. **Α** would require recurrence; the vignette says “repeatedly,” so pattern formation is more plausible than in a purely static example, though still not fully measured. **Ω** is visible as candidate exposure asymmetry. **Θ** becomes more relevant because repeated requests and delayed recognition suggest temporal extension. **Φ** is central: the repair frame may recontextualize uneven exposure as shared responsibility. **Χ** is required to avoid accusing the mediation group of bad faith. **Σ** remains contested: shared repair language may either integrate conflict or smooth over unequal burden. **Ψ** remains conditional: only if the group durably binds itself to this repair pattern would self-binding become a stronger question.

The PMS-EDEN overlay sharpens what PMS Core only makes structurally visible. EDEN asks whether the symmetrical repair frame truthfully coordinates real asymmetry or false-coordinates it. Here, candidate real Ω is stronger than in the first example because exposure, explanation burden, and waiting cost are more concretely described. EDEN would ask: Who must speak? Who must listen? Who must wait? Who receives recognition? Who bears the cost of making the conflict legible? Who has Action-Power over the repair process? Who can alter the meeting format, stop repeated exposure, or require reciprocal accountability?

Still, EDEN must not jump to a finding. It would need to know whether the asymmetry is acknowledged, whether it is justified by the nature of the conflict, whether the exposed subgroup asked for public explanation opportunities, whether the burden is temporary, whether review points exist, and whether the other subgroup carries different but less visible costs. False coordination requires more than unequal exposure. It requires that real Ω be coordinated under a frame that displaces responsibility, blocks or weakens Σ/Ψ, and erodes correctability. This example shows a stronger candidate risk, not a completed EDEN result.

MIP becomes highly relevant as a maturity lens, but still without scoring. A mature repair practice would likely need awareness of unequal exposure, coherence between shared-responsibility language and actual burden distribution, responsibility for process design, and power-to-act over meeting structure. MIP would ask whether the mediation group can know the asymmetry, whether it has levers to redistribute exposure, whether consequences are predictable, whether alternatives exist, and whether serious integration attempts have been made. The IA-box would be central: Is the asymmetry transparent? Is it justified? Is it time-bound? Is it reversible? Can the exposed subgroup stop, contest, or reshape the process without being framed as resisting repair?

D remains a protection signal only. The vignette includes possible dignity risks because public explanation burden can become humiliating if one group must repeatedly render itself understandable under asymmetrical conditions. But the analysis must not produce a dignity score or declare that dignity has been violated. It can only mark a D-sensitive risk: repair language must not become a socially acceptable way to intensify exposure while calling it mutuality.

AHP would flag several attack points if this example were inserted into the article. First, readers may treat the vignette as obvious false coordination, especially because it sounds morally familiar. Second, “shared responsibility” language may be too easily framed as hypocrisy. Third, the exposed subgroup may be implicitly idealized as innocent. Fourth, the less exposed subgroup may be implicitly blamed without evidence of knowledge, power, or alternatives. AHP would therefore require boundary language: this is a candidate structure; no person-level finding follows.

Forbidden claims include: that the mediation group is manipulative; that one subgroup is morally superior; that the other subgroup is guilty; that the process is legally discriminatory; that repair language is always false coordination; that public explanation is always degrading; or that EDEN has established Reciprocity Loss. None of that follows.

The non-capture note should remain visible. A restorative justice framework, trauma-informed practice framework, discourse ethics framework, or political theory of recognition may capture dimensions that PMS and EDEN only partially render. Some public explanation burdens may be requested, empowering, or necessary for repair. Some asymmetries may reflect prior harm, differentiated roles, or safety needs. The example must remain open to those alternatives.

The article takeaway is that Pseudo-Symmetry becomes analytically sharper when parity-language governs repair while exposure costs are uneven. But sharper does not mean settled. The example demonstrates why PMS-EDEN asks for cost distribution, Action-Power, Σ/Ψ status, and correctability before naming false coordination.

### Example 3 — Aspirational parity in an unfinished reform process

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions,” before “Misuse boundary.”

A public institution announces a reform initiative under the slogan “Equal access for all users.” At the time of announcement, access remains uneven. Some users still face longer waiting times, unclear forms, language barriers, and limited appeal options. The institution openly acknowledges this and publishes a staged plan: first, translation support will be expanded; second, waiting-time data will be reviewed quarterly; third, appeal procedures will be simplified; fourth, user groups will be invited to contest whether the reforms are working. The slogan asserts parity before parity is fully realized.

This vignette is designed to test a common falsifier. The surface structure resembles Pseudo-Symmetry: parity is asserted while distribution remains uneven. But the reform frame may be aspirational rather than descriptive. If the institution says “Equal access for all users” as a future-oriented commitment, while openly naming current asymmetries and building correction pathways, then the parity claim may not be pseudo-symmetrical in the relevant sense. It may be a norm-setting declaration under transparent incompletion.

The PMS Core hooks show why the case is still analyzable. **Δ** marks the difference between the equality slogan and current uneven access. **∇** appears as reform pressure. **□** is the institutional reform frame, not merely the publicity slogan. **Λ** could name missing access, missing translation, delayed appeal, or absent clarity, but those absences are acknowledged rather than denied. **Α** may become relevant if unequal access has been recurrent, but the vignette focuses on the reform process rather than the historical attractor. **Ω** is present as access asymmetry. **Θ** is stronger here than in the abstract case because the reform plan has stages and review cycles. **Φ** is delicate: the slogan recontextualizes current inequality through future parity, but this may be legitimate aspirational framing rather than false smoothing. **Χ** requires the analyst to resist cynicism and institutional credulity alike. **Σ** depends on whether the reform actually integrates asymmetry into practice through review and correction. **Ψ** may become relevant only if the institution durably binds itself to transparent review and user contestation.

The PMS-EDEN overlay is especially useful because it can distinguish false coordination from truthful coordination of real Ω. The institution does not deny asymmetry. It names waiting times, language barriers, and appeal limitations. It offers review points. It creates potential correctability. It gives affected users some contestation standing. These are precisely the kinds of conditions that may prevent the equality slogan from becoming false coordination. The slogan may still be risky, especially if it is used externally to claim success before reform succeeds. But as bounded here, the stronger EDEN reading is suspended in favor of a counter-scene: aspirational parity with transparent asymmetry and correction design.

Adult Reciprocity is not fully established, but it is more visible than in the previous examples. The institution acknowledges real Ω and begins to coordinate it through review, translation, and appeal reforms. Action-Power is partially located in the institution’s process owners, but user groups are given some contestation power. Correctability is not proven, but it is explicitly designed as a procedural aim. Reciprocity Loss is not established; indeed, the vignette describes an attempted recovery of correctability.

MIP reads the vignette as a maturity question, not a maturity score. A mature practice would require the institution to know the unevenness, communicate it coherently, take responsibility for its own levers, and use its power to act in ways that reduce access asymmetry. The IA-box is particularly clear: transparency appears stronger because the institution names current unevenness; justification depends on whether the staged timeline is reasonable; time-boundedness is supported by quarterly reviews; reversibility or correction is supported by simplified appeals and user contestation. But none of this proves maturity. Implementation may fail. Review may become symbolic. User contestation may be ignored. The slogan may still become a reputational shield. Therefore the result remains conditional.

AHP status is important because this example can be misused in the opposite direction from Example 2. A reader may overcorrect and treat any open reform plan as mature. AHP would flag that as premature. The presence of review language does not prove review efficacy. The presence of user consultation does not prove voice. The presence of transparency does not prove equal access. The example demonstrates a counter-reading, not an institutional validation.

Forbidden claims include: that the institution has achieved equality; that the reform is empirically effective; that the slogan is honest in all respects; that users’ dignity is protected in practice; that legal obligations are met; that EDEN is disproved; or that PMS validates the reform. The example also forbids the opposite claims: that the slogan is hypocrisy, that the institution acts in bad faith, or that incomplete reform is pseudo-symmetry by default.

The non-capture note is broad. Administrative law, public policy evaluation, disability studies, translation-access research, organizational sociology, or user-experience auditing may be better suited to assess whether the reform works. PMS can map the structure, EDEN can test false-coordination risk, MIP can ask about maturity conditions, and AHP can harden the analysis, but none replaces empirical evaluation or affected-user testimony.

The article takeaway is that asserted parity under uneven distribution can be aspirational rather than pseudo-symmetrical when the asymmetry is named, reviewable, time-bound, contestable, and correctable. This example prepares the later conclusion by showing why the case cannot close on the slogan alone. The decisive issue is not whether parity is spoken before it is achieved, but whether the speech makes asymmetry more accountable or less accountable.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The completed YAMLs preserve rival readings as genuine alternatives, not defects. This is central to the case. A rival reading is not merely an objection to be defeated; it may preserve a dimension that PMS, EDEN, or MIP does not fully capture.

One rival reading is **formal equality with material difference**. Under this reading, the parity claim may be explicitly formal rather than practical. A constitution, rule, policy, or relational agreement may state equality of status while leaving material outcomes or obligations differentiated. If the frame is formal equality, uneven distribution does not automatically falsify parity. PMS must therefore ask what kind of parity is claimed. EDEN must not treat formal parity as false coordination unless practical equivalence, displaced responsibility, and impaired correctability are shown.

Another rival reading is **justified functional asymmetry**. Some roles are asymmetrical because their functions differ. Parent and child, mentor and apprentice, care recipient and caregiver, judge and litigant, teacher and student, administrator and applicant, or expert and novice may have unequal obligations and exposures for reasons built into the role frame. Such asymmetry may still be abusive, opaque, or unjustified in a concrete case, but it is not pseudo-symmetrical by default. This rival reading preserves the possibility that uneven distribution is transparent and role-justified.

A third rival reading is **aspirational parity or transitional reform**. A parity claim may not describe current distribution but orient future correction. An institution may say “we are equal” as a normative commitment not yet fully realized. A relation may use parity-language as an aspiration. A reform process may name parity before material implementation is complete. EDEN must distinguish this from masking. MIP must distinguish immaturity from transitional incompleteness. PMS must keep Θ open: is the asymmetry persisting without correction, or is it time-bound within a reform trajectory?

A fourth rival reading is **symbolic parity**. Parity-language may serve symbolic recognition rather than distributional equivalence. Symbolic parity can be meaningful even when burdens differ materially. It can also become a cover for material inequality. The point is that symbolic function must be specified before the analysis decides. PMS alone cannot collapse symbolic parity into false symmetry. EDEN cannot treat symbolism as false coordination unless it obscures real Ω and blocks responsibility or correctability.

A fifth rival reading is **administrative simplification**. Institutions often use symmetrical language to simplify complex distributions. This can be harmless, useful, negligent, or ideological depending on context. Administrative neutrality may conceal asymmetry, but it may also coordinate diverse roles without claiming material sameness. This rival reading preserves the possibility that parity-language functions as a practical shorthand rather than a moral or ontological claim.

A sixth rival reading is **discourse conflict over the relevant frame**. The disagreement may not be about whether distribution is uneven but about which frame should govern. One party may read the situation through formal equality; another through distributive justice; another through recognition; another through responsibility; another through institutional efficiency; another through care. PMS can map frame conflict, but it must not decide the frame by fiat. EDEN may identify false-coordination risk, but only after frame contestation is handled. MIP may ask about maturity under disagreement, but it cannot score maturity without knowing what each role could know or do.

A seventh rival reading is **distributive justice or recognition theory**. These frameworks may capture dimensions that PMS formalization does not. Distributive justice may better analyze allocation of burdens and benefits. Recognition theory may better analyze whether parity-language grants or withholds social standing. Contract theory may better analyze obligations and consent. Institutional analysis may better analyze incentives, procedures, and accountability structures. Systems theory may better analyze feedback loops. PMS does not defeat these rivals by translating their concerns into operators.

An eighth rival reading is **anti-capture**. Some cases may resist formalization not because the model is weak, but because the phenomenon includes rupture, refusal, ambiguity, absence, or lived meaning that should not be smoothed into structure. Non-fit may be real. Affected-role disagreement may not be evidence of drift. Silence may not be Λ. Resistance may not be low Σ. Refusal may not be failed maturity. Anti-capture preserves the ethical and epistemic possibility that the model should stop.

The examples inserted above demonstrate why these rival readings matter. The research-institute vignette shows formal parity with transparent role asymmetry. The mediation vignette shows a stronger candidate risk where symmetrical repair language may distribute exposure unevenly, but still without proving false coordination. The reform vignette shows aspirational parity under transparent incompletion. Together, they demonstrate that Pseudo-Symmetry is not identifiable merely by the co-presence of parity language and uneven distribution. The relevant question is how the asymmetry is framed, whether it is transparent, whether it is justified, whether it is correctable, and whether rival grammars preserve something the PMS/EDEN/MIP stack should not erase.

These rival readings are not weaknesses in the analysis. They are part of its discipline. A model that cannot tolerate rivals becomes authoritarian. The YAML stack therefore treats rival readings as standing alternatives. They can weaken, suspend, revise, or defeat the PMS/EDEN/MIP reading if they explain the pattern better.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would weaken if the parity claim and distributional asymmetry cannot be distinguished. It would also weaken if the alleged unevenness disappears once the correct frame is specified. If parity is explicitly formal, symbolic, or aspirational rather than practical, then the Δ between claimed parity and uneven distribution may not carry the same force. If the uneven distribution is transparent, role-justified, bounded, voluntarily accepted, or already subject to correction, then Ω cannot support a pseudo-symmetry reading in the same way. If no recurrence, persistence, or distributional consequence is shown, then Α and Θ remain too weak to support stronger downstream claims.

The PMS reading would become under-discriminating if it cannot separate pseudo-symmetry from legitimate role differentiation, transitional reform, formal equality, symbolic parity, strategic masking, administrative simplification, or contested frame selection. Under-discrimination is one of the key dangers in this case. If PMS treats all parity/asymmetry mismatches alike, it loses analytic value. A good PMS reading must show why this pattern is pseudo-symmetry rather than something else. The completed Core YAML does not claim to have done that fully; it marks discrimination as partial.

The PMS reading would become over-assimilative if every disagreement, rupture, resistance, missing evidence, or rival framing were redescribed as Φ, Λ, low Σ, AD_A>>E, AD_Sigma_low, or maturity failure. This is the danger of a closure machine. The model would appear generous because it can translate everything, but the translation would erase the possibility that the phenomenon exceeds or resists PMS capture.

The EDEN reading would weaken if no concrete real Ω remains after frame specification. If the uneven distribution is transparently justified by role, capacity, consent, function, or temporary transition, then false coordination is not established. If parity-language is explicitly formal or aspirational and not a practical equivalence claim, EDEN cannot treat it as a false-coordination frame without further evidence. If R, cost, Σ/Ψ, and correctability are not displaced or blocked, then the EDEN mechanism does not activate. If a rival model discriminates the case better without over-assimilation, EDEN must release the case.

The EDEN reading would also fail if it proceeds by vibe. It cannot begin from an intuition that the structure “feels” false-coordinated. It must map real Ω, responsibility distribution, Action-Power, cost-bearing, non-action/non-binding relevance, Σ/Ψ status, and correctability. It must expose counter-scenes before affirming the reading. It must allow affected-role contestation in any future concrete application. If no falsifier can matter, EDEN use is invalid.

The MIP reading would weaken if it were treated as a maturity score. The completed MIP analysis explicitly suspends A-score and M-score. A stronger MIP claim would require a concrete role, time window, context, and evidence packet. A-score would require observable awareness, coherence, responsibility, and power-to-act in enactment, not in abstract pattern. M-score would require knowledge, power/control, predictability, access to alternatives, and serious integration attempts. Because predictability and alternatives are absent, high M is capped or suspended by the ceiling rule. Any attempt to assign M anyway would violate MIP score hygiene.

The IA-box remains suspended. To strengthen it, one would need to show whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. Transparency would require that relevant roles can see and name the asymmetry. Justification would require a protected good, function, consent structure, or role basis. Time-boundedness would require review points, duration limits, correction conditions, or transition markers. Reversibility would require real exit, appeal, correction, redistribution, or return options. None is supplied.

AHP identifies additional weakening conditions at the level of analysis quality. If model-chain evidence is mistaken for scene evidence, the article becomes overconfident. If “suspended” is read as weak confirmation, the article smuggles a finding. If non-operational A-band references detach from their boundary, MIP score hygiene fails. If D protection language is read as full D-module activation, dignity-language becomes too strong. If IA review ownership remains unspecified in a concrete application, protection measures become performative rather than operational. If publicness is not staged, transmission risk increases. These are not case findings; they are hardening conditions for the analysis artefact.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled across the stack. For PMS, operationalization would require concrete scene indicators for each operator: specific parity statements for Δ, practical or argumentative pressure for ∇, frame specification for □, expected absence for Λ, recurrence for Α, direction and kind of imbalance for Ω, temporal evidence for Θ, source and target frames for Φ, distance conditions for Χ, integration criteria for Σ, and durable commitment for Ψ. For EDEN, operationalization would require scene-established real Ω, Action-Power vectors, cost distribution, displaced R, Σ/Ψ status, correctability conditions, counter-scenes, and affected-role contestation. For MIP, operationalization would require A/M evidence, IA-box conditions, publicness analysis, D guardrails, and transmission review. For AHP, operationalization would require applying hardening actions before external transmission.

The falsifier logic is therefore robust. The reading can be weakened by formal parity, justified asymmetry, transitional correction, symbolic use, transparent role differentiation, missing recurrence, absent temporal trajectory, intact correctability, better rival models, or insufficient scene evidence. This does not make the analysis indecisive. It makes it falsifiable.

### Misuse boundary

The misuse boundary must be stated strongly because the case’s vocabulary is powerful. “Pseudo-Symmetry” can easily become a weapon. It can be used to accuse a person, institution, group, or relation of hypocrisy, manipulation, bad faith, immaturity, or false equality. The completed YAML stack forbids that use.

There is no diagnosis. The case does not diagnose a person, institution, group, sex, relation, discourse, pathology, maturity level, personality type, clinical state, or mental condition. PMS drift patterns are not diagnostic categories. EDEN profiles are not person labels. MIP scores are not person scores. AHP attack points are not defects in persons.

There is no person ranking. The analysis does not rank maturity, dignity, responsibility, worth, awareness, coherence, or moral status of persons. It does not compare beings. It does not classify roles as superior or inferior. Dignity-in-Practice and MIP D are not dignity hierarchies. D₀ remains untouched and outside scoring.

There is no legal or forensic conclusion. The analysis does not establish liability, culpability, intent, discrimination, admissibility, legal violation, HR finding, policy breach, or governance conclusion. It does not provide evidence. It does not meet a standard of proof. It does not authorize investigation, sanction, enforcement, or institutional action.

There is no metaphysical proof. The case does not prove what equality, dignity, personhood, agency, subjectivity, or selfhood ultimately is. SELF_FIXPOINT remains analytic-only. Dignity is not ontologically assessed. Parity is not metaphysically validated or invalidated.

There is no empirical proof. The case contains no empirical data, no measurement, no observation, no recurrence record, no role testimony, and no comparison set. Smooth model fit is not evidence. Cross-model coherence is not scene evidence. AHP specifically flags this as an attack point.

There is no implementation validation. The YAML form does not make the analysis executable as a classifier. It does not authorize automated detection of pseudo-symmetry, false coordination, immaturity, or dignity risk. It does not validate a computational model, policy tool, governance system, or agent architecture.

There is no PMS Base validation. The case does not prove PMS. It uses PMS. PMS.yaml remains the only PMS Base reference. PMS-EDEN remains overlay only. MIP and AHP remain additional layers only. The case cannot be used to claim that PMS is complete, superior, uniquely ordered, final, or exhaustive.

There is no add-on-as-operator. Adult Reciprocity, Action-Power, correctability, Reciprocity Loss, PFO, AEP, MIP A-score, MIP M-score, IA-box, and AHP Precision Heuristic are not PMS operators. They do not alter Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, or Ψ. They organize application visibility only.

There is no drift-as-verdict. AD_A>>E and AD_Sigma_low are possible risks, not findings. A risk marker is not a label. A weak operator is not a deficiency. Under-specification is not immaturity. Suspended is not weak confirmation. Non-assignment is not low score.

There is no closure-by-authority. The analysis cannot say “because PMS maps it, it is so”; “because EDEN sees false-coordination risk, it is false coordination”; “because MIP sees maturity questions, the practice is immature”; or “because AHP gives high precision, the case is settled.” Each layer adds formal responsibility to avoid overclaim. Authority lies not in the model’s vocabulary but in its capacity to stop.

The examples are subject to the same misuse boundary. They are demonstrations, not evidence. The institute example does not prove formal parity is safe. The mediation example does not prove shared-responsibility language is false coordination. The reform example does not prove aspirational parity is mature. They prepare discrimination, not conclusion-by-illustration.

The description-to-action firewall is active. Nothing in the analysis authorizes confrontation, intervention, punishment, exposure, binding, demand, obligation, therapy, legal action, HR action, public accusation, or relational prescription. If someone wanted to apply the framework to a concrete case, the first step would not be action. It would be a bounded scene packet, affected-role contestation, counter-scenes, falsifiers, and independent review where needed.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of the case is a disciplined, layered non-verdict. Pseudo-Symmetry is structurally intelligible as a pattern in which asserted parity and uneven distribution stand in tension. PMS Core makes that tension legible through Δ, □, Ω, Φ, and Χ, while keeping ∇, Λ, Α, Θ, Σ, and Ψ weak, conditional, or analytic where the scene does not support stronger activation. PMS-EDEN then sharpens the question: does asserted parity truthfully coordinate real asymmetry, or does it false-coordinate it by displacing responsibility, weakening or simulating integration and self-binding, obscuring cost, and eroding correctability? The EDEN answer remains suspended because no scene-established real Ω, displaced R, blocked Σ/Ψ, or correctability loss is shown. MIP then reframes the same structure as a maturity-in-practice question but refuses to score it. A-score and M-score remain suspended; D remains only a protection and communication-hygiene signal. AHP finally hardens the completed analysis by identifying how the article itself could be misread, overextended, or converted into a stronger claim than the YAMLs allow.

The examples contribute discrimination. The research-institute example shows that parity-language beside asymmetry may be transparent and role-justified when frames are distinguished. The mediation example shows a stronger candidate risk: shared-responsibility language may distribute exposure unevenly and therefore calls for PMS-EDEN cost, Action-Power, Σ/Ψ, and correctability checks. The reform example shows that parity may be aspirational rather than false when current asymmetry is acknowledged, reviewable, time-bound, contestable, and open to correction. Together the examples demonstrate the article’s central discipline: pseudo-symmetry is not identified by the co-presence of parity and unevenness. It is assessed by frame calibration, dependency discipline, counter-scenes, and falsifier conditions.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is claimed. The article does not claim that the conceptual case is an empirical finding. It does not claim that any concrete person, institution, group, relation, or policy is pseudo-symmetrical. It does not claim that parity-language is false by default. It does not claim that asymmetry is failure. It does not claim that PMS proves itself by mapping the pattern. It does not claim that EDEN has found false coordination. It does not claim that MIP has scored maturity. It does not claim that D has been assessed. It does not claim that AHP precision makes the case settled. The entire stack remains reversible, non-diagnostic, non-prescriptive, and non-operational.

Boundary discipline is the methodological achievement of the case. PMS supplies operator legibility but must not become a closure machine. EDEN supplies false-coordination sensitivity but must not become suspicion by vocabulary. MIP supplies maturity-in-practice structure but must not become a scorecard. AHP supplies analysis-quality hardening but must not become validation. Each layer can clarify only by accepting its limit. That is why non-capture remains possible. Rival readings are not defects. Formal equality, functional asymmetry, aspirational parity, symbolic parity, administrative simplification, discourse conflict, distributive justice, recognition theory, and anti-capture may all preserve dimensions the stack should not erase.

The final methodological takeaway is that Pseudo-Symmetry is a case about disciplined seeing. It teaches that structural analysis must be strong enough to notice when parity-language may hide asymmetry, but restrained enough to notice when asymmetry is formal, functional, aspirational, transparent, or better captured otherwise. Coherence is not proof. Risk is not finding. Non-assignment is not low score. Suspended is not weak confirmation. Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological, not ontological. Add-on constructs remain overlay handles, not PMS operators. The case closes only in one sense: it establishes how not to close too early.
