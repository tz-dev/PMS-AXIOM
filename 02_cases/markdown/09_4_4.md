### Why this case matters

This case matters because it exposes one of the most difficult closure-demands in practical reasoning: the demand to decide whether movement from privacy into publicness is a repair of asymmetry or an erosion of boundary. The structural temptation is strong in both directions. One can say: “The private frame protected repair, trust, contextual integrity, and dignity; publicness therefore violated the original boundary.” One can also say: “The private frame concealed power, delayed accountability, and denied affected voice; publicness therefore corrected a failed or suppressive internal process.” Both readings can sound compelling, and both can become premature closure.

The case therefore belongs in AXIOM because it is not primarily about a moral preference for privacy or publicity. It is about how frames, asymmetries, temporal sequences, obligations, reply capacities, and dignity protections change when material crosses a boundary of audience and use. A statement, feedback item, complaint, memory, document, or expectation does not remain praxeologically identical merely because its words or content remain the same. The same material may function differently when held in a bounded repair conversation, an internal process, a confidential feedback channel, a public accountability discourse, a legal forum, a media environment, or a tribunal-like public scene. AXIOM is concerned precisely with these transformations: the structure of praxis under changing frames, not merely the content of utterances.

The philosophical pressure arises from the desire to force the case into a stable closure-form: privacy as dignity, publicity as accountability; or privacy as concealment, publicity as correction. This closure-demand is intensified by the fact that privacy and publicness both carry dignity rhetoric. Confidentiality can be described as humane restraint, protection of trust, non-humiliation, and preservation of repair. Publicness can be described as recognition, democratic answerability, protection of affected parties, and refusal of hidden asymmetry. Conversely, confidentiality can become impunity, silence, containment, institutional self-protection, or suppression. Publicness can become spectacle, reputational exposure, audience capture, and public tribunalization. The case is structurally difficult because the same operators that make one reading plausible also make rival readings plausible.

The case is not being solved metaphysically. PMS does not ask whether privacy or publicness has an ultimate essence, nor whether public disclosure reveals some deeper truth of a person or institution. PMS-EDEN does not treat false coordination as a metaphysical stain, and MIP does not convert maturity into an ontological attribute. The analysis remains praxeological: it concerns situated structures of action, frame, asymmetry, temporality, responsibility, power-to-act, and dignity-in-practice.

The case is not being solved empirically. The completed analyses explicitly mark the case as non-empirical. There is no record of what happened, who acted, who consented, which legal or institutional duties applied, what public was involved, whether repair failed, whether publicness was proportionate, whether harm was ongoing, or whether reply and correction were available. Empirical claims would require a concrete scene packet and external evidentiary standards. In the absence of those, the analysis can only map conditional structures.

The case is not being solved clinically or diagnostically. No actor is diagnosed, typed, pathologized, or psychologized. Impulse, absence, self-binding, dignity stress, public exposure, and responsibility are not read as symptoms or traits. The analysis never infers motive, personality, trauma, pathology, manipulativeness, maturity deficit, or inner state from the movement between privacy and publicness.

The case is not being solved legally or forensically. Terms such as confidentiality, disclosure, public accountability, tribunal, breach, right of reply, protected disclosure, defamation, retaliation, duty to warn, or public interest may become legally relevant in concrete settings, but the analyses do not decide any legal question. PMS and its overlays do not replace due process, evidence, jurisdiction, institutional procedure, or legal interpretation.

The case is not being solved by model authority. This is crucial. PMS Core fits the case well: Δ distinguishes private from public, □ marks competing frames, Θ marks transfer over time, Φ names recontextualization, Ω marks altered exposure and capacity, Χ suspends verdict, and Σ/Ψ remain conditional. PMS-EDEN also fits: real Ω may be falsely coordinated; R and Action-Power may be displaced; publicness may alter correctability; counter-scenes must remain active. MIP fits as well: A can name what is visible, C can test coherence, R can remain conditional, P can identify real levers, IA can flag asymmetry risk, and D can guard against humiliation. But cross-model coherence is not validation. The fact that a case can be rendered through PMS, EDEN, and MIP does not prove that the case has been exhausted, settled, or captured without remainder.

The deeper importance of the case lies in this refusal of forced closure. Privacy and publicness are not treated as moral substances. They are treated as frames of praxis that may protect, distort, conceal, expose, repair, or degrade depending on how they coordinate real asymmetry. A mature analysis must therefore resist the closure-demand twice: it must resist the desire to sanctify privacy, and it must resist the desire to sanctify public accountability. The case becomes philosophically serious because each side can become an alibi. Confidentiality can become the alibi of repair while blocking answerability. Publicness can become the alibi of accountability while intensifying exposure. The analysis must keep both possibilities open without dissolving into indecision.

That is why this case is structurally difficult. It is not difficult because nothing can be said. On the contrary, a great deal can be said. It is difficult because the analytical grammar is powerful enough to say too much too quickly. PMS can render the frame transfer elegantly. EDEN can identify possible false coordination. MIP can describe awareness, coherence, responsibility, power, IA-risk, and D-stress. AHP can identify attack points and hardening actions. But every layer also creates a new risk of pseudo-certainty. The harder the model works, the more strongly the case requires restraint.

### Scene and frame structure

The scene boundary is conceptual. The minimal scene is this: material, utterance, or expectation initially described as confidential feedback, bounded repair, or internal resolution is later placed into public accountability discourse or tribunal-like publicness. This formulation already contains a frame transfer, but it does not yet tell us whether that transfer is warranted, excessive, protective, suppressive, legal, democratic, retaliatory, corrective, or degrading.

The source frame is the bounded repair / confidentiality frame. Within that frame, material may be governed by expectations of limited audience, contextual integrity, non-humiliation, repair, trust, internal handling, or role-bound discretion. The target frame is public accountability / disclosure / tribunal-like publicness. Within that frame, the same material may be governed by expectations of answerability, public interest, recognition, correction, exposure, proportionality, evidence, reply, and audience-legibility.

The PMS operator □ is therefore central even before the operator reading begins. The question is not merely what the material “says.” The question is what frame makes it usable, legible, and binding in a particular way. In a repair frame, the material may be oriented toward correction without exposure. In a public accountability frame, it may be oriented toward recognition, warning, or answerability. In a tribunal-like frame, it may acquire accusatory or reputational force. A frame shift changes not only audience but also burden, expectation, dignity risk, and possible responsibility.

What is inside the conceptual frame is the structural movement itself: source-frame boundedness, target-frame publicness, recontextualization, temporality, candidate asymmetry, possible non-event, integration risk, conditional commitment, publicness risk, and correctability. What is outside the frame is equally important: concrete actor blame, psychological motive, legal judgment, diagnosis, person ranking, policy decision, empirical finding, and any final claim that privacy or publicness is inherently superior.

The protected constraints are Χ, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. Χ requires reflective distance: the analysis must not fuse with urgency, exposure, containment, moral closure, or institutional self-protection. Reversibility requires that all readings remain revisable, bounded, and non-totalizing. Dignity-in-Practice requires enacted restraint under asymmetry: no shaming, humiliating, ranking, or ontological evaluation of persons or groups. These constraints apply not only to the case material but also to the analysis itself. A PMS analysis that uses PMS vocabulary to produce tribunal-style closure would fail its own application conditions.

The roles are abstract role-positions, not persons. Role_A is the bounded repair / confidentiality position. It may protect repair and dignity, or it may control access to accountability. Role_B is the public accountability / disclosure position. It may correct failed internal handling, or it may expand exposure and explanation burden. The field or medium is the public audience, institution, platform, or discourse space through which circulation, reputational pressure, reply capacity, and correction access may be shaped. The observer or analyst is also a role: analysis itself has power to stabilize language and must therefore avoid pseudo-objective verdict.

Temporality Θ is built into the case. The material is not simply private and public at once; it is generated or held under one frame and later moved into another. This sequence matters. Delay, escalation, failed repair, failed confidentiality, missing response, accumulated harm, or changing audience may all alter the meaning of the transfer. But because no chronology is supplied, Θ is conceptually active without becoming a concrete timeline.

The reversibility window is indeterminate and potentially fragile. In a public setting, circulation can reduce reversibility: once material enters an audience, platform, institution, or reputational field, correction may not fully undo exposure. Yet privacy can also reduce reversibility for affected parties if it delays recognition, prevents documentation, or blocks accountability. Reversibility is therefore not a simple privacy value. It must be mapped across both frames.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are candidate, not established. Publicness can change who is visible, who must explain, who can answer, who controls context, who benefits from audience power, who bears reputational risk, and who can access correction. Confidentiality can change who is protected, who is silenced, who controls process, who can delay, and who remains uncriticizable. PMS, EDEN, and MIP all preserve this crossed structure. No direction of asymmetry is decided without a concrete scene.

Because the case is not concrete, the analysis cannot assign responsibility to actors. It can only state the structural questions that would govern a responsible scene reading. Who knew the frame terms? Who had real alternatives? Who could preserve confidentiality without suppressing accountability? Who could disclose without humiliating or overexposing? Who controlled timing and audience? Who had right of reply? Who could correct? Who was burdened by silence, and who was burdened by publicness? These questions remain open.

The frame structure also carries a publicness gradient that the completed AHP layer identifies as insufficiently specified. Publicness may be small, bounded, semi-public, institutional, platform-public, or mass-public. It may be persistent or fleeting, searchable or ephemeral, identifiable or anonymized, correctable or practically irreversible. Without that gradient, the analysis risks treating all publicness as equivalent. The case therefore keeps “publicness” as structurally central but empirically under-described.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core pass reads the case through the eleven operators Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, and Ψ, preserving the PMS_1.3 dependency order. The operators are not psychological categories, moral verdicts, or empirical findings. They are structural functions within a grammar of praxis. In this case, several operators are active at the conceptual level, while others are weak, conditional, or analytic-only because the concrete scene is missing.

**Δ — Difference.** Δ is strongly active. The case is organized by differences: confidential versus public, internal resolution versus public accountability, repair frame versus tribunal-like frame, bounded audience versus open audience, original-use material versus secondary-use material. These distinctions are the minimal condition for any boundary-erosion reading. Without them, there would be no structural transfer to analyze.

The calibration issue is that Δ must not be moralized. The difference is not private-good versus public-bad, nor public-good versus private-bad. PMS marks the distinction; it does not decide legitimacy. Privacy may protect repair or conceal power. Publicness may correct asymmetry or overrun the original frame. If Δ were pushed too far, the analysis would convert a structural contrast into a moral binary. That would be an overclaim.

**∇ — Impulse.** ∇ is weak. The abstract case implies directional pressure: movement from containment toward disclosure, from internal repair toward public accountability, from silence toward exposure, or from private handling toward public answerability. But the completed analysis explicitly refuses to infer subjective impulse. The pressure is structural, not psychological.

The calibration issue is that ∇ cannot be used to infer motive. No desire, fear, retaliation, protection, urgency, avoidance, strategy, or affect is established. If the operator were pushed too far, the analysis would smuggle psychology into a structural model. The legitimate claim is narrower: the frame difference creates possible directional tension between boundedness and publicness.

**□ — Frame.** □ is strongly active. The case turns on competing frames. The source frame is confidential feedback, bounded repair, or internal resolution. The target frame is public accountability discourse or tribunal-like publicness. The meaning of the material depends on which frame governs its use, audience, obligation, and consequences.

The calibration issue is that neither frame may be presumed legitimate. The internal frame is not automatically restorative, and the public frame is not automatically tribunalizing. A bounded repair process can be meaningful, but it can also be suppressive. Public accountability can be necessary, but it can also degrade into exposure without integration. If □ were used cheaply, the analysis would treat the mere existence of a source frame as proof that public transfer is erosion, or the mere existence of public accountability language as proof that public transfer is justified. PMS does neither.

**Λ — Non-Event.** Λ is weak-to-moderate and conditional. Boundary erosion may be driven by a meaningful non-event: expected repair does not occur, expected confidentiality is breached, expected response is missing, expected closure is delayed, or expected accountability fails. But the abstract case does not identify which absence is operative.

The calibration issue is strict: absence must be specified within a frame. A missing reply only matters if reply was expected; failed repair only matters if repair was promised or structurally required; failed accountability only matters if accountability was due. Λ must not become a way to redescribe every gap, refusal, silence, or missing evidence as failure. Non-event may also mark principled refusal, legal constraint, evidence limits, or PMS non-capture. If pushed too far, Λ becomes a closure device: whatever is absent becomes proof of drift. That would be invalid.

**Α — Attractor.** Α is weak. The case title and language of drift suggest possible stabilization, recurrence, or path dependence, but the material does not show repeated transfers, habitual escalation, routine containment, or stable role scripts. Attractor status would require evidence of recurrence.

The calibration issue is that one abstract transfer cannot be converted into a pattern merely because PMS can describe it elegantly. If Α were overclaimed, the analysis would treat a possible one-off disclosure, escalation, or public correction as a stabilized drift pattern. The completed analysis therefore keeps Α as a risk marker, not a finding.

**Ω — Asymmetry.** Ω is structurally salient and conceptually active, but dependency-sensitive. Publicness may alter exposure, vulnerability, reputation, audience power, reply capacity, obligation, and institutional control. Confidentiality may also carry asymmetry by limiting affected voice, delaying accountability, or protecting those who control the private frame. Ω is therefore not located automatically on one side.

The calibration issue is directionality. The analysis must ask: who becomes exposed, who controls the frame, who can answer, who bears consequences, who benefits from privacy, who benefits from publicness, and who lacks correction access? Asymmetry is not moral judgment. It is a structural imbalance in power, exposure, capacity, vulnerability, obligation, or role. Because PMS_1.3 places Ω after Α, full recurrent asymmetry would require more evidence than this abstract case supplies. Still, candidate Ω is visible enough to remain a major operator. If pushed too far, Ω would become a moral verdict or power-label rather than a calibrated structural question.

**Θ — Temporality.** Θ is active at the conceptual level. The case is temporal because material is generated or held under one frame and later placed into another. Meaning changes through delay, escalation, accumulation, reuse, failed repair, widening audience, or altered reply conditions. The movement is not a static contrast; it is a trajectory.

The calibration issue is that the concrete chronology is absent. Θ may mark conceptual sequence, but it cannot establish who knew what when, whether publicness was premature, whether internal repair had failed, whether disclosure was delayed, or whether alternatives were available. If pushed too far, Θ becomes hindsight. The analysis would retroactively impose a developmental arc on a scene whose timing is unknown.

**Φ — Recontextualization.** Φ is the central PMS Core operator. The case is primarily a recontextualization case: existing material or expectation is placed into a new frame, changing its relevance, audience, exposure, and possible meaning. Material made for bounded repair or internal resolution becomes material in public accountability discourse or tribunal-like publicness.

The calibration issue is decisive. Φ names the transformation; it does not judge it. Recontextualization may be erosion, correction, escalation, protection, whistleblowing, procedural disclosure, democratic publicness, or distortion. The same operator can support several rival readings. PMS must distinguish frame transfer from mere repetition, consented publication, legally required disclosure, anonymized learning, independently generated criticism, or material that was public from the outset. If Φ were pushed too far, every public movement would be redescribed as drift, and every rival account would be absorbed into PMS vocabulary. The completed analysis explicitly blocks that over-assimilation.

**Χ — Distance.** Χ is active both as a case question and as an application constraint. The analysis must hold privacy and publicness at reflective distance. Confidentiality is not automatically dignity-preserving; public accountability is not automatically tribunalizing. The analysis must refuse to become either a public verdict machine or a private-containment defense.

The calibration issue is self-application. A PMS analysis that suspends distance in order to force clarity invalidates itself as a PMS application. Χ requires non-ranking, reversibility, rival-openness, and refusal of premature closure. At the case level, actor-distance cannot be established because no actors are supplied. At the analytic level, Χ is mandatory. If pushed too far in the wrong way, Χ could become endless suspension that evades responsibility; if suspended, the model becomes tribunal or concealment. The YAML-derived reading keeps it as disciplined distance, not paralysis.

**Σ — Integration.** Σ is weak but analytically important. The case asks whether privacy and accountability can be integrated without concealment or humiliation. The central risk is that Φ outpaces Σ: material moves frames before repair, accountability, exposure, reply, proportionality, and protection are coherently coordinated.

The calibration issue is that integration is not smoothing. Σ does not mean reconciliation, narrative closure, institutional self-protection, public consensus, or the mere fact that PMS can produce a coherent formula. Real integration would discriminate: repair without concealment, accountability without tribunal capture, privacy without impunity, publicness without degradation. Because the concrete scene lacks evidence of such coordination, Σ remains weak. If pushed too far, Σ would become forced coherence.

**Ψ — Self-Binding.** Ψ is weak and conditional at the case level, active as application discipline. It would become case-active only where actual commitments are specified: confidentiality promises, repair duties, public-interest obligations, non-retaliation norms, answerability commitments, or institutional self-binding across time. None is supplied.

The calibration issue is that Ψ must not infer literal selfhood, maturity failure, person responsibility, or blame. In the Core case, Ψ primarily governs the analysis itself: the PMS application must bind itself to Χ, reversibility, Dignity-in-Practice, and non-capture. If pushed too far, Ψ would convert abstract frame movement into a claim about selfhood, maturity, or culpability. The completed analysis explicitly prevents that.

The Core reading therefore does not simply list operators. It shows a structured profile: Δ and □ are clearly active; Θ and Φ are conceptually active; Ω is salient but directionally indeterminate; Χ is mandatory as application discipline; Λ, Α, Σ, and Ψ are weak, conditional, or under-specified in different ways; ∇ remains structural rather than psychological. The resulting reading is strong enough to map the case-family, but not strong enough to close it.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The PMS Core pass preserves dependency discipline because later operators are analytically tempting in exactly the places where evidence is weakest. Once the case is seen as a frame transfer, it is easy to say that recontextualization proves failed integration, failed responsibility, or self-binding breakdown. The analysis blocks this shortcut. PMS operators must be activated through their dependencies and calibrated against the actual scene status.

Later operators cannot be used cheaply. Ω depends on pattern formation in the repository path, so established asymmetry as recurrent role structure requires more than an abstract exposure contrast. Θ depends on Ω and Α, so a concrete trajectory requires more than the thought that material moved from one frame to another. Φ depends on Θ, Ω, and □, so recontextualization is central but still needs the source frame, target frame, temporal transfer, and asymmetry context to be specified. Χ depends on Φ, Θ, and □, and in this case is strongest as an application constraint. Σ depends on Χ and Φ, so integration cannot be asserted unless the analysis shows how conflicting frames are held together without collapse. Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ, so self-binding cannot be inferred where commitments and integration are not evidenced.

Σ and Ψ therefore remain conditional. The case raises integration questions but does not establish integration failure. It raises commitment questions but does not establish broken self-binding. The analysis may say: if confidentiality promises existed, if repair duties were assumed, if public-interest duties applied, if non-retaliation commitments were present, then Ψ could become active in a stronger way. But it may not infer those commitments from the abstract case title.

Coherence is not proof. The PMS chain fits: Δ → ∇ → □ → Λ → Α → Ω → Θ → Φ → Χ → Σ → Ψ can be read across the case-family. But a smooth chain can under-discriminate. Harmful exposure, warranted disclosure, whistleblowing, public-interest correction, legal obligation, institutional concealment, and ordinary recontextualization can all be made legible in PMS terms. The completed analysis therefore identifies high coherence risk: the model can cover the case too easily. Coverage is not discrimination.

Non-capture is not failure. Rival readings are not treated as model defects. Some dimensions may resist PMS capture or require other grammars: legal-formal analysis, procedural justice, contextual integrity, whistleblowing ethics, public-sphere theory, institutional power analysis, phenomenology of exposure, or language-practice accounts of confidentiality. PMS does not lose legitimacy by preserving these zones. It loses legitimacy if it absorbs them automatically as drift, non-event, failed integration, or immaturity.

PMS must not become a closure machine. The entry condition is not ornamental. Distance, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice are conditions of valid application. A PMS reading that uses structural vocabulary to shame, rank, expose, diagnose, or declare final truth violates PMS. In this case, the danger is especially acute because the topic itself concerns publicness and tribunal-like framing. The analysis must not reproduce the very exposure dynamics it maps.

Invalid dependency shortcuts include: treating public transfer as Φ drift without testing consent, legality, necessity, safeguards, prior repair failure, and proportionality; treating missing response as Λ failure without specifying the expected event; treating candidate exposure as Ω verdict; treating weak Σ as proof of immaturity; treating conditional Ψ as person-level responsibility; treating derived Responsibility_R as blame; treating Dignity-in-Practice as a dignity score; treating YAML representation as operational adequacy; and treating model coherence as PMS Base validation.

Dependency discipline also protects against the false comfort of a complete chain. In a model like PMS, a full chain can be formally available while materially under-specified. The case can be rendered through all eleven operators, but not all operators carry equal evidentiary weight. The analysis therefore distinguishes active operators from weak, conditional, analytic-only, or under-specified operators. This is not an aesthetic preference; it is the main guardrail against turning structural elegance into authority.

Since those conditions are not supplied, R remains conditional. Non-assignment is not exoneration; it is disciplined non-attribution.

**Action_E** is under-specified. Its strict PMS formula is Σ, Θ, and ∇: integrated realization of directedness across time. The case contains possible action — public transfer, disclosure, containment, repair, escalation, refusal — but no concrete enactment is supplied. There is no actor, sequence, constraint map, or evidence of integrated directedness.

This matters because publicness is often treated as action by default. PMS resists that. A transfer may be an action, but action in the derived PMS sense requires more than behavior or occurrence. It requires directedness, temporality, and integration. Without Σ and concrete sequence, Action_E remains under-specified. EDEN’s “Action-Power” later adds visibility around the capacity to structure praxis conditions, but it does not replace PMS Action_E.

**Dignity_in_Practice_D** is active as an application constraint, not as a metaphysical dignity claim. Its formula is Ψ, Χ, and Ω: self-bound reflective restraint and protection in asymmetrical relations. In this case, D requires the analysis to handle asymmetry without humiliation, ranking, shame, or irreversible labels. It also requires publicness and confidentiality to be assessed in terms of how they protect or endanger dignity under asymmetry.

D does not say that one side is dignified and the other is not. It does not produce a dignity score. MIP later distinguishes D0, D1, D2, and D-P: ontological dignity remains untouched; self-treatment is not assessed; relational and public dignity are structurally relevant but not person-level findings. Dignity-in-Practice therefore functions as a restraint on analysis, not as a verdict.

**SELF_FIXPOINT** is analytic-only. The PMS Core YAML includes SELF_FIXPOINT as a derived chain: Ψ ∘ Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ. In this case, however, SELF_FIXPOINT is not the primary structure and is not applied as literal selfhood. It can appear only as analytic self-binding of the PMS application itself, or, with additional evidence, as institutional norm-binding across privacy and accountability frames.

The non-capture note is important: SELF_FIXPOINT must not be applied to abstract concepts, institutions, or role positions as if they possessed literal subjectivity. It does not prove agency, identity, selfhood, maturity, or consciousness. In this case, its main function is disciplinary: the analysis binds itself not to overclaim.

The derived-structure profile therefore mirrors the operator profile. Awareness_A is active at structural level. Coherence_C is weak. Responsibility_R is weak and conditional. Action_E is under-specified. Dignity_in_Practice_D is active as a constraint rather than a finding. SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only. The profile is deliberately uneven. A mature PMS application does not force all derived structures into equal activation simply because the formal grammar can name them.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core analysis treats drift patterns as possible structural risks, not as findings. This distinction is essential. A drift-pattern in PMS is not a diagnosis, not a person-type, not a moral verdict, not a maturity score, and not a claim that a particular actor failed. It is a way of naming how a configuration of operators can become distorted or unstable under certain conditions. In this case, the central drift risks are linked to the movement from bounded repair / confidentiality into public accountability / tribunal-like publicness. But because the case remains a conceptual domain projection, drift remains conditional.

The first relevant pattern is **AD_A>>E**, excessive distance between Awareness and Enactment. In PMS terms, this pattern concerns a configuration in which evaluative or power asymmetry becomes stabilized, while recontextualization reinforces evaluation rather than supporting integrated action. In the completed analysis, AD_A>>E is marked as possible but not entailed. It would become relevant if public accountability discourse expanded awareness, interpretation, judgment, or exposure while integrated repair, answerability, and proportionate action remained delayed or suppressed.

In the present case, the possibility is easy to see. Publicness can increase awareness. More people may know about the material; the material may become legible as evidence, accusation, testimony, warning, or accountability claim; the field may become saturated with interpretation. Yet greater awareness does not automatically produce mature action. It may even widen the gap between what is publicly noticed and what can be responsibly enacted. If audience expansion produces commentary, reputational pressure, or public suspicion without corresponding repair, correction, reply, proportionality, or procedural handling, Awareness_A may outrun Action_E.

But this remains a risk, not a finding. Publicness may also enable action where internal repair failed. It may protect affected parties, trigger procedure, correct concealment, or make previously invisible asymmetry answerable. Conversely, confidentiality may suppress both awareness and enactment. Therefore AD_A>>E cannot be assigned simply because material became public. The completed analysis preserves this distinction carefully: risk is not finding; possible drift is not established distortion.

The second relevant pattern is **AD_Sigma_low**, fragmented integration. This is more central to the case. It concerns situations in which recontextualization outpaces integration: frames shift, meanings change, audiences expand, and obligations are redistributed before the conflicting elements are sufficiently coordinated. In this case, the possible fragmentation lies between repair and accountability, privacy and publicness, protection and exposure, confidentiality and answerability, source-frame expectations and target-frame consequences.

AD_Sigma_low is plausible because the Core case itself is organized around Φ outpacing Σ. The material is transferred into a new frame, but the analysis cannot establish that the transfer was integrated: Were confidentiality terms considered? Was publicness necessary? Was there prior repair failure? Was disclosure proportionate? Was right of reply preserved? Was the audience bounded or unlimited? Was contextual integrity maintained? Did publicness restore correctability or destroy it? Were affected parties protected without humiliating others? These questions all belong to Σ. Their unresolved status makes fragmented integration a structural risk.

Still, fragmented integration is not a conclusion. The completed analysis explicitly states that public recontextualization may be an integrative correction rather than fragmentation. If the internal frame was suppressive, if public disclosure was legally required, if safeguards existed, if right of reply was preserved, and if the public move improved correctability without degradation, then the same Φ movement may show stronger integration rather than weak Σ. PMS therefore refuses to treat under-specification as failure. The absence of evidence for integration is not evidence of failed integration.

The PMS Core analysis also identifies a broader **coherence and under-discrimination risk**. The case fits PMS too smoothly. It can be rendered as Δ plus □ plus Θ plus Φ plus Ω, with Χ discipline and Σ/Ψ risk. That formal fit is valuable because it makes the structure visible. But it is also dangerous because it may under-discriminate between very different phenomena: harmful exposure, warranted accountability, whistleblowing, democratic publicness, legal disclosure, institutional concealment, restorative escalation, ordinary frame change, or consented publication.

Under-discrimination is not the same as failure. It is a warning about model use. PMS is strong enough to cover the case-family; the challenge is whether it can distinguish cases within that family. The completed analysis answers by adding calibration tests, rival readings, falsifier conditions, and non-capture zones. A responsible PMS reading must not say: “Because the case can be formalized, the case is settled.” It must say: “Because the case can be formalized, the decisive missing distinctions become visible.”

Non-assignment is likewise not a low score. The analysis does not assign AD_A>>E or AD_Sigma_low as findings, not because the scene is clean, but because the evidence is insufficient. In PMS, EDEN, and MIP alike, refusing to assign a drift pattern is not exoneration, minimization, or neutrality in the everyday sense. It is an epistemic and praxeological guardrail. Drift-as-verdict would collapse the model into exactly the closure machine it is designed to resist.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant add-on is **PMS-EDEN**, applied as a subordinate overlay over the completed PMS Core case. It is not a replacement for the Core pass. It does not redefine PMS operators. It does not change PMS dependencies. It does not introduce Adult Reciprocity, Action-Power, correctability, false-coordinated real asymmetry, AEP, Reciprocity Loss, or any EDEN construct as a PMS Base operator. It is an overlay profile that adds a more specific visibility layer around asymmetry, responsibility, publicness, cost distribution, correctability, and the description-to-action firewall.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer here because the Core reading can identify the frame transfer and the operator structure but does not fully isolate the particular EDEN question: whether real asymmetry is being falsely coordinated under an apparently legitimate frame. PMS Core says: there is Δ, there is □, there is Θ, there is central Φ, there is candidate Ω, there is Χ discipline, and there is weak or conditional Σ/Ψ. PMS-EDEN asks an additional question: does the public/private frame transfer truthfully coordinate real Ω, R, costs, Σ/Ψ, and correctability, or does it shift burdens while appearing as repair, protection, accountability, or legitimacy?

The EDEN overlay begins from the principle that **real Ω is not itself failure**. Asymmetry is structurally unavoidable in many scenes. Confidentiality may create asymmetry; publicness may create asymmetry; institutions, audiences, timing, exposure, and reply capacities all create asymmetries. The EDEN concern is not asymmetry as such, but false coordination of real asymmetry. False coordination would occur where a real imbalance of exposure, power, cost, or obligation is presented as properly handled while responsibility, integration, self-binding, or correctability is displaced.

The primary EDEN construct in this case is **false-coordinated real asymmetry**, but its status is suspended. The overlay formula is: real Ω plus false coordination frame plus displaced R plus blocked, weakened, simulated, avoided, or externalized Σ/Ψ yields false-coordinated real Ω. The completed EDEN overlay does not affirm this formula as a finding. It says that the Core case supplies candidate structure but not the scene evidence required to establish false coordination.

This suspended status is crucial. The conceptual case shows possible real Ω: publicness may alter exposure, audience scale, reply capacity, explanation burden, reputational risk, and correction access; confidentiality may alter accountability access, repair safety, protection, concealment risk, and frame control. But EDEN requires more than possible Ω. It requires a concrete scene packet showing how responsibility and integration are distributed. Without that packet, false-coordination remains a test, not a claim.

The overlay also introduces **Adult Reciprocity** as a positive baseline or normative benchmark, not as a PMS operator or axis. Adult Reciprocity asks whether real asymmetry is truthfully coordinated rather than hidden, displaced, or stabilized under misleading legitimacy frames. In this case, Adult Reciprocity remains indeterminate. The available material does not show whether privacy and accountability were coordinated reciprocally, whether each role-position had meaningful voice, whether real obligations were held, or whether correctability remained available.

The overlay introduces **Action-Power** as an EDEN scene-bound attribution handle. This is not PMS Action_E. PMS Action_E remains under-specified because strict action requires integrated directedness across time. EDEN Action-Power asks a different question: who has the capacity to structure praxis conditions? In this case, possible Action-Power lies in controlling confidentiality, timing, disclosure, audience, public framing, circulation, contextualization, right of reply, correction, and withdrawal. Yet the actual distribution of Action-Power is indeterminate because no concrete scene is supplied.

The EDEN overlay treats **responsibility** through a scene-bound principle: R follows real structuring effect under available legibility, capacity, alternative possibility, Θ trajectory, and Ω cost/exposure conditions. This aligns with PMS Responsibility_R but sharpens it. Responsibility is neither guilt nor blame. It is not assigned by identity, side, role label, or rhetorical position. It becomes stronger only where a role-position actually structures conditions and had enough knowledge, capacity, alternatives, and temporal relation to those effects. In the current case, responsibility distribution remains conditional and indeterminate.

The overlay makes visible **Σ/Ψ load shifts**. One of EDEN’s distinctive contributions is to ask whether low binding or weak integration on one side increases integration or self-binding load on another side. For example, if a confidential frame remains under-bound to accountability, affected parties may bear the burden of waiting, explaining, escalating, or preserving options. Conversely, if public accountability proceeds without adequate safeguards, the exposed position may bear intensified explanation, reputational, or reply burden without proper legibility. The overlay identifies these as possible load shifts, but not established ones.

The overlay also tracks **visible costs**. These include visibility cost, explanation cost, reaction cost, waiting cost, binding cost, protection cost, speech cost, repair cost, dignity cost, legibility cost, and option loss. PMS Core can name asymmetry and recontextualization; EDEN adds a more granular cost-distribution map. The same public move may increase accountability access while increasing exposure cost. The same private containment may protect repair while increasing waiting or silencing cost. EDEN insists that these costs be mapped before responsibility is assigned.

The overlay treats **non-action and non-binding** with caution. Non-action is not praxis by default. Silence, delay, refusal, non-response, restraint, or absence becomes relevant only where it structures a scene under □, Λ, Ω, and Θ. Non-binding is likewise not avoidance by default. A role-position may remain unbound because of uncertainty, legal limits, legitimate boundary, incapacity, need for Χ, or genuine openness. In this case, possible non-events and possible non-binding are visible but not established. The EDEN overlay therefore prevents the analysis from treating absence as manipulation or responsibility by default.

The overlay also checks **immunization, overexposure, and AEP**. Immunization would be possible if privacy or publicness allowed real effects to remain insufficiently answerable. Overexposure would be possible if a role-position received responsibility, suspicion, or explanation burden without adequate legibility, protection, or reply capacity. AEP, however, is not supported. The AEP signature requires high exposure, low legitimate language, critique-to-threat switch, and responsibility overload. The conceptual case does not supply these components. High exposure is possible but not scene-established; low legitimate language is possible but not shown; critique-to-threat switch is absent; responsibility overload is not evidenced.

Reduced EDEN signatures are overlay shorthand only. The completed overlay explicitly prevents them from becoming new PMS dependency paths or substitute case packets. A reduced signature may summarize an already expanded scene-bound check, but it may not replace scene evidence, counter-scene exposure, falsifiers, or PMS dependencies. This is part of the overlay’s subordination to PMS Base.

The EDEN overlay’s central drift risks remain risks, not findings. Privacy-as-repair may falsely coordinate asymmetry if it externalizes accountability cost. Public-accountability-as-correction may falsely coordinate asymmetry if it externalizes exposure or reply cost. Tribunal-publicness may present itself as legitimacy while weakening integration and Dignity-in-Practice. Yet none of these is asserted. The strongest counter-scenes remain active: warranted public accountability, whistleblowing, legal disclosure, procedural correction, democratic publicness, necessary escalation after failed internal repair, or confidentiality-as-concealment.

The overlay’s final status is suspended_reading. It identifies candidate real Ω and possible false-coordination mechanisms but refuses affirmation. It remains description-only and non-binding. It does not authorize confrontation, intervention, exposure, punishment, enforcement, obligation, or application authority. The final EDEN guardrail is explicit: this remains a scene-bound structural overlay, not a diagnosis, verdict, prescription, application authority, person-status claim, sex truth, or PMS-Core revision.

### Layer interaction

The PMS Core reading remains the base layer. It provides the operator grammar: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. It defines the case as a conceptual frame transfer. It preserves dependency order, derived-structure discipline, non-capture, and application guardrails. Everything later depends on this base but does not rewrite it.

The EDEN overlay sharpens what PMS Core makes visible but does not isolate in the same terms. PMS Core can say that Ω is candidate and Φ is central. EDEN asks whether that candidate Ω is truthfully coordinated or falsely coordinated. PMS Core can say that Σ and Ψ remain weak or conditional. EDEN asks whether weak Σ/Ψ creates shifted load, displaced responsibility, or lost correctability. PMS Core can say that publicness changes frame and exposure. EDEN asks who bears visibility cost, explanation cost, waiting cost, speech cost, and option loss. PMS Core can preserve non-capture. EDEN operationalizes that preservation through counter-scene and falsifier requirements.

MIP then adds a maturity-in-practice grid over the already layered PMS / EDEN analysis. It does not rescore PMS operators. It does not decide EDEN false coordination. It asks how the constellation appears when viewed through Awareness, Coherence, Responsibility, Power-to-act, optional D, IA-box, trajectory, and transmission discipline. MIP’s output is again cautious: A is structurally strong at model level but incomplete at scene level; C is mixed; R is conditional; P is structurally visible but not allocated; M is not assignable to persons; IA returns IA-risk; D is activated only as qualitative dignity-in-practice protection.

The MIP layer is important because it forces the case to distinguish structural visibility from practical maturity. A case can be analytically visible without being maturely handled. A frame transfer can be intelligible without being integrated. A public move can increase awareness without creating responsible action. A private frame can preserve dignity while also decreasing accountability access. MIP therefore reframes the case not as “What is the correct verdict?” but as “What would be required for this constellation to be handled maturely in practice?”

MIP also prevents responsibility inflation. It requires knowledge, power/control, predictability, access to alternatives, and serious integration attempt before stronger M claims can be made. Since none of these are supplied concretely, M is not assigned to persons or concrete role-holders. This is not a finding of low responsibility. It is a methodological ceiling: high responsibility cannot be attributed from a conceptual frame-transfer alone.

The IA-box adds a specific asymmetry check. The current result is IA-risk, not an inadult-asymmetry verdict. The asymmetry is structurally visible, but it is not shown to be transparent, justified, time-bound, or reversible. That means the case is not safe for stronger use without additional evidence. IA-risk is a warning about missing asymmetry conditions, not a conclusion that confidentiality or publicness was unjustified.

The D-module adds a qualitative dignity-in-practice protection layer. It is activated because the case concerns publicness, exposure, shame risk, confidentiality, recognition, repair, and right of reply. But D remains qualitative, role-bound, and non-scoring. D0 is untouched. No person-level dignity reading is produced. D-language therefore protects the analysis from humiliation, but it must not become a hidden dignity verdict.

MIP-AHP then adds a quality overlay. It does not rescore A, M, IA, or D. It identifies attack points: missing concrete scene packet, A-band misreading risk, M-ceiling misreading risk, IA-risk overread risk, compressed publicness context, D-language risk, counter-reading priority gap, and model-convergence validation risk. AHP hardens the analysis by proposing next-iteration constraints: scene packet, score-hygiene boundary, M-ceiling check, IA threshold table, publicness gradient, D-language hygiene, falsifier matrix, and model-convergence caveat.

What remains PMS Core is the operator analysis, dependency discipline, derived structures, drift-pattern status, and base guardrails. EDEN must not redefine any of these. It must not turn Adult Reciprocity into a PMS axis, Action-Power into PMS Action_E, correctability into a new operator, or false-coordinated real Ω into a replacement for Ω, Σ, or Ψ. MIP must not turn A/M/IA/D into person scores. AHP must not turn attack points into findings.

Overlay language could overreach in several ways. “False coordination” could sound like accusation. “Action-Power” could sound like blame. “D-stress” could sound like dignity deficit. “IA-risk” could sound like failed asymmetry. “Boundary erosion” could sound like a verdict against publicness. “Public accountability” could sound like a verdict against privacy. The completed analyses repeatedly block these overreads. Every layer must remain role-bound, scene-bound, reversible, and non-operational.

Non-closure remains visible at every level. PMS keeps rival readings open. EDEN suspends its primary mechanism. MIP marks working-note status. AHP identifies vulnerabilities rather than pretending the analysis is sealed. The layered stack therefore demonstrates a crucial AXIOM principle: adding analytic layers should increase discrimination and humility, not closure pressure.

### Example 1 — Confidential feedback becomes public accountability material

**Type:** worked
**Function in article:** Illustrates the basic frame-transfer problem before the later sections generalize it structurally.

A bounded repair process is opened inside an organization after a conflict between two role-holders. One person submits written feedback to a small internal group under an explicit expectation that the feedback will be used to clarify harm, improve procedure, and support repair. The material is not written as a public statement. It includes descriptions of experienced strain, unclear expectations, and perceived failures of response. Several weeks later, after the internal process stalls, parts of the feedback appear in a public accountability document circulated to a wider professional community. The public document does not simply repeat the feedback; it places it in a new frame. What had originally functioned as confidential repair material now functions as evidence of broader accountability failure.

This vignette demonstrates why the case cannot be solved by saying either “privacy was violated” or “public accountability was necessary.” Both may be structurally plausible. The original internal frame may have protected repair, contextual integrity, and dignity-in-practice. It may also have delayed accountability or concentrated interpretive control in the hands of those managing the internal process. The later public frame may have corrected a failed repair process by making hidden asymmetry visible. It may also have expanded exposure, reduced reply capacity, or converted repair material into a tribunal-like public object. The example therefore demonstrates the core difficulty of the case: the same material changes praxeological status when it crosses audience, use, and obligation boundaries.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. Δ appears in the distinction between internal repair material and public accountability material. ∇ appears only weakly as structural pressure: the stalled internal process may generate pressure toward publicness, but no psychological motive is inferred. □ is central because the meaning of the feedback depends on whether it is governed by the repair frame or the public accountability frame. Λ is possible if the internal process promised response, repair, or closure and failed to provide it; but this must be established, not assumed. Α remains weak unless similar transfers recur or become normalized. Ω is visible as candidate asymmetry: the internal process may control confidentiality and timing, while the public document may control audience, exposure, and reputational pressure. Θ is active because the material moves through time from one frame to another. Φ is the central hook: the feedback is recontextualized. Χ is required so the analysis does not fuse with either side’s framing. Σ is the open question: can repair and accountability be integrated without concealment or humiliation? Ψ remains conditional because the scene would need explicit commitments: confidentiality promises, public-interest obligations, repair duties, or non-retaliation norms.

The PMS-EDEN overlay would ask whether real Ω is truthfully coordinated or falsely coordinated. The internal process might falsely coordinate asymmetry if it presents confidentiality as repair while externalizing the cost of silence onto the person who submitted feedback. The public accountability document might falsely coordinate asymmetry if it presents disclosure as accountability while externalizing exposure and explanation cost onto those named or implied. EDEN therefore does not choose privacy or publicness in advance. It asks where responsibility, Action-Power, Σ/Ψ load, cost, correctability, and reply capacity actually sit. In this example, the EDEN finding would remain suspended unless the scene specified who controlled the process, what was promised, whether internal repair failed, whether disclosure was proportionate, and whether reply or correction was available.

MIP would treat this as a working-note scene unless more evidence were supplied. Awareness may be structurally strong because the frame transfer is visible. Coherence remains mixed because “repair” and “accountability” may each be coherent in their own terms while failing to integrate with each other. Responsibility is conditional: it cannot be assigned without knowing who knew what, who had real alternatives, who controlled timing and audience, and who could reasonably predict the effects. Power-to-act would need careful mapping: who could keep the feedback bounded, who could escalate it, who could contextualize it, who could correct it, and who could answer publicly without disproportionate cost. IA-risk is plausible because asymmetry is visible but transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility are not yet established. D may be activated qualitatively because publicness, shame, exposure, recognition, and repair are all implicated. AHP would identify the missing scene packet as the main attack point and would require a publicness gradient before stronger claims.

The forbidden claims are important. The example does not show that the person who disclosed the feedback acted wrongly. It does not show that the internal process was suppressive. It does not show breach of confidentiality, defamation, retaliation, whistleblowing validity, organizational guilt, or moral failure. It does not diagnose participants, rank their maturity, or infer motives such as revenge, cowardice, avoidance, courage, manipulation, or care. It also does not validate PMS, EDEN, or MIP as empirically sufficient models.

The non-capture note is that several rival readings may fit. A procedural-justice reading may focus on whether the internal process had fair review and reply. An information-ethics reading may focus on contextual integrity and secondary use. A whistleblowing reading may treat publicness as necessary after failed repair. An institutional-power reading may treat confidentiality as containment. A phenomenological reading may attend to what exposure or silence felt like to those involved. None of these is defeated by the PMS rendering.

The article takeaway is that this example should be used to show the central Φ problem without deciding it. The crucial analytic move is to distinguish material continuity from frame continuity. The same words may travel, but their praxeological function changes when the frame changes. That is why PMS Core identifies recontextualization, EDEN suspends false-coordination judgment pending scene evidence, and MIP refuses person-level maturity assignment.

#### Example 2 — A closed mediation process becomes a public “lack of accountability” claim

**Type:** worked
**Function in article:** Shows that not only content but also a closed repair process can be recontextualized into public accountability discourse.

A small collective sets up a closed mediation process after a conflict has become disruptive but before it has become public. The process is explicitly framed as bounded repair: participants are told that the goal is to clarify expectations, reduce harm, and agree on future conduct without turning the conflict into a public contest. The mediation is not secret in an absolute sense, but its details are treated as context-bound. Participants are asked not to quote statements outside the process, partly to preserve trust and partly to make it possible for people to speak without immediately defending themselves before a wider audience.

The process produces no public statement. Several weeks pass. One participant later writes a public post saying that the collective has “failed accountability” and “kept everything behind closed doors.” The post does not quote private statements and does not name all participants, but it describes the mediation process itself as evidence that the collective protects insiders from consequences. Some readers interpret the post as a necessary act of public accountability after a failed internal process. Others understand it as a breach of the mediation frame: not because confidential words were disclosed, but because the process itself has been recontextualized from bounded repair into public proof of institutional avoidance.

This example demonstrates that boundary erosion need not involve a leaked document, a quoted message, or explicit disclosure of confidential content. A process can be recontextualized. The object that moves into publicness is not only “what was said” inside the closed frame, but the meaning of the frame itself. The mediation frame initially presents boundedness as a condition of repair. The public post presents boundedness as evidence of avoidance. The structural question is not which description is automatically correct. It is whether the frame was truthfully coordinated with accountability, repair, reply, and correction, or whether one side’s frame-language concealed a real asymmetry.

The PMS Core hooks are therefore slightly different from a simple quotation case. Δ appears in the distinction between closed mediation and public accountability claim. ∇ appears only as structural pressure: frustration, delay, or perceived non-response may generate movement toward publicness, but no motive is inferred. □ is central because the mediation frame and the public accountability frame organize the same process differently. Under the mediation frame, closedness may be a repair condition; under the public frame, closedness may appear as opacity. Λ becomes especially important but remains conditional. Did the process fail to produce the promised clarification, protection, response, or future conduct agreement? Or did the public post treat an incomplete but still-active process as failure? The answer cannot be assumed. Α remains weak unless the collective repeatedly uses closed processes to avoid accountability or unless public accusations repeatedly convert mediation frames into reputational evidence. Ω is salient: closed mediation can restrict public voice and concentrate procedural control, while public accusation can expose participants and shift explanation burden. Θ is active because the meaning of the process changes over time, especially across the period between mediation and public post. Φ is central because the mediation process is recontextualized as public evidence of “lack of accountability.” Χ is required to keep the analysis from siding prematurely with either confidentiality or publicity. Σ asks whether repair and accountability were actually integrated. Ψ remains conditional on what the process promised, what participants agreed to, and whether any commitments were binding beyond the closed setting.

This example is useful because it makes Λ and Σ more visible than a simple text-transfer case. The decisive question is not only whether something private became public, but whether a promised event failed to occur. If the mediation promised a follow-up statement, changed conduct, protection for affected members, or a time-bound review, then a missing follow-up may become a genuine Λ. If the process promised only confidential exploration and was still ongoing, the public claim of “failed accountability” may overread absence as failure. Likewise, Σ cannot be inferred from the existence of mediation. A closed process may integrate repair and accountability well; it may also simulate integration while shifting the burden of patience, silence, or trust onto affected parties. The mere presence of a process is not proof of integration.

The PMS-EDEN overlay would ask whether the closed process truthfully coordinated real Ω. If the collective used mediation language to keep affected parties quiet while no real answerability, protection, or change occurred, confidentiality could function as immunization. If the public post framed the closed process as avoidance while omitting available safeguards, timelines, or correction efforts, publicness could create overexposure and displaced responsibility. EDEN therefore does not treat “closed process” as innocence and does not treat “public accountability claim” as maturity. It asks where Action-Power sits: who designed the process, who controlled timelines, who could speak, who could correct the public account, who bore reputational cost, and who had real alternatives.

The cost structure is also clearer here. Closed mediation can impose waiting cost, silence cost, trust cost, and uncertainty cost on participants who seek recognition or protection. Public accusation can impose exposure cost, explanation cost, reputational inference cost, and reply cost on those who are directly or indirectly implicated. The public audience can intensify both: it may create pressure for accountability, but it may also reduce nuance and reversibility. EDEN’s question is whether these costs remain attached to real responsibility and correctability, or whether they are shifted under the rhetoric of either “repair” or “accountability.”

AEP is not supported by the vignette. The public post may create exposure, but the example does not establish low legitimate language, critique-to-threat switch, or responsibility overload. Overexposure remains only a risk. Immunization also remains only a risk. The closed mediation may have protected repair; it may also have shielded inaction. The public post may have opened necessary accountability; it may also have converted an unfinished process into a public verdict. None of those readings is decided by the example.

MIP would keep this as a working-note scene unless additional evidence were supplied. Awareness may be structurally strong because the competing frames are clear: mediation as repair versus mediation as avoidance. Coherence remains mixed because both frames can be internally coherent. Responsibility is conditional: it depends on who knew the expectations, who controlled the process, who could reasonably predict the effects of delay or publicity, who had alternatives, and whether serious integration attempts occurred. Power-to-act is central: the collective may have had power to time-bound the process, communicate limits, protect affected members, or create a correction channel; the public author may have had power to anonymize, contextualize, wait, escalate, or publish; participants may have had unequal ability to respond. IA-risk is plausible because asymmetry is visible, but transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility remain open. Was the closedness transparent? Was it justified by repair needs? Was it time-bound? Could public misunderstanding be corrected without forcing further exposure?

Dignity-in-Practice is relevant but must remain qualitative. Closed mediation may protect dignity by preventing humiliation and giving people room to speak without immediate public defense. It may also undermine dignity if it denies affected persons recognition, voice, or protection. Public accountability may protect dignity by refusing silence around harm. It may also undermine dignity if it converts an unresolved process into a public object of suspicion. The example therefore does not ask which frame is dignified. It asks how dignity-in-practice is preserved under crossed asymmetry.

AHP would identify the missing scene packet as decisive. A stronger analysis would need to know the mediation terms, timeline, promises, review points, participants’ ability to speak, publicness level of the later post, identifiability of those implicated, available correction channels, and whether the collective had already implemented changes. Without those details, the example remains illustrative rather than evidentiary.

Forbidden claims are especially important here. The example does not prove that the collective concealed wrongdoing. It does not prove that the public author violated confidentiality. It does not prove that mediation was manipulative, that publicity was courageous, that closedness was cowardly, or that public accusation was tribunalizing. It does not assign legal breach, institutional liability, moral guilt, maturity failure, dignity failure, or psychological motive. It also does not establish that “lack of accountability” was true or false.

The non-capture note is that several rival readings remain strong. A procedural-justice reading may focus on whether the mediation process included fair timelines, participation, safeguards, and review. An institutional-power reading may ask whether closed repair processes routinely protect insiders. A public-sphere reading may ask whether public critique was necessary to make an invisible problem discussable. A language-practice reading may examine how “mediation,” “closed doors,” “accountability,” and “safety” distribute legitimacy. A legal-formal reading may matter if confidentiality duties, defamation risk, or protected disclosure are implicated. PMS and EDEN make the frame-transfer visible; they do not settle these rival grammars.

The article takeaway is that privacy-to-publicness movement can involve the public reframing of a process, not only the disclosure of content. This makes the case more structurally demanding. A closed process is not automatically repair, and a public “lack of accountability” claim is not automatically correction. The decisive questions are whether the process was transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible; whether repair and accountability were integrated; and whether publicness improved correctability or merely shifted exposure.

### Example 3 — An anonymized internal lesson becomes a public pattern claim

**Type:** worked
**Function in article:** Shows how the EDEN overlay and rival-reading discipline handle publicness that is anonymized, generalized, or indirect.

A small association conducts an internal review after several members report confusion about how concerns are handled. The review produces an anonymized internal lesson: future reports should receive clearer timelines, written response expectations, and explicit review points. Months later, a member writes a public essay about “patterns of silence in small organizations.” The essay does not name the association or any individual, but it describes a pattern that resembles the internal review. Some readers interpret the essay as public accountability. Others see it as a disguised escalation of internal material into public accusation. The association fears reputational inference; the author argues that anonymized public reflection is necessary to prevent recurring harm.

This vignette is useful because it complicates the case. The material is not simply leaked. It is anonymized, transformed, generalized, and placed into a public language-practice about patterns. This makes the example safer and more abstract, but also analytically richer. It shows that publicness can occur without direct naming, and that boundary erosion can be alleged even when identifiability is reduced. It also shows that confidentiality and public learning may conflict in ways that cannot be solved by literal secrecy alone.

The PMS Core hooks begin with Δ: internal lesson versus public pattern claim; bounded review versus public reflection; specific case material versus generalized discourse. ∇ is weak as structural pressure from unresolved organizational learning toward public articulation. □ is central because the internal review frame is oriented toward organizational correction, while the public essay frame is oriented toward broader discourse, warning, or cultural critique. Λ may be relevant if the internal review failed to produce promised timelines, written expectations, or review points. But it may also be inactive if the internal process genuinely implemented improvements. Α becomes more plausible here than in the previous examples, but still not established. The public essay speaks of a pattern, yet the analysis cannot infer recurrence from rhetoric alone. Ω is visible in several directions: the association may fear reputational exposure despite anonymization; members may have lacked voice inside the internal frame; the author may control public framing; the public audience may lack context. Θ is active because internal review becomes later public reflection. Φ is central because the internal lesson is recontextualized as a public pattern. Χ is crucial: anonymization must not be dismissed as evasion, and public pattern language must not be accepted automatically as accountability. Σ asks whether internal learning and public warning can be integrated. Ψ is conditional on commitments: confidentiality, learning duties, member-protection duties, public-interest obligations, or non-retaliation norms.

The PMS-EDEN overlay is especially relevant because the case turns on false coordination risk without direct naming. Real Ω may be coordinated well if anonymization preserves dignity while making a broader structural issue discussable. Real Ω may be falsely coordinated if anonymization gives the author public framing power without answerability, or if confidentiality language gives the association protection from legitimate critique. The question is not whether anonymization is innocent. The question is whether it truthfully coordinates exposure, responsibility, correctability, and integration.

Action-Power is distributed across several positions. The association may control internal review procedures and confidentiality expectations. The author may control public abstraction, timing, and framing. The audience may amplify pattern claims without knowing the source context. Affected members may gain language for previously invisible experiences. EDEN would therefore map cost distribution carefully: reputational inference cost, explanation cost, speech cost, repair cost, legibility cost, option loss, and protection cost. The fact that no one is named reduces one kind of exposure but does not eliminate all publicness effects.

The example also tests EDEN’s non-action and non-binding boundaries. If the association did not implement the internal lesson, that non-action may become structurally relevant under Λ, Ω, and Θ. But non-action cannot be assumed. If the author refuses to identify the association, that non-binding to public specificity may be legitimate: anonymization may preserve D and reduce legal or reputational harm. It may also shift burden by making the association unable to answer without self-identifying. Both possibilities must remain open.

MIP would read this example as a good candidate for a worked scene only if more facts were added. Awareness is structurally high because the example distinguishes internal review, anonymized public reflection, pattern language, publicness, and correctability. Coherence is mixed: the public essay may coherently generalize from internal learning, or it may blur the boundary between anonymized reflection and implied accusation. Responsibility remains conditional. Power-to-act includes internal reform, anonymization, public writing, clarification, reply, and non-identifying correction. IA-risk is present but not decisive. The asymmetry may be more transparent if the essay names its abstraction level and evidence limits; more justified if internal learning failed or the pattern is broader; more time-bound if the essay avoids indefinite implication; more reversible if correction and reply are possible without forced exposure. D is relevant because anonymization may protect dignity, while insinuation may create a shadow form of public exposure.

AHP would likely identify publicness compression as the main risk. The essay is public, but not in the same way as naming individuals in a mass platform. The publicness gradient matters: Is the essay read by a small professional circle, a broad online audience, members of the same community, or external observers with no context? Is the association identifiable through clues? Is the essay persistent and searchable? Can the association or affected members respond without revealing themselves? These questions determine whether the example demonstrates careful public learning, boundary erosion, or an indeterminate mixture.

Forbidden claims must be explicit. The example does not prove that the author disguised an accusation. It does not prove that the association concealed harm. It does not prove that anonymization was adequate. It does not prove that public pattern language was necessary. It does not assign maturity, guilt, bad faith, courage, cowardice, or dignity failure. It does not validate the public essay as whistleblowing or condemn it as tribunalization.

The non-capture note is strong here. A language-practice reading may preserve how “pattern” language works: it can reveal structures that individual complaint language cannot, but it can also imply more than it demonstrates. An information-ethics reading may better analyze anonymization, contextual integrity, and audience inference. A public-sphere reading may ask whether anonymized critique contributes to democratic learning. A legal reading may ask whether identifiability still creates risk. PMS and EDEN can map the structural transfer, but they should not absorb these questions as mere operator residue.

The article takeaway is that this example prepares the later conclusion without writing it: the privacy-to-publicness problem is not limited to direct exposure. Even anonymized, generalized, or educational publicness can alter asymmetry, responsibility, and correctability. But the correct analytic response is not suspicion by default. It is calibrated mapping: What moved? From which frame to which frame? With what audience? With what safeguards? With what right of reply? With what remaining non-capture?

### Non-capture and rival readings

The completed analyses preserve rival readings as epistemically legitimate. Rival readings are not treated as defects, residues, or failures of PMS. They are part of the case’s structural honesty. Because the case concerns privacy, publicness, confidentiality, accountability, asymmetry, and dignity, no single grammar can be presumed exhaustive.

A **procedural-justice reading** would center due process, consent, proportionality, right of reply, safeguards, and procedural fairness. It preserves what PMS may under-specify if it focuses too heavily on operator structure: the formal conditions under which a public move becomes fair, unfair, premature, or necessary. Such a reading would ask whether the transfer respected process, whether affected parties could respond, whether evidence was contextualized, and whether publicness exceeded the procedural need.

An **information-ethics reading** would center contextual integrity, confidentiality, secondary use, audience collapse, and informational norms. It preserves the fact that material can be wronged not only by being made public but by being moved into the wrong informational context. It would ask whether the material’s original norms of flow were violated, whether consent covered secondary use, whether audience expansion changed meaning, and whether anonymization or contextualization preserved integrity.

A **whistleblowing or public-interest reading** would treat publicness not as erosion but as possible correction. It preserves the possibility that internal repair was unavailable, suppressive, compromised, or dangerous. It would ask whether public disclosure was necessary to prevent ongoing harm, whether internal channels failed, whether the public interest outweighed confidentiality, and whether disclosure was proportionate and safeguarded.

An **institutional-power reading** would examine confidentiality as a possible stabilizer of asymmetry. It preserves the possibility that privacy may function as containment, reputation management, or institutional self-protection. It would ask who benefits from boundedness, who controls internal resolution, whether affected parties are silenced, whether accountability is delayed, and whether confidentiality prevents public scrutiny.

A **legal-formal reading** would center legal duties, privileges, protected disclosures, defamation, retaliation, duty to warn, confidentiality agreements, evidentiary standards, and jurisdiction. It preserves what PMS cannot decide: the legally relevant status of the material and the transfer. In a concrete case, legal analysis could override or radically reshape the structural reading.

A **public-sphere reading** would ask whether publicness is democratic, deliberative, protective, or tribunalizing. It preserves the political dimension of accountability discourse: publicness can be a condition of recognition, not merely a risk of exposure. It would ask whether the public frame enabled contestation and correction or collapsed into reputational spectacle.

A **phenomenological reading** would attend to lived exposure, shame, voice, trust, and rupture. PMS deliberately avoids phenomenological claims, but that does not mean phenomenology is irrelevant. Publicness may be experienced as recognition, humiliation, relief, danger, vindication, silencing, or loss of control. A phenomenological grammar could preserve experiential dimensions that PMS abstracts away.

A **language-practice reading** would focus on how terms such as “confidential,” “repair,” “accountability,” “public interest,” “tribunal,” “disclosure,” and “harm” function in practice. It preserves the fact that language itself distributes legitimacy. Calling a frame “repair” may protect it from critique; calling a disclosure “accountability” may protect it from scrutiny; calling publicness “tribunal” may delegitimize affected speech. This reading would examine the use of terms rather than only the structural frame.

An **anti-capture reading** would resist the entire formalization if it begins to domesticate rupture, refusal, or non-fit. It preserves the possibility that some conflicts should not be translated into PMS, EDEN, or MIP terms without remainder. It would ask whether formal analysis itself risks smoothing over pain, power, legality, or political conflict.

The completed analyses do not treat these rival readings as threats. They are safeguards against over-assimilation. PMS translation does not defeat them. EDEN mapping does not defeat them. MIP scoring discipline does not defeat them. AHP hardening makes them more operationally visible by recommending a falsifier matrix. The article therefore preserves rivalry as a sign of analytical maturity.

The three examples clarify why these rival readings must remain alive. The confidential-feedback example may be procedural, informational, whistleblowing-related, or institutional. The closed-mediation example may hinge on procedural justice, institutional power, language-practice, and public-sphere interpretation as much as operator structure. The anonymized-public-pattern example may require public-sphere theory and information ethics before any stronger judgment is possible. The examples therefore demonstrate the analysis; they do not replace it.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would be weakened or invalidated if the material was never confidential, never bounded by an internal repair frame, already public, shared with consent, legally required to be disclosed, or originally produced for accountability use. In such cases, the frame-transfer reading loses its foundation. Δ would no longer mark the relevant private/public distinction, □ would no longer support source-to-target frame contrast, and Φ would weaken as the central operator.

The PMS reading would also weaken if no temporal transfer occurred. If the material did not move across time from one frame into another, Θ would be overextended and Φ would lose case-specific support. If there was no expectation of repair, confidentiality, response, closure, or accountability, Λ should remain inactive. If there was no recurrence or stabilization, Α should not be treated as pattern formation. If no directional imbalance in exposure, obligation, capacity, vulnerability, or reply could be identified, Ω claims would need to be removed or marked weak.

The reading would become under-discriminating if it could not distinguish harmful exposure from warranted disclosure, or protective confidentiality from institutional concealment. This is the central discrimination test. If PMS says “frame transfer” but cannot tell whether the transfer corrected a failure or created one, PMS remains useful only as a first-pass structure. Stronger claims would require scene evidence.

The reading would become over-assimilative if it treated every public transfer, rupture, disagreement, missing response, rival framing, refusal, or legal constraint as Φ drift, Λ absence, Σ failure, or maturity failure. This is one of the most serious risks. PMS must not absorb resistance into its own grammar by default. Non-fit may be a genuine limit of the model.

The EDEN reading would be weakened if public transfer truthfully coordinated real Ω, kept R attached to actual effects, preserved D, maintained Χ, left Σ/Ψ and correctability open, and included proportionate safeguards. More concretely, EDEN false-coordination would weaken if the material was already public or authorized for public accountability; if consent or legal duty supported disclosure; if internal confidentiality blocked accountability or concealed harm; if public disclosure was proportionate and allowed reply; or if publicness improved correctability without humiliation or unbounded exposure.

The EDEN reading would fail if no real Ω were shown, if responsibility were not displaced, if Σ/Ψ were not blocked, weakened, simulated, avoided, or externalized, or if counter-scenes materially explained the situation better. EDEN also includes a methodological no-proceed rule: do not affirm EDEN if the counter-scene cannot matter or no falsifier can be named. This protects the overlay from becoming accusation by vocabulary.

The MIP reading would strengthen only with a concrete scene packet. Required data include roles, chronology, confidentiality terms, consent or legal/public-interest basis, prior repair attempts, harm context, audience scale, safeguards, right of reply, correction channels, and evidence limits. MIP would then test knowledge, power/control, predictability, access to alternatives, and serious integration attempt for each role-position. Without these, M cannot be assigned to persons or concrete role-holders.

The IA-box would require evidence for transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. Transparency asks whether the asymmetry was visible to those affected. Justification asks what protected good or purpose justified it. Time-boundedness asks whether review points or end conditions existed. Reversibility asks whether real return, exit, correction, or reply options were available. The current result is IA-risk because these conditions are not scene-established. That does not mean the asymmetry is unjustified; it means the analysis cannot yet show that it is justified.

The D-module would require concrete evidence before any stronger D-reading. Public dignity risk depends on audience scale, identifiability, persistence, reply access, correction access, shame dynamics, and humiliation risk. Relational dignity depends on whether role-positions are treated under asymmetry with restraint and recognition. D0 remains untouched regardless. A concrete case may show high D-risk with low responsibility, in which case protection rather than blame is required.

AHP identifies additional operationalization conditions. It marks the missing concrete scene packet as a high-severity attack point. It warns that A-bands may be misread as person scores, M ceilings as low responsibility findings, IA-risk as failed asymmetry, publicness as too compressed, D-language as too rhetorically heavy, counter-readings as under-prioritized, and cross-model convergence as false validation. Its hardening actions are not case conclusions; they are requirements for safer future analysis.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled across all layers. Stronger claims would require empirical validation where empirical claims are made, comparative validation against rival readings, explicit calibration thresholds, external review, legal analysis where legal questions arise, and public accountability where outputs affect persons or institutions. None of this is supplied by the current analytical artefacts.

The examples show how operationalization would proceed without yet performing it. In the confidential-feedback example, stronger analysis would require knowing whether internal repair failed and whether public disclosure was proportionate. In the closed-mediation example, stronger analysis would require knowing the mediation terms, timeline, promises, review points, participants’ ability to speak, publicness level of the later post, identifiability of those implicated, available correction channels, and whether changes had already been implemented. In the anonymized-pattern example, stronger analysis would require mapping identifiability, audience, publicness gradient, and whether internal learning had actually occurred. The examples therefore prepare concrete scene questions; they do not answer them.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. No person, institution, group, public, or discourse community is diagnosed. PMS drift patterns are not pathologies. EDEN false-coordination risks are not traits. MIP A/M bands are not psychological maturity scores. AHP attack points are not faults in actors.

This case must not be used for person ranking. No one is ranked as mature, immature, dignified, undignified, responsible, irresponsible, adult, inadult, coherent, incoherent, exposed, protected, guilty, or superior. The role labels are abstract positions in a structural case-family. They are not identities.

This case must not be used as a legal or forensic conclusion. It does not decide breach of confidentiality, liability, protected disclosure, defamation, retaliation, evidentiary value, privilege, duty to warn, procedural validity, or public-interest legality. Legal questions require legal standards and concrete facts.

This case must not be used as metaphysical proof. PMS does not prove subjectivity, selfhood, consciousness, dignity, essence, or moral worth. SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only. Dignity-in-Practice is a praxeological constraint, not an ontology.

This case must not be used as empirical proof. It does not prove that boundary erosion happened. It does not prove that publicness occurred, that confidentiality existed, that repair failed, that disclosure was justified, or that anyone was harmed. It maps conditions under which such claims would need to be tested.

This case must not be used as implementation validation. YAML is not a software system, decision engine, moderation tool, governance model, HR procedure, therapeutic method, or enforcement protocol. Computational representation does not imply operational adequacy.

This case must not be used as PMS Base validation. A successful case rendering does not prove PMS completeness, uniqueness, finality, or superiority. PMS remains open to rival grammars and revision. The case does not alter the PMS operator inventory or dependency order.

This case must not treat add-ons as operators. EDEN constructs such as Adult Reciprocity, Action-Power, false-coordinated real asymmetry, AEP, correctability, and Reciprocity Loss are overlay handles, not PMS operators. MIP A/C/R/P/D submodules are application fields, not PMS Base replacements. AHP attack points and hardening actions are quality controls, not new analytic primitives.

This case must not treat drift as verdict. AD_A>>E, AD_Sigma_low, false coordination, overexposure, immunization, IA-risk, and D-stress are not conclusions about persons. They are conditional structural risks or analysis-quality signals. Risk language must not become accusation.

This case must not produce closure by authority. The layered stack is sophisticated, but sophistication can create false authority. PMS + EDEN + MIP + AHP convergence demonstrates structured compatibility and analytic discipline. It does not authorize final capture. The more layers are added, the more important it becomes to preserve non-capture, counter-scenes, and reversibility.

This case must not authorize action. No confrontation, disclosure, punishment, intervention, public statement, HR step, legal move, therapeutic plan, or relational demand follows from the article. The only safe next step named by the analyses is analytic: construct a concrete scene packet and test counter-readings before any stronger use.

The examples must also remain inside this misuse boundary. They are illustrative vignettes, not evidence. They do not demonstrate that any actual person, organization, association, recipient, author, or audience has acted immaturely or wrongly. They are designed to show where the analysis would look, not to supply conclusions.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is a disciplined, multi-layer structural reading of a frame-transfer case-family. PMS Core establishes the base grammar: the case turns on Difference, Frame, Temporality, Recontextualization, candidate Asymmetry, reflective Distance, and weak or conditional Integration and Self-Binding. It shows why the movement from bounded repair or confidentiality into public accountability or tribunal-like publicness is structurally significant. But PMS Core does not decide whether the movement is erosion, correction, whistleblowing, legal disclosure, democratic publicness, institutional concealment, or ordinary recontextualization.

PMS-EDEN adds a sharper asymmetry and responsibility lens. It asks whether real Ω is truthfully coordinated or falsely coordinated; whether R follows real structuring effect; whether Action-Power sits in confidentiality, disclosure, audience, timing, correction, or reply; whether Σ/Ψ load is shifted; and whether correctability remains available. But EDEN’s primary mechanism remains suspended. False-coordinated real asymmetry is a test in this case, not a finding. The scene packet is missing, and the counter-scenes remain strong.

MIP adds maturity-in-practice discipline. It distinguishes structural awareness from scene-level knowledge, coherence from forced narrative, responsibility from blame, power-to-act from formal possibility, IA-risk from verdict, and Dignity-in-Practice from dignity scoring. Its central contribution is methodological restraint: high maturity or responsibility cannot be assigned where knowledge, control, predictability, alternatives, and integration attempts are not evidenced. AHP then hardens the analysis by naming where it remains vulnerable: missing scene packet, score misreadings, IA overread, compressed publicness, D-language heaviness, counter-reading priority, and model-convergence-as-validation.

The examples contribute by making the abstract case readable without replacing the analysis. The confidential-feedback example shows the central Φ problem: material continuity is not frame continuity. The closed-mediation example shows that not only content but also a process-frame can be recontextualized, and that Λ and Σ cannot be inferred cheaply from the mere presence or public criticism of a closed process. The anonymized-pattern example shows that publicness is not limited to direct naming and that anonymization can protect, distort, or both. Each example demonstrates the analysis, but none functions as evidence. Each remains bounded, reversible, and non-verdictive.

What remains unclaimed is extensive and decisive. The article does not claim that boundary erosion occurred. It does not claim that publicness was justified or unjustified. It does not claim that confidentiality protected dignity or concealed power. It does not claim legal breach, moral guilt, diagnosis, person-level maturity, institutional fault, empirical truth, metaphysical status, implementation readiness, or PMS Base validation. It does not claim that PMS-EDEN’s false-coordination mechanism is active. It does not assign PMS drift patterns as findings. It does not assign M scores to persons. It does not treat IA-risk as failed asymmetry. It does not treat D-stress as dignity deficit.

The boundary discipline is therefore not a marginal caution; it is the article’s core method. Risk is not finding. Coherence is not proof. Non-assignment is not a low score. Non-capture is not failure. Resistance, refusal, non-fit, disagreement, rupture, silence, delay, or absence are not automatically drift. Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological, not ontological. Add-on terms remain overlay handles, not PMS operators. AHP and QC notes harden the analysis but do not rescore it or authorize use.

The final methodological takeaway is that privacy-to-publicness cases demand a grammar that can see both protection and concealment, both accountability and exposure, both repair and tribunalization, without collapsing them into a single verdict. The layered PMS → EDEN → MIP → AHP stack demonstrates one way to hold that difficulty open. It does not solve the case by closing it. It makes the conditions of any responsible closure visible: a concrete scene packet, calibrated operator activation, rival-reading comparison, falsifier discipline, publicness gradient, dignity protection, and a strict description-to-action firewall. Only under those conditions could a stronger scene-bound analysis begin.
