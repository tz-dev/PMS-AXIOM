### Why this case matters

This case matters because it exposes a closure-demand that is philosophically tempting and practically dangerous. The movement from privacy to publicness often calls for an immediate verdict. Either the public move is treated as accountability, courage, transparency, and the refusal of concealment; or it is treated as betrayal, exposure, tribunalization, and the destruction of repair conditions. Both readings can become plausible very quickly. Both can also become overconfident very quickly.

The underlying difficulty is that the same material changes when its frame changes. A confidential feedback statement, internal complaint, repair note, apology, institutional response, or bounded resolution attempt may function one way inside a constrained repair setting and another way when placed in a public field. Inside a bounded frame, the material may support repair, trust, procedural containment, role-specific responsibility, candor, learning, or non-humiliating correction. In a public frame, the same material may support accountability, warning, public learning, protection from institutional concealment, or democratic contestation. But the public frame may also convert it into evidence for standing loss, moral ranking, reputational punishment, or tribunal-like judgment. Conversely, the private frame may also convert confidentiality into silence, procedural closure, accountability avoidance, or protection of power.

This is the philosophical closure-demand: the case asks the reader to decide whether privacy or publicness is the mature frame. PMS refuses that shortcut. PMS does not say that internal repair is mature because it is bounded, nor that public accountability is mature because it is public. It also does not say that the frame transfer is automatically drift. The structural question is more precise: what happens when a material whose practical meaning was shaped by one frame is recontextualized into another frame under changed asymmetry, temporality, exposure, and responsibility conditions?

This is why the case belongs in AXIOM. It is not merely an applied ethics problem, not merely a media problem, not merely a confidentiality problem, and not merely a legal-process problem. It tests whether a structural grammar can remain disciplined when the case pulls toward public moral closure. AXIOM-level interest lies in the pressure on operators: difference, frame, temporality, asymmetry, recontextualization, distance, integration, and self-binding all become salient, but none can be cheaply used as a verdict. The case asks whether a model can name drift-risk without becoming a drift machine; whether it can preserve non-capture without becoming evasive; whether it can map dignity risk without producing dignity judgment; and whether it can preserve accountability without turning publicness into tribunal authority.

The case is structurally difficult because it is not only about what happened to a text, statement, complaint, or feedback item. It is about the practical status of a material across frames. That status depends on who created it, who received it, what expectations governed it, what repair was promised, what accountability was owed, what was absent, what alternatives existed, what the public audience could do to the material, how reply capacity was preserved or lost, and how reversible the consequences remained. None of those facts is supplied in the abstract case material. The analysis therefore cannot be a scene verdict. It must remain a pre-legal, non-diagnostic, non-metaphysical, structurally reversible case-family analysis.

This is also why the case is not solved metaphysically. PMS does not ask whether privacy or publicness has a higher essence. It does not rank forms of discourse by ontological dignity. It does not infer inner motives from disclosure, silence, escalation, refusal, repair language, or accountability language. It does not claim that public truth is metaphysically superior to private repair, or that boundedness is metaphysically superior to publicity. Dignity, where it appears, is treated only as Dignity-in-Practice: a praxeological constraint on how asymmetry is handled.

The case is not solved empirically, because there is no evidence record. It is not solved clinically, because no mental state, trauma pattern, diagnosis, pathology, maturity trait, or personality structure is inferred. It is not solved legally, because confidentiality, privilege, defamation, retaliation, protected disclosure, duty to warn, evidentiary admissibility, professional secrecy, and procedural due process all require external legal facts. It is not solved diagnostically, because no person or group is classified. The point is to hold the case open structurally without dissolving the problem into vagueness.

The case therefore matters as a test of disciplined non-closure. A weak model would either decide too quickly or refuse to say anything. The completed analyses attempt a third option: they identify the structural pressure points while withholding claims that the material does not warrant. Boundary erosion is not denied, but neither is it asserted as fact. Public accountability is not romanticized, but neither is it treated as drift by default. Confidentiality is not sanctified, but neither is it dismissed as suppression. The case matters because this balanced non-closure is not indecision; it is the substance of the analysis.

### Scene and frame structure

The scene is conceptual, not empirical. It consists of a frame transfer: a material originally associated with confidential feedback or internal resolution becomes available to public accountability discourse or a tribunal-like public field. Because the case is not concrete, the scene boundary is defined by the structural contrast itself rather than by an event record.

The source frame is the bounded repair or internal-resolution frame. Inside that frame are expectations of confidentiality, trust, repair, role-specific feedback, internal handling, procedural containment, or non-public resolution. These expectations may be legitimate. They may support learning, non-humiliating correction, candor, and repair. But they are not self-justifying. The private frame may also conceal asymmetry, delay accountability, silence affected parties, or convert confidentiality into closure.

The target frame is public accountability discourse or tribunal-like publicness. Inside that frame are expectations of answerability, public scrutiny, warning, transparency, democratic contestation, exposure of institutional failure, or public-interest disclosure. These expectations may be legitimate. They may protect vulnerable parties or correct failed internal processes. But they too are not self-justifying. Publicness may convert repair material into spectacle, reputational punishment, ranking, humiliation, or irreversible status regulation.

What is outside the frame is equally important. Outside the PMS Core claim are legal validity, empirical proof, diagnostic interpretation, metaphysical ranking, and implementation authority. Outside the EDEN overlay are group labels, ideology claims, person labels, and regime findings not supported by concrete evidence. Outside the MIP layer are person scores, dignity scores, and actionable maturity rankings. Outside AHP are first-order case findings; AHP only evaluates the precision and attack surface of the MIP artefact.

The protected constraints are Distance, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. Distance means that the analysis must not fuse with public verdict or private containment. Reversibility means that findings remain revisable and non-totalizing. Dignity-in-Practice means that asymmetry is handled without humiliation, person-ranking, or ontological evaluation. These constraints are not optional style preferences; they are entry conditions for valid PMS application.

The role structure is also conceptual. The YAMLs name role-positions rather than persons: a bounded repair or confidentiality-holder role; a possible institution or internal process-holder; a possible publicizing or accountability-seeking position; a possible exposed position; a possible harmed, silenced, or accountability-claiming position; a public audience or tribunal-like discourse field; and a possible procedural or legal frame-holder. These are not identities or classes of people. They are praxeological positions that may carry different knowledge, power, vulnerability, exposure, and responsibility gradients.

Temporality is central. Boundary erosion is not a static difference between private and public. It is a movement across time. At t0, the material is generated, received, or held under a bounded repair frame. At t1, it becomes available within publicness. The meaning of the material changes across that transition. The publicness threshold may increase awareness of options, but awareness is not maturity by itself. Publicness may create accountability, but it may also create irreversibility. Confidentiality may preserve repair, but it may also freeze unresolved non-events.

The reversibility window is therefore fragile. Once material becomes public, it may become searchable, quotable, decontextualized, reputationally persistent, and difficult to repair. But private containment can also become irreversible in another way: unresolved silence, blocked accountability, or procedural closure may harden over time. The case cannot decide which irreversibility dominates. It can only require that reversibility be explicitly considered.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are under-specified but structurally salient. Publicness can increase exposure, audience power, interpretive loss, reputational cost, and need for reply capacity. Confidentiality can concentrate control over context, process, timing, and accountability. The direction of asymmetry cannot be assumed. The holder of confidentiality may hold power. The publicizing actor may hold power. The institution may hold power. The audience may hold diffuse power. The affected or exposed role may carry disproportionate costs. PMS, EDEN, and MIP all converge on this point: asymmetry must be mapped, not moralized.

Because this is not a concrete scene, no stronger conclusion follows. The analysis cannot say that the boundary was wrongly crossed. It cannot say that the boundary should have been crossed. It cannot say that publicness was tribunalization. It cannot say that confidentiality was concealment. It can say only that the frame transfer is structurally high-risk and requires disciplined calibration.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading begins with **Δ, Difference**. Difference is active. The case depends on distinctions: confidential versus public, internal versus external, repair versus accountability, bounded audience versus open audience, original-use material versus secondary-use material, contextualized feedback versus public evidence. These distinctions do not carry moral verdicts by themselves. The error would be to convert Δ into a value opposition: privacy good, publicness bad; or publicness good, privacy bad. The proper role of Δ is to mark the structural difference that makes the case intelligible.

**∇, Impulse**, is weak and conditional. The case implies directional pressure from one frame toward another: containment toward disclosure, repair toward accountability, silence toward exposure, or internal handling toward public answerability. But the abstract material does not specify motive. ∇ must therefore remain structural, not psychological. It would be overclaim to infer retaliation, courage, fear, strategic exposure, bad faith, moral urgency, or avoidance from the mere existence of frame transfer.

**□, Frame**, is active and central. The case is not only about material moving from one place to another. It is about the frame that gives material practical meaning. In a confidential repair frame, material may be oriented toward correction, trust, internal response, role-specific feedback, or bounded accountability. In a public accountability frame, the same material may become evidence, accusation, warning, public learning, or tribunal material. Calibration requires naming both frames without granting automatic legitimacy to either. The internal frame must not be assumed restorative; the public frame must not be assumed tribunalizing.

**Λ, Non-Event**, is weak-to-moderate and requires careful handling. Boundary erosion may be driven by a structured absence: expected internal repair did not occur; expected confidentiality was not preserved; expected accountability was delayed; expected response or closure was absent; expected proportionality, context, or reply capacity was missing. But the active non-event is not supplied. It would be overclaim to decide that repair failed or confidentiality was breached. It would also be overclaim to treat absence as maturity failure. Λ must remain a candidate structure until a scene specifies the expectation and its failure.

**Α, Attractor**, is weak. The language of drift suggests possible stabilization, but one abstract frame transfer does not prove recurrent patterning. Α would become stronger if there were repeated conversions of internal repair material into public pressure, repeated uses of confidentiality to block accountability, repeated tribunalization of repair material, or repeated context-repair loops without interface change. Without recurrence, path dependence, or role-script evidence, Α remains a risk marker, not an established pattern.

**Ω, Asymmetry**, is active as a structural concern but dependency-sensitive. Publicness can alter exposure, vulnerability, obligation, leverage, reply capacity, and audience power. Confidentiality can also alter power by controlling access, timing, process, and accountability. Ω therefore cannot be assigned to one side by default. Calibration must ask who controls the frame, who bears consequences, who can answer, who is exposed, who is protected, who is silenced, and who benefits from private or public handling. Overclaim occurs when Ω becomes moral judgment: publicness as inherently oppressive, or confidentiality as inherently suppressive.

**Θ, Temporality**, is active. Boundary erosion is a temporal movement, not merely a conceptual contrast. Material is created or held in one frame and later becomes available in another. Across time, expectations accumulate, repair may fail or succeed, public-interest thresholds may arise, consequences may become less reversible, and silence or exposure may harden. Calibration requires chronology. Without chronology, Θ is conceptually plausible but scene-under-specified. The overclaim would be to imagine delay, escalation, recurrence, or accumulation that the case material does not provide.

**Φ, Recontextualization**, is the central PMS Core operator. Existing material is placed into a new frame, transforming its relevance, audience, exposure, and meaning. But Φ does not decide whether the transformation is erosion, correction, protection, disclosure, distortion, or warranted escalation. It only names the structural fact that material is being handled under a new frame. The completed YAML analysis correctly expands this point: the analysis must distinguish frame transfer from mere repetition, quotation, documentation, anonymized learning, consented publication, legally required disclosure, or independently generated public criticism. Φ would be overextended if every public movement were called drift without testing consent, necessity, proportionality, safeguards, prior repair failure, and audience.

**Χ, Distance**, is active primarily as application discipline. The analysis must maintain reflective distance from both frames. It must not fuse with public tribunal logic, and it must not fuse with private containment. It must refuse premature closure, person-ranking, and verdict language. Case-level Χ is unknown: actors may or may not have maintained restraint. But analytic Χ is mandatory. Without it, PMS becomes exactly what it is meant to avoid: a closure machine that uses structural language to force a judgment.

**Σ, Integration**, is weak but analytically important. The case asks whether privacy and accountability can be integrated: repair without concealment, accountability without humiliation, privacy without impunity, publicness without spectacle. The central risk is that Φ outpaces Σ. Material is recontextualized before the competing frames are integrated. But Σ must not be confused with smooth coherence. Integration is not reconciliation at any cost, not narrative closure, not institutional self-protection, and not public consensus. It is a structural question: can the burdens of both frames be carried without externalizing costs?

**Ψ, Self-Binding**, is weak and conditional at the case level. It becomes active only if commitments are specified: confidentiality promises, repair duties, accountability obligations, public-interest duties, non-retaliation norms, restraint commitments, or procedural self-binding across time. The analysis itself is bound by Ψ as application discipline: it must not overclaim, shame, rank, or collapse into verdict. But case-level Ψ cannot be inferred. It would be overclaim to assign responsibility, maturity, or selfhood failure without concrete commitments.

Taken together, the PMS Core formula is a Φ-driven boundary-transfer under Δ, □, Ω, and Θ, with Λ and Α conditional, Σ at risk, Χ required, and Ψ conditional. This is a strong structural reading, but its strength is precisely why it requires discipline. The fit is smooth; smoothness is not proof.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

PMS dependency discipline matters because later operators are powerful. If used cheaply, they turn structural description into verdict. The completed YAMLs explicitly preserve the PMS operator order and dependency logic: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. Later operators are not free-standing labels. They depend on earlier structures and must be calibrated against scene evidence.

Φ cannot be used merely because a public discussion occurs. It depends on Θ, Ω, and □. There must be a frame transfer across time, under asymmetry, from one context to another. If the material was public from the beginning, consented for public use, legally required to be public, or independently generated as public criticism, Φ weakens as the central operator.

Ω cannot be cheaply moralized. In the PMS dependency table, Ω depends on Α, meaning that established asymmetry often emerges from stabilized patterns. In this case, Ω is structurally salient even though Α is weak, because publicness itself creates plausible exposure gradients. But that must remain a dependency warning: the analysis may name Ω-risk, not established asymmetric patterning, unless recurrence or stable role structure is supplied.

Θ cannot be inflated into a story. The case gives temporal movement but not chronology. It does not show how long internal repair lasted, whether there was delay, whether escalation followed non-response, whether publicness was immediate, or whether consequences accumulated. Any stronger temporal narrative would be invented.

Χ is not optional. Without Χ, PMS collapses into either public accusation or private defense. Distance is not neutrality in the sense of indifference; it is the reflective discipline that prevents the model from becoming captured by one frame’s urgency.

Σ remains conditional because integration is not shown. The mere fact that the YAML can produce a coherent formula does not mean the case is integrated. PMS coherence may become under-discriminating. It can make boundary erosion look formally settled while failing to distinguish harmful exposure from warranted disclosure, restorative confidentiality from suppression, democratic accountability from tribunalization, or legal necessity from drift.

Ψ remains conditional because commitments are not supplied. Self-binding cannot be inferred from rhetoric. Invoking confidentiality is not automatically Ψ. Invoking accountability is not automatically Ψ. Ψ would require role-specific, temporally stable commitments actually carried across the frame transfer.

Non-capture is not failure. The case may exceed PMS in ways that matter. Legal privilege may be decisive. Whistleblowing law may be decisive. Procedural justice may be decisive. Information ethics may preserve distinctions PMS does not fully encode. Public-sphere theory may better describe democratic accountability. Restorative practice may better describe internal repair failure. PMS must preserve these rival grammars rather than absorb them as incomplete integration.

Invalid shortcuts would include: treating publicness as Φ-drift without testing legitimacy; treating confidentiality as □-repair without testing power; treating absence as Λ-failure without specifying expectation; treating recurrence as Α because the word drift appears; treating asymmetry as Ω-blame; treating public discourse as moral frame capture by default; treating Σ as coherence of the model rather than integration of praxis; and treating Ψ as person-level responsibility without commitments.

The most important calibration discipline is therefore not only formal but ethical-practical. The model must not become a closure device. It can organize inquiry; it cannot decide what the scene does not supply. It can show where the burden of proof would lie; it cannot fill that burden itself. It can identify where publicness raises dignity risk; it cannot convert that risk into a dignity finding. It can identify where confidentiality raises accountability risk; it cannot convert that risk into concealment finding. Dependency hygiene is thus also misuse prevention.

### Derived structures

The derived structures in PMS are formula-derived, not primitive. They do not add independent axes of judgment. They emerge from operator compositions and remain dependent on the same calibration discipline.

**Awareness_A** is active but bounded. Its formula is Θ / □ / Δ: sustained, framed differentiation across time. The case clearly contains structural awareness in this sense. It differentiates private and public, internal and external, repair and accountability, bounded and open, original use and secondary use. This is not phenomenological consciousness. It does not mean actors were aware. It means the case structure can be rendered as sustained framed difference across time.

The case is therefore structurally intelligible as Awareness_A, but only in a restricted sense. Awareness_A does not establish empirical awareness, moral clarity, or mature reflection. The publicness threshold may make an option visible, but visibility is not responsible action. An actor may see that disclosure is possible without understanding its consequences. An institution may know that confidentiality exists without understanding how silence distributes cost. Awareness_A provides a structural condition for further analysis, not a completed maturity claim.

**Coherence_C** is weak. Its formula is Θ / Λ / □ / ∇: temporally stabilized structuring of impulse and expectation within a frame. The case suggests possible coherence problems, but it does not specify the operative non-event, expectation, impulse, or sequence. Coherence is therefore fragile. The private and public frames may each have internal coherence; their transition is not shown to be coherent. Model coherence is not enough.

The private frame could be coherent if it actually supports repair, review, answerability, and non-humiliating correction. The public frame could be coherent if it supports proportionate accountability, context, right of reply, and public-interest learning. But the transition between them is the weak point. Without a specified non-event and without a specified repair or escalation sequence, the analysis cannot say whether the shift into publicness is coherent escalation, reactive rupture, legal necessity, public-interest disclosure, or frame collapse.

**Responsibility_R** is weak and conditional. Its formula is Ψ / Φ / Θ / Ω: self-binding orientation toward asymmetry across time and recontextualization. The case raises responsibility questions, but does not provide commitments, roles, knowledge, power, or alternatives. Responsibility could attach to confidentiality, repair, accountability, proportionality, restraint, or public-interest duties in a concrete scene. Here it remains structural and conditional, not moral blame or legal liability.

Responsibility_R would require specification of who had what obligations under which asymmetry. A confidentiality-holder may have duties of care, repair, and escalation. A publicizing actor may have duties of accuracy, proportionality, context, and non-humiliation. An institution may have duties of transparency, process, and protection. A public audience may have duties of restraint or may not be a coherent responsibility-bearing role at all. Without such specification, Responsibility_R remains a map of possible responsibility vectors, not an assignment.

**Action_E** is under-specified. Its formula is Σ / Θ / ∇: integrated realization of directedness across time. The case names frame transfer, but not a concrete integrated enactment. Publicization, disclosure, containment, refusal, or escalation are not Action_E unless Σ carries integrated directedness across Θ. The YAMLs correctly refuse to treat breach-like realization, disclosure, or publicness as Action_E by default.

This is especially important because publicness can look like action. Posting, quoting, disclosing, publishing, or escalating may be visible and consequential. But PMS Action_E is not mere movement or event. It requires integrated direction over time. A disclosure may be reactive, protective, strategic, proportionate, reckless, necessary, or legally required. Without integration evidence, the action structure is not established.

**Dignity_in_Practice_D** is active as an application constraint, not as an ontological dignity claim. Its formula is Ψ / Χ / Ω: self-bound reflective restraint under asymmetry. In this case, D requires non-humiliating handling of publicness, exposure, confidentiality, voice, and accountability. It prevents person-ranking, dignity scoring, shaming, and public verdict. It does not say that one frame is more dignified than the other. It does not assign dignity status to persons.

The MIP analysis later reinforces this point by distinguishing D0 from D1/D2 enactment risks. D0 remains untouched and non-rankable. Dignity-in-Practice concerns how asymmetry is handled in praxis: whether a person or role is exposed without reply, silenced without accountability, ranked publicly, humiliated, erased, or forced to carry costs that another role externalizes. But even these are risks in this case, not findings. D works here primarily as a guardrail for the analysis itself.

**SELF_FIXPOINT** is analytic-only or not applicable as literal selfhood. Its formula is the full derived chain Ψ ∘ Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ. The case is not about selfhood. SELF_FIXPOINT could become metaphorically relevant to institutional self-binding if an institution’s commitments across confidentiality and accountability were specified. But no such concrete institutional self-binding is supplied. The valid use here is only analytic: the PMS application itself must remain self-bound to its guardrails.

The derived structures therefore deepen the analysis without changing its claim status. Awareness_A is active as structural differentiation. Coherence_C is weak. Responsibility_R is conditional. Action_E is under-specified. Dignity_in_Practice_D is active as application constraint. SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only. None of these derived structures becomes a primitive, a score, a diagnosis, or a verdict.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core analysis treats drift patterns as conditional structural risks, not findings. This distinction is central. A drift-pattern label does not say that someone has failed, that a person is immature, that publicness is wrong, that confidentiality is wrong, or that a stable pathology exists. Drift patterns describe possible distortions in praxis configurations. In this case, both relevant PMS drift patterns remain possible but unproven.

**AD_A>>E**, the pattern of excessive distance between Awareness and Enactment, is possible but not entailed. The YAML frames this as a risk in which awareness expands while integrated action remains delayed, suppressed, or displaced. In the boundary-erosion case, public accountability discourse may generate enormous evaluative awareness: more people know, more people interpret, more people judge, more people compare versions, and more discourse circulates around what the material now means. Yet this expansion of awareness may not produce repair, response, proportional accountability, or integrated action. The public frame can become a field of evaluation rather than enactment.

But that is only one possible trajectory. Publicness may also enable action when internal repair failed. It may create the first real accountability channel. It may protect vulnerable parties, warn others, or force institutional response. Therefore AD_A>>E cannot be assigned merely because the material enters public discourse. Its axis effects remain under-specified: the awareness axis may be over-expanded, but the case does not prove it; the action axis may be suppressed or chronically delayed, but the case does not prove that either. The YAML correctly marks both as under-specified. This is not a low score. It is a refusal to convert structural risk into finding.

**AD_Sigma_low**, fragmented integration, is more directly relevant but still not established. The case is structurally close to AD_Sigma_low because the central risk is that Φ outpaces Σ. Recontextualization occurs faster than integration. A material moves from a bounded repair frame into publicness before confidentiality, accountability, exposure, public interest, proportionality, reply capacity, and context have been coordinated. In this sense, the case is a natural site for Σ-risk.

Yet even here, the PMS reading must remain reversible. Public recontextualization may be an integrative correction rather than fragmentation. If internal repair failed, concealed harm, blocked voice, or preserved asymmetry, publicness may carry integration that the private frame could not. Conversely, if publicness strips context, produces humiliation, or bypasses reply, it may fracture integration. The question cannot be answered abstractly. The coherence axis and responsibility axis remain under-specified. Coherence may be volatile, but this is not shown. Responsibility may be inconsistent, but commitments and levers are unknown.

The larger coherence risk is that PMS itself fits the case too neatly. The formula Δ + □ + Θ + Φ + Ω + Σ-risk is elegant. That elegance is dangerous. It can make the case look already captured. The YAMLs explicitly warn against this. A coherent PMS derivation must still show discriminative force. It must distinguish boundary erosion from legitimate public accountability, whistleblowing, legal disclosure, anonymized learning, democratic publicness, restorative escalation, and protective confidentiality. If PMS cannot discriminate these cases, then its smoothness becomes an under-discrimination risk.

Under-specification is therefore not failure. It is part of the correct result. The case is abstract. The analysis should not compensate for missing scene data by inventing drift, motive, recurrence, or failure. Non-assignment is not a low maturity score. It is methodological restraint.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant add-on is **PMS-EDEN**, used as an overlay/application profile. EDEN is not PMS Base. PMS.yaml remains the only PMS Base reference. EDEN does not redefine PMS operators, change dependencies, add PMS axioms, or introduce new primitive structures. Its reduced signatures are shorthand lenses and threshold markers. Its constructs are paper-internal overlay handles. They may make certain risks more visible, but they do not become operators.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer here because the case is not only a generic frame-transfer problem. It also has a specific publicness structure: material crosses from a bounded repair context into public accountability or tribunal-like discourse. That movement can activate comparison, pseudo-symmetry, switch-risk, cost externalization, and reciprocity strain. PMS Core gives the structural grammar: Δ, □, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. EDEN sharpens certain publicness and relational-drift questions that PMS Core names more generally.

The EDEN overlay makes visible the **threshold** problem. Once bounded material becomes selectable as public material, a new option appears: publicizing, quoting, framing as evidence, inviting public judgment, using the material for public learning, or placing it into accountability discourse. EDEN insists that threshold visibility is not knowledge gain. The fact that a public option becomes available does not mean it is legitimate, necessary, mature, or true. The threshold only marks that a negative or risky praxis option has become selectable within a frame across time.

EDEN also makes visible possible **breach as chain interruption**. If there was an expected chain from confidential feedback to internal repair, publicization may interrupt it. If there was an expected chain from harm to accountability, confidentiality may interrupt it. But the YAML marks breach as weak. There is no evidence of an actual breached promise, legal duty, repair chain, or accountability chain. EDEN therefore cannot say that a breach occurred. It can only name what would have to be checked.

The **Negative Responsibility Kernel**, or NRK, is also weak. EDEN treats NRK as a paper-internal composite, not a PMS operator and not Action_E. NRK would require awareness of real options, effective asymmetry, temporal trajectory, realization under impulse within a frame, and failure or displacement of integration and self-binding. The case does not supply this. Public disclosure, containment, escalation, or refusal cannot be called NRK merely because they are consequential. The YAML explicitly preserves the distinction: NRK is not Action_E, and Action_E remains under-specified.

**Fig leaves / legibility management** appear as a risk. In EDEN terms, a post-breach response may manage exposure rather than integrate the underlying issue. In this case, confidentiality can become shielding if it reduces visibility without carrying repair or accountability. Publicness can also become display if it exposes selected material while obscuring context, reply capacity, proportionality, or repair history. But neither concealment nor display is established. The overlay marks the risk without making a finding.

The **false comparison pivot** is one of the most case-relevant EDEN risks. The movement into publicness can convert a praxis relation into a value relation. Instead of asking how repair and accountability are coordinated, the public field may ask who is right, who is credible, who failed, who should lose standing, or whose interpretation wins. Likewise, a private frame can convert public accountability demand into a value judgment against the claimant: disloyal, excessive, improper, or untrustworthy. EDEN helps name this pivot. Yet the YAML keeps it as risk, not finding. There is no evidence of ranking language, standing loss, verdict scripts, or stabilized comparison.

**Dissatisfaction as system cost** is weak. EDEN would use this handle where false equivalence or unresolved frame conflict produces recurring correction, comparison, adjustment, and non-event load. The abstract case does not show recurrence. It does not show repeated publicization, repeated containment, chronic correction cycles, or continuing dissatisfaction. This handle remains an operationalization prompt only.

**Pseudo-Symmetry**, or PS, is a risk and possible temporary buffer, not an active regime finding. PS appears when a comparison-dominant frame asserts rhetorical parity while real asymmetry remains operative in consequences. In this case, a parity surface might say: confidentiality and public accountability are just two equal options; or all parties are equally bound by confidentiality; or all parties are equally subject to public scrutiny. But real costs may not be equal. Publicness may impose reputational, contextual, and reply-capacity burdens. Confidentiality may impose silence, blocked accountability, or failed repair. PS becomes problematic if parity appearance replaces real Ω naming, Σ work, and Ψ commitment. But PS may also function as a temporary buffer if it slows premature verdict and reopens integration. Persistent PS is not established.

**Asymmetry-switch availability** is a central EDEN risk. Either frame may activate asymmetry selectively under pressure. Confidentiality may invoke vulnerability, trust, repair, legal duty, or process integrity to restrict publicness. Public accountability may invoke harm, public interest, institutional failure, or protection to justify exposure. Such activation can be valid if marked, bounded, and re-bound into integration and self-binding. It becomes drift only if unmarked, repeatable, and responsibility-shifting. The case supplies no evidence that such a switch occurred. The overlay therefore marks switch-risk, not switch-finding.

**Permanentized Exception Switch** is not applicable. The case does not show a standing lever, recurrent exception logic, preserved parity surface, or protocolized asymmetry activation. A single abstract movement from privacy to publicness cannot support this EDEN construct.

**Parity-to-exit** is a bidirectional risk. The privacy frame may use parity language to deny additional responsibility: everyone agreed this was internal, therefore no public answerability is owed. The public frame may use parity language to deny additional responsibility in the opposite direction: everyone is subject to public accountability, therefore no special burden exists for context, proportionality, or reply capacity. Neither route is established. EDEN’s contribution is to show that parity can become an exit from cost-bearing.

**Responsibility-to-authority** is another bidirectional risk. Responsibility for repair, protection, confidentiality, or accountability may become authority over the frame. The internal process-holder may claim authority to decide what can be said. The public accountability actor may claim authority to decide what must be exposed. The audience may claim interpretive authority without carrying costs. EDEN marks this conversion risk, but does not assert that it occurred.

**PFO**, Postfeminist Override / Over-steering, is not applicable. The YAML is emphatic here. PFO is a paper-internal regime label, not a group label and not an ideology claim. The case does not show Ω-taboo governance, comparison metrics, permanentized switch availability, protocolized attractors, or regime-level drift. To apply PFO would overreach and politicize the case beyond the material. The correct EDEN reading is local threshold and switch-risk without full PFO.

**Reciprocity** is weak as a demonstrated condition and active as a calibration requirement. EDEN defines reciprocity not as symmetry but as coordinated asymmetry. In this case, confidentiality must not externalize accountability costs, and publicness must not externalize exposure, context-loss, or reply-capacity costs. Reciprocity loss is therefore a risk: if neither frame carries its own burdens, the transition becomes non-reciprocal. But loss is not shown. Public disclosure may restore reciprocity after internal failure; confidentiality may preserve reciprocity by preventing disproportionate exposure.

**Devaluation and humiliation** remain residual stabilization risks. Publicness may lower standing-in-practice if material becomes status evidence rather than accountable repair. Confidentiality may lower standing if it treats accountability demand as disloyal or improper. Humiliation may emerge when exposure replaces proportionate accountability or when private containment erases voice. But these are risks, not findings. EDEN treats humiliation structurally, not as accusation, diagnosis, or inner shame state.

The EDEN-MAP output condenses the case into a mapping formula: hybrid internal repair to public accountability frame; candidate non-events around repair, confidentiality, accountability, or reply; no established attractor; exposure, obligation, reply-capacity, and frame-control risk; weak or under-specified integration; conditional and unproven self-binding. This map is useful because it shows the cost geometry. It is also dangerous if mistaken for completeness. The YAML therefore preserves compression risk: EDEN-MAP is cartographic, not ontological.

### Layer interaction

The PMS Core reading remains the base structural pass. It provides the operator grammar, dependency discipline, derived structures, and non-capture guardrails. PMS Core identifies the case as Φ-driven boundary transfer under Δ, □, Ω, and Θ, with Λ and Α conditional, Σ-risk, Χ-required, and Ψ-conditional.

EDEN sharpens what happens near the publicness threshold. It does not change the PMS formula; it asks what the formula makes newly visible when the target frame is public accountability or tribunal-like discourse. Its strongest contributions are threshold explicitness, false comparison risk, pseudo-symmetry risk, local switch-risk, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, reciprocity as non-externalization of unequal burdens, and the distinction between local switch drift and full regime drift.

MIP then translates the PMS/EDEN case into a maturity-in-practice grammar. MIP does not assign person maturity. It asks how awareness, coherence, responsibility, and power-to-act are distributed across roles and publicness conditions. It renders the case as an under-specified IA-risk configuration. Its strongest contribution is practical hygiene: do not assign A or M to persons; do not use D as a dignity score; do not infer maturity failure from publicness, silence, rupture, or refusal; and do not use the analysis beyond internal structural reflection.

AHP then analyses the MIP artefact, not the case. It finds high precision but identifies attack surfaces: polished YAML may be mistaken for a public-ready decision artefact; the provisional A-band may be extracted as a maturity score; D-module activation may invite dignity-profile over-reading; publicness thresholds remain qualitative; the model-mediated evidence chain could be separated more clearly; dignity notation could be tightened; and hardening steps could be linked more explicitly to attack points.

The layers therefore interact as follows. PMS Core gives the formal grammar. EDEN gives publicness-threshold and reciprocity-risk visibility. MIP gives maturity-in-practice and dignity-protection discipline. AHP gives artefact-quality and misuse-hardening discipline. None of the later layers is allowed to redefine the earlier one. EDEN does not become PMS. MIP does not become a scoring authority over persons. AHP does not upgrade the analysis into a decision instrument.

Overlay language could overreach in several ways. EDEN terms such as pseudo-symmetry, switch drift, PFO, devaluation, and humiliation could be misread as accusations. MIP terms such as maturity, A-band, M-score, IA-risk, and D-profile could be misread as evaluations of actors. AHP attack points could be misread as defects in people rather than vulnerabilities in the analysis artefact. The completed YAMLs repeatedly block these misuses. The article must preserve that discipline.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. PMS does not close the case. EDEN does not close it. MIP does not close it. AHP does not close it. The case remains open to legal, procedural, ethical, institutional, information-theoretic, restorative, and public-sphere readings.

The following examples are illustrative stress tests for the analysis, not evidence for the case. They are deliberately bounded: each vignette makes one or more structural pressures readable while preserving the rule that risk is not finding, publicness is not automatically erosion, confidentiality is not automatically repair, and rival readings remain open.

### Example 1 — The internal feedback note that becomes public evidence

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** After “PMS Core structural reading,” before “PMS dependency and calibration discipline.”

A small organization runs an internal review after a difficult project. Participants are invited to submit confidential feedback. The stated purpose is repair: identify what went wrong, reduce future harm, and let people speak candidly without fear of immediate public exposure. One participant writes that the project lead repeatedly ignored warnings, dismissed concerns, and created a climate in which junior staff stopped speaking. The feedback is submitted through the internal channel.

Several weeks pass. The organization announces that the project is closed and that “all concerns were addressed internally.” One participant believes this is false. They quote part of the confidential feedback in a public post, arguing that the organization is using confidentiality to avoid accountability. The quoted passage circulates widely. Some readers treat it as evidence that the project lead is unfit for future work. Others say the public quotation violated the repair process and turned confidential feedback into a public tribunal.

This vignette demonstrates the core structure of boundary erosion without deciding whether the public quotation was justified. The material changes frame. In the first frame, the feedback note belongs to a bounded repair process. Its audience, purpose, and expected use are constrained. In the second frame, the same material becomes public evidence in an accountability discourse. The public move may be warranted if the internal process failed, concealed harm, or falsely claimed resolution. It may also be erosive if it strips context, bypasses reply, or converts repair material into reputational judgment. The example is therefore not a verdict about disclosure. It is a readable scene in which the frame-transfer problem becomes visible.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. Δ appears in the distinction between confidential/internal and public/accountability-facing use. ∇ appears only weakly as structural pressure: repair pressure, disclosure pressure, accountability pressure, and exposure pressure. No motive is inferred. □ is central because the same sentence functions differently inside an internal review and a public post. Λ is a candidate because the participant may be responding to an absent repair, an absent truthful institutional account, or an absent accountability step; but this must be specified. Α is not established unless the organization repeatedly closes internal complaints without repair, or participants repeatedly publicize confidential material as the only available route. Ω is active as risk: the project lead may bear public reputational exposure; junior staff may have carried unequal silence or dependency; the organization may control the internal record; the public may exert diffuse judgment power. Θ appears in the sequence from internal submission to public quotation after a period of alleged non-response. Φ is the strongest operator: the note is recontextualized from repair material into public evidence. Χ is required from the analysis: the reader must not fuse with either the public post or the confidentiality claim. Σ is weak and at risk because repair and accountability are not yet integrated. Ψ is conditional: confidentiality promises, repair duties, public-interest obligations, and reply duties would need to be specified before responsibility could be assigned.

The EDEN overlay helps sharpen the threshold. The feedback note becomes selectable as public material. That threshold does not itself create truth, legitimacy, or wrongdoing. It only marks that a new option is now available: quotation, public framing, audience expansion, and accountability pressure. EDEN also marks false comparison risk. Public readers may compare standing: who is credible, who failed, who should be blamed. That may replace praxis coordination. Pseudo-symmetry may appear if people treat “confidentiality” and “public accountability” as formally equal options while real costs differ. Asymmetry-switch risk is also bidirectional: the organization may invoke confidentiality to block accountability, while the publicizing actor may invoke harm or public interest to bypass proportionality and reply. None of these risks is a finding.

MIP renders the vignette as high-publicness and under-specified unless more facts are supplied. It would ask what each role knew, what each role could do, what was predictable, what alternatives existed, and whether any serious integration attempt occurred. The internal process-holder may have had power to repair or respond. The publicizing participant may have had power to anonymize, contextualize, delay, or seek bounded disclosure. The exposed project lead may or may not have had reply capacity. The public audience may amplify consequences without carrying procedural responsibility. MIP would not assign a global maturity score. AHP would warn that a polished analysis of this example could itself become a public judgment if transmitted without guardrails.

Forbidden claims are essential here. The example does not show that the publicizing participant acted maturely or immaturely. It does not show that the project lead is guilty, unsafe, incompetent, or morally deficient. It does not show that the organization committed legal wrongdoing. It does not show that confidentiality is manipulation or that publicness is shaming. It does not diagnose anyone and does not rank dignity.

The non-capture note is equally important. A legal whistleblowing frame might be decisive. A procedural justice analysis might ask whether the project lead had notice and reply rights. An institutional-power analysis might focus on whether the internal process was structurally incapable of accountability. A restorative-practice reading might ask whether the feedback process had any genuine repair mechanism. PMS and EDEN illuminate the frame transfer; they do not exhaust the case.

The article takeaway is that boundary erosion becomes analytically visible when a material crosses from bounded repair into public accountability, but the crossing alone does not decide legitimacy. The mature question is whether the transition carries its asymmetries: repair without concealment, accountability without humiliation, publicness without context collapse, and confidentiality without impunity.

### Example 2 — The anonymized lesson that nearly becomes a tribunal

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** After “Add-on / overlay reading,” where EDEN threshold, pseudo-symmetry, and reciprocity risks are discussed.

A university department holds a confidential mediation after a conflict between a supervisor and several graduate students. The mediation produces a short internal resolution document. It names communication failures, unclear expectations, delayed responses, and a need for better supervisory structures. The document is not meant to identify individuals publicly. Its purpose is to support local repair and prevent recurrence.

Months later, the department wants to publish a public-facing “lessons learned” memo. The memo is intended to acknowledge that supervisory structures failed and to explain new safeguards. A draft includes several details from the confidential mediation. Although names are removed, the small size of the department makes the supervisor and students easily identifiable to insiders. Some faculty argue that transparency is necessary because the department has a public responsibility to show that it has changed. Others object that the memo converts a bounded repair process into public reputational exposure. A revised draft removes identifying details, describes structural changes rather than personal failures, adds a right-of-reply process for affected participants, and states what will remain confidential.

This example demonstrates that publicness is not automatically boundary erosion. The case is still structurally risky because material from a bounded repair process is being moved toward public communication. But the vignette includes a possible integrative path: anonymization, structural framing, reduction of identifying details, explicit limits, and reply capacity. The example therefore helps the article avoid a false binary. Public communication may be necessary and still require strict boundary work. Confidentiality may protect participants and still allow public learning if the transition is properly carried.

PMS Core hooks remain visible. Δ distinguishes confidential mediation from public lessons learned. ∇ appears as pressure toward transparency and pressure toward protection. □ is again central: the same mediation findings function differently as internal repair material and public institutional communication. Λ may include a public expectation of accountability after prior failure, but it may also include an expectation that mediation details remain bounded. Α remains weak unless the department has a repeated pattern of either overexposing mediation material or hiding behind confidentiality. Ω is strong as a structural concern: the supervisor, students, department, and public have different exposure, authority, dependency, and reply capacities. Θ appears in the movement from mediation to later public memo. Φ is active because mediation findings are recontextualized into public institutional learning. Χ appears not only as analytic distance but also as a possible case-level practice: the revision process pauses before publication and distinguishes structural learning from public identification. Σ is partially visible as a risk and possible achievement: confidentiality, accountability, learning, and dignity protection are being coordinated. Ψ remains conditional but more plausible than in the first example because the revised process begins to articulate commitments: what will be disclosed, what will remain confidential, how reply will be handled, and how future supervision will change.

EDEN is especially useful here because it distinguishes threshold activation from drift outcome. The threshold is active: confidential material becomes selectable for public use. But the example shows an attempt to prevent the threshold from becoming false comparison. The revised memo avoids turning the mediation into a ranking of persons. It shifts away from “who failed” and toward “which structures failed and what will change.” That is an EDEN-relevant move from value-relation back toward praxis-relation.

Pseudo-symmetry risk is present but contained. One side might say, “The public has a right to know everything.” Another might say, “Mediation confidentiality means nothing can be public.” Both can become parity-to-exit moves if used to avoid carrying unequal burdens. The revised memo attempts a different structure: public responsibility is carried through structural learning, while confidentiality is carried through non-identification and bounded detail. Asymmetry activation becomes more marked and bounded. The department does not simply invoke public accountability to justify exposure; nor does it invoke confidentiality to avoid all public answerability. This is not proof of mature integration, but it demonstrates what EDEN means by non-externalization of unequal burdens.

MIP would treat this example as more operationally promising than the first, but still not as a person-level maturity finding. Awareness is higher because publicness risk is explicitly named. Coherence is more visible because the memo attempts to align public communication with confidentiality constraints. Responsibility is still role-specific: the department has process power; participants have dignity and reply interests; the public has an accountability interest but not unlimited entitlement to mediation detail. Power-to-act is more concrete: revise, anonymize, contextualize, create reply procedure, define confidentiality boundary. The IA-box would still require transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility checks. AHP would likely mark lower transmission risk if the memo itself included clear boundaries, but it would still warn that public-facing text can be overread.

Forbidden claims must remain explicit. This example does not prove that anonymization is always enough. It does not prove that public lessons learned are always legitimate. It does not prove that the department acted maturely or that objectors were immature. It does not decide whether the mediation details were legally protected. It does not diagnose the supervisor or students. It does not rank the dignity of participants against the public interest.

The non-capture note is strong. Information ethics may better decide whether the anonymization is sufficient in a small community. Legal confidentiality rules may prohibit even anonymized disclosure. Restorative practice may ask whether participants consented to public learning. Institutional accountability theory may ask whether structural language is being used to avoid naming actual power abuse. PMS and EDEN can describe the transition and its risks, but they cannot settle those external questions.

The article takeaway is that not every privacy-to-publicness movement is erosion. A bounded public communication may preserve the core repair frame while carrying accountability, but only if the transition names asymmetry, limits exposure, preserves reply, avoids person-ranking, and does not use public learning as a substitute for actual repair.

### Example 3 — The public accusation that reveals a failed private frame

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** After “Non-capture and rival readings,” as an example of why rival readings must remain open.

A professional association receives several confidential complaints about a senior member’s conduct at events. The association tells complainants that the matter will be handled internally. Months pass. No clear outcome is communicated. Some complainants believe the senior member continues to hold visible roles and that others remain at risk. One complainant writes a public statement saying that internal channels have failed. The statement does not publish the full complaint file, but it describes the pattern in general terms and names the association’s non-response. The association replies that the public statement undermines confidentiality and damages the fairness of the internal process.

This vignette is high-risk because it looks like boundary erosion and may also be warranted public accountability. It is useful precisely because the article must not collapse publicness into drift. The complainant moves from a bounded internal complaint process to public discourse. That is a frame transfer. But the public move may be a response to a failed internal frame. If internal confidentiality protects inaction, the public statement may be the first available accountability route. If the public statement overstates, identifies people unnecessarily, or bypasses fair process, it may create exposure and tribunal risk. The example forces the analysis to preserve both possibilities.

The PMS Core hooks are strong. Δ distinguishes internal complaint handling from public accountability statement. ∇ includes pressure for protection, warning, answerability, and perhaps frustration, though no motive is inferred. □ is contested: the association frames the matter as internal process; the complainant frames it as public accountability after internal failure. Λ is highly relevant as a candidate: delayed response, absent outcome, absent protection, absent communication, or absent accountability. But even here, Λ must be evidenced. The mere belief that nothing happened is not the same as proof that nothing happened. Α remains unproven unless the association repeatedly uses confidentiality to avoid accountability. Ω is central: the senior member may have status and institutional protection; complainants may bear vulnerability or retaliation risk; the association controls internal process; public discourse creates new exposure. Θ is visible in months of delay. Φ is active as recontextualization of internal complaint material into public accountability language. Χ is difficult but essential: the analysis must not become either a public accusation or an institutional defense. Σ is weak because internal process and public accountability are not integrated. Ψ is conditional: the association’s process duties, the complainant’s public-interest obligations, and the senior member’s reply rights would need specification.

EDEN’s strongest contribution here is the distinction between boundary transfer and local switch drift without full PFO. The complainant may activate asymmetry by invoking risk, vulnerability, and institutional failure to justify publicness. The association may activate asymmetry by invoking confidentiality, fairness, and process integrity to demand silence. Either activation may be valid if marked, bounded, and re-bound into responsibility. Either may become drift if it becomes an unmarked lever: publicness as exposure without safeguards, or confidentiality as closure without accountability.

Pseudo-symmetry risk is also sharp. The association may say all complaints are handled equally through confidential procedure, while real asymmetry leaves complainants without voice and the senior member with continued public standing. The public statement may say public accountability applies equally, while real asymmetry exposes some parties to reputational or procedural costs they cannot answer. EDEN does not decide which side is right. It shows that formal parity can hide unequal burden distribution.

PFO is still not applicable unless a broader regime is shown. The example may show local switch-risk, but not a regime of permanentized exception, Ω-taboo governance, or protocolized comparison. Devaluation and humiliation remain risks: public accusation may lower standing-in-practice before process is complete; internal confidentiality may lower complainants’ standing by treating public concern as improper. But neither humiliation nor devaluation is automatically found.

MIP would emphasize that responsibility cannot be assigned without role-specific knowledge and power. What did the association know? What could it disclose? What legal constraints applied? What risks did complainants face? What alternatives existed? Was there a serious internal integration attempt? Did the public statement preserve proportionality, context, and right of reply? Did the association preserve safety and accountability, or only procedure? Without these answers, MIP can identify IA-risk but cannot assign M. It can say that publicness and confidentiality both create burden distributions requiring transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. AHP would warn that any article rendering of this example could easily become a covert verdict if it uses vivid details, identifies roles too closely, or lets public failure language outrun evidence.

Forbidden claims are especially important. The example does not prove that the senior member did anything. It does not prove that the association concealed misconduct. It does not prove that the complainant acted responsibly or irresponsibly. It does not determine whether public disclosure was protected, defamatory, necessary, or excessive. It does not diagnose institutional bad faith. It does not label resistance as immaturity. It does not convert delay into guilt.

The non-capture note is central. A legal analysis may dominate this scene. Confidentiality obligations, defamation risk, protected disclosure, duty of care, and process fairness may determine what can be said. A trauma-informed or safety-oriented framework may better capture why internal silence can be harmful. A procedural justice framework may better specify reply rights and evidentiary thresholds. A public-interest framework may better determine whether naming the association’s non-response is warranted. PMS, EDEN, and MIP provide structural discipline, but they must remain open to these grammars.

The article takeaway is that boundary erosion cannot be identified merely by the fact that confidential material becomes public. Sometimes publicness may reveal that the private frame had already failed. The mature structural question is not “privacy or publicness?” but whether the transition carries the unequal burdens of protection, accountability, process, exposure, reply, and reversibility without turning either confidentiality or public accountability into an authority that escapes its own costs.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The YAMLs preserve substantial non-capture. This is not an apology for weak analysis; it is part of the analysis. A model that cannot name what it may not capture becomes unsafe in precisely the kinds of cases that most demand restraint.

An **information ethics** or **contextual integrity** reading may be stronger than PMS or EDEN if the decisive issue is secondary use of information. This rival reading asks whether material created for one audience and purpose may be transferred to another audience and purpose. It preserves consent, contextual norms, audience control, privacy expectations, and information-flow integrity. PMS captures frame transfer structurally, but information ethics may better specify when secondary use violates contextual norms.

A **procedural justice** reading may be stronger if the issue is due process, right of reply, proportionality, transparency, and review. PMS marks Χ, Σ, and Dignity-in-Practice, but procedural justice may better define what fair public accountability requires. It may also distinguish public accountability from tribunalization more sharply than PMS alone.

A **whistleblowing or public-interest disclosure** reading may supersede the boundary-erosion reading if internal repair failed, harm continued, or public disclosure was necessary. This rival preserves the possibility that publicness is not drift but responsibility. PMS and EDEN must not absorb whistleblowing as recontextualization failure or switch drift. The YAMLs explicitly preserve this.

A **legal-formal** reading may be decisive where confidentiality, privilege, defamation, retaliation, protected disclosure, duty to warn, evidentiary admissibility, or professional secrecy applies. PMS, EDEN, and MIP cannot decide these. Legal categories may not merely add detail; they may change the case type.

A **restorative-practice** reading may preserve what PMS only structurally indicates: whether the internal frame genuinely aimed at repair, whether the parties had voice, whether accountability was relationally carried, and whether publicness bypassed or protected restoration. PMS can mark repair/accountability tension, but restorative practice may better assess repair quality.

An **institutional-power** reading may focus on whether confidentiality stabilizes asymmetry by preventing external accountability. This rival is important because boundary-erosion language can protect institutions too easily. PMS and EDEN both note that privacy may conceal power. Institutional analysis may be more concrete about how.

A **public-sphere** reading may preserve democratic accountability. Public discourse is not automatically tribunal discourse. It may be deliberation, contestation, watchdog action, journalism, collective learning, or public reason. PMS can mark publicness as frame transfer; public-sphere theory may better distinguish deliberation from spectacle.

A **phenomenological** reading may preserve lived exposure, shame, trust, betrayal, voice, and vulnerability in ways PMS intentionally does not claim. PMS avoids phenomenological and psychological claims; that restraint is correct, but it means phenomenological accounts may capture dimensions PMS brackets.

A **language-practice** reading may focus on how phrases such as “confidential,” “accountable,” “public interest,” “internal resolution,” “tribunal,” “repair,” or “safety” function in actual discourse. PMS names frames; language-practice analysis may better show how those frames are enacted in speech.

An **anti-capture** reading may refuse both privacy and publicness frames as imposed. It may say that the case cannot be adequately formalized without reproducing the very tribunal or containment structure it describes. PMS must leave this possibility open. Non-fit is not automatically drift. Resistance to the model is not immaturity.

These rival readings are not defects in PMS, EDEN, or MIP. They are part of the case’s methodological integrity. PMS and add-ons are useful only if they remain open to being outperformed by a rival grammar where the rival preserves distinctions that the model would otherwise smooth over. In this case, that openness is not peripheral. It is one of the main results.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading weakens if the material was never confidential, never bounded, never part of an internal repair frame, or always intended for public accountability. If there is no source frame and target frame, Φ loses its centrality. If there is no temporal movement, Θ weakens. If there is no exposure, obligation, capacity, leverage, or reply asymmetry, Ω weakens. If there is no expected repair, confidentiality, response, accountability, proportionality, or reply, Λ should be removed. If there is no recurrence, Α remains inactive.

The reading is falsified or strongly weakened if the public use was consented to, legally required, anonymized enough to preserve bounded repair, part of the original accountability frame, or necessary to prevent ongoing harm after internal failure. These conditions do not automatically make the public move mature, but they defeat a simple boundary-erosion reading.

The PMS reading becomes under-discriminating if it cannot distinguish harmful exposure from warranted disclosure, protective confidentiality from institutional concealment, public accountability from tribunalization, or repair from suppression. It becomes over-assimilative if every rupture, refusal, public statement, missing evidence, disagreement, or rival framing is redescribed as Φ drift, Λ absence, Σ failure, or maturity failure.

The EDEN reading weakens if comparison is not dominant, if parity language is absent, if asymmetry activation is clearly marked and bounded, if reciprocity is carried, if publicness remains praxis-coordinative, if confidentiality remains genuinely restorative, or if rival frameworks preserve decisive distinctions better. EDEN becomes overextended if local switch-risk is inflated into PFO, if pseudo-symmetry is treated as a finding without parity surface and real Ω evidence, if devaluation or humiliation is asserted without standing-loss dynamics, or if NRK is treated as Action_E.

The MIP reading remains under-specified until role-specific knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and serious integration attempts are known. A global M-score is invalid. A person-level A-score is invalid. D-profile use remains qualitative only. The MIP reading would change if a concrete scene showed robust early integration, clear role-specific self-binding, transparent escalation criteria, public-interest justification, right-of-reply safeguards, or legitimate restorative confidentiality.

The AHP overlay identifies artefact weaknesses rather than case weaknesses. It would recommend hardening if the analysis is to be reused: add an early transmission banner; clarify that the A-band is configuration-only; add a D-profile boundary line; create non-prescriptive publicness thresholds; separate the evidence ladder visually; align dignity notation; and link hardening actions to attack points.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. Stronger analysis would require a scene packet with roles, dates, material type, confidentiality basis, consent, legal constraints, repair history, audience scale, publicness medium, reversibility, response channels, harm context, institutional process, and recurrence evidence. It would also require external evaluation if used in legal, organizational, public, or computational settings. Nothing in the completed YAMLs supplies that evidence.

Operationalization would also require differentiating what kind of publicness is involved. A private message forwarded to two people is not the same as a public statement, a media article, a searchable archive, a court filing, a whistleblower report, an anonymized lessons-learned memo, or a viral social-media post. Each form changes audience, reversibility, reply capacity, and dignity risk differently. Without this differentiation, publicness remains too broad.

Similarly, operationalization would require differentiating confidentiality. A therapeutic confidence, a restorative mediation agreement, an HR process, a legal privilege, a professional complaint channel, an informal repair conversation, and a private apology are not equivalent. Each frame carries different expectations and constraints. PMS can mark □, but real use requires specifying the frame.

The case therefore remains a structured pre-analysis even in its final article form. Its value lies in the precision of what it withholds as much as in what it names.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. No person, group, institution, public, or role-position is diagnosed. No pathology, trauma structure, personality trait, maturity deficit, narcissism, cowardice, moral weakness, or psychological motive is inferred.

It must not be used for person ranking. PMS operators do not rank persons. EDEN labels are not person labels. MIP A-bands and M-scores are not actor scores here. Dignity-in-Practice is not a dignity score. No one is classified as mature, immature, dignified, undignified, accountable, unaccountable, or shameful.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusion. It does not decide breach of confidentiality, privilege, defamation, protected disclosure, retaliation, duty to warn, liability, evidence, or due process. Any such use would require independent legal analysis.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. It does not prove that publicness reveals truth, that privacy conceals truth, that dignity has a structural essence, or that PMS captures personhood.

It must not be used as empirical proof. It does not prove that disclosure happened, harm happened, repair failed, public shaming occurred, or asymmetry was exploited. It maps risks from a conceptual case-family.

It must not be used as implementation validation. It is not a policy, moderation rule, HR process, public communication protocol, or automated decision system. MIP’s possible steps are viability prompts, not instructions.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. The fact that PMS can structure the case does not prove PMS is complete, uniquely ordered, empirically valid, or superior to rival grammars.

It must not treat add-ons as operators. EDEN constructs such as PS, NRK, PFO, AS, PTE, RTA, devaluation, and humiliation are overlay handles. They do not become PMS operators. MIP and AHP are application layers, not PMS revisions.

It must not treat drift as verdict. Drift-risk is not wrongdoing. Boundary erosion is not automatically blame. Non-event is not automatically failure. Low Σ is not automatically immaturity. Weak Ψ is not irresponsibility unless commitments are specified.

It must not create closure by authority. The polished structure of YAML or article prose must not make the analysis seem more decisive than the evidence allows. The AHP overlay specifically warns that polished artefacts may be mistaken for public-ready decision documents. This article therefore remains a chapter-ready structural rendering, not a public adjudication.

It must not treat resistance, refusal, rupture, non-fit, disagreement, or absence as drift by default. A refusal to stay private may be principled. A refusal to go public may be principled. A missing response may reflect legal constraint, process protection, fear, incapacity, or incomplete evidence. A rival framing may be more accurate. PMS application fails if it absorbs all such non-fit as incomplete integration.

Finally, it must not use dignity language as a weapon. Dignity-in-Practice exists here to prevent humiliation and person-ranking, not to produce a higher-status rhetoric of concern. A public frame can misuse dignity by claiming that exposure is necessary for recognition while humiliating the exposed. A private frame can misuse dignity by claiming protection while silencing accountability. The misuse boundary applies to both.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is that “Boundary erosion (Privacy → Publicness)” is best understood as a high-risk structural frame-transfer case, not as a resolved finding of wrongdoing, accountability, betrayal, or justified disclosure. PMS Core identifies the central structure: Φ-driven recontextualization from a bounded repair or confidential feedback frame into public accountability or tribunal-like publicness, under Δ, □, Ω, and Θ, with Λ and Α conditional, Σ at risk, Χ required, and Ψ conditional. That formula is strong enough to organize the case, but not strong enough to decide it. The analysis therefore remains reversible.

PMS-EDEN adds a second layer of visibility. It shows that the case is not only about recontextualization but also about threshold, comparison, pseudo-symmetry, switch-risk, reciprocity, and cost externalization. Once bounded material becomes selectable as public material, unequal burdens can be shifted. Publicness can become accountability or exposure. Confidentiality can become repair or concealment. EDEN’s most appropriate reading is local threshold and switch-risk without full PFO. PFO, permanentized exception, NRK as finding, and humiliation as finding remain unsupported. EDEN sharpens the publicness geometry without becoming PMS Base.

MIP adds a maturity-in-practice discipline. It prevents the structural reading from becoming a score. Awareness is conceptually present, but not actor-proven. Coherence is fragile. Responsibility is role-specific and conditional. Power-to-act is unknown. IA-risk is visible because publicness and confidentiality both distribute burdens asymmetrically, but IA-risk is not a verdict. Dignity is active only as a qualitative guardrail. No person is scored. No maturity level is assigned. No dignity profile is applied to identifiable persons.

AHP adds a final artefact-level caution. The analysis is precise, but its very precision creates risk. A polished case article or YAML can be mistaken for a decision document. A provisional A-band can be extracted as a score. A D-profile can be overread. Publicness thresholds can remain too qualitative. The evidence chain can appear stronger than it is if prior PMS and EDEN structures are not visibly separated from primary case material. AHP therefore hardens the analysis without rescoring it.

The three examples contribute by showing how the same structural architecture behaves under different conditions. The internal feedback note that becomes public evidence demonstrates the core frame-transfer risk: the same material shifts from repair to public accountability, but the crossing alone does not decide legitimacy. The anonymized lessons-learned memo shows that publicness is not automatically boundary erosion; bounded public communication may carry accountability while preserving repair, if asymmetry, context, reply, and non-identification are handled. The public accusation after a failed private process shows why rival readings remain indispensable: sometimes publicness may reveal that the private frame had already failed. The examples are not evidence. They are controlled demonstrations of the analytical grammar.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is claimed. The article does not claim empirical facts, legal validity, clinical diagnosis, metaphysical truth, implementation readiness, or PMS validation. It does not decide whether confidentiality or publicness is correct. It does not decide whether a real actor should disclose, remain silent, apologize, repair, litigate, publish, delete, or sanction. It does not assign maturity, dignity, responsibility, or blame to persons. It does not treat drift-risk as drift-finding.

The boundary discipline is therefore the article’s central achievement. PMS coherence is not proof. EDEN visibility is not verdict. MIP maturity language is not person scoring. AHP precision is not decision authority. Non-capture remains possible. Rival readings remain live. Legal, procedural, information-ethical, restorative, institutional, public-sphere, phenomenological, and anti-capture grammars may preserve distinctions that PMS and its overlays only partially render.

The final methodological takeaway is that boundary erosion cannot be analysed responsibly by asking only whether material became public. The decisive question is how the transition carries its burdens. Does confidentiality carry repair and accountability, or does it externalize silence and protect power? Does publicness carry context, proportionality, reply, and reversibility, or does it externalize exposure and produce standing loss? Does the frame transfer become integrated praxis, or does it become comparison, switch leverage, or simulated closure? The case remains open, but not vague. It is open because the model has preserved the conditions under which a stronger claim would have to be earned.
