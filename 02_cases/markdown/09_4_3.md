### Why this case matters

Trust is one of the most closure-demanding relational concepts in philosophical, ethical, institutional, and everyday language. It appears simple because ordinary discourse treats it as immediately intelligible: one trusts or does not trust; a promise is kept or broken; betrayal occurs or does not occur; repair is possible or impossible. Yet this apparent simplicity hides a dense structural field. Trust is never only a mental state, never only a prediction, never only an obligation, never only a feeling, and never only a social expectation. It is a relational regime in which reliance, vulnerability, expectation, confidence, doubt, withdrawal, repair, and betrayal are made meaningful inside frames.

The closure-demand is strong because trust constantly asks to be settled. Is this relation still trustworthy? Was the absence a betrayal? Is doubt appropriate, excessive, defensive, or wise? Is withdrawal a failure of trust or a preservation of dignity? Does repair require renewed reliance, or can repair end in refusal? Does an institution that fails to respond merely underperform, or does it betray the relation it claims to hold? Each of these questions presses toward closure. They ask not only what happened, but what the relation now is.

That is why this case belongs in AXIOM. AXIOM is not interested in trust as a doctrine to be solved by metaphysics, psychology, moral theory, legal classification, or empirical social science. It maps closure-demands: where a concept asks to become final, where the frame determines what can count as resolution, where residue remains, and where add-ons are justified because a case contains domain-specific drift risks. Trust is a particularly useful case because it sits at the crossing point between relational exposure and interpretive closure. It can easily become a verdict machine: one party is trustworthy or untrustworthy, mature or immature, loyal or disloyal, safe or unsafe, innocent or guilty. The PMS and EDEN analyses resist that conversion.

The structural difficulty of trust is that it carries both expectation and non-control. To trust is not simply to predict; prediction can be detached, external, and non-vulnerable. Trust includes exposure. A relying role becomes open to another role’s action, omission, delay, interpretation, institution, or repair capacity. This exposure creates asymmetry even when the parties are formally equal. One party may carry more cost if the expected act does not occur. One institution may possess more authority to define whether a response was sufficient. One claim may appear neutral while distributing vulnerability unevenly. Trust therefore cannot be analyzed without Ω, asymmetry.

Trust is also temporal. Confidence accumulates, doubt grows, repair takes time, betrayal is remembered, silence becomes meaningful only because a response was expected within a temporal window. A single event can matter, but trust itself is rarely a single event. It is a trajectory of expectation and reliance. For that reason, Θ is central. The analysis must ask how long expectation persists, when absence becomes meaningful, whether repair remains open, and when withdrawal is no longer premature but structurally appropriate.

Trust also contains a pronounced Λ dimension. Much of trust is governed by what does not happen: the reply that never comes, the protection that fails to appear, the explanation withheld, the expected fidelity not enacted, the repair not attempted, the institutional response delayed beyond relevance. Yet absence cannot be automatically converted into betrayal. Some silence may be boundary. Some non-response may be incapacity. Some withdrawal may be dignified refusal. Some rupture may mark a rival frame rather than failed integration. The trust case therefore requires a strong non-capture discipline.

This case is not solved metaphysically because PMS does not define the essence of trust. It does not say what trust is “in itself.” It reconstructs what trust does structurally: how it frames expectation, organizes exposure, generates meaningful non-events, stabilizes or destabilizes patterns, and invites repair or withdrawal. It is not solved empirically because no data about actual trust relations, institutions, individuals, or populations are supplied. It is not solved clinically or diagnostically because doubt, withdrawal, hesitation, confidence, and betrayal-language are not symptoms. It is not solved legally because legal breach requires jurisdictional standards, evidence, duties, thresholds, and procedures that the case does not provide. It is not solved morally because PMS coherence is not moral verdict. It is not solved by EDEN because EDEN adds overlay visibility, not authority.

The case matters precisely because trust tempts analysis to overreach. It invites smooth stories about relational maturity, repair, reciprocity, betrayal, and restoration. The completed YAML analyses deliberately refuse such smoothness. They show where trust becomes structurally legible, but also where the reading must stop.

It gives a philosophical field: trust as a relational condition shaped by reliance, vulnerability, expectation, confidence, doubt, withdrawal, repair, and betrayal. Therefore, the analysis may structurally project the PMS operator grammar onto that field, but it may not adjudicate an event.

The claim status is consequently limited. The PMS Core pass claims only that trust can be modeled as a relational praxis-configuration in which distinctions, frames, non-events, asymmetries, temporal trajectories, recontextualizations, distance, possible integration, and possible self-binding become relevant. The claim is not that every instance of trust is exhaustively captured by PMS. Nor is it that PMS provides the correct philosophical theory of trust. It is a bounded structural reading.

The concrete-scene status is explicitly negative: the case is not a concrete scene. This matters for every subsequent layer. Because there is no concrete scene, operator activation remains case-type-bound rather than adjudicative. The analysis can say that Ω is structurally central to trust because reliance involves vulnerability and exposure. It cannot say who is asymmetrically responsible in a given relationship. It can say that Λ is central because expected events may fail to occur. It cannot say that a particular silence is betrayal. It can say that Φ may be involved in repair or reinterpretation. It cannot say that recontextualization has occurred or should occur in a particular case.

The empirical boundary is strict. No empirical finding is made about how trust functions psychologically, sociologically, institutionally, politically, or culturally. The analysis does not claim that people generally trust in a certain way, that institutions statistically produce certain trust failures, or that betrayal follows a measurable pattern. Empirical operationalization would require observable indicators, scene evidence, comparative cases, timelines, role mapping, and external validation.

The metaphysical boundary is equally strict. The case does not define the essence of trust, the ontology of relation, the nature of personhood, the metaphysical status of promise, or the ultimate ground of fidelity. Trust is not treated as an ontological substance. It is treated as a structural relation under frames. This is not a reduction of trust to mechanism; it is a scoped formalization of the closure-demand generated by trust-language.

The diagnostic and clinical boundaries are non-negotiable. The analysis does not infer attachment style, trauma, pathology, personality, emotional maturity, avoidance, dependency, narcissism, paranoia, or any other clinical or psychological classification. Doubt is not a symptom. Withdrawal is not a disorder. Refusal is not immaturity. Confidence is not health. Betrayal-language is not clinical evidence. PMS and EDEN are structural grammars here, not diagnostic instruments.

The legal and forensic boundary is also explicit. Betrayal appears as case material and structural possibility, not as a legal finding. Legal breach, fiduciary duty, negligence, fraud, evidentiary sufficiency, liability, culpability, and remedy all require external legal standards. PMS can help map the frame and residues of a trust claim; it cannot adjudicate breach.

The implementation boundary prevents the YAML representation from becoming an operational system. The completed YAMLs are structured artefacts. They are machine-readable in form, but they do not implement trust, agency, governance, therapy, decision-making, AI safety, institutional policy, or repair architecture. The PMS Base validation boundary is central. This case does not validate PMS.yaml. PMS coherence is insufficient by itself. A smooth PMS derivation can itself become a risk if it under-discriminates the phenomenon.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. The YAML analysis lists what would be needed: observable distinctions among trust, reliance, doubt, refusal, repair, and betrayal; indicators of directional movement; explicit trust frames; specified expected occurrences; evidence of recurrence; mapped asymmetries; timelines; evidence of actual recontextualization; signs of reflective distance; proof of practical integration; and evidence of promise, commitment, role ownership, or self-binding. None of those are supplied by the abstract case. The result is therefore valid as a conceptual projection with limits, not as an applied finding.

### Scene and frame structure

The conceptual scene is a relational regime of trust. The frame □ is not one fixed social setting but a structural field in which persons, roles, claims, or institutions become related through reliance and expectation. Inside the frame are the elements named by the case material: reliance, vulnerability, expectation, confidence, doubt, withdrawal, repair, and betrayal. These are not treated as inner psychological states, but as relational positions or movements that become meaningful within a frame.

The scene boundary is therefore conceptual rather than event-specific. It includes relational configurations where one role relies upon another, where expectation gives absence meaning, where vulnerability distributes exposure, where repair or betrayal can become legible, and where trust may stabilize, weaken, or collapse across time. The frame can later be instantiated interpersonally, institutionally, testimonial, fiduciary, professional, political, or communicative, but the present case does not choose one of those as the concrete scene.

What is outside the frame is equally important. Outside the frame are metaphysical claims about trust as essence, clinical claims about trust capacity, legal conclusions about breach, empirical findings about trust behavior, moral rankings of parties, and implementation claims about how trust should be repaired. Also outside the frame are totalizing readings that treat every rupture as failure, every refusal as immaturity, every silence as betrayal, or every repair as mature reconciliation.

The protected constraints are Χ, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. Χ requires reflective distance. The analysis must not rush from trust-language to verdict. Reversibility requires that classifications remain revisable, especially because the case is abstract. Dignity-in-Practice requires that vulnerability and asymmetry be handled without humiliation, ranking, shaming, or person-level degradation.

The roles are abstract. There is a relying role or trust-giver, exposed through dependence, expectation, or vulnerability. There is a relied-upon role or trust-receiver, positioned as capable of acting, omitting, clarifying, repairing, or refusing. There may be an institutional role that stabilizes or mediates expectations. There is also the analyst role, which holds interpretive power over the case representation and therefore must avoid over-naming. None of these roles is a person-label. They are structural positions.

Temporality Θ is built into the frame. Trust is not merely a present attitude. It persists through expectation, memory, repeated responsiveness, accumulated confidence, delayed repair, or remembered betrayal. The temporal horizon may be short, as in a promised reply, or long, as in institutional trust or durable role commitment. Because no concrete timeline is supplied, the analysis cannot determine when absence becomes rupture, when repair closes, or when trust has been restored or ended. It can only state that these questions require temporal calibration.

The reversibility window remains open. Since the case is abstract, all readings remain revisable. A later concrete scene might activate operators differently. It might show no recurrence, no asymmetry, no breach, no possibility of repair, or no valid self-binding. It might show that what appeared as trust was mere prediction, compliance, dependence, fear, loyalty, or procedural reliance. It might also show that withdrawal is not breakdown but boundary. The frame is therefore open enough to map trust, but bounded enough to prevent overclaim.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are central but not adjudicated. Trust usually involves unequal exposure: the one who relies may bear costs that the relied-upon role does not bear. But asymmetry is not automatically injustice, guilt, legal duty, or moral superiority. It is a structural imbalance in vulnerability, capacity, information, authority, dependence, or obligation. In a concrete case, the direction of asymmetry would need to be mapped carefully. In the current case, Ω is active as a structural condition of trust, but not as a finding against any role.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading treats trust as a relational praxis-configuration. Its primary formula in the completed YAML is:

Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ

This formula models trust as temporally extended, asymmetrically exposed, pattern-stabilized, expectation-sensitive praxis within a relational frame. It deliberately does not include Σ or Ψ in the primary formula, because integration and self-binding are not established by the abstract case. Higher operators remain available for repair, withdrawal, recontextualization, integration, or promise-based commitment only where concrete material warrants them.

**Δ — Difference.**
Trust first becomes legible through difference. The case distinguishes trusting from not trusting, reliance from non-reliance, confidence from doubt, repair from non-repair, expected occurrence from meaningful absence, and betrayal from ordinary failure. Without Δ, trust would dissolve into an undifferentiated relational mood. Yet Δ must not be overpushed. Distinction alone does not prove that a trust relation exists. A difference between confidence and doubt might belong to prediction, risk assessment, contractual compliance, or fear. The calibration point is therefore to name which distinctions are trust-specific and which belong to adjacent phenomena. If trust cannot be distinguished from mere prediction, dependency, credibility, loyalty, institutional compliance, or faith, the PMS reading loses discriminative force.

**∇ — Impulse.**
Trust contains directional tension. A relation may move toward reliance, renewed confidence, repair, or restored expectation. It may also move away through doubt, withdrawal, suspension, or refusal. ∇ captures this directedness without inferring inner desire, motive, pathology, or emotion. The case-specific role of ∇ is to mark the movement generated by trust-differences: toward or away, openness or suspension, repair or closure. The dependency risk is psychologization. If ∇ is treated as inner urge or trait, the analysis leaves PMS discipline. If no directional movement is present, ∇ becomes weak or under-specified.

**□ — Frame.**
The frame is decisive. Trust does not operate outside context. The same act can be trustworthy in one frame, insufficient in another, irrelevant in a third. Interpersonal trust, institutional trust, testimonial trust, procedural trust, role-based trust, and fiduciary trust each define different expectations and thresholds. In this case, □ is active and major because trust only becomes analyzable once its relational regime is framed. The calibration point is frame specificity: the analysis must not silently collapse interpersonal vulnerability into institutional legitimacy, testimonial credibility into moral fidelity, or procedural reliability into intimate trust. Overclaim occurs when the word “trust” is treated as if it carried the same meaning across all frames.

**Λ — Non-Event.**
Λ is one of the strongest operators in this case. Trust is often shaped by what does not occur: the message not answered, the repair not attempted, the protection not provided, the promise not enacted, the institution not responding, the fidelity not preserved. These absences matter only within a frame. Silence is not automatically betrayal; it becomes structurally meaningful when a response was expected. Λ therefore names structured absence, not mere lack. The calibration issue is severe: the expected occurrence must be specified. What exactly was expected—response, recognition, action, protection, explanation, repair, fidelity, procedural review? Without that specification, absence cannot bear the analytic weight assigned to it. Overclaim occurs when any non-response, delay, refusal, or non-fit is coded as betrayal, failed integration, or maturity failure. Non-event is not verdict.

**Α — Attractor.**
Trust stabilizes through repetition. Repeated reliability builds confidence. Repeated absence builds doubt. Repeated repair can stabilize resilience; repeated non-repair can stabilize withdrawal. Institutions produce routines that become trust-attractors. However, in the abstract case, Α is moderate rather than fully established. The material names relational regimes and patterns, but it does not supply recurrence evidence. The operator is structurally relevant because trust often depends on pattern, but it cannot be treated as proven. The dependency risk is converting a single event into a stable trust profile. A concrete case would need repeated actions, omissions, responses, repair cycles, or routines before Α could carry strong analytic weight.

**Ω — Asymmetry.**
Ω is major. Trust involves vulnerability, exposure, dependence, capacity differences, authority gradients, informational imbalance, role obligation, or institutional power. A relying role can become exposed to another’s action or omission. A trusted institution may define whether its own failure counts as failure. A role may carry obligation without equal capacity. Yet asymmetry is not blame. It does not automatically identify victimhood, guilt, moral superiority, legal liability, or diagnosis. The calibration task is to specify the kind and direction of asymmetry: who is exposed, who can act, who can define the frame, who bears repair cost, who can withdraw, who can contest? Without that specification, Ω remains general. Overclaim occurs when vulnerability is moralized or when responsibility is inferred from exposure alone.

**Θ — Temporality.**
Θ is major because trust unfolds across time. Reliability accumulates, confidence persists, doubt grows, betrayal is remembered, repair takes time, and institutions carry histories. Trust is not only whether something is currently believed; it is whether expectation can persist through delay, absence, rupture, and possible restoration. The calibration issue is temporal grain. A first encounter, repeated relationship, long institutional history, or post-betrayal repair arc each uses Θ differently. Overclaim occurs when temporality is invoked vaguely, without specifying horizon, delay, persistence, memory, or revision window. If no temporal extension is required, Θ would be overextended. In this case, because the material explicitly includes expectation, repair, withdrawal, and betrayal, Θ remains strongly relevant.

**Φ — Recontextualization.**
Φ is active as a possible trust operation. Repair often involves recontextualization: a failure becomes misunderstanding, a betrayal becomes confessed rupture, suspicion becomes clarified expectation, a personal claim becomes institutional role issue, or a damaged relation becomes a boundary relation rather than a restored one. Yet Φ is dangerous if used too smoothly. Recontextualization can clarify, but it can also absorb rupture and erase non-capture. The calibration requirement is to name the source frame and target frame. What is being recontextualized into what? Promise into misunderstanding? Betrayal into repair? Trust into procedure? Vulnerability into role obligation? Overclaim occurs when every disagreement, refusal, rupture, or absence is automatically treated as an opportunity for development or repair. Some ruptures should remain ruptures.

**Χ — Distance.**
Χ is central both inside the case and in the analysis of the case. In trust, distance appears as doubt, hesitation, suspension, withdrawal, refusal to rush judgment, or pause before renewed reliance. Analytically, Χ prevents the case from becoming a verdict. It blocks the slide from betrayal-language into blame, from repair-language into obligation, from refusal into immaturity, and from trust into moral rank. Calibration requires distinguishing reflective distance from avoidance, denial, punishment, empty neutrality, or coerced withdrawal. Distance is not always virtuous, but without it the analysis becomes coercive. Overclaim occurs if Χ is suspended to force closure: “this was betrayal,” “repair is required,” “withdrawal is immature,” “trust has failed,” or “integration must occur.”

**Σ — Integration.**
Σ is weak and conditional. Trust may involve integration where confidence and doubt, reliance and vulnerability, memory and repair, role and person, or institution and lived exposure are coordinated. But the abstract case does not show actual coordination in practice. It only names possible trust dynamics. Therefore, PMS coherence must not be mistaken for Σ. The analysis can be coherent while the relation remains unintegrated, under-specified, or outside capture. The calibration point is whether integration discriminates trust from confidence, loyalty, credibility, repair, refusal, and compliance rather than smoothing them into one conceptual story. Overclaim occurs when formal PMS coherence is treated as proof of adequacy.

**Ψ — Self-Binding.**
Ψ is weak, conditional, and mostly analytic in this case. It becomes relevant where trust involves promise, role ownership, accountability, institutional commitment, durable responsibility, or self-binding to a repair or refusal stance. But the abstract material does not establish a promise, actor, institution, commitment, or SELF_FIXPOINT. Ψ also applies to the analysis itself insofar as the analyst remains bound to PMS application discipline: no diagnosis, no person ranking, no PMS Base validation, no drift-as-verdict. The calibration issue is whether Σ, Θ, and Χ are genuinely present. Without them, Ψ cannot be applied cheaply. Overclaim occurs when trust-language alone is treated as evidence of commitment, responsibility, or stable self-relation.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The completed PMS YAML is especially careful about dependency discipline. Later operators cannot be used cheaply. Σ and Ψ are structurally expensive because they presuppose prior operators and because they easily create closure. In trust, that matters because the desire for closure is strong. A relation feels unresolved; betrayal-language asks to settle responsibility; repair-language asks to restore order; withdrawal asks to be interpreted as either failure or boundary. PMS prevents those pressures from becoming authority.

The first dependency risk is jumping from Δ and ∇ directly to conclusion. Because a difference appears and a directional pressure follows, one may want to say that trust has either succeeded or failed. PMS blocks that shortcut. The frame □ must first be specified. A delay in one frame may be betrayal; in another it may be normal; in another it may be illegible. Without the frame, closure is premature.

The second dependency risk concerns Λ. Meaningful absence cannot be used without expectation. A non-event requires a framed expected event. The analysis therefore cannot infer betrayal from silence, refusal, delay, or absence alone. It must ask what was expected, by whom, under which frame, with what temporal horizon, and with what contestability. If those elements are missing, Λ remains under-specified.

The third risk concerns Ω and Θ. Asymmetry and temporality can easily be inflated. Trust is indeed often vulnerable and temporal, but a concrete reading must show the direction of exposure and the relevant time arc. Otherwise Ω becomes moral atmosphere and Θ becomes narrative smoothing. PMS requires specifying who bears cost, who can act, who can contest, who waits, who remembers, and when repair or refusal remains possible.

The fourth risk concerns Φ. Recontextualization is not automatically repair. A new frame can reveal, clarify, or protect. It can also launder harm, erase refusal, or make rupture appear developmentally productive when it is not. PMS requires Χ before Σ: reflective distance must protect against premature harmonization.

The fifth and most important risk concerns Σ and Ψ. Trust tempts integration. We want to say what the relation means, who is responsible, whether repair is possible, and whether renewed reliance is appropriate. But the completed YAML keeps Σ weak and Ψ conditional because no concrete scene supplies integrated action or self-binding. A promise, role-duty, institutional obligation, or repair commitment would change this; absent such evidence, the analysis must stop before binding.

Coherence is not proof. A PMS reading can be elegant and still under-discriminating. In fact, this case has high coherence risk precisely because trust maps smoothly onto many PMS operators. Difference, frame, expectation, absence, asymmetry, time, repair, distance, and possible integration all fit. That fit is analytically useful, but also dangerous. If the model can explain everything too smoothly, it may fail to distinguish trust from dependence, prediction, credibility, loyalty, institutional compliance, fear, faith, or social habit.

Non-capture is not failure. PMS is allowed to say that some aspects of trust remain outside its current grammar. Lived vulnerability, affective warmth, grief, shame, cultural meaning, theological trust, legal standards, empirical trust dynamics, and principled refusal may not be fully captured. The correct response is not to force them into drift or incomplete integration. Non-capture is a protected outcome.

PMS must therefore not become a closure machine. It maps closure-demands; it does not satisfy them by authority. It shows where trust becomes structurally legible and where the analysis must remain open. Invalid shortcuts include treating absence as betrayal without expectation, asymmetry as guilt without role evidence, recontextualization as repair without source and target frame, coherence as integration, responsibility-language as Ψ, or refusal as maturity failure.

### Derived structures

The derived structures are not primitive. They are formula-derived PMS constructs and must remain so. The completed YAML treats them with careful status distinctions: Awareness_A and Coherence_C are active as structural projections; Responsibility_R is weak and conditional; Action_E is under-specified; Dignity_in_Practice_D is an application constraint; SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only and not established.

**Awareness_A.**
Awareness_A is derived from Θ, □, and Δ: sustained, framed differentiation across time. In this case it is active. Trust requires differentiating reliance from non-reliance, confidence from doubt, repair from non-repair, expected occurrence from non-event, betrayal from ordinary failure, and role from person. These distinctions must persist across time inside a frame. Awareness_A is therefore not consciousness, introspection, or phenomenological awareness. It is a structural capacity of the analysis to hold relevant distinctions over time. It becomes overclaimed if treated as inner experience or if its structural clarity is confused with lived understanding.

**Coherence_C.**
Coherence_C is derived from Θ, Λ, □, and ∇: temporally stabilized structuring of impulse and expectation within a frame. It is active. Trust becomes coherent when directional reliance or withdrawal is framed by expectation, meaningful absence, and temporal persistence. Confidence, doubt, repair, and betrayal are not isolated states; they become coherent as trajectories. Yet Coherence_C does not equal truth, moral adequacy, successful repair, or justified trust. It only shows that the trust field can be structurally organized. The YAML explicitly warns that PMS coherence is insufficient by itself.

**Responsibility_R.**
Responsibility_R is derived from Ψ, Φ, Θ, and Ω: self-binding orientation toward asymmetry extended across time and recontextualization. In this case it is weak. Responsibility becomes active only where asymmetry, temporal obligation, recontextualization, and self-binding commitment are concretely shown. A promise, fiduciary relation, institutional duty, or owned accountability could activate it. But the abstract case does not assign responsibility. It does not identify who owed what, who failed, who could repair, or who had authority. Responsibility_R therefore remains conditional. It is not blame, legal liability, diagnosis, or moral rank.

**Action_E.**
Action_E is derived from Σ, Θ, and ∇: integrated realization of directedness across time. It is under-specified. The case names possible actions—reliance, withdrawal, repair, refusal—but does not provide an enacted sequence in which directedness is integrated over time. Without concrete action, Action_E cannot be assigned. This matters because later MIP and EDEN layers also preserve the distinction: NRK is not Action_E, breach is not Action_E, and abstract mention of betrayal is not an enacted action.

**Dignity_in_Practice_D.**
Dignity_in_Practice_D is derived from Ψ and Χ applied to Ω-handling. It is active as an application constraint, not as ontological dignity and not as moral superiority. In this case, D constrains how vulnerability and asymmetry are handled. The analysis must not shame those who trust, pathologize those who doubt, rank those who withdraw, humiliate those associated with betrayal, or force restoration in the name of maturity. Dignity-in-Practice protects the analysis from becoming a new asymmetry.

**SELF_FIXPOINT.**
SELF_FIXPOINT is treated as analytic-only and not established. Its formula sequence involves the full PMS chain ending in Ψ, but the abstract trust case does not establish selfhood, subjectivity, identity, or stable self-relation. SELF_FIXPOINT might become relevant in a case of durable promise, long-term role ownership, institutional self-binding, or accountability across time. Here it remains a possible higher-order extension, not a finding. It must not be applied literally to institutions, roles, claims, or abstract trust unless explicitly scoped as structural self-binding rather than subjectivity.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core YAML treats drift patterns as conditional structural risks, not diagnoses, not person-types, and not findings. This is crucial for the trust case because trust-language is unusually prone to moral and relational escalation. Doubt, withdrawal, refusal, rupture, non-fit, and silence can easily be interpreted as deficient trust, betrayal, failed maturity, or incomplete integration. The completed analysis blocks this conversion.

The first drift pattern is **AD_A>>E**. This pattern would describe a configuration in which awareness or reflective differentiation expands while enactment remains suppressed, delayed, or under-specified. In the trust case, it is not an active finding. The case contains many awareness-relevant distinctions: trust versus prediction, reliance versus dependency, doubt versus betrayal, withdrawal versus refusal, repair versus reconciliation, and expectation versus absence. It also contains many possible enactments: reliance, withdrawal, repair, renewed trust, refusal, boundary-setting, or institutional response. But the case does not provide a concrete scene in which awareness expands while action is chronically suppressed. Therefore, the axis effects remain under-specified rather than active.

The axis-effects discipline for AD_A>>E distinguishes the awareness axis and the action axis. The awareness axis may be over-expanded, not applicable, or under-specified; the action axis may be suppressed or chronically delayed, not applicable, or under-specified. For this case, the best reading is under-specification. There is conceptual expansion of awareness, but no concrete action field. There is no evidence that action is suppressed, avoided, postponed, or blocked. Action_E is under-specified, not low, immature, failed, or pathological.

The second drift pattern is **AD_Sigma_low**. This pattern would describe low or unstable integration, where coherence becomes volatile and responsibility becomes inconsistent. In the trust case, Σ is indeed weak and conditional, but that does not by itself activate AD_Sigma_low as a drift finding. A case can lack established integration because the material is abstract. Under-specification is not failure. A weak Σ result means that the analysis cannot show practical coordination among reliance, vulnerability, expectation, repair, and responsibility; it does not mean that the relation is disintegrated.

The required axis-effects form for AD_Sigma_low distinguishes the coherence axis and the responsibility axis. The coherence axis may be volatile, not applicable, or under-specified; the responsibility axis may be inconsistent, not applicable, or under-specified. Here again, the correct status is under-specified. Coherence_C is active at the conceptual level, but concrete coherence of a trust relation is not shown. Responsibility_R is weak because no promise, role-duty, breach, repair obligation, or institutional commitment is supplied. Therefore, the drift pattern is a risk marker only.

The larger coherence risk is that the PMS reading may be too smooth. Trust fits the operator grammar very well: Δ marks trust distinctions; ∇ marks movement toward or away from reliance; □ frames the relation; Λ captures meaningful absences; Α captures repeated reliability or unreliability; Ω captures vulnerability; Θ captures temporal persistence; Φ captures repair or reframing; Χ captures distance; Σ captures possible integration; Ψ captures possible self-binding. Because this fit is strong, the analysis must work harder to remain discriminating. A model that can map trust elegantly can also over-assimilate trust if it cannot say when trust is not the right frame.

The under-discrimination risk is therefore explicit. PMS must distinguish trust from mere prediction, dependence, credibility, loyalty, institutional compliance, strategic confidence, faith, fear, and procedural reliability. It must distinguish betrayal from miscommunication, silence, delay, incapacity, boundary-setting, confidentiality, role conflict, or legitimate refusal. It must distinguish repair from narrative smoothing, coerced reconciliation, reputational restoration, legal settlement, or mere reclassification. If it cannot preserve these distinctions, the PMS reading becomes too broad.

The final PMS drift status is conservative: AD_A>>E and AD_Sigma_low are not active findings. They remain conditional risks and calibration checks. The case does not show low maturity, failed integration, deficient action, unstable responsibility, or immature trust. It shows a conceptual field in which such risks could be examined if a concrete scene were later supplied.

The add-on used for this case is **PMS-EDEN**. It is applied as an overlay/application profile only. PMS.yaml remains the only PMS Base reference. PMS-EDEN does not redefine PMS operators, does not change PMS dependencies, does not introduce new base structures, and does not convert its paper-internal terms into PMS operators. EDEN reduced signatures are shorthand lenses and threshold markers. They compress dominant operator involvement, but they do not replace PMS dependency logic.

PMS Core alone is already sufficient to map trust structurally. It identifies trust as framed, expectation-sensitive, asymmetrical, temporal praxis with possible non-events, possible recontextualization, possible distance, and conditional integration or self-binding. EDEN is not added because PMS Core failed. It is added because trust contains a family of relational risks that PMS Core makes visible but does not isolate in EDEN’s own vocabulary: threshold explicitness, breach as chain interruption, Negative Responsibility Kernel as non-action, pseudo-symmetry risk, asymmetry-switch availability, reciprocity as coordinated asymmetry, and reciprocity loss.

The overlay’s strongest contribution is to sharpen the difference between trust as **symmetry** and trust as **coordinated asymmetry**. Trust is often rhetorically described as reciprocal: mutual reliance, mutual expectation, mutual confidence. EDEN asks whether that reciprocity is actually coordinated under real Ω. If exposure, repair burden, informational access, or institutional power are unequal, then trust cannot be evaluated as simple equality. The positive EDEN reading is that trust may require coordinated asymmetry: Ω must be legible, Χ must prevent premature coercive restoration, Σ must coordinate vulnerability and expectation, and Ψ must anchor role-specific self-binding where warranted. This remains weak because no concrete scene demonstrates it, but it is the most constructive EDEN addition.

The EDEN **ground-state** construct is weak and analogical. EDEN ground-state describes a non-deficit praxis configuration: differentiation is present, frames are stable, awareness is available, and relation is not dominated by deficit pressure. In the trust case, this can illuminate a condition in which reliance is available without immediate suspicion, comparison, coercive verification, or anticipated betrayal. But the case does not supply an Eden scene, an initial non-deficit baseline, or a loss sequence. Ground-state is therefore not active. It is a possible overlay lens for future concrete scenes.

The **threshold marker** is weakly applicable. Trust becomes fragile when negative options become explicit: betrayal, withdrawal, refusal, strategic doubt, non-repair, or exit. EDEN’s threshold signature marks the moment when such an option becomes selectable within a frame across time. The present case names such possibilities but does not provide a concrete threshold event. Therefore, threshold explicitness is a weak option-visibility lens, not a finding of breach or fall.

The EDEN construct **breach as chain interruption** is also weak. In EDEN, breach is not knowledge gain and not moral revelation. It is interruption of a praxis chain: realization occurs under directional praxis within a frame, but Σ does not carry integration and Ψ does not carry durable binding. In trust, betrayal could take this form. But the case only names betrayal as a possible trust phenomenon. It does not show a framed expectation, action or omission, consequence field, failed integration, or absent self-binding. Therefore, breach-as-chain-interruption remains a possible later reading, not an active conclusion.

The **Negative Responsibility Kernel (NRK)** is weak and explicitly not Action_E. This is a key EDEN/MIP hygiene point. NRK describes a configuration in which a real option-set exists, Ω is effective, Θ is trajectory-bearing, and realization occurs while Σ does not carry and Ψ is absent, aborted, displaced, externalized, or simulated. The Core case already marked Action_E as under-specified. EDEN therefore refuses to treat possible betrayal, refusal, or responsibility-language as enacted action. NRK is a breach-risk lens, not a PMS derived action structure.

The **false comparison pivot** is a risk handle. Trust can drift when relation is converted into comparison: who trusted more, who was more loyal, who suffered more, who is owed more, who is stronger or weaker, who controls the interpretive frame. Such comparison can replace coordination. But no comparison-dominant frame is supplied. False comparison is therefore not active.

The **dissatisfaction as system cost** construct is not applicable. EDEN treats dissatisfaction as a system cost of maintaining false equivalence under real Ω across Θ. The trust case contains doubt, withdrawal, repair, and betrayal as possible terms, but it does not show permanent comparison, correction loops, equivalence maintenance, repeated Φ repair, accumulating Λ, or stabilized Α in the form required by this construct.

**Pseudo-Symmetry (PS)** is a risk, not a finding. PS occurs where a parity or symmetry surface is preserved while real asymmetry continues to govern consequences. In trust, this is a plausible danger. A relation may present itself as equally reciprocal while one role bears greater exposure, repair burden, dependence, or institutional disadvantage. But the abstract case does not show a parity surface, appearance-management, blocked Σ, or misbound Ψ. Therefore, PS remains a risk handle.

The **asymmetry switch** is a risk handle, not a person label. It describes a shift from parity claim to asymmetry activation under real or invoked Ω/Θ pressure. It is valid where marked, bounded, and re-bound into Σ/Ψ; it becomes drift where unmarked, repeatable, and responsibility-shifting. Trust relations often require valid asymmetry activation. For example, care, emergency response, institutional duty, or repair burden may be unequal for good structural reasons. The case supplies no switch moment, no parity frame, and no responsibility-shift. Therefore, asymmetry-switch availability is risk-only.

The **permanentized exception switch** is not applicable. It would require repeated or standing asymmetry activation under preserved parity surface, with blocked Σ/Ψ coordination across time. No such regime appears.

**Parity-to-exit** is not applicable. It would involve parity rhetoric being used to deny additional responsibility under unequal Ω/Θ consequences. The trust case contains no equality rhetoric and no demonstrated refusal of role-specific burden.

**Responsibility-to-authority** is not applicable. It would involve required self-binding under asymmetry being converted into control, frame authority, protection entitlement, or governance over another role-position. The Core case keeps Responsibility_R weak and conditional; EDEN therefore cannot infer authority drift.

**PFO**, the Postfeminist Override / Over-steering regime label, is not applicable. The completed EDEN YAML explicitly rejects it as too strong. PFO requires Ω-taboo, comparison governance, permanentized exception-switch availability, protocol stabilization, suppressed Σ, and displaced Ψ across Θ. The trust case provides none of these. PFO is also not a group label or ideology claim. It remains entirely outside the active analysis.

**Reciprocity** is the strongest positive EDEN construct. EDEN defines reciprocity not as symmetry, but as coordinated asymmetry: Ω legible as functional gradient, Σ active, Ψ anchored, Χ present, with D constraining Ω-handling. In the trust case, this adds depth because trust often involves unequal exposure or repair burden while still requiring non-domination. However, the present abstract case cannot show whether reciprocity is coordinated, simulated, displaced, or lost. Reciprocity remains a calibration target.

**Reciprocity loss** is a risk. It would require temporal erosion of Σ/Ψ-carried coordination under real Ω, with chronic Λ and stabilized Α. Trust can certainly fail this way: repeated non-events, delayed repairs, unacknowledged vulnerability, or externalized responsibility can erode coordination. But the case does not show chronic residue, stabilized pattern, or failed coordination. Therefore, reciprocity loss is risk-only.

**Devaluation** and **humiliation** are not applicable. The case does not show standing-lowering, status regulation, devaluation scripts, or blocked coordination under named Ω. However, Dignity-in-Practice remains active as a guardrail against producing humiliation through the analysis itself. This is an important distinction: humiliation is not found in the case, but the analysis must not become humiliating.

The final EDEN overlay formula from the YAML is:

TRUST_EDEN_overlay = RECIPROCITY? as coordinated Ω across Θ with Χ, Σ?, and Ψ? under D, while THRESHOLD?, BREACH?, NRK?, PS?, and RECIPROCITY_LOSS? remain evidence-dependent risk or calibration handles.

The question marks are essential. They do not mark hidden activation. They mark conditional applicability. The EDEN overlay’s strongest result is negative as much as positive: it illuminates trust, but mostly prevents over-reading.

### Layer interaction

The PMS Core layer remains the base reading. It supplies the operator grammar, dependency discipline, derived structures, and claim boundaries. Trust remains primarily modeled as framed, temporally extended, asymmetrically exposed, pattern-sensitive praxis with meaningful non-events and possible repair or withdrawal. EDEN does not change this formula. It does not add operators. It does not change the meaning of Ω, Θ, Λ, Χ, Σ, or Ψ.

What EDEN sharpens is the relational drift field around trust. PMS Core can say that trust involves Ω; EDEN asks whether asymmetry is coordinated or hidden under pseudo-symmetry. PMS Core can say that trust involves Λ; EDEN asks whether non-events become breach-chain interruption or chronic reciprocity loss. PMS Core can say that trust may require Φ, Χ, Σ, and Ψ; EDEN asks whether repair is real coordination, coercive restoration, false comparison, or responsibility-to-authority. PMS Core can say that refusal is not automatically failure; EDEN reinforces that refusal may preserve dignity by blocking premature restoration.

The add-on must not be allowed to redefine PMS Core. EDEN terms such as ground-state, threshold, NRK, PS, PFO, asymmetry switch, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, devaluation, and humiliation remain overlay handles. They are paper-internal constructs. They do not become PMS operators, derived structures, diagnoses, person labels, group labels, ideology claims, or verdict engines.

Overlay language could overreach in several ways. First, betrayal-language could be converted into Fall logic. EDEN blocks this: breach is not established by the abstract case. Second, asymmetry-language could be converted into PFO logic. EDEN blocks this: PFO is not applicable. Third, refusal could be converted into reciprocity loss. EDEN blocks this: refusal may be dignity-preserving. Fourth, responsibility-language could be converted into authority or diagnosis. EDEN blocks this: Responsibility_R is weak, NRK is not Action_E, and AHP later confirms that no rescore or D activation occurs.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. PMS Core preserves Λ as meaningful absence and structural residue. EDEN preserves weak, risk, and not-applicable statuses rather than activating all its drift catalogue. MIP preserves non-assignment of A-score, M-score, and IA result. ### Example 1 — The unanswered message and the frame of expectation

**Type:** micro
**Recommended insertion point:** after the discussion of Λ in the PMS Core structural reading

A person sends a message after a difficult conversation: “Can we talk tomorrow?” No reply comes that day. On its own, this non-reply is not yet betrayal, avoidance, immaturity, or proof that trust has failed. It becomes structurally meaningful only if a frame of expectation already exists. If the relation normally allows delayed answers, the silence may remain ordinary. If the prior conversation ended with an explicit promise to respond, the same silence may become a trust-relevant Λ, a meaningful non-event inside a frame.

The example demonstrates why trust cannot be analyzed from absence alone. PMS Core first requires Δ: reply / non-reply, expectation / no expectation, ordinary delay / meaningful omission. ∇ appears as the movement toward renewed relation, clarification, or withdrawal. □ is decisive: the same missing reply means different things in friendship, workplace procedure, care relation, institutional complaint, or casual acquaintance. Λ becomes active only if the non-reply fails a framed expectation. Ω becomes relevant only if the silence exposes one role to vulnerability, uncertainty, dependency, or repair cost; without that exposure, the scene may remain ordinary communication delay rather than trust-relevant non-event. Θ matters because the meaning of silence changes with time: one hour, one day, and one month do not carry the same structural weight. Χ remains necessary because the analyst must not rush from silence to verdict.

The EDEN overlay is only weakly relevant here. The example may show threshold explicitness if the silence makes the option of withdrawal, distrust, or non-repair newly visible. It does not show breach-as-chain-interruption unless the expected reply, the relevant action, and the failure of Σ/Ψ are concretely established. It does not activate pseudo-symmetry, PFO, reciprocity loss, devaluation, or humiliation.

MIP status remains non-scored. No A-score, M-score, IA finding, or D-profile is warranted. AHP would treat this only as a useful example if its wording preserves the boundary: “silence may become trust-relevant” is acceptable; “the silence proves betrayal” is not.

Forbidden claims are therefore clear. The vignette does not diagnose avoidance, emotional immaturity, manipulation, or bad faith. It does not assign blame. It does not establish legal breach. It does not prove that repair is owed. It does not show that the person who waited is right, or that the person who did not answer is wrong.

The non-capture note is also important. The lived feeling of waiting may involve anxiety, grief, hope, anger, or shame. PMS can map the structural non-event, but it does not exhaust the affective experience of being left without response.

**Article takeaway:** the same absence can be ordinary, ambiguous, protective, negligent, or trust-relevant depending on frame, expectation, and time. Λ is powerful in trust analysis, but only when calibrated.

### Example 2 — Repair without automatic restoration

**Type:** compact
**Recommended insertion point:** after the EDEN discussion of breach, NRK, and reciprocity as coordinated asymmetry

A project coordinator misses an agreed internal deadline. A colleague had relied on that deadline to prepare a presentation. The coordinator later explains that another urgent task intervened and offers to help repair the delay. The colleague accepts the explanation but does not immediately return to the previous level of reliance. For the next phase, the colleague asks for written checkpoints rather than informal assurances.

This vignette is concrete enough to show the structure of trust, but bounded enough to avoid adjudication. It does not ask whether the coordinator is trustworthy as a person. It asks how reliance, expectation, non-event, explanation, repair, and modified future reliance become structurally related.

PMS Core hooks are visible throughout. Δ distinguishes missed deadline, explanation, repair offer, and modified future reliance. ∇ appears in two directions: the coordinator moves toward repair, while the colleague moves toward guarded reliance rather than full restoration. □ is the project frame, not a total moral frame. Λ is the missed deadline as meaningful non-event, but only because the deadline was agreed and relied upon. Α may become relevant only if such delays repeat; one missed deadline is not yet an attractor. Ω appears because the colleague bore downstream presentation risk. Θ matters because trust does not reset instantly after explanation; repair unfolds across later checkpoints. Φ appears when the missed deadline is recontextualized as an intervening-task problem rather than immediate betrayal. Χ appears in the colleague’s refusal to restore informal trust too quickly. Σ is possible but not complete: the new checkpoint structure may coordinate reliability and caution. Ψ remains conditional unless the coordinator durably binds themselves to the revised responsibility structure.

The EDEN overlay sharpens the example by preventing two opposite overreadings. First, the missed deadline should not automatically become “Fall” or breach-as-chain-interruption. The repair offer and explanation matter. Second, the colleague’s guarded response should not be treated as failed reciprocity. Trust as coordinated asymmetry may require a changed burden structure: written checkpoints make Ω legible instead of pretending the relation is simply symmetrical. NRK is not active unless the scene shows realization under ∇ with Σ failing and Ψ absent or displaced. Pseudo-symmetry would become relevant only if both parties pretended nothing had changed while the colleague continued silently carrying the cost.

MIP status remains qualitative. No score should be assigned from the vignette. It could support a later MIP analysis only if the actual role conditions were known: what each party knew, what alternatives were available, whether the deadline was realistically controllable, whether the repair offer was viable, and whether the colleague’s checkpoint request was proportionate. AHP would flag any wording that turned “modified reliance” into a maturity judgment.

Forbidden claims include: the coordinator is irresponsible; the colleague is distrustful; written checkpoints are punitive; repair requires renewed informal trust; the missed deadline is legal breach; the scene proves institutional dysfunction. None follows from the bounded vignette.

The non-capture note is that ordinary workplace context may include workload, hierarchy, organizational culture, private stress, or team norms not captured by the structural reading. These factors may change the interpretation without invalidating the structural hooks.

**Article takeaway:** repair does not automatically mean restoration. A mature trust structure may preserve distance, revise expectations, and coordinate asymmetry without turning caution into blame or repair into coercion.

### Example 3 — Institutional trust, publicness, and the danger of over-reading drift

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after the layer interaction section, before non-capture and rival readings

A small community group submits a formal request to a local institution. The institution’s public materials say that such requests will receive a response within four weeks. Six weeks pass without reply. Members of the group begin to disagree about what the silence means. One member says the institution has betrayed public trust. Another says the delay may be administrative overload. A third wants to publish a public accusation. A fourth suggests sending a clarification request first and documenting the timeline before drawing conclusions.

This example demonstrates why the full stack is needed, but also why the stack must remain restrained. It contains trust, expectation, asymmetry, publicness, possible non-event, possible repair, and possible escalation. Yet it does not establish betrayal, legal breach, institutional bad faith, PFO, humiliation, or reciprocity loss.

PMS Core begins with Δ. The analysis must distinguish formal expectation from informal hope, delay from refusal, silence from rejection, administrative overload from avoidance, and public accusation from clarification request. ∇ appears in competing directions: escalation, patience, clarification, withdrawal, or documentation. □ is institutional-public rather than intimate or clinical. The four-week response policy frames the expectation. Λ becomes relevant because no response occurs after the expected window, but the meaning of that non-event remains open. Α is not yet active unless the institution repeatedly misses such timelines. Ω is significant because the institution controls response channels and public legitimacy, while the community group bears uncertainty and possible dependence on institutional recognition. Θ is central: four weeks, six weeks, and repeated silence over months would not mean the same thing.

Φ may appear if the group reframes the silence. It might become an administrative delay, a procedural failure, a public trust problem, or a reason for formal complaint. But Φ must not be used to smooth away the issue. Reframing silence as “just bureaucracy” could under-read vulnerability; reframing it as “betrayal” could over-read the evidence. Χ is the key discipline: the fourth member’s proposal to document and ask for clarification preserves distance. It delays public closure without denying the trust-relevant residue. Σ remains conditional. The group may later integrate concern, evidence, and action into a coherent response, but that has not yet occurred. Ψ is also conditional. The institution may have a self-binding policy obligation, but the example does not establish whether the four-week statement is binding, aspirational, legally relevant, or operationally constrained.

The EDEN overlay adds useful caution. Threshold explicitness is visible: the group now recognizes that public accusation, formal complaint, renewed waiting, or withdrawal are selectable options. Breach-as-chain-interruption is possible but not established. NRK is not active, because the example does not show realization under ∇ with failed Σ and displaced Ψ. Pseudo-symmetry is a risk if the institution publicly presents itself as equally responsive while retaining all interpretive and procedural control. But this is not yet a finding. PFO is not applicable. There is no evidence of the EDEN-specific regime conditions. Parity-to-exit and responsibility-to-authority are also not active. Reciprocity loss is only a risk: it would require chronic Λ, stabilized Α, real Ω, Θ erosion, and failed or displaced Σ/Ψ. Six weeks of silence may be concerning, but it is not enough by itself to prove such a drift profile.

MIP becomes relevant because publicness appears. The group’s possible public accusation increases D-risk. A public claim may be warranted in some concrete cases, but MIP would require role clarity, information basis, proportionality, reviewability, and distinction between structural criticism and reputational attack. No A-score or M-score is assigned. The institution is not scored; the group is not scored; the members are not ranked. The IA-box remains under-specified: transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility all require more evidence. D is relevant only as protection. Public dignity risks arise if the institution is personified into individual blame, or if members of the group are shamed for caution or urgency.

AHP would treat the example as high-risk for language hygiene. It would flag terms like “betrayal,” “bad faith,” “institutional humiliation,” or “reciprocity loss” unless the article clearly marks them as possible readings, not findings. AHP would recommend precise wording: “the silence creates a trust-relevant Λ under a public institutional frame” is safer than “the institution betrayed the group.”

Forbidden claims are extensive. The vignette does not prove legal breach. It does not prove institutional bad faith. It does not diagnose bureaucratic dysfunction. It does not show that public accusation is wrong or right. It does not show that caution is mature and urgency immature. It does not establish PFO, pseudo-symmetry, NRK, humiliation, devaluation, or reciprocity loss. It does not validate PMS, EDEN, MIP, or AHP.

The non-capture note matters because institutions have operational constraints that may not be visible from the outside: staffing, jurisdiction, emergency workload, unclear submission status, procedural backlog, or legal limits. Conversely, affected groups may experience silence as exclusion, disrespect, or structural vulnerability in ways that administrative explanation alone does not capture. PMS can map the structural residue, but it cannot replace affected-party account or institutional evidence.

**Article takeaway:** institutional trust cases are structurally tempting because public silence easily invites closure. The disciplined reading preserves the trust-relevant non-event while refusing premature drift labels, legal conclusions, or public shaming.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The YAML analyses preserve multiple rival readings. These are not defects. They are necessary because trust is not exhausted by PMS, EDEN, MIP, or AHP.

A **phenomenological reading** may better capture lived openness, exposure, confidence, fear, grief, hope, shame, relief, or felt betrayal. PMS can map vulnerability as Ω and temporality as Θ, but the lived texture of trusting another may exceed structural grammar. The warmth of trust, the shock of betrayal, the intimacy of reliance, or the bodily feel of withdrawal are only partially captured.

A **moral-philosophical reading** may better capture fidelity, promise, forgiveness, betrayal, loyalty, obligation, and repair. PMS can frame responsibility and self-binding, but it does not settle moral status. It does not say who owes forgiveness, whether betrayal is blameworthy, or whether trust should be restored. Moral theory may preserve evaluative distinctions that PMS intentionally brackets.

A **legal-institutional reading** may better capture fiduciary duty, reliance damages, procedural legitimacy, evidentiary breach, liability, and institutional accountability. PMS can map frames and residues; it cannot determine legal thresholds. If trust appears in law, the relevant legal frame must supply duty, standard, evidence, jurisdiction, remedy, and procedure.

A **sociological reading** may better capture social capital, institutional legitimacy, norm stabilization, public confidence, and role-based trust. PMS can describe trust frames and asymmetries, but empirical social trust requires data, institutions, populations, and historical context.

A **psychological or clinical reading** may better capture attachment, trauma, expectation, threat response, confidence, or repair difficulty. But the present analysis explicitly does not diagnose. Psychological accounts remain open, but they are not activated by structural vocabulary.

A **game-theoretic reading** may better capture cooperation, defection, repeated interaction, incentives, risk under uncertainty, and strategic trust. PMS captures some structural aspects of repeated reliance and expectation, but it does not replace formal strategic modeling.

A **linguistic-pragmatic reading** may better capture testimony, sincerity, promise, uptake, credibility, speech acts, and communicative repair. PMS can map frames and non-events, but speech-specific analysis may identify details of utterance, uptake, and normativity that PMS does not isolate.

A **political-theory reading** may better capture legitimacy, representation, public confidence, institutional authority, domination, and distrust of power. PMS and EDEN can map asymmetry and reciprocity risk, but they do not convert political conflict into PFO or switch drift without concrete evidence.

A **cultural or theological reading** may better capture faith, fidelity, covenant, ritual trust, spiritual trust, or symbolic meanings of Eden. EDEN uses Eden as a praxeological overlay profile; it does not make theological claims.

The anti-capture reading is also preserved: some trust phenomena may resist structural capture altogether. Trust may be a lived practice whose deepest features are not rendered by operator grammar. This is not a failure of the case. It is part of the boundary discipline. PMS-AXIOM maps closure-demands and residues; it does not erase residue by naming it.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would be weakened if the case material did not actually require a trust frame. If the relation were better described as mere prediction, statistical expectation, technical reliability, procedural compliance, dependency, obedience, fear, loyalty, credibility, or contractual performance, then the trust reading would need to be narrowed or withdrawn.

It would also be weakened if no meaningful expectation were present. Without expected occurrence, Λ cannot carry trust-specific absence. A missing event matters only if the frame establishes that it was expected. If no response, repair, protection, fidelity, acknowledgment, or action was reasonably expected, then non-event analysis weakens.

The PMS reading would become under-discriminating if it could not distinguish doubt from betrayal, withdrawal from failure, silence from refusal, repair from reconciliation, or trust from dependency. It would become over-assimilative if every rupture were interpreted as drift, every refusal as incomplete integration, every absence as betrayal, every reframe as repair, or every coherence pattern as proof of adequate analysis.

The EDEN reading would be weakened if no threshold, breach, reciprocity, false comparison, asymmetry-switch, or reciprocity-loss question were structurally relevant. If a later concrete trust scene showed simple procedural reliability without vulnerability, no meaningful threshold, no asymmetry, no repair burden, and no reciprocity issue, EDEN would add little. EDEN would also be weakened if its constructs obscured more than they clarified, for example by making ordinary trust difficulties look like Eden/Fall drift.

The EDEN reading would become overextended if PFO, pseudo-symmetry, NRK, devaluation, humiliation, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, or permanentized exception switch were activated without concrete evidence. The completed YAML explicitly rejects that move. A stronger EDEN claim would require specific evidence: parity surface, real Ω, blocked or simulated Σ, misbound Ψ, comparison governance, unmarked asymmetry activation, responsibility-shifting, chronic Λ, stabilized Α, and temporal erosion.

The MIP reading would be weakened if it were treated as a score despite non-assignment. A-score and M-score are not assigned. IA is not assessable. D is protection-oriented only. If later readers used the MIP output as maturity rating, blame allocation, governance verdict, HR decision, public D-reading, or dignity profile, that would violate the artifact’s claim boundary.

The AHP reading would be weakened if it were treated as a validation of the MIP analysis. AHP gives a high Precision Heuristic for the working note, not for trust itself. It says the analysis artifact is precise enough to be a robust working note. It does not prove the case, validate the models, or make the artifact transmittable.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled across the stack. For PMS, stronger application would require a concrete trust frame, specified roles, expectations, temporal sequence, meaningful non-events, asymmetry mapping, recurrence evidence, recontextualization evidence, distance markers, integration evidence, and self-binding evidence. For EDEN, stronger application would require threshold events, breach sequence, NRK conditions, parity surfaces, asymmetry-switch evidence, reciprocity coordination or loss, and concrete drift markers. For MIP, stronger application would require role knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, integration attempts, publicness, and affected perspectives. For AHP, hardening actions would need to be applied to the artifact and reviewed before transmission.

Falsifier conditions include: no trust-specific frame; no meaningful expectation; no vulnerability or exposure; no temporal trajectory; no role or institutional asymmetry; no repair or withdrawal relevance; rival framework explains the case with greater discrimination; concrete evidence contradicts the mapped operator statuses; or use of the analysis creates more closure, humiliation, or overreach than clarity.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It does not diagnose trust issues, attachment patterns, avoidance, pathology, immaturity, trauma, narcissism, dependency, paranoia, or relational dysfunction. Structural vocabulary must not be converted into clinical vocabulary.

It must not be used for person ranking. No person is trustworthy, untrustworthy, mature, immature, dignified, undignified, integrated, disintegrated, responsible, irresponsible, asymmetry-switching, pseudo-symmetrical, PFO-coded, or reciprocity-losing on the basis of this analysis.

It must not be used as legal or forensic conclusion. Betrayal-language is not breach finding. Trust-asymmetry is not liability. Non-event is not evidence. Repair failure is not legal culpability. Legal use requires independent legal standards and procedures.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. PMS does not prove what trust is in itself. EDEN does not prove a Fall structure. MIP does not prove maturity. AHP does not prove artifact truth.

It must not be used as empirical proof. No data are supplied. No population claim, causal claim, institutional claim, or behavioral generalization follows.

It must not be used as implementation validation. YAML structure is not an operational system. It does not justify automated trust scoring, institutional triage, relational advice, AI moderation, therapy design, or governance deployment.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation or EDEN validation. A successful mapping does not prove the base model.

It must not use add-ons as operators. EDEN labels are overlay handles only. PFO, NRK, PS, asymmetry switch, reciprocity loss, devaluation, humiliation, parity-to-exit, and responsibility-to-authority are not PMS operators and not verdicts.

It must not treat drift as verdict. Drift risks are conditional structural risks. Risk is not finding. Weak status is not evidence. Not applicable means not applied. Under-specification is not failure.

It must not use closure by authority. The stack is structured, but structure is not authority. PMS-AXIOM maps where closure is possible, where add-ons are justified, and where Λ-residue must remain visible. It does not force closure.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of this case is a disciplined structural reading of trust as temporally extended, asymmetrically exposed, expectation-sensitive praxis within a relational frame. PMS Core provides the primary grammar: Δ distinguishes trust-relevant terms, ∇ marks relational movement, □ frames expectation, Λ captures meaningful non-event, Α marks possible stabilization, Ω identifies vulnerability and exposure, Θ gives trust its temporal arc, Φ marks possible reframing, Χ protects reflective distance, while Σ and Ψ remain conditional rather than cheaply activated. The final PMS Core result is not that trust has been solved, but that trust becomes structurally legible only when expectation, vulnerability, temporality, and non-event are calibrated without forcing closure.

The EDEN overlay adds a second-order discrimination layer. It does not replace PMS Core and does not become PMS Base. Its strongest contribution is the distinction between reciprocity as simple symmetry and reciprocity as coordinated asymmetry. Trust may require unequal burdens to be named and coordinated rather than hidden behind parity or converted into authority. EDEN also sharpens threshold, breach, NRK, pseudo-symmetry, asymmetry-switch, and reciprocity-loss questions. Yet in this abstract case, these remain weak, risk-only, or not applicable. PFO, permanentized exception switch, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, devaluation, and humiliation are not findings. The overlay therefore functions as restraint as much as amplification.

The examples contribute by making the boundary discipline readable. The unanswered-message example shows why Λ cannot be inferred from absence alone: silence becomes trust-relevant only inside a frame of expectation. The repair-without-restoration example shows why repair does not automatically require renewed trust, and why distance may preserve rather than damage a relation. The institutional-trust example shows how publicness, asymmetry, and non-event create strong pressure toward closure, while the full stack prevents premature legal, moral, EDEN-drift, or dignity claims. The examples demonstrate the analysis; they do not provide evidence for stronger claims.

What remains unclaimed is substantial. The case does not claim empirical truth about trust. It does not define trust metaphysically. It does not diagnose persons, groups, roles, or institutions. It does not identify legal breach, liability, or forensic responsibility. It does not prescribe repair, forgiveness, withdrawal, or public accusation. It does not validate PMS, EDEN, MIP, or AHP. It does not convert vulnerability into guilt, refusal into immaturity, silence into betrayal, coherence into proof, or drift risk into finding.

The boundary discipline is therefore the result, not an accessory. Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological rather than ontological: it constrains how the analysis handles vulnerability and asymmetry, without ranking worth. MIP preserves the downstream guardrail status of the artefact: no A-score, no M-score, no IA finding, no public D-reading, and no decision use. AHP hardens the analysis artifact by identifying wording and transmission risks, but it does not rescore or upgrade the case. Non-assignment is not a low score. Under-specification is not failure. Non-capture remains possible.

The final methodological takeaway is that trust is a high-closure concept that must be analyzed without becoming a closure machine. AXIOM can map the operator structure of trust, show why EDEN is useful as an overlay, and mark where MIP/AHP guardrails are needed. But the cleanest result is not a verdict about trust. It is a disciplined visibility structure: trust becomes analyzable where framed expectation, vulnerability, absence, temporality, distance, and possible repair are held together; and it remains ethically and methodologically safe only where the analysis also preserves residue, rivalry, reversibility, and the right not to force trust back into closure.
