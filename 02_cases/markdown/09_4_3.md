### Why this case matters

Trust is a closure-demanding phenomenon. It invites the observer to decide whether a relation is intact or broken, whether an absence is ordinary delay or betrayal, whether doubt is prudence or immaturity, whether withdrawal is protection or failure, whether repair is genuine or merely rhetorical, and whether responsibility belongs to the one who relied, the one relied upon, the institutional frame, or the scene itself. This makes trust philosophically attractive and structurally dangerous. It is attractive because it concentrates many of the core materials of praxis: expectation, vulnerability, asymmetry, recurrence, temporal extension, non-event, repair, responsibility, and dignity. It is dangerous because those same materials tempt the analysis into premature adjudication.

The completed PMS Core analysis refuses that temptation. It does not ask what trust “is” as a metaphysical essence, nor whether trust is ultimately a psychological attitude, moral virtue, legal reliance relation, sociological norm, epistemic stance, or affective openness. Instead, it asks what happens when trust is treated as a relational praxis-configuration whose structural conditions can be projected through the PMS operators. The point is not to defeat rival accounts; it is to make visible the operator conditions under which trust becomes structurally legible while preserving reversibility, distance, and non-capture.

This case belongs in AXIOM because trust is a high-pressure test of whether a formal system can resist the pleasures of closure. If a model can describe trust only by deciding too quickly whether trust has been violated, repaired, deserved, refused, or betrayed, then the model becomes a tribunal. If it can describe trust only by turning vulnerability into moral rank, refusal into immaturity, silence into guilt, or repair into integration, then it becomes an assimilation machine. AXIOM requires something more disciplined: a layered reading in which the Core operator grammar, the EDEN asymmetry overlay, the MIP maturity layer, and the AHP precision layer each add visibility without claiming what they are not allowed to claim.

The structural difficulty lies in the fact that trust seems to fit many PMS operators smoothly. It involves distinctions, frames, expectations, non-events, recurrent patterns, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and sometimes self-binding. This smoothness is exactly the danger. A case that fits too well may be under-discriminated. The more easily trust can be described through PMS, the more carefully the reading must ask whether it has collapsed trust into adjacent phenomena: prediction, dependence, credibility, loyalty, institutional compliance, confidence, faith, fear, procedural reliability, or moral expectation. Trust is not merely expectation; not every absence is betrayal; not every refusal is failure; not every repair is integration; not every vulnerability generates responsibility; and not every coherent model reading captures the phenomenon.

The case therefore matters because it demonstrates mature non-closure. It shows how a formal grammar can make trust more legible without declaring itself complete. It also shows how layered analysis can deepen rather than settle the case. PMS Core organizes the operator conditions. EDEN asks whether real asymmetry is truthfully coordinated or falsely coordinated. MIP asks how maturity in practice would be documented without person-scoring. AHP asks whether the MIP analysis itself is precise enough, and where it remains vulnerable to misreading. The stack is not a staircase toward verdict. It is a discipline of bounded legibility.

The case is not being solved metaphysically because it does not claim to discover the essence of trust. It is not being solved empirically because no data set, observation protocol, interview material, behavioral evidence, institutional archive, or comparative study is supplied. It is not being solved clinically because no person, symptom, diagnosis, trauma structure, attachment pattern, or treatment need is assessed. It is not being solved legally because no duty, breach, causation, liability, fiduciary role, evidentiary threshold, or procedural rule is established. It is not being solved diagnostically because doubt, withdrawal, non-binding, refusal, repair, and rupture are explicitly not converted into traits, deficits, pathologies, maturity failures, or person rankings.

The case is solved only in a limited sense: it is structurally rendered as a reversible, non-totalizing, non-diagnostic conceptual projection. This is why the case is not weaker for refusing closure. Its refusal is part of the result. Trust is precisely the kind of object that reveals whether a grammar of praxis can remain strong without becoming coercive. The AXIOM relevance of the case lies in that test: to make trust structurally visible while keeping open the possibility that a concrete scene, rival theory, or lived experience may require the reading to be narrowed, revised, or suspended.

### Scene and frame structure

The “scene” of this case is deliberately not a concrete event. It is a conceptual scene: trust as a relational regime. The boundary of the scene is therefore not a time-stamped interaction but a field of intelligibility in which reliance, vulnerability, expectation, confidence, doubt, withdrawal, repair, and betrayal become structurally relevant. The case asks how such a field can be read through PMS without turning the reading into a verdict.

The frame, □, is the relational trust regime itself. It may be interpersonal, role-based, testimonial, institutional, moral, procedural, or claim-based, but the abstract case does not choose one of these as the concrete frame. What is inside the frame are relations of reliance and exposure: a party, role, or position may trust, rely, expect, wait, doubt, withdraw, refuse, ask for repair, or experience non-response as meaningful. Another party, role, institution, claim, or frame may be relied upon, answerable, non-bound, unable, silent, responsive, protective, or implicated in repair. What is outside the frame are metaphysical essence claims, person-totalizing claims, clinical diagnosis, empirical validation, legal breach determination, and action authorization.

The frame is protected by several constraints. Distance, Χ, must remain active; the analysis may not fuse trust into urgency, judgment, enforcement, or closure. Reversibility must remain active; the reading must remain bounded, revisable, and non-final. Dignity-in-Practice must remain active; vulnerability and asymmetry must not be used to shame, rank, humiliate, or ontologically evaluate persons or groups. Non-capture must remain active; resistance, rupture, disagreement, non-fit, or rival framing may not automatically be redescribed as drift, incomplete integration, calibration failure, or structured absence.

The roles are generic rather than personal. The MIP layer names a relying or exposed position, a relied-upon or answerable position, a relational/institutional/testimonial/moral trust frame, an observer or analyst position, and an affected-role position if a later concrete scene is supplied. These are not persons. They are role positions in a possible trust configuration. The relying or exposed position may bear vulnerability, waiting cost, dependency, lower frame control, or lower repair access. The relied-upon or answerable position may have capacity difference, information difference, authority, timing control, explanation power, protection power, or role-based obligations. The frame itself may control what counts as expectation, repair, delay, refusal, betrayal, credibility, or legitimate non-binding. The observer has interpretive and formalization power, but no application authority over real persons or institutions.

Temporality, Θ, is central because trust is rarely exhausted by an instant. Trust accumulates, persists, weakens, repairs, remembers, and anticipates. Reliability can be built over time; confidence can persist; doubt can grow; betrayal can be remembered; repair can unfold; institutions can carry histories. But in this case, temporality is structurally relevant rather than scene-proven. There is no concrete sequence from expectation to absence to rupture to repair. The temporal arc is conceptual: a trust configuration may move from framed expectation through reliance, non-event, doubt, withdrawal, repair possibility, or rupture, but the case does not decide which trajectory occurred.

The reversibility window is therefore methodological. Since no concrete scene is supplied, the analysis must remain revisable until a scene packet exists. A later concrete case would need to specify roles, events, expectations, non-events, information, power, alternatives, publicness, costs, and affected-role perspectives. Without that, the model can state only calibration requirements. It cannot establish that a relation was betrayed, repaired, mature, immature, integrated, falsely coordinated, or responsibility-bearing.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are visible but uncalibrated. Trust entails possible vulnerability: the one who relies may be more exposed than the one relied upon; the one who waits may bear time cost; the one who lacks frame control may be less able to define what counts as adequate response; the one who depends on institutional recognition may be exposed to procedural silence. Yet asymmetry is not failure. Ω marks structural imbalance of power, exposure, capacity, role, obligation, information, or protection. It does not by itself identify blame, victim status, moral superiority, legal wrong, or maturity. EDEN later sharpens this point by distinguishing real asymmetry from false coordination of real asymmetry, but PMS Core already requires that Ω be calibrated rather than moralized.

Because the case is not concrete, scene discipline becomes a central result. The analysis can say: trust requires framing; expectations matter only within frames; absences become meaningful only when an expected occurrence is named; asymmetry requires direction and kind; temporality requires trajectory; repair requires more than reframing; integration requires enacted coordination; self-binding requires commitment, role ownership, promise, or accountability. But it cannot say: this trust was betrayed; this refusal was immature; this non-response was evasive; this repair was genuine; this role is responsible; this institution failed; this person lacks trustworthiness. That distinction is the scene discipline on which the article depends.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading proceeds through the eleven operators Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, and Ψ. The point of presenting the operators in order is not ritual completeness. It is dependency hygiene. Each later operator carries assumptions from earlier operators, and the abstract status of the case prevents later operators from being used cheaply.

#### Δ — Difference

Difference is the root operator. In this case, trust first becomes analyzable because relevant distinctions can be made: trust versus non-trust, reliance versus non-reliance, confidence versus doubt, role versus person, claim versus institution, expected occurrence versus meaningful absence, repair versus non-repair, and betrayal versus ordinary failure. Without such distinctions, trust would collapse into a vague field of affect, dependence, belief, or social expectation.

The operator is active and major. It is also calibration-sensitive. The distinctions must be explicitly framed because the case is abstract. Trust must not be confused with prediction, dependency, credibility, loyalty, fear, faith, or institutional compliance. If those adjacent phenomena cannot be distinguished, the PMS reading loses its entry point. This is not a minor technicality. In trust analysis, many errors begin at Δ: dependence is mistaken for trust; accurate prediction is mistaken for trust; institutional compliance is mistaken for trust; non-reliance is mistaken for distrust; refusal is mistaken for betrayal. Difference prevents the analysis from beginning with moralized blur.

The overclaim would be to assume that because distinctions are possible, PMS has captured the case. Δ opens structural analysis; it does not prove adequacy. Difference can make the trust field legible while leaving affective, historical, linguistic, legal, or cultural dimensions outside PMS capture.

#### ∇ — Impulse

Impulse names directional tension arising from difference. In the trust case, it appears as movement toward reliance, confidence, repair, renewed relation, or continued openness, and movement away through doubt, withdrawal, refusal, or suspension. This directedness is structural rather than psychological. PMS does not infer desire, fear, pathology, attachment, motive, trauma, or hidden intention from the fact that a trust relation moves toward or away from reliance.

The operator is active but moderate. It depends on Δ because directional tension must arise from distinctions in the field. If there is no differentiated trust/non-trust or reliance/non-reliance distinction, then movement toward or away from trust cannot be identified. The calibration issue is that “movement” must not be read as inner motive. A person may withdraw for many reasons: protection, incapacity, refusal, non-capture, prudence, fatigue, institutional constraint, or lack of obligation. The analysis may name direction only at the structural level.

The overclaim would be to psychologize ∇. If the analysis treated withdrawal as avoidance, refusal as hostility, renewed reliance as neediness, or repair-seeking as dependency, it would break the non-diagnostic boundary. In this case, impulse remains structural directedness.

#### □ — Frame

Frame makes the trust relation structurally specific. The active frame is the relational regime of trust itself: interpersonal, role-based, claim-based, testimonial, moral, procedural, or institutional. Within such a frame, reliance, vulnerability, expectation, confidence, doubt, withdrawal, repair, and betrayal acquire relevance. Without a frame, the same behavior may mean very different things. Silence in one frame may be ordinary; in another, it may be refusal; in a third, it may be institutional neglect; in a fourth, it may be protection.

□ is active and major. It depends on Δ and ∇ because a frame constrains distinctions and directedness. The main calibration issue is frame-shift. A personal trust frame cannot be silently collapsed into an institutional frame; testimonial credibility cannot be treated as moral fidelity without argument; procedural trust cannot be reduced to interpersonal warmth; legal reliance cannot be inferred from affective expectation. The abstract case names trust as a relational regime but does not decide which subframe governs any concrete scene.

The overclaim would be frame imperialism: treating “trust” as one frame that absorbs all adjacent frames. If the frame remains unspecified, the analysis becomes too abstract to discriminate trust from confidence, dependence, credibility, loyalty, compliance, or institutional routine.

#### Λ — Non-Event

Non-Event is central to trust because trust is often structured by what does not happen. A promised reply does not arrive; a protection does not materialize; recognition is delayed; repair is absent; fidelity is not enacted; an institution does not respond; a silence becomes meaningful. Λ names structured absence within a frame, not mere absence as such.

Λ is active and major. It depends on □ because an absence matters only inside a frame of expectation. No response is not automatically a betrayal; no repair is not automatically refusal; delay is not automatically neglect. The expected occurrence must be named: response, protection, reliable action, fidelity, repair, explanation, or recognition. The calibration is especially important here: absence, silence, delay, refusal, or non-fit may mark legitimate boundary, rival framing, incapacity, or PMS non-capture. It must not automatically be coded as betrayal or failed integration.

The overclaim would be to turn every non-event into moralized failure. Trust analysis often collapses here: because someone expected something, the absence becomes breach; because repair did not occur, the relation becomes betrayal; because silence hurts, it becomes culpable. PMS rejects that shortcut. Λ must be calibrated by frame, expectation, obligation, temporality, asymmetry, and available alternatives.

#### Α — Attractor

Attractor names recurrent stabilization. Trust rarely rests on a single event alone. It stabilizes through repeated reliability, repeated failure, repeated withdrawal, repair cycles, institutional routines, recurrent disappointment, or path-dependent expectations. Α makes trust analyzable as pattern rather than isolated incident.

In this case, Α is structurally relevant but not scene-proven. Its analytic weight is moderate. The case invokes relational regimes and possible patterns of confidence, withdrawal, repair, or betrayal, but no concrete recurrence is supplied. The operator’s dependency is thick: Α depends on Δ, ∇, □, and Λ. A pattern must have distinctions, direction, frame, and meaningful expectation or absence. Without recurrence, stabilization, role-script, or path dependence, Α is over-read.

The calibration risk is conceptual overgeneralization. If one missed reply becomes a “pattern,” or one doubt becomes “chronic mistrust,” or one repair attempt becomes “repair culture,” the operator has been pushed too far. In the present case, Α functions as a pattern requirement rather than a pattern finding.

#### Ω — Asymmetry

Asymmetry is one of the strongest operators in the trust case. Trust involves structural imbalance through vulnerability, exposure, dependence, authority, capacity, information, role obligation, or institutional power. The one who relies may be more exposed than the one relied upon. The one who waits may bear costs that the other does not feel. The one with institutional frame control may decide what counts as response, delay, legitimacy, or repair.

Ω is active and major, but calibration-dependent. It depends on Α in PMS dependency logic because asymmetrical roles emerge from stabilized patterns. In the abstract trust case, vulnerability and reliance create structurally plausible exposure and obligation gradients, but the direction, magnitude, legitimacy, and consequences of the asymmetry cannot be concretely mapped. A reciprocal trust relation may contain crossed asymmetries; an institution may have power in one dimension and constraint in another; a relying party may be vulnerable in exposure but powerful in public accusation. Ω must therefore remain multidimensional.

The overclaim would be moralizing asymmetry. Ω does not identify blame, victim status, moral superiority, legal wrong, maturity, or dignity rank. It says: a directional imbalance of exposure, capacity, power, role, or obligation may structure the trust relation. If no such imbalance is identifiable, Ω claims must be removed or marked weak. If asymmetry is real but justified, transparent, time-bound, and reversible, it is not automatically a failure.

#### Θ — Temporality

Temporality is central because trust unfolds. Reliability accumulates; confidence persists; doubt grows; betrayal is remembered; repair takes time; institutions carry histories; expectations endure; non-events may become more meaningful with delay. Θ makes trust a trajectory rather than a static comparison of attitudes.

Θ is active and major. It depends on Ω and Α: patterns and asymmetries become trajectories. In this case, the dependency is plausible but abstract. Trust depends on temporal persistence, expectation, memory, delay, repair, and betrayal, but no concrete timeline is supplied. First encounters, repeated relations, institutional histories, and post-betrayal repair arcs all require different temporal analysis.

The calibration risk is temporal overextension. If a momentary doubt is treated as a durable trajectory, or a single absence becomes a history of betrayal, Θ is overclaimed. Conversely, if a long history of asymmetric non-response is treated as a neutral isolated event, Θ is underused. The present case preserves temporality as structurally central while withholding concrete temporal judgment.

#### Φ — Recontextualization

Recontextualization names transformation through placing an existing structure into a new frame. In trust, this may occur as repair, reinterpretation, forgiveness, renegotiated expectation, institutional reform, or reframing betrayal as misunderstanding. A trust rupture may be recontextualized as miscommunication; personal reliance may be shifted into institutional role expectation; suspicion may be transformed by clarified boundaries; repair may create a new frame in which prior absence is not simply erased but differently understood.

Φ is active and major as a possibility, not as a guaranteed repair mechanism. It depends on Θ, Ω, and □: a frame-shift must occur within temporal trajectory, under asymmetry, and within a frame. The calibration issue is severe. The source frame and target frame must be named. A recontextualization that clarifies is not the same as one that absorbs rupture, erases refusal, or smooths over non-fit. Repair is not merely a better story; forgiveness is not necessarily integration; renegotiation is not necessarily justice; institutional reform is not necessarily recognition.

The overclaim would be over-assimilation. If every rupture, disagreement, refusal, or resistance is automatically absorbed as Φ, PMS becomes a closure machine. The YAML analysis explicitly prevents that. Recontextualization must preserve possible non-capture.

#### Χ — Distance

Distance is central both inside the trust relation and in the analysis itself. Inside trust, Χ appears as doubt, hesitation, withdrawal, refusal to rush judgment, suspension before renewed reliance, or reflective pause before repair. In the analysis, Χ prevents verdict, diagnosis, closure, person-ranking, and forced reconciliation.

Χ is active and major. It depends on Φ, Θ, and □. That dependency matters: reflective distance is not mere absence of action; it occurs in relation to frame, temporal trajectory, and possible recontextualization. Doubt can be mature, immature, protective, punitive, avoidant, or simply unclassifiable depending on scene conditions. Withdrawal may be reflective distance, but it may also be denial, punishment, incapacity, boundary, or non-involvement. The operator requires discrimination.

The overclaim would be to use Χ as aesthetic neutrality. The analysis cannot call every suspension “distance” or every non-action “reflection.” At the same time, it cannot suspend Χ in order to force a verdict. If Χ is suspended to decide betrayal, maturity, immaturity, integration, or failure, the PMS application becomes invalid. Distance is the operator that keeps the model from becoming an authority machine.

#### Σ — Integration

Integration names synthesis of disparate or conflicting elements into a more coherent whole. In trust, Σ may appear where confidence and doubt, reliance and vulnerability, memory and repair, role and person, or institutional claim and lived exposure are practically coordinated. A repair process may integrate harm and renewed relation. A refusal may integrate vulnerability and boundary. An institution may integrate procedural rules and lived exposure. But this is not automatic.

In this case, Σ is weak. It is plausible but not scene-proven. The case names possible coordination, but no concrete enactment of integrated trust action is supplied. Σ depends on Χ and Φ: integration requires distance and recontextualization. If those are not demonstrably active, Σ cannot be used as a higher-order claim.

The calibration risk is coherence fetishism. PMS can produce elegant formal coherence; that does not prove adequacy. If trust, confidence, reliance, credibility, loyalty, refusal, repair, and betrayal are smoothed together in one graceful synthesis, the analysis has lost discriminative power. If coherence is treated as proof, Σ is overclaimed. In this case, the correct result is weak integration: structurally relevant, not established.

#### Ψ — Self-Binding

Self-Binding names formation of self-relation through commitment to integrated structures across time. In trust, Ψ may appear where trust involves promise, role ownership, accountability, institutional commitment, durable responsibility, or analytic self-binding of the application itself. It may matter in promises, fiduciary roles, long-term caregiving, institutional offices, or explicit accountability practices.

In this case, Ψ is weak and conditional. It depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. Since Σ is weak, and since no concrete promise, role ownership, accountability act, or durable self-commitment is supplied, Ψ cannot be treated as established. The abstract case does not establish SELF_FIXPOINT. It also does not attribute literal selfhood to institutions, roles, claims, or abstract trust.

The overclaim would be subjectivizing the structure. PMS can model self-binding as a derived praxeological structure, but it does not prove subjectivity, consciousness, metaphysical selfhood, or institutional personhood. In this case, Ψ remains primarily a discipline of the analysis: the case is self-bound to its own guardrails, especially distance, reversibility, non-diagnosis, and dignity-in-practice.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

Dependency discipline is not decorative in this case. It is the main safeguard against closure. Later operators cannot be used cheaply because they presuppose earlier structural work. A claim about integration requires more than the formal availability of Σ. A claim about self-binding requires more than the presence of trust language. A claim about responsibility requires more than vulnerability. A claim about betrayal requires more than absence. A claim about maturity requires more than coherence.

The completed Core reading follows the PMS operator inventory and dependency order: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. It also allows the compositional reading to be expressed in reverse chain form from Ψ down to Δ, but that does not license top-down imposition. If Ψ is invoked, its dependencies must be respected; if Σ is invoked, Χ and Φ must be active; if Φ is invoked, Θ, Ω, and □ must be calibrated; if Ω is invoked, patterned role asymmetry must be sufficiently grounded; if Λ is invoked, the frame and expectation must be named.

Σ remains conditional because the case does not show actual coordination. It identifies possible sites of coordination—confidence and doubt, reliance and vulnerability, memory and repair, role and person, institutional claim and lived exposure—but no concrete praxis enactment is given. This prevents the model from declaring maturity by formal elegance.

Ψ remains conditional for the same reason, but more strongly. Self-binding requires integration, temporality, and distance. It also requires some commitment structure: promise, role ownership, accountability, institutional self-limitation, or explicit analytic self-binding. The abstract concept of trust is not itself a subject. An institution is not literally self-bound unless the claim is scoped as structural role-commitment or policy constraint. The case therefore withholds SELF_FIXPOINT as a finding.

Coherence is not proof. The analysis explicitly recognizes high coherence risk: trust fits many PMS operators smoothly, and that smooth fit can obscure under-discrimination. A reading may be coherent because the model is powerful, because the phenomenon is structurally rich, or because the analysis has smoothed over difference. Only discrimination tests can protect against the third possibility. The analysis must be able to say when trust is not mere dependence, when doubt is not failure, when refusal may be adequate praxis, when repair is not merely recontextualization, and when betrayal cannot be inferred from absence alone.

Non-capture is not failure. Affective texture, lived vulnerability, cultural meaning, legal thresholds, empirical trust dynamics, and principled refusal of trust may remain outside PMS capture. This does not mean PMS has failed; it means PMS has preserved its boundary. In a closure machine, everything outside the model becomes deficiency, drift, immaturity, calibration residue, or incomplete integration. In this PMS application, non-fit may indicate genuine limit, rival adequacy, or a domain that requires another grammar.

Invalid shortcuts include treating Λ as betrayal without frame and obligation; treating Ω as blame without calibrated asymmetry; treating Θ as long-term pattern without temporal evidence; treating Φ as repair without source and target frame; treating Χ as neutrality without reflective suspension; treating Σ as coherence without enacted coordination; treating Ψ as selfhood without commitment; treating derived structures as primitive; treating EDEN handles as PMS operators; treating MIP non-assignment as low maturity; and treating AHP precision as first-order case strength.

The calibration discipline of this case is therefore double. It calibrates operators to the abstract trust field, and it calibrates the limits of that calibration. The case can say what must be asked of a trust scene. It cannot answer those questions in place of the scene.

### Derived structures

Derived structures in PMS are formula-derived, not primitive. This is central to the case. Awareness, Coherence, Responsibility, Action, Dignity-in-Practice, and SELF_FIXPOINT are not added axioms. They arise from operator compositions and remain bounded by those compositions. The completed YAML analysis treats this discipline explicitly: derived axes may be active, weak, under-specified, application-constraint, analytic-only, or not applicable, but they are not independent foundations.

#### Awareness_A

Awareness_A is active. It is modeled structurally as sustained, framed differentiation across time, not as phenomenological consciousness. In the trust case, Awareness_A appears where trust versus non-trust, reliance versus withdrawal, confidence versus doubt, expected event versus non-event, repair versus betrayal, and role versus person remain distinguishable within a relational regime.

Its activity is justified because Δ, □, and Θ are all structurally relevant. There are distinctions, they are framed by trust, and they persist across temporal relation. But Awareness_A does not establish inner experience. It does not mean that a subject is consciously aware, affectively aware, morally aware, or psychologically mature. It means the analysis can sustain framed distinctions over time.

The calibration issue is to avoid converting structural awareness into phenomenology or diagnosis. A trust relation may be structurally legible without capturing how vulnerability feels, how fear or hope is lived, or how cultural histories shape trust. Awareness_A is therefore active as a PMS-derived structure, but non-exhaustive.

#### Coherence_C

Coherence_C is active. It arises from temporally stabilized structuring of impulse and expectation within a frame. In the trust case, directional reliance or withdrawal is framed by trust expectations, meaningful absences, and temporal persistence. This explains why confidence, doubt, repair, or betrayal are not isolated moments; they can become trajectories.

Yet Coherence_C is not truth. It does not prove that the trust relation is valid, repaired, justified, mature, or adequately captured. It gives narrative stability to the configuration, but it does not require full integration or self-binding. It is weaker than Σ and Ψ. The case can be coherent as a conceptual reading while remaining underdetermined as a concrete scene.

The calibration issue is the risk of narrative smoothing. A coherent story about trust may hide refusal, non-fit, cultural difference, legal boundary, or institutional constraint. Coherence_C is active, but it must remain answerable to discrimination tests and rival readings.

#### Responsibility_R

Responsibility_R is weak and conditional. It is derived from Ψ, Φ, Θ, and Ω: self-binding orientation toward asymmetry extended across time and recontextualization. In the trust case, responsibility becomes active only if asymmetry, temporal obligation, recontextualization, and self-binding commitment are concretely shown. Examples would include a promise, fiduciary role, institutional duty, owned accountability, or durable role commitment.

The abstract case does not supply those conditions. It shows possible vulnerability and possible obligation gradients, but vulnerability alone does not generate responsibility. It shows possible repair and recontextualization, but repair is not established. It shows possible self-binding, but no concrete commitment is specified. Therefore Responsibility_R is not assigned as a finding.

This weak status is not a failure. It is the correct result of dependency discipline. Responsibility without scene conditions becomes blame. Responsibility without capacity, information, alternatives, and temporal trajectory becomes moral pressure. Responsibility without Dignity-in-Practice becomes degradation. The case therefore preserves Responsibility_R as a calibration requirement.

#### Action_E

Action_E is under-specified. It is derived from Σ, Θ, and ∇: integrated realization of directedness across time. The trust case names possible relational actions—reliance, withdrawal, repair, refusal, response, non-response, protection, fidelity, explanation—but it does not supply a concrete enactment in which directedness is integrated across time.

This matters because PMS distinguishes action from mere behavior. Action_E is not any movement, utterance, silence, or event. It requires integrated directedness within temporal trajectory. Since Σ is weak and no concrete scene is supplied, Action_E cannot be assigned robustly.

The under-specification of Action_E also supports the later MIP non-assignment of ordinary A-score and M-score. Without concrete action, role capacity, predictability, alternatives, and integration attempts, maturity and shared responsibility cannot be responsibly banded. Non-assignment here is not low action. It is the refusal of false precision.

#### Dignity_in_Practice_D

Dignity_in_Practice_D is active as an application constraint. It is derived from Ψ, Χ, and Ω: self-bound reflective restraint and protection in asymmetrical relations. In the trust case, dignity-in-practice governs how vulnerability and asymmetry are handled. Trust failures, doubt, withdrawal, refusal, repair, and betrayal language must not become shaming, ranking, humiliation, diagnosis, or irreversible labeling.

The key distinction is that D is not ontological dignity. PMS does not measure worth. It does not rank beings. Dignity-in-Practice concerns enacted restraint under asymmetry. It asks whether the analysis preserves standing when exposure, responsibility, and trust are at stake.

D is active because trust is a dignity-risking field. Reliance can expose; betrayal language can humiliate; repair demands can coerce; responsibility language can degrade; protection language can immunize; public trust accusations can damage standing. But D remains a constraint, not a score. This is why MIP later activates the D-module only as a qualitative protection signal and not as a person-level D-reading.

#### SELF_FIXPOINT

SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only and not established. The formal chain Ψ ∘ Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ represents a derivational strategy for self-binding as structured self-relation. But the trust case does not establish a stable self-relation, subjectivity, identity, or institutional selfhood.

SELF_FIXPOINT may become relevant only in a higher-order extension: where trust is specified as promise, role ownership, accountability, durable commitment, institutional self-binding, or autobiographical continuity. The abstract case does not supply these. Therefore SELF_FIXPOINT remains a boundary marker: it shows what would be required for a stronger self-binding claim, and it prevents the analysis from attributing literal selfhood to abstract trust, roles, institutions, or claims.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core case treats drift-pattern language as risk language, not as finding language. This distinction is crucial. A drift pattern is a structurally derived distortion possibility; it is not a diagnosis, not a person type, not a moral verdict, not a clinical classification, and not a hidden accusation. In the trust case, the drift-pattern layer asks whether the conceptual regime of trust may invite recognizable distortions in awareness, enactment, recontextualization, integration, responsibility, and coherence. It does not assert that such distortions have occurred.

The first relevant pattern is **AD_A>>E**, excessive distance between awareness and enactment. In the analysis, this pattern remains possible but not entailed. Trust may drift toward excessive awareness where the relation becomes dominated by interpretation, suspicion, monitoring, vigilance, or evaluation while concrete action remains delayed, suppressed, or displaced. In a concrete scene, such a drift might appear where actors repeatedly analyze whether trust has failed without creating conditions for repair, boundary-setting, clarification, withdrawal, or renewed reliance. It could also appear where institutions continually assess trust legitimacy while leaving exposed roles without response or remedy. But none of this is established in the conceptual case. The abstract trust regime supplies the possibility of divergence between awareness and enactment; it does not supply the recurrence, effect trace, or action-blocking evidence required for a drift finding.

The core risk in AD_A>>E is that trust becomes over-interpreted. The relation may be saturated with awareness of possible betrayal, vulnerability, non-response, or asymmetry, but lack integrated enactment: no clear boundary, no answerable repair, no responsible refusal, no clarified frame, no accountable non-binding. The awareness axis and action axis are under-specified in relation to this drift. That is not a weak score. It means the case has not supplied the material needed to decide whether awareness has outrun enactment. Non-assignment protects the reading from inventing a pathology of suspicion.

The second relevant pattern is **AD_Sigma_low**, fragmented integration. This pattern concerns cases in which recontextualization outpaces integration. Trust, betrayal, repair, refusal, and non-binding can be repeatedly reframed without being practically coordinated. In a concrete case, one might see shifting explanations: the absence was delay, then misunderstanding, then boundary, then institutional constraint, then repair opportunity, then non-capture. Such recontextualization may be legitimate, but if it repeatedly prevents integration of effects, costs, responsibilities, and expectations, it can produce unstable coherence.

In the completed analysis, AD_Sigma_low is again possible but not entailed. The case does not show chronic recontextualization, unstable repair, or failed integration. PMS Core already marks Σ as weak, and that weak status must not be reinterpreted as fragmented integration. A weak operator is not a drift finding. It is simply a dependency-respecting refusal to overstate the case. In this respect, AD_Sigma_low is a warning against a specific misuse of Φ: not every frame-shift is repair, and not every new interpretation integrates what the prior frame failed to hold.

The case also contains a broader **coherence risk**. Trust fits PMS too well. It activates many operators smoothly: difference, impulse, frame, non-event, attractor, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and self-binding. The danger is that formal coverage may be mistaken for discriminative power. A reading can cover trust while still failing to distinguish trust from dependence, confidence, credibility, loyalty, faith, compliance, fear, or institutional reliability. It can also fail to distinguish betrayal from absence, repair from smoothing, refusal from immaturity, non-binding from avoidance, and doubt from failure. The PMS Core analysis treats this as a high coherence risk and mitigates it through discrimination tests, weak marking of Σ and Ψ, non-capture zones, rival readings, falsifiers, and refusal to treat PMS coherence as sufficient.

Under-discrimination is therefore one of the major risks of the case. If the reading cannot say when trust is not merely dependency, when doubt is not a failure of maturity, when silence is not betrayal, when repair is not recontextualization, and when refusal is adequate praxis, then the model has become too broad. Conversely, under-specification is not a failure. In this case, under-specification is a disciplined result. The analysis refuses to assign ordinary A-score and M-score; it refuses to classify IA; it refuses to establish false coordination; it refuses to diagnose drift. These refusals are not analytic gaps to be patched by assertion. They are part of the maturity of the reading.

The PMS drift-pattern status can therefore be summarized as follows: **AD_A>>E is a possible risk, not a finding; AD_Sigma_low is a possible risk, not a finding; coherence risk is high; under-discrimination must be actively resisted; non-assignment is not a low score; weak status is not failure; and drift-language must never become verdict-language.**

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant add-on in this case is **PMS-EDEN**. It is not an alternative PMS Core. It is not a new operator system. It does not redefine Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, or Ψ. It does not alter PMS dependencies. It does not convert EDEN constructs into PMS Base. It is an overlay/application profile applied after the completed Core reading. Its role is to make visible a specific family of questions that PMS Core alone can leave distributed across several operators: how real asymmetry is coordinated, how responsibility and action-power are distributed, how costs enter or fail to enter integration, how non-action and non-binding structure trust scenes, and how description must be firewalled from action authority.

PMS Core already identifies trust as a field of vulnerability, expectation, non-event, asymmetry, temporality, distance, possible repair, and non-capture. EDEN sharpens one question: **when trust involves real asymmetry, is that asymmetry truthfully coordinated, or is it falsely coordinated?** The overlay begins from Adult Reciprocity as a positive benchmark. Adult Reciprocity does not mean symmetry. It does not mean equal vulnerability, equal power, equal obligation, or equal cost. It means that real Ω remains visible, Dignity-in-Practice is preserved, responsibility follows real structuring effect, costs and effects enter integration, relevant positions remain bound to their effects where appropriate, and Χ keeps revision possible.

For this case, Adult Reciprocity is available as a benchmark but not established as a concrete finding. The case is abstract. There is no bounded scene in which to decide whether asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, reversible, integrated, or falsely coordinated. EDEN therefore functions as a test architecture rather than an adjudication. It asks what would need to be shown: real exposure, real dependency, real timing control, real explanation burden, real repair access, real cost distribution, real Action-Power, and real responsibility conditions. Without those, EDEN cannot affirm false coordination.

The primary EDEN mechanism, **false-coordinated real asymmetry**, is not supported as a finding. The overlay formula requires more than asymmetry. It requires real Ω plus a false coordination frame plus displaced responsibility plus blocked, weakened, simulated, avoided, or externalized Σ/Ψ. In this case, real Ω is structurally relevant, but its direction and magnitude are uncalibrated. No concrete false coordination frame is established. No displaced R is established. No blocked or externalized Σ/Ψ is established. Therefore the EDEN reading remains a benchmark and boundary test, not a conclusion.

EDEN also introduces **responsibility and Action-Power distribution** as overlay visibility. Trust positions may structure scenes through reliance, waiting, withdrawal, refusal, repair participation, response, non-response, protection, fidelity, explanation, withholding, and institutional role answerability. Yet these are action-power vectors, not accusations. EDEN’s responsibility principle requires real structuring effect under legibility, capacity, alternatives, Θ trajectory, and Ω cost/exposure conditions. Since the abstract case supplies none of the concrete data required for those conditions, EDEN cannot assign responsibility to any role. It can only state what later scene analysis must examine.

The overlay also clarifies **non-action and non-binding**. This is especially important for trust. Silence, delay, refusal, withdrawal, and non-binding can matter deeply in trust relations. But EDEN rejects the shortcut that non-action is praxis by default or that non-binding is avoidance by default. A silence may structure a scene, but it may also be legitimate boundary, incapacity, restraint, non-involvement, uncertainty, or non-capture. A refusal to bind may shift costs, but it may also preserve honest openness or prevent coercive repair. The EDEN overlay therefore treats non-action and non-binding as indeterminate test categories. To classify them, a later scene would need to show that the non-event is expected within □, alters options or costs through Λ, affects real Ω, and accumulates through Θ.

The EDEN layer also asks about **Immunization, Overexposure, and AEP**, but all three are not supported in this case. Immunization would require a role position with effect without full responsibility. Overexposure would require a role position carrying responsibility without full legibility or protection. AEP would require high exposure, low legitimate language, a critique-to-threat switch, and responsibility overload. The abstract case supplies none of these. Therefore these remain absent as findings. Their presence in the overlay is prophylactic: they prevent future concrete readings from ignoring such dynamics, but they do not license their assertion here.

Likewise, **PFO and Reciprocity Loss** are not claimed. The overlay explicitly avoids starting from PFO, Fall, False Comparison, sex category, identity claim, or ideological profile. It begins from Adult Reciprocity and real asymmetry. This matters because EDEN would become invalid if it converted abstract trust into a profile, person label, sex-truth claim, or regime diagnosis. The case remains non-diagnostic and non-person-ranking.

The EDEN reduced signatures and QC-EXT reduced signatures are treated as shorthand only. They do not replace full PMS dependencies. Χ still presupposes Φ, Θ, and □. Σ still presupposes Χ and Φ. Ψ still presupposes Σ, Θ, and Χ. EDEN constructs such as Adult Reciprocity, Action-Power, correctability, non-binding load, PFO, AEP, Immunization, Overexposure, and Reciprocity Loss are non-operator overlay handles. They organize PMS-relevant visibility; they do not become PMS operators, PMS axes, or PMS dependency nodes.

For this case, the EDEN overlay signature is therefore deliberately restrained. **Adult Reciprocity** is active as a positive benchmark, not as a finding that reciprocity is present. **Real Ω visibility** is active at the conceptual level, because trust involves possible vulnerability, exposure, timing, cost, repair access, and role asymmetry; it is not calibrated as a concrete asymmetry finding. **False-coordinated real asymmetry** remains not established, because the case lacks a bounded scene showing real Ω plus false coordination frame plus displaced responsibility plus blocked, weakened, simulated, avoided, or externalized Σ/Ψ. **Responsibility and Action-Power distribution** are active as mapping requirements, not assignments. **Σ/Ψ status** remains weak or indeterminate, matching the PMS Core finding that integration and self-binding cannot be claimed without concrete enactment. **Non-action and non-binding** are relevant test handles, but not findings of avoidance, evasion, or failure. **Immunization, Overexposure, AEP, PFO, and Reciprocity Loss** are not supported as case findings. Their value here is guardrail visibility: they indicate what a later concrete scene would have to prove, not what the abstract trust case already proves.

The overlay remains subordinate to PMS Base through several rules. It may depend on PMS, reference PMS operators, define case-application structure, organize PMS operators for overlay analysis, and support structural reasoning. It must not silently redefine meta-axioms, alter operator order, treat derived axes as primitives, override PMS guardrails, or claim PMS Base validation. It also cannot convert description into action. EDEN-MAP supplies no independent authority to confront, bind, diagnose, intervene, punish, prescribe, enforce, or transform structural coherence into obligation.

The MIP layer is the next add-on in the broad stack, though it is not a PMS operator overlay. MIP adds a maturity-in-practice mapping across the completed PMS Core and EDEN readings. It asks what awareness, coherence, responsibility, power-to-act, and dignity-in-practice require when the object is a conceptual trust regime. Its major result is disciplined non-assignment. Awareness is strong at model-level but incomplete at scene-evidence level. Coherence is strong as method discipline but not proof of capture. Responsibility remains a calibration requirement. Power-to-act is visible at the model level but under-specified at the role level. D is active only as a protection signal. Ordinary A-score and M-score are withheld. IA remains unclear. The D-module is activated only to protect language and standing, not to assess dignity.

The AHP layer is even more explicitly second-order. It analyzes the MIP output as an analysis artifact. It assigns a high Precision Heuristic, not a maturity score. It identifies attack points in wording, scope, IA thresholding, D-language extraction, rival comparison, score hygiene, and mapping vocabulary. It does not rescore A, M, IA, or D. It does not activate D. It does not upgrade transmission status. Its role is to harden the analysis against misreading, especially in a publication context.

### Layer interaction

The PMS Core reading remains the base structural grammar. It defines the operator sequence, dependencies, derived structures, drift-pattern risk status, non-capture rules, and claim boundaries. Everything else in the stack depends on this Core reading and must remain subordinate to it. PMS Core says what trust looks like as a praxeological configuration: difference, directedness, frame, non-event, pattern, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, possible integration, and conditional self-binding.

EDEN sharpens what PMS Core already makes visible but does not isolate as a distinct overlay problem. PMS Core names Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ, D, and responsibility dependencies. EDEN asks whether real Ω is truthfully coordinated: whether responsibility follows real structuring effect, whether costs enter Σ, whether Ψ-load is displaced, whether protection immunizes, whether responsibility degrades, whether non-action structures the scene, and whether non-binding preserves openness or shifts burden. EDEN therefore adds a vocabulary for Adult Reciprocity, Action-Power, cost distribution, non-action/non-binding boundaries, correctability, and the description-to-action firewall.

What remains PMS Core is the operator architecture. EDEN cannot redefine Ω as “oppression,” cannot redefine Χ as “adult distance,” cannot redefine Σ as “reciprocity,” and cannot redefine Ψ as “accountability.” Those may be overlay interpretations, but the PMS operators retain their PMS meanings. Similarly, EDEN cannot insert new dependencies, replace full operator chains with reduced signatures, or treat overlay constructs as derived PMS axes. The overlay must remain a reading of PMS-structured material, not a revision of the grammar.

MIP sharpens a different layer: how one should document maturity in practice without turning the analysis into person scoring. It takes the PMS and EDEN readings as fixed anchors and asks how A–C–R–P–D, IA, trajectory, RVR(t), D-module, and transmission discipline apply. Its strongest contribution is not a new claim about trust; it is a disciplined refusal of false precision. Because the case is conceptual, MIP withholds ordinary A-score and M-score assignment. It treats IA as unclear because transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility cannot be scene-tested. It activates D only as a protection signal. It marks transmission as internal structural working layer only.

AHP then sharpens the analysis of analysis. It does not add case content; it checks whether the MIP output is precise, attackable, and hardenable. Its high Precision Heuristic band indicates that the MIP analysis defines terms, states scope, gives thresholds, links evidence, preserves reversibility, and maintains language hygiene. This is not a score for the case, for trust, for maturity, or for any person; it is a second-order quality marker for the analysis artifact. Its attack points show where even a structurally careful output could be misread: “structurally transmittable” could be detached from “internal”; full-depth formatting could look more decisive than evidence; D-module activation could be extracted as dignity assessment; rival readings could be listed without comparison criteria; score withholding could be mistaken for emptiness.

The stack therefore moves through four kinds of discipline. PMS Core gives structural legibility. EDEN gives asymmetry and reciprocity visibility. MIP gives maturity-documentation discipline. AHP gives precision and misuse-hardening. Each layer adds visibility, but each layer also creates a possible overreach. PMS can become closure machine; EDEN can become profile or false-coordination detector by vibe; MIP can become maturity scoring; AHP can become meta-authority. The completed analyses prevent these overreaches by repeated non-interference rules.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. PMS Core preserves rival readings and non-capture. EDEN preserves counter-scenes and falsifiers. MIP preserves missing information, uncertain assumptions, weak points, and review requirements. AHP preserves attack points and hardening backlog. This layered non-closure is not indecision. It is the form of rigor appropriate to a conceptual trust case.

### Example 1 — The unanswered repair request

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** After “Scene and frame structure” or after “PMS Core structural reading,” where the article first explains Λ, Ω, Θ, Χ, and the difference between absence and betrayal.

A small research collective has an informal but stable practice: when a member publicly corrects another member’s work, they later write privately to clarify whether the correction was meant as a technical amendment or as a broader loss of confidence. This practice has developed over time because the group works with reputationally sensitive material. One member, after being corrected in a meeting, writes to the correcting member asking for clarification. No answer comes for two weeks. During that time, the unanswered member continues to participate but becomes more cautious, no longer shares early drafts, and asks a third colleague whether the silence should be understood as ordinary workload, disapproval, or refusal of repair.

The vignette is intentionally bounded. It contains a frame, a non-event, a temporal extension, and a possible asymmetry, but it does not contain enough material for a verdict. The example demonstrates why the PMS reading must distinguish absence from betrayal. The non-answer is structurally relevant only because there is a prior group practice that makes clarification expected. Without that frame, the same silence might be ordinary delay. With the frame, the silence becomes a Λ: a meaningful non-event within a trust relation. But Λ alone does not establish breach, intention, avoidance, or bad faith. The absence is legible; it is not yet adjudicated.

The PMS Core hooks are dense. Δ appears in the distinctions between correction and rejection, delay and refusal, reliance and withdrawal, clarification and repair. ∇ appears in the movement toward clarification and then toward cautious withdrawal. □ is the research collective’s trust frame, including its informal practice around reputational correction. Λ is the unanswered request. Α is only weakly available: the group has a recurring practice, but the specific silence is not yet a repeated pattern by the same actor. Ω appears as possible exposure: the corrected member bears reputational uncertainty and may have less control over how the correction is interpreted. Θ is active because two weeks of waiting change the relation; silence becomes temporally structured rather than momentary. Φ remains possible but not established: the silence might later be recontextualized as overload, misunderstanding, refusal, or negligence, but no recontextualization has yet occurred. Χ is central: the corrected member does not immediately accuse; they pause, ask a third party, and withhold public judgment. Σ is weak because no integrated repair or clarified boundary has yet occurred. Ψ is conditional only: the group practice may imply role responsibility, but no explicit self-binding or accountability has been enacted in the vignette.

The EDEN overlay helps clarify what PMS Core alone may leave distributed. The example involves possible real Ω: one member’s silence may affect another member’s reputational exposure, draft-sharing behavior, and sense of repair access. EDEN asks whether this asymmetry is being truthfully coordinated. But the example does not establish false coordination. It does not show displaced responsibility, blocked Σ/Ψ, Immunization, Overexposure, AEP, PFO, or Reciprocity Loss. The correcting member may have been overloaded, may not have understood the request as repair-relevant, may have assumed the meeting correction was already clear, or may be constrained by another institutional process. EDEN therefore functions as a test: what costs are being carried, who has Action-Power to clarify, what would count as responsible non-response, and how could the relation remain correctable without coercing an answer?

MIP remains restrained. No A-score or M-score is assigned. The vignette does not supply enough information about knowledge, control, predictability, alternatives, or serious integration attempts. IA is also not decided. There may be an inadult asymmetry if the correcting member has disproportionate control over reputational meaning while remaining unavailable for clarification, but the example does not establish whether that asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, or reversible. The D-module would be active only as protection: the corrected member should not be shamed for needing clarification, and the correcting member should not be degraded by premature blame. AHP would likely flag the language risk: “unanswered repair request” may already sound accusatory unless the article keeps the non-verdict boundary visible.

Several claims are forbidden. The example does not allow the article to say that the correcting member betrayed trust, avoided responsibility, manipulated silence, or showed immaturity. It does not allow the article to say that the corrected member is overly sensitive, dependent, mistrustful, or immature. It does not establish legal duty, clinical meaning, moral guilt, empirical pattern, or institutional failure. It also does not validate PMS, EDEN, MIP, or AHP as complete models of trust.

The non-capture note is important. The example may involve lived shame, academic hierarchy, personality history, cultural communication norms, workload stress, fear of conflict, or field-specific reputational stakes. PMS can mark structure; EDEN can test asymmetry; MIP can preserve score hygiene; AHP can harden the analysis language. None of these fully captures how it feels to wait for repair after public correction.

The article takeaway is that a trust-relevant non-event can be structurally serious without being a verdict. The example shows why Λ, Ω, Θ, and Χ must be held together: absence matters only in frame; asymmetry must be calibrated; time changes the relation; and distance prevents the analysis from converting silence into betrayal.

### Example 2 — The protective refusal to promise

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** After “Add-on / overlay reading” or “Layer interaction,” where the article explains non-action, non-binding, Adult Reciprocity, and why EDEN cannot treat non-binding as avoidance by default.

Two colleagues are invited to co-author a public statement after a controversial institutional decision. One colleague wants the statement released quickly and asks the other to commit by the end of the day. The second colleague refuses to promise immediate co-authorship. They say that the issue matters, that they may still contribute, but that they need to read the institutional documents, speak with two affected people, and understand whether a public statement would help or expose those people further. The first colleague experiences this refusal as a failure of trust: in their view, the urgency of the situation requires solidarity. The second colleague experiences the request for immediate commitment as a pressure to bind themselves before they understand the costs.

This vignette demonstrates why non-binding must not be treated as avoidance by default. The second colleague’s refusal to promise is a non-action in one respect: they do not immediately join the statement. It is also an action in another respect: they define conditions under which participation might become responsible. The example is therefore ideal for the EDEN overlay, because it distinguishes trust, loyalty, solidarity, protection, publicness, and responsibility without collapsing them.

The PMS Core hooks begin with Δ: commitment versus non-commitment, solidarity versus premature binding, refusal versus abandonment, protection versus delay, public statement versus private inquiry. ∇ appears in the first colleague’s movement toward urgent collective action and the second colleague’s movement toward guarded inquiry. □ is the frame of public institutional response, not merely personal loyalty. Λ appears as the missing immediate promise. For the first colleague, the non-promise is meaningful because commitment was expected by the end of the day. Yet Λ must be calibrated: the expected event may not have been legitimate, shared, or sufficiently framed. Α is weak unless the vignette becomes a repeated pattern of refusal or pressure. Ω is multidimensional. The first colleague may experience exposure because public silence could be read as complicity. The second may experience exposure because premature signature could harm affected people or bind them to claims they cannot yet verify. Θ is active because urgency compresses time, but responsible inquiry also requires time. Φ remains possible: the refusal may be recontextualized as care rather than disloyalty, or as evasion rather than care, depending on later facts. Χ is central because the second colleague suspends immediate action. Σ is not yet established: no integrated public response has been formed. Ψ is conditional: a public signature would be a self-binding act, but it has not yet occurred.

EDEN sharpens the main issue: Adult Reciprocity does not require immediate sameness of commitment. It requires truthful coordination of real Ω. The first colleague may carry urgency, reputational risk, and responsibility toward affected people. The second may carry epistemic responsibility, publicness risk, and concern about overexposing those same people. A false-coordination reading would require more evidence: for instance, that one party demands solidarity while externalizing all verification costs, or that the other claims protection while avoiding all responsibility. The vignette does not establish either. It shows a field in which Action-Power and cost distribution must be mapped carefully.

The EDEN non-action and non-binding boundaries are especially relevant. The second colleague’s non-binding may be legitimate openness. It preserves the possibility of responsible future commitment. It may also shift waiting cost onto the first colleague, but that cost shift is not automatically blameworthy. The question is whether the refusal remains legible, time-bounded, contestable, and tied to real inquiry, or whether it becomes indefinite non-commitment that externalizes risk. Similarly, the first colleague’s urgency may be responsible, but it may also pressure the other into public self-binding before adequate awareness. EDEN therefore does not decide; it makes the cost and responsibility structure visible.

MIP would again withhold ordinary scores. A-score cannot be assigned because the vignette does not establish what each colleague actually knows or can know. M-score cannot be assigned because predictability, alternatives, power, and serious integration attempts remain incomplete. IA is unclear. There may be asymmetry in urgency, publicness, access to affected perspectives, or reputational risk, but whether it is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible is not yet known. The D-module is relevant only as a guardrail: the first colleague must not be shamed as coercive merely for wanting solidarity, and the second must not be shamed as avoidant merely for refusing immediate commitment. AHP would likely identify a terminology risk around “protective refusal,” because the phrase may already favor the second colleague unless counter-readings remain active.

Forbidden claims are numerous. The article must not say that the first colleague is coercive, morally superior, reckless, or manipulative. It must not say that the second colleague is mature, evasive, cowardly, loyal, disloyal, or ethically superior. It must not diagnose urgency as anxiety or refusal as avoidance. It must not declare the institutional decision legally wrong or the statement politically necessary. It must not treat refusal as dignity-preserving by default or urgency as immature by default.

The non-capture note concerns political and ethical context. A real case might involve institutional harm, legal exposure, affected communities, professional risk, or historical patterns of silence. PMS and EDEN can structure the trust relation, but they cannot replace political judgment, legal analysis, testimony from affected people, or ethical deliberation about public speech.

The article takeaway is that trust sometimes requires refusing premature self-binding. Non-binding may damage trust, preserve trust, clarify trust, or suspend trust; the model cannot decide that by form alone. The example demonstrates why EDEN’s Adult Reciprocity benchmark is stronger than a simple demand for mutual commitment: mature coordination may require keeping real asymmetry, cost, publicness, and correctability visible before asking for a promise.

### Example 3 — The repair meeting that becomes a performance of closure

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** After “PMS drift-pattern status” or before “Misuse boundary,” where the article discusses AD_Sigma_low, false integration, repair versus recontextualization, and the danger of closure-by-authority.

A small organization schedules a repair meeting after several members report that their concerns about workload distribution have been ignored. The facilitator opens by saying that the purpose is “to rebuild trust and move forward.” Each person is invited to name one feeling and one constructive request. The meeting is calm. Several members say they are tired and want clearer timelines. One member says the issue is not only scheduling but the repeated pattern that some people must absorb last-minute changes while others retain control over deadlines. The facilitator acknowledges this but quickly reframes it as a “communication gap” and asks the group to agree on a new check-in rhythm. The meeting ends with a written note stating that trust has been restored through shared understanding.

This vignette demonstrates the difference between repair, recontextualization, and integration. It is concrete enough to show a bounded scene, but still bounded enough to avoid verdict. The organization may indeed have improved something: a new check-in rhythm could reduce confusion. But the example also shows how repair language can prematurely close a trust problem by converting asymmetry into communication failure and treating calm agreement as integration.

The PMS Core hooks are clear. Δ distinguishes workload concern from communication gap, repair from closure, participation from voice, shared understanding from actual redistribution. ∇ appears in the organization’s movement toward restoration and in the affected members’ movement toward recognition of burden. □ is the organizational repair meeting. Λ appears in the prior ignored concerns and possibly in the meeting’s failure to address deadline control. Α is more active than in the previous examples because repeated workload absorption is alleged; however, it remains a reported pattern, not a proven finding. Ω is central: some members may carry last-minute changes while others retain timing and deadline control. Θ is active because the issue is repeated over time, not a single scheduling mistake. Φ is explicit: the facilitator recontextualizes the issue as a communication gap. Χ is ambiguous: the meeting may create reflective distance, but it may also suppress conflict by steering participants toward constructive closure. Σ is weak or contested: a check-in rhythm may integrate some scheduling issues, but it may fail to integrate the asymmetry of deadline control and burden distribution. Ψ is conditional: if the organization binds itself to new procedures and accountability, Ψ may become relevant; a meeting note alone does not establish self-binding.

This example is especially useful for PMS drift-pattern discussion. It may suggest AD_Sigma_low as a risk: frequent or rapid recontextualization may outpace integration. The facilitator’s reframing may be legitimate if the problem is primarily communicative, but if the deeper issue is repeated asymmetrical burden, then recontextualization smooths rather than integrates. The example may also suggest AD_A>>E if the organization becomes highly articulate about trust restoration while failing to enact changes in workload control. But both remain risks, not findings. A single vignette does not establish chronic drift.

EDEN adds important visibility. The repair meeting may falsely coordinate real Ω if real workload asymmetry is redescribed as mutual communication gap, responsibility is diffused into shared tone, and Σ/Ψ are simulated through a meeting note without changing burden or deadline control. Yet EDEN cannot claim that this has occurred without further evidence. The facilitator may have had limited authority; the check-in rhythm may be a first step; the deadline-control issue may be more complex; the member’s report may need corroboration; the organization may later revise responsibilities. EDEN therefore frames the question: does the repair process keep real asymmetry visible, or does it create the appearance of Adult Reciprocity while leaving costs externalized?

MIP would treat this example as closer to a possible scene packet, but still not enough for scoring. A-score might be partially discussable at the organizational-practice level because several relevant distinctions are visible, but no A-band should be assigned in the article. M-score would require knowledge, power/control, predictability, alternatives, and serious integration attempts for specific roles. IA would require testing whether the asymmetry in deadline control is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. The D-module is important: the repair meeting must not humiliate those who raise burden issues, but it must also not degrade the facilitator or deadline-holding members by converting structural critique into person blame. AHP would likely flag the phrase “trust has been restored” as a high-risk closure phrase: it may overstate what the process has shown.

Forbidden claims include saying that the facilitator manipulated the group, that the organization is abusive, that trust was falsely restored, that the burdened member is correct in all respects, or that deadline-holding members are morally deficient. The article must not infer motive from recontextualization. It must not diagnose organizational pathology. It must not make HR, legal, forensic, or clinical conclusions. It must not treat a calm meeting as proof of repair or an unresolved burden claim as proof of betrayal.

The non-capture note matters because organizational repair has affective, political, managerial, legal, and material dimensions. PMS can distinguish recontextualization from integration; EDEN can test cost distribution; MIP can preserve score hygiene; AHP can warn against closure wording. But a real case would require workload data, role descriptions, authority structures, prior meeting records, affected perspectives, and possibly labor or governance analysis.

The article takeaway is that repair language is one of the highest-risk zones for trust analysis. A process may appear coherent, calm, and forward-looking while leaving the decisive asymmetry unresolved. Conversely, a process may be partial without being false. The example shows why the article must keep Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ, Ω, and EDEN’s Adult Reciprocity benchmark in tension: repair is not the same as recontextualization, integration is not the same as closure, and restored trust cannot be declared by meeting form alone.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The completed YAML analyses preserve rival readings as epistemically open. Rival readings are not defects in PMS, EDEN, or MIP. They identify what alternative grammars may preserve that the current stack does not fully capture.

A **phenomenological reading** may preserve lived openness, felt vulnerability, warmth, fear, hope, grief, shame, relief, hesitation, and the affective texture of trust. PMS can model framed differentiation, temporality, asymmetry, and distance; it does not claim to capture the lived feel of trusting or being betrayed. EDEN can name cost and exposure; it does not exhaust how exposure is experienced. MIP can protect dignity language; it does not describe the interior phenomenology of wounded trust. A phenomenological rival therefore preserves first-person or lived dimensions that the operator grammar intentionally brackets.

A **game-theoretic or decision-theoretic reading** may preserve trust as risk-sensitive expectation under uncertainty. It may analyze incentives, probability, payoff, signaling, cooperation, reputation, and strategic dependence. PMS can distinguish trust from prediction but does not replace formal decision theory. EDEN can ask whether asymmetry is truthfully coordinated, but it does not calculate risk. MIP can document maturity conditions, but it does not model strategic equilibria. This rival reading may be stronger where trust is primarily a problem of uncertainty, risk, and repeated interaction.

A **moral-philosophical reading** may preserve trust as fidelity, promise, virtue, betrayal, recognition, respect, or obligation. PMS treats responsibility structurally rather than moralistically; EDEN prevents responsibility from becoming degradation; MIP separates responsibility from guilt. But moral philosophy may better articulate duties, virtues, wrongs, forgiveness, culpability, and moral repair. The PMS stack does not deny this. It refuses to derive moral verdicts from structural mapping alone.

A **legal-institutional reading** may preserve fiduciary duty, reliance, breach, evidence, liability, procedural legitimacy, institutional obligation, and standards of proof. PMS can frame trust institutionally, and EDEN can make responsibility distribution visible, but neither layer decides legal breach. MIP similarly refuses HR, forensic, policy, clinical, or legal use. Where the case concerns law, legal grammar is not optional; it is a rival or governing frame with its own thresholds.

A **sociological reading** may preserve trust as social capital, norm stabilization, institutional legitimacy, role expectation, collective confidence, or system reliability. PMS can model attractors, frames, institutions, and temporal patterns, but sociology may provide empirical accounts of how trust forms and decays in social systems. EDEN’s cost and asymmetry language may help, but it does not replace sociological explanation.

A **linguistic-pragmatic reading** may preserve trust as uptake of testimony, sincerity, communicative reliability, promise, speech act, silence, implicature, or recognition. PMS uses frame and non-event, but it does not fully analyze language games. EDEN can ask whether non-response structures a scene, but it cannot decide communicative meaning without pragmatic context. MIP can require role and publicness checks, but it cannot infer speech meaning from structure alone.

An **epistemological reading** may preserve trust as dependence on testimony, authority, warrant, credibility, epistemic injustice, or reliability of knowledge transmission. PMS can distinguish credibility from trust, but it does not replace epistemology. EDEN may expose asymmetry in whose speech counts; MIP may protect against dignity and publicness harms; but epistemic warrant and testimonial justice require their own criteria.

An **anti-capture reading** preserves the possibility that trust resists formalization. Some trust relations may be culturally, religiously, historically, affectively, or ethically saturated in ways that no operator grammar captures without residue. Some refusals of trust may be principled non-participation in the analytic frame itself. In such cases, non-fit must not be redescribed as drift, immaturity, failed integration, or calibration deficit. PMS explicitly preserves non-capture; EDEN preserves counter-scenes; MIP preserves weak points and missing perspectives; AHP preserves attack-surface review.

The three examples reinforce the need for rival readings rather than eliminating it. The unanswered repair request may require phenomenology of shame, sociology of academic hierarchy, or pragmatic analysis of silence. The protective refusal to promise may require political ethics, legal awareness, or testimony from affected people. The repair meeting may require organizational sociology, labor analysis, or governance procedure. The PMS stack helps structure the questions, but it does not absorb these other grammars.

These rival readings are not concessions that weaken the article. They are part of its method. A formal model that cannot name what it may not capture is unsafe. A trust analysis that cannot allow rival grammars will tend toward closure by authority. The completed stack resists that tendency.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would be weakened if trust cannot be distinguished from adjacent phenomena. If a concrete scene shows only prediction without vulnerability, dependence without reliance, compliance without exposure, credibility without trust, loyalty without expectation, or institutional routine without meaningful relation, then the PMS trust reading loses force. The first falsifier is therefore discriminative: if Δ cannot hold, the chain cannot begin.

The PMS reading would also be weakened if no framed expectation exists. Λ requires an expected occurrence within □. If there is no frame in which a response, protection, fidelity, recognition, repair, or institutional action was expected, then absence cannot carry the meaning assigned to it. Without a framed expectation, non-event language becomes projection.

The reading would be weakened if no recurrence, stabilization, role-script, or path dependence can be shown where Α is invoked. Pattern claims require pattern evidence. A single event cannot automatically become a trust regime. Conversely, if the case remains a single abstract conceptual field, Α must remain weak or structural rather than scene-proven.

Ω claims would be weakened if no directional imbalance of exposure, capacity, power, role, obligation, information, timing, or protection can be identified. If a later scene shows no consequential asymmetry, EDEN’s real Ω mapping also weakens. If asymmetry is present but transparent, justified, time-bound, reversible, and non-degrading, then it may not support any failure reading.

Θ claims would be weakened if temporal extension is not required. Some trust-relevant events may be immediate or first-contact. Others may require long histories. If no delay, memory, persistence, anticipation, accumulation, or repair trajectory is at stake, then temporal claims must be narrowed.

Φ claims would be weakened if no actual source-frame and target-frame shift can be specified. Recontextualization cannot be inferred merely because a new explanation is offered. It must transform how the prior structure is understood. If every rupture is automatically reinterpreted as repair or learning, the reading becomes over-assimilative.

Χ claims would be weakened if what is called distance is actually avoidance, denial, punishment, empty neutrality, incapacity, or coerced silence. But the PMS application would also fail if Χ is suspended in order to force verdict. Distance must be discriminated, not bypassed.

Σ claims would be weakened if coherence is merely formal. If the analysis harmonizes trust, doubt, refusal, repair, betrayal, and institutional reliability without showing practical coordination, Σ is overclaimed. Since the current case lacks concrete enactment, Σ remains weak.

Ψ claims would be weakened if no promise, commitment, accountability, role ownership, institutional self-binding, or durable responsibility can be shown. If Ψ is applied without demonstrable Σ, Θ, and Χ, the self-binding reading is under-specified. SELF_FIXPOINT remains analytic-only unless those conditions are supplied.

The EDEN reading would be weakened by several falsifiers. If no consequential real Ω exists, EDEN narrows to a general benchmark. If non-action or non-binding is legitimate openness, boundary, incapacity, restraint, or non-involvement, it cannot be treated as avoidance or responsibility displacement. If responsibility remains proportionate and not displaced, false coordination weakens. If Σ/Ψ remain open rather than blocked, simulated, avoided, or externalized, EDEN cannot claim false coordination. If costs and effects are jointly nameable without degradation, Immunization or Overexposure weakens. If a rival framework explains decisive features better, EDEN must narrow or suspend.

The MIP reading would be weakened if it attempted ordinary scoring without evidence. A-score and M-score require a bounded scene, role position, time window, action context, information, power/control, predictability, alternatives, and integration attempts. Since these are absent, non-assignment is the correct result. If a later scene supplies them, MIP can be rerun; until then, score withholding is not optional.

The IA-box would require concrete data for transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. To move from unclear to partial or decidable, a later scene would need to show who knew what, who could know what, what protected good or purpose justified asymmetry, what review points existed, what exit or return options were real, and how affected roles could contest the frame. Without such data, IA remains unclear.

The examples do not change these conditions. The unanswered repair request would become stronger only if the group practice, expectation, timing, knowledge, response capacity, and repair access were documented. The protective refusal to promise would become stronger only if the costs, publicness, affected perspectives, institutional facts, and alternatives were specified. The repair meeting would become stronger only if workload data, authority structure, prior reports, meeting effects, and later accountability traces were available. In all three cases, the examples demonstrate what to ask, not what to decide.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled across the stack. Empirical validation is absent. Comparative validation is absent. Applied validation is absent. Computational validation is not required for the conceptual projection, but would be required if the YAML were used in an architecture or simulation. Legal and clinical validation are explicitly not in scope. Any operational use would require architecture-specific calibration, implementation constraints, empirical testing where applicable, external evaluation, context-sensitive interpretation, public accountability, scene evidence, observable indicators, comparison with rival theories, affected-role contestation, and independent application authority where action is contemplated.

### Misuse boundary

The misuse boundary is substantial because trust language is easily weaponized. A structural analysis of trust can be misused to diagnose persons, rank maturity, assign guilt, demand repair, shame refusal, expose vulnerability, or authorize intervention. The completed stack blocks these uses at every layer.

There is **no diagnosis**. The analysis does not infer mental states, traits, pathology, trauma, attachment style, personality, avoidance, narcissism, immaturity, manipulativeness, paranoia, or trustworthiness. It does not treat doubt, withdrawal, refusal, non-response, silence, rupture, repair-seeking, or non-binding as symptoms.

There is **no person ranking**. No person, role, group, institution, sex, or identity category is ranked by maturity, dignity, coherence, responsibility, trust capacity, integration, or self-binding. PMS analyzes structures of praxis, not persons as ontological entities. MIP scores are withheld; D is not a score; AHP attack points describe the analysis artifact, not people.

There is **no legal or forensic conclusion**. The case does not determine breach, duty, evidence, liability, culpability, fiduciary failure, procedural violation, institutional wrongdoing, or sanction. Legal language may be a rival reading, but it is not activated as verdict.

There is **no metaphysical proof**. The analysis does not establish the essence of trust, the nature of subjectivity, the ontological worth of persons, or the metaphysical status of institutions. Dignity-in-Practice is a praxeological constraint, not a ranking of being.

There is **no empirical proof**. The case is not based on observation, data, interviews, experiments, legal records, organizational documents, or behavioral traces. It is a conceptual projection. Empirical adequacy remains untested.

There is **no implementation validation**. YAML form does not implement praxis, maturity, trust, responsibility, dignity, or agency. Machine-readability is not operational reliability.

There is **no PMS Base validation**. The case does not prove PMS complete, final, uniquely ordered, or superior to rival grammars. It uses PMS under PMS discipline.

There is **no add-on-as-operator**. EDEN constructs are overlay handles. MIP categories are maturity documentation fields. AHP precision is second-order artifact review. None of them becomes a PMS operator or dependency.

There is **no drift-as-verdict**. AD_A>>E and AD_Sigma_low are possible risks, not findings. Immunization, Overexposure, AEP, PFO, and Reciprocity Loss are not claimed. A weak operator status is not a defect; under-specification is not immaturity; non-assignment is not low maturity.

There is **no closure-by-authority**. The fact that the analysis is formally coherent, layered, and YAML-complete does not grant authority to decide a concrete trust relation. It cannot be used to confront, bind, prescribe, punish, enforce, shame, expose, or demand repair. EDEN’s description-to-action firewall and MIP’s transmission discipline both preserve this boundary. The AHP layer further warns that “structurally transmittable” must remain tied to “internal structural working layer only,” not external applicability.

The examples are also governed by this misuse boundary. The unanswered repair request cannot be turned into proof of betrayal. The protective refusal to promise cannot be turned into proof of maturity or avoidance. The repair meeting cannot be turned into proof of organizational manipulation or restored trust. The examples make the architecture more readable; they do not become evidence.

The governing misuse principle is this: **the more precise the model language becomes, the more carefully it must refuse unauthorized use.** Precision without boundary becomes coercion. Coherence without reversibility becomes closure. Dignity language without humility becomes shame. Responsibility language without scene conditions becomes blame. Trust analysis without non-capture becomes tribunal.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of this case is a disciplined structural rendering of trust as a relational regime without closure by verdict. PMS Core shows why trust is analyzable through difference, impulse, frame, non-event, attractor, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and conditional self-binding. But the same Core reading also shows why these operators must be calibrated: absence is not betrayal; asymmetry is not guilt; recontextualization is not repair; coherence is not proof; integration is not guaranteed; self-binding is not established by trust language alone.

The EDEN overlay adds a second layer of precision by asking how real asymmetry is coordinated. It makes visible the difference between asymmetry and failure, between responsibility and degradation, between non-binding and avoidance, between repair and simulated integration, between Adult Reciprocity and symmetry. Yet EDEN does not establish false-coordinated real asymmetry in this case. The case remains conceptual. There is no scene packet sufficient to prove displaced responsibility, blocked Σ/Ψ, Immunization, Overexposure, AEP, PFO, or Reciprocity Loss.

The MIP layer adds maturity-in-practice discipline by refusing premature scoring. Awareness and coherence are visible at the level of model discipline; responsibility and power-to-act remain calibration requirements; Dignity-in-Practice remains a protection signal; A-score and M-score are not assigned; IA remains unclear. This is not analytic emptiness. It is the correct form of analysis where concrete scene evidence is absent.

The AHP layer then hardens the analysis as an artifact. It does not make the case more true; it makes the presentation more accountable. It identifies how the article could be misread: as externally transmittable, as more decisive than its evidence, as a dignity assessment, as a score, as an IA verdict, or as a closure claim. Its contribution is methodological restraint under publication pressure.

The three examples contribute readability and transferability. The unanswered repair request shows how a non-event can become structurally serious without becoming betrayal. The protective refusal to promise shows how non-binding can preserve responsibility rather than evade it, while still requiring cost and asymmetry analysis. The repair meeting shows how recontextualization and calm procedure can appear to restore trust without necessarily integrating the underlying asymmetry. Each example demonstrates the architecture; none replaces the analysis, and none functions as evidence for a general claim.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is claimed. The case does not claim an essence of trust. It does not claim empirical validation. It does not diagnose persons. It does not assign maturity. It does not decide responsibility. It does not determine legal breach. It does not prove PMS, EDEN, MIP, or AHP. It does not turn drift risks into findings. It does not treat non-capture as failure. It does not turn refusal, rupture, silence, disagreement, or non-fit into immaturity by default.

The boundary discipline is the case’s central achievement. Trust is one of the phenomena most likely to make analysis hungry for closure. The stack resists that hunger by preserving dependency, reversibility, dignity, rival readings, score hygiene, and description-to-action separation. It allows trust to become structurally legible while refusing to make legibility into authority.

The final methodological takeaway is therefore not that PMS “solves” trust. It is that a layered praxeological grammar can render trust with enough precision to be analytically useful and enough restraint to remain non-coercive. Trust, in this reading, is not reduced to a formula. It is held open as a structurally intelligible but non-exhausted relational regime: one in which reliance, vulnerability, absence, asymmetry, temporality, repair, refusal, and self-binding may matter deeply, but only under conditions that the analysis itself must not pretend to satisfy without a scene.
