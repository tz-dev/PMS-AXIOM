### Why this case matters

“Asymmetry lock-in” matters because it names a closure-demand that appears whenever a morally difficult situation is not merely difficult in content, but difficult because positions within it do not share the same exposure, dependency, responsibility, or exit-capacity over time. The philosophical pressure is not simply: “What should be done?” Nor is it only: “Who is responsible?” The deeper structural pressure is: how should one understand situations in which the possibility of revision, interruption, or leaving is itself distributed unequally and may become harder to access as time passes?

This case belongs in AXIOM because it makes visible one of the central dangers of closure-demand thinking: a scene can look formally open while being practically hardening. A role may appear able to leave, object, pause, reframe, or withdraw, while the costs of doing so are not equally distributed. The case therefore tests whether the PMS stack can hold a structurally difficult moral field without converting it into a verdict, diagnosis, policy command, metaphysical thesis, or person-ranking. It also tests whether PMS can preserve non-capture when a case strongly fits its vocabulary. “Asymmetry,” “temporality,” “lock-in,” “distance,” “integration,” and “responsibility” are all PMS-legible terms. Precisely because the fit is strong, the case carries a high risk of becoming too smooth.

The philosophical closure-demand is therefore double. On one side, there is a demand to name the difficulty: asymmetry can harden; exit can become unequal; revision can become costly; responsibility can be distributed unevenly; dependency can alter what counts as free response. On the other side, there is a demand not to over-close: not every asymmetry is domination, not every non-exit is coercion, not every rupture is immaturity, not every unresolved responsibility structure is drift, and not every coherent PMS mapping is an adequate account of the moral situation.

The case is structurally difficult because it sits at the junction of several problems that are often conflated. There is a difference between formal exit and real exit. There is a difference between temporary asymmetry and lock-in. There is a difference between responsibility-pressure and responsibility-as-self-binding. There is a difference between recontextualization that clarifies and recontextualization that merely absorbs resistance. There is a difference between integration and forced coherence. There is also a difference between morally important asymmetry and an established legal, clinical, empirical, or diagnostic finding.

For that reason, this article does not solve the case in the ordinary sense. It does not propose a metaphysical theory of difficult moral situations. It does not empirically establish that any given asymmetry exists. It does not diagnose persons or institutions. It does not determine legal coercion, consent failure, negligence, liability, abuse, or culpability. It does not prescribe enforcement, repair, or governance action. It does not validate PMS by showing that PMS “captures” the case. Its purpose is more disciplined: to render the completed YAML analyses as a chapter-ready structural article showing what each layer can make visible, what each layer must not claim, and where non-capture remains constitutive rather than defective.

This matters especially because a case like “Asymmetry lock-in” can attract premature moral certainty. Once the phrase is available, it can be used as if it already proves entrapment, wrongdoing, coercion, domination, immaturity, failed maturity, or responsibility failure. PMS, CONFLICT, MIP, and AHP all resist that move in different ways. PMS makes the operator chain visible but keeps claim boundaries. CONFLICT sharpens how incompatibility can stabilize under asymmetric cost but remains an overlay. MIP checks whether the analysis can responsibly discuss maturity-in-practice without person-scoring. AHP checks whether the analysis artefact itself has become too persuasive, too complete-looking, or too easy to misuse.

The result is not closure-by-authority. The result is a meta-artefact that shows why the case is legible and why legibility must not be confused with finality. As a meta-artefact, the analysis cannot fully “solve” the case. PMS does not have that ambition. The point is not to replace unresolved moral difficulty with a final structural answer, but to show where closure is requested, where it is not warranted, and where Λ-residue must remain visible.

### Claim status and scope discipline

The case is typed as a philosophical and structural-praxeological case-type. It is a conceptual domain projection, not an empirical case, not a concrete scene, and not a tribunal-style analysis. The claim being made is not: “this asymmetry occurred.” It is not: “these actors were trapped.” It is not: “one party failed maturely.” The core claim is narrower:

Certain morally difficult situations may be structurally modeled as differentiated positions and pressures within a frame, where expected revision, interruption, recognition, or exit may fail or become delayed; repeated interactions may stabilize; asymmetries may become temporally extended; and the resulting configuration may become difficult to revise, interrupt, or leave.

This is a structural possibility claim, not an evidential conclusion. The YAML analyses consistently mark the case as not a concrete scene. That status is decisive. Because no actors, roles, sequence, institution, event, decision, communication, harm, or observed pattern is supplied, the analysis cannot move from structural projection to concrete attribution. It can identify what would matter; it cannot say that those things happened.

The empirical boundary is therefore closed. The analysis does not establish observable facts. It does not prove that exit was blocked, that dependency existed, that responsibility was misassigned, that delay occurred, that revision failed, or that any role had more or less power than another. It can say that such differences would matter if present; it cannot say they are present.

The metaphysical boundary is equally strict. The case does not claim anything about consciousness, inner experience, essence, moral worth, ultimate freedom, ontological dignity, or final moral reality. PMS does not turn asymmetry into a metaphysical theory of persons. MIP does not turn Dignity-in-Practice into an ontological ranking. AHP does not strengthen the analysis into a proof of capture.

The diagnostic boundary is non-negotiable. No actor, group, role-holder, institution, personality structure, clinical pattern, maturity deficit, avoidance pattern, pathology, or psychological state is diagnosed. The case cannot say that someone is immature, coercive, dependent, avoidant, manipulative, weak, rigid, pathological, or morally defective. Even drift patterns remain conditional structural risks, not person-types.

The legal and forensic boundary is also closed. Nothing in the case establishes coercion, liability, consent failure, negligence, abuse, culpability, duty breach, or legal responsibility. Some rival readings may require legal analysis, especially if the case later becomes concrete and involves consent, rights, liability, dependency, or duty. But this analysis does not supply that framework and does not smuggle legal conclusions through structural language.

The implementation boundary is closed as well. This is not an algorithm, protocol, decision tool, governance workflow, intervention plan, or institutional procedure. The YAML format makes the analysis machine-readable, but machine readability is not implementation. The case cannot be deployed as a scoring tool, risk engine, HR instrument, compliance procedure, or automated decision basis.

The PMS Base validation boundary is central. The case does not validate PMS. A strong fit between a case and PMS vocabulary is analytically useful, but it does not prove PMS completeness, correctness, superiority, or exhaustiveness. Indeed, the MIP-AHP analysis explicitly treats “too smooth” fit as a potential attack surface. The case can demonstrate disciplined application; it cannot become evidence that PMS as a base model is true.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. To move beyond conceptual analysis, one would need a concrete scene, role map, timeline, information basis, evidence of recurrence, documented asymmetries, exit-cost analysis, review points, affected-side perspective, and comparison with rival formalisms. None of that is supplied. The correct status is therefore: reversible, bounded, non-diagnostic structural projection.

### Scene and frame structure

The scene is not a concrete praxis scene. This has several consequences. First, the analysis cannot identify actual agents. Second, it cannot determine which role occupies which asymmetry. Third, it cannot infer motive, awareness, intention, responsibility, or failure. Fourth, it cannot determine whether asymmetry is harmful, protective, tragic, necessary, temporary, institutional, relational, coercive, or ambiguous. Fifth, it cannot activate legal, clinical, empirical, forensic, or governance conclusions.

The conceptual scene is instead a frame for analyzing a family of possible situations. The frame is the moral-philosophical question of how difficult situations should be understood when asymmetries of position, exposure, dependency, responsibility, or available exit may become hard to revise, interrupt, or leave. The frame includes structural distinctions between positions, pressures toward revision or exit, possible absences of interruption or recognition, stabilization over time, asymmetry, recontextualization, distance, integration, and self-binding as a possible but unestablished responsibility structure.

Inside the frame are the structural features named by the case material: position, exposure, dependency, responsibility, available exit, difficulty of revision, difficulty of interruption, difficulty of leaving, and temporal hardening. Also inside the frame are the analytic constraints established by the completed YAMLs: reversibility, non-diagnosis, non-ranking, Dignity-in-Practice, rival openness, and non-capture.

Outside the frame are concrete determinations not supplied by the material. The analysis cannot identify a victim, perpetrator, liable party, patient, defendant, policy subject, responsible institution, or pathological structure. It cannot determine whether one position caused another’s constraint. It cannot determine whether non-exit was chosen, imposed, necessary, protective, tragic, or structurally unavailable. It cannot determine whether responsibility was rightly or wrongly assigned. It cannot determine whether an asymmetry was justified. It cannot determine whether there was coercion or domination.

The protected constraints are therefore methodological. First, the case must remain reversible: any operator status may be weakened, revised, or rejected when concrete evidence or rival formalization is supplied. Second, the analysis must preserve Χ, reflective distance: no fusion into verdict, enforcement, urgency, or person attribution. Third, Dignity-in-Practice must remain an application constraint: no shaming, humiliation, ranking, diagnosis, or irreversible labeling. Fourth, PMS coherence must not be treated as sufficient. Fifth, the add-on must remain an overlay and must not redefine PMS operators.

The conceptual roles are abstract. One may distinguish a role-position with greater capacity for delay, withdrawal, reframing, or exit; a role-position with greater exposure, dependency, liability, or reduced exit capacity; a responsibility-bearing position; a possible frame-maintaining or institutional position; and the analyst or model-user position. These are not persons. They are structural placeholders showing what kinds of roles would need to be specified in a concrete scene.

Temporality is central. The case does not concern static asymmetry alone. It concerns asymmetry under hardening: repeated delay, accumulated cost, path-dependence, shrinking revision windows, closing exit possibilities, or responsibilities that become harder to redistribute over time. Θ therefore provides the trajectory dimension. Yet the YAMLs also mark that temporality alone is insufficient. Duration is not lock-in. Delay is not necessarily coercion. Long-term obligation may be care, commitment, dependency, institutional continuity, danger, tragedy, or ordinary responsibility. The temporal frame must therefore distinguish persistence, accumulation, path-dependence, irreversibility, and reviewability.

The reversibility window is conceptually at risk but empirically unknown. The case asks about situations where revision, interruption, or exit becomes difficult. This makes reversibility central. But the analysis cannot say that reversibility was actually absent. It can only say that a future concrete analysis would need to ask: were review points available? Was exit formal or real? Who bore the cost of delay? Could the frame be challenged? Was refusal permitted? Was interruption safe? Were alternatives viable? Was distance equally available?

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are likewise central but not evidenced. The case names asymmetries of position, exposure, dependency, responsibility, and exit. PMS Core can map these under Ω. PMS-CONFLICT can further ask whether Χ, distance or exit, becomes asymmetrically priced. MIP can ask whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. But without a scene, all of these remain conceptual risk structures, not findings.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading is the foundation of the article. It treats “Asymmetry lock-in” through the canonical operator sequence Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. The completed YAML analyses represent all eleven operators, but they do not activate them equally. Δ, □, Ω, and Θ are strongly central. ∇, Λ, Α, Φ, Χ, and Σ are relevant with varying degrees of calibration. Ψ remains weak and conditional.

**Δ — Difference** is active because the case begins with distinctions. Position is not exposure. Exposure is not dependency. Dependency is not responsibility. Responsibility is not exit. Formal exit is not real exit. Revision is not interruption. Interruption is not leaving. PMS begins by holding these differences apart. Without Δ, the case collapses into a vague moral difficulty. The calibration issue is that difference must not be moralized too quickly. A difference in position may be neutral, protective, necessary, procedural, unjust, or ambiguous. Δ only makes difference legible; it does not determine its normative meaning.

Overclaim would occur if every difference were treated as morally decisive. It would also occur if the mere presence of differentiated positions were treated as evidence of domination, coercion, failure, or blame. The operator provides the initial condition for structured analysis, not a conclusion.

**∇ — Impulse** is weak to active. The case implies directional tensions: pressure to revise, pressure to interrupt, pressure to leave, pressure to remain, pressure to assume responsibility, pressure to stabilize, pressure to avoid destabilization. These pressures are structural, not psychological. ∇ does not license claims about fear, desire, denial, avoidance, bad faith, or motive. It marks that the differentiated field is not inert. There is movement or tension within it.

The calibration issue is to identify what kind of pressure is being described. Is it practical, institutional, moral, relational, argumentative, or explanatory? Does it come from an obligation, an expectation, an exposed position, a dependency, a deadline, a threat, or a desire for closure? None of this can be known from the abstract case. The operator remains structurally useful but under-evidenced. Overclaim would occur if ∇ were psychologized.

**□ — Frame** is active and indispensable. Asymmetry becomes meaningful only inside a frame. A dependency may be caring, exploitative, tragic, ordinary, institutional, temporary, or legally relevant depending on the frame. A delayed exit may be a commitment, an entrapment, a duty, a danger, a protective measure, or an unresolved ambiguity depending on the frame. The case therefore requires a declared frame before any stronger claim can be made.

The YAML analyses treat the operative frame as a moral-philosophical case-type rather than a concrete relationship or institution. This frame allows structural mapping but blocks concrete attribution. The calibration issue is that frame shifts must be tracked. A situation may be described under care ethics, domination theory, legal theory, institutional theory, phenomenology, systems theory, or tragedy. PMS must not silently substitute one frame for another. Overclaim occurs when the analysis treats the PMS frame as if it had already displaced all rival frames.

**Λ — Non-Event** is weak to active. The case concerns what becomes hard to revise, interrupt, or leave. That language points toward possible non-events: a revision that does not occur, an interruption that fails, an exit that remains unavailable, a recognition that does not arrive, a response that is delayed, a handover that is missing, or a transformation that is postponed. However, Λ requires an expected event within a frame. Absence alone is not sufficient.

This is a major calibration point. A missing exit is meaningful only if an exit was expected, promised, required, materially possible, formally available, or structurally relevant. Non-response matters differently in a legal frame, a care frame, a conflict frame, and an institutional frame. PMS must not turn every silence into structured non-event, every delay into drift, or every non-fit into incomplete integration. Overclaim occurs when absence is automatically coded as failure, evasion, immaturity, or lock-in.

**Α — Attractor** is active as a conceptual lock-in operator but weak as an evidenced scene operator. The term “lock-in” suggests stabilization, recurrence, role-script formation, and path-dependence. An asymmetry becomes more than a static difference when patterns repeat and become harder to interrupt. Α therefore matters because it marks how pressures can stabilize into recurrent configurations.

But the abstract case supplies no recurrence evidence. No sequence is given. No repeated act is described. No role-script is shown. No institutional routine is documented. Therefore Α must remain conditional. It is conceptually central because lock-in implies patterning, but it is not empirically established. Overclaim occurs if one difficult moment is treated as an attractor, or if a single asymmetry is treated as path-dependence without evidence of repetition or stabilization.

**Ω — Asymmetry** is strongly active. The case explicitly names asymmetries of position, exposure, dependency, responsibility, and available exit. Ω is the central PMS operator for the case. It tracks directional imbalance: who can delay, who must respond, who can reframe, who bears exposure, who depends on whom, who has exit, who can impose responsibility, who can contest.

The central calibration issue is that Ω is structural, not automatically condemnatory. Asymmetry can be harmful, protective, necessary, tragic, institutional, temporary, reciprocal across dimensions, justified, unjustified, or contested. A caregiving dependency is not structurally identical to domination. A legal obligation is not structurally identical to coercion. A temporary exposure difference is not lock-in. The operator must specify kind and direction of asymmetry.

Overclaim occurs when asymmetry is treated as moral blame by default. It also occurs when formal symmetry is used to erase real asymmetry. The analysis must therefore avoid both moral inflation and pseudo-symmetry.

**Θ — Temporality** is strongly active. Lock-in is temporal. It implies persistence, delay, accumulation, shrinking windows, path-dependence, horizon effects, and the increasing cost of revision. Θ makes the difference between a static imbalance and a trajectory. The case is not merely about who has what at a single moment; it is about how asymmetry may become harder to undo.

The calibration issue is to distinguish forms of temporality. Delay is not always lock-in. Persistence is not always harm. Irreversibility is not always failure. Long-term responsibility may be commitment. Repeated exposure may be institutional routine. Stabilized dependency may be care. PMS must therefore ask: what kind of temporal process is involved? What is the time scale? What accumulates? What becomes harder? What remains reversible? What is merely ongoing?

Overclaim occurs when any duration is treated as temporal hardening or when any hardening is treated as illegitimate. Θ must be linked to Ω and Α, not floated as an independent drama of time.

**Φ — Recontextualization** is active but uncertain. The case asks how difficult situations should be understood when revision, interruption, or leaving becomes difficult. That question directly invites possible recontextualization. A situation may need to be reframed from duty to domination, care to coercion, obligation to dependency, delay to exposure, responsibility to burden displacement, or conflict to tragedy. Φ makes such reframing visible.

Yet Φ is also a danger. Recontextualization can clarify, but it can also assimilate. A PMS reading can redescribe resistance, rupture, refusal, or rival framing as if it were merely incomplete PMS integration. That would be overreach. The YAMLs therefore treat Φ as calibration, not closure. The analysis must ask whether recontextualization is available, blocked, contested, rhetorical, or itself part of the lock-in.

Overclaim occurs if every non-fit is treated as needing more PMS. Non-fit may indicate that PMS is not the best grammar for the morally decisive feature.

**Χ — Distance** is active both as PMS application condition and as possible scene operator. As application discipline, Χ requires reflective distance: no immediate collapse into verdict, diagnosis, enforcement, or person-level conclusion. It is what keeps the analysis reversible and non-tribunal. As scene content, Χ may concern whether roles can step back from the asymmetry, pause, reflect, interrupt, or create distance.

The calibration issue is that distance must not become false neutrality. A demand for distance can erase vulnerability if it treats unequal exposure as if all parties have the same capacity to wait. Conversely, refusal of distance can become enforcement if urgency fuses analysis with verdict. PMS requires distance without detachment from asymmetry. It requires restraint without denial.

Overclaim occurs when Χ is treated as passivity, objectivity, superiority, or withdrawal from responsibility. It also occurs when distance is suspended in order to force closure.

**Σ — Integration** is active as a question but not established as an achievement. The case involves several elements that would need integration: dependency, responsibility, exposure, exit, temporality, possible non-events, rival frames, and dignity constraints. Σ would be the work of holding these together without collapse.

But the completed YAMLs repeatedly warn that coherence is not proof. A coherent PMS formula may still be inadequate. Integration may be local rather than shared. It may be narrative rather than enacted. It may smooth over moral remainder. It may hide material constraint. It may make the case feel resolved when it remains structurally and morally open.

Overclaim occurs when Σ is inferred from the fact that the YAML is coherent. That is precisely the kind of artefact-level risk AHP later identifies. Σ must be tested, not assumed.

**Ψ — Self-Binding** is weak to active only as a question. Responsibility is thematically present in the case material, but self-binding is not established. PMS defines Ψ as formation of self-relation through commitment to integrated structures across time. That requires more than being placed under obligation. It requires a stable relation to commitment, role, responsibility, or normativity across time.

The abstract case gives no agent, commitment, promise, role ownership, or self-relation. Therefore Responsibility_R remains weak, and SELF_FIXPOINT is not established. Ψ may become relevant in a future concrete scene if roles are shown to bind themselves or be legitimately bound into commitments under Ω and Θ. But the present case cannot claim that.

Overclaim occurs if responsibility is automatically converted into self-binding, maturity, culpability, guilt, or person-level finding.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The dependency discipline is one of the most important parts of the case. Later operators cannot be used cheaply. PMS does not permit the analysis to jump from difference and pressure directly to self-binding, verdict, or responsibility conclusion. Δ and ∇ make a field legible and directed. □ frames it. Λ marks expected absences or residues only within a frame. Α requires stabilization. Ω depends on patterning if it is to describe stabilized asymmetry. Θ depends on asymmetry and attractor structure if it is to describe lock-in trajectory. Φ depends on frame, asymmetry, and temporality. Χ depends on recontextualization, temporality, and frame. Σ depends on Χ and Φ. Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ.

This matters because “Asymmetry lock-in” is rhetorically tempting. The phrase already feels like a conclusion. But PMS prevents it from becoming a shortcut. One cannot say there is lock-in merely because asymmetry is named. One cannot say there is responsibility merely because a role is exposed. One cannot say there is failed maturity merely because exit did not occur. One cannot say there is self-binding merely because responsibility is thematically present. One cannot say there is integration merely because the analysis is elegant.

Σ remains conditional because no concrete integration is shown. The YAMLs identify what would need to be integrated, but not that integration has occurred. If a later scene shows actors balancing responsibility, dependency, exposure, and exit in a coherent way under reflective distance, Σ might become stronger. If it shows competing local integrations that cannot be jointly integrated, Σ might fail or become conflict-relevant. But neither is established here.

Ψ remains even more conditional. Self-binding requires integrated structures across time under reflective distance. The abstract case does not provide a subject of binding. It does not provide a promise, role commitment, duty acceptance, norm internalization, or identity commitment. Therefore Ψ is a watch structure, not a finding.

Coherence is not proof. This is perhaps the central calibration point of the entire case. PMS can produce a coherent structural formula: Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ, with Φ, Χ, and Σ as calibration layer and Ψ as watch condition. PMS-CONFLICT can produce a coherent reduced signature: Ω/Θ/Χ asymmetry lock-in. MIP can produce a coherent A–C–R–P–D reading. AHP can produce a coherent attack surface and hardening backlog. But none of this proves that PMS has captured the full moral truth of the case. It only proves that the case can be rendered in a disciplined structural grammar.

Non-capture is not failure. PMS must allow that some aspects of a concrete case might be better described by care ethics, domination theory, legal theory, phenomenology, systems theory, or tragedy. It must allow that lived vulnerability, shame, bodily dependence, fear, legal coercion, or institutional violence may exceed the PMS article’s structural vocabulary. It must also allow that some apparent failures of integration are not immaturity but real tragedy, real material constraint, or real external danger.

Invalid dependency shortcuts would include the following. Treating Ω as blame. Treating Θ as inevitability. Treating Λ as failure without identifying the expected event. Treating Α as present without recurrence. Treating Φ as automatic repair. Treating Χ as neutrality or detachment. Treating Σ as proof of adequacy. Treating Ψ as responsibility assignment. Treating drift patterns as diagnoses. Treating CONFLICT reduced signatures as PMS operators. Treating MIP scores as person scores. Treating AHP hardening as validation.

PMS must therefore not become a closure machine. It is a structural grammar for rendering closure-demands explicit. In this case, its success lies in showing why closure is demanded, where it may be possible, where it is not yet warranted, and where Λ-residue must remain visible.

### Derived structures

The completed YAML analyses treat PMS derived structures as formula-derived, not primitive. They are not independent axioms. They are not additional operators. They do not override Δ–Ψ. They provide higher-order renderings of operator compositions, and each must be assessed according to the evidence and case type.

**Awareness_A** is active as a structural analytic projection. Its formula basis is Θ, □, and Δ: sustained, framed differentiation across time. In this case, Awareness_A is strong because the case explicitly differentiates position, exposure, dependency, responsibility, and exit, and asks how those differences persist or harden over time. Awareness here does not mean consciousness, introspection, insight, or subjective awareness. It means that the analysis can hold framed distinctions across a temporal horizon.

The case-specific role of Awareness_A is to prevent collapse. If the analysis collapses formal exit into real exit, or responsibility into guilt, or dependency into coercion, Awareness_A fails. If it preserves these differences across the temporal structure of lock-in, Awareness_A is active. But because there is no concrete scene, Awareness_A applies to the analysis structure, not to any actor’s awareness.

**Coherence_C** is weak to moderate. Its formula basis is Θ, Λ, □, and ∇: temporally stabilized structuring of impulse and expectation within a frame. The case can be coherently narrated: a framed situation generates pressure around revision, interruption, or exit; expected events may fail; patterns may stabilize; asymmetry may harden. But the YAMLs repeatedly mark that such narrative coherence is not enough.

Coherence_C is therefore not an adequacy claim. It is a formal property of the reading, not proof that the reading captures the moral situation. In fact, coherence is risky here because the case strongly fits PMS language. The danger is not incoherence but over-coherence: a smooth structural rendering that under-discriminates between coercion, care, tragedy, ordinary commitment, institutional inertia, and material constraint.

**Responsibility_R** is weak and analytic-only. Its formula basis is Ψ, Φ, Θ, and Ω. Responsibility_R would require asymmetry over time, recontextualization of obligation, and self-binding to commitments. The case includes responsibility as a theme, but it does not show self-binding. It does not show a role accepting or carrying obligation. It does not show a commitment that persists across time. It does not show whether responsibility is assigned, assumed, imposed, refused, distributed, or contested.

Therefore Responsibility_R remains a question rather than a result. The analysis may ask how responsibility would be structured under asymmetry lock-in. It may not assign responsibility. It may not infer culpability, blame, betrayal, maturity, or legal duty.

**Action_E** is under-specified. Its formula basis is Σ, Θ, and ∇: integrated realization of directedness across time. The case names revision, interruption, and leaving, but it does not describe any actual action. No actor revises, interrupts, leaves, fails to leave, attempts repair, delays, responds, or refuses in the supplied material. Therefore Action_E cannot be activated as a scene finding.

The only sense in which Action_E is relevant is negative or preparatory: future operationalization would need to distinguish actual action from pressure, possibility, omission, or structural condition. The completed YAMLs therefore connect Action_E to the drift risk AD_A>>E only as a possible watch condition, not as an established finding.

**Dignity_in_Practice_D** is active as an application constraint, not as an ontological or evaluative result. Its formula basis is Ψ, Χ, and Ω, but in this case it operates chiefly as a guardrail: asymmetry must be handled with reflective restraint, without shaming, ranking, humiliating, diagnosing, or irreversibly labeling persons or roles. Dignity-in-Practice does not mean that PMS has discovered dignity. It does not rank dignity. It does not compare persons. It does not derive D₀. It constrains how the analysis may speak.

This matters because asymmetry language is easily weaponized. Saying “asymmetry lock-in” can sound accusatory. It can imply that one side trapped another, or that one party failed to act, or that someone is immature. Dignity-in-Practice prevents that slippage. The analysis may map structural conditions, but it may not convert structural visibility into humiliation or status judgment.

**SELF_FIXPOINT** is not established. The full sequence Ψ ∘ Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ would represent self-binding as the result of integrated, reflexively modulated, temporally extended, asymmetry-aware patterns of praxis bound into stable self-relation. The abstract case does not supply the conditions for that. There is no self, no autobiographical continuity, no stable self-relation, no integrated praxis, and no demonstrated self-binding.

SELF_FIXPOINT remains only a possible downstream structure for a later concrete scene. It must not be applied metaphorically to the abstract case as if the case itself had selfhood. It must not be used to infer maturity or identity. It is derived, conditional, and unavailable as a present finding.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS drift-pattern layer remains deliberately cautious. The YAML analyses do not treat drift as a finding. They treat drift as a conditional structural risk that becomes relevant only if the necessary operator conditions are present and if a concrete scene or stronger evidence supports them. This is especially important because “Asymmetry lock-in” sounds like a drift label already. The title names a possible structural pattern, but PMS must still ask whether the pattern is actually established.

The first drift-pattern status is **AD_A>>E**. This pattern concerns an imbalance between awareness and action: awareness becomes over-expanded while action is suppressed, chronically delayed, or under-realized. In the present case, the awareness axis is conceptually strong. The analysis can name asymmetry, exposure, dependency, responsibility, exit, temporality, lock-in, and non-capture. It can map what would need to be checked. It can distinguish formal exit from real exit, responsibility pressure from self-binding, and asymmetry from moral verdict.

But Action_E is under-specified. No concrete action is supplied. There is no actor who tries to leave and cannot, no institution that blocks revision, no role-holder who delays repair, no observable refusal, no documented interruption, no missed exit, and no enacted attempt at transformation. Therefore the AD_A>>E pattern is not a finding. At most, it is a watch condition: a later concrete case might show high awareness combined with suppressed or delayed action. The current case does not.

### Example 1 — Formal exit, real exposure

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** After “Scene and frame structure,” or inside “PMS Core structural reading” after Ω / Θ / Χ.

A small organization introduces a formal review channel for employees who feel overburdened by project assignments. On paper, anyone may request a review, pause a task, or ask for reassignment. The procedure is written clearly, and no rule explicitly punishes use of the channel.

A junior employee, however, depends on the same manager for references, future assignments, and contract renewal. The employee’s workload has become difficult to revise because each missed review point creates additional obligations: deadlines accumulate, other team members depend on the work, and the employee becomes the person who “knows the project best.” The manager can delay a decision without much visible cost. The employee can also delay, but delay increases exposure: the task remains theirs, the burden becomes more visible, and asking for review later may look like unreliability.

This vignette demonstrates the difference between **formal availability** and **real availability**. It does not establish coercion, exploitation, illegality, or blame. It shows only why “available exit” cannot be read from formal procedure alone. An exit route may exist as a rule while remaining unevenly priced as practice.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ** distinguishes formal exit from real exit, manager position from junior position, and procedural permission from practical exposure. **∇** appears as pressure to continue, delay, request review, or remain silent. **□** is the organizational frame in which the review channel has meaning. **Λ** may appear if review is expected but repeatedly not used, not offered, or not made practically reachable; however, this remains a possible non-event, not an automatic finding. **Α** becomes relevant only if the pattern repeats and the junior employee’s role stabilizes around being the person who absorbs delay. **Ω** is central because exposure, dependency, and exit-cost are unevenly distributed. **Θ** matters because the asymmetry hardens over time as deadlines and role-dependence accumulate. **Φ** would concern whether the situation can be recontextualized from “individual workload problem” to “asymmetrically priced review structure.” **Χ** is the key calibration operator: stepping back is formally allowed, but the cost of stepping back is not equal. **Σ** is not established merely because the organization has a coherent review procedure. **Ψ** is not established unless the employee or manager is shown to bind themselves to a stable responsibility structure across time.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay sharpens the example by naming the reduced signature without turning it into a verdict. The relevant shorthand is **DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in**, because distance, delay, and exit may be asymmetrically priced under Ω and Θ. Yet the vignette does not establish a full CONFLICT terminal regime. It does not show failed Σ under binding collision. It does not show that the manager acted wrongly. It does not show legal coercion. It only illustrates how a review channel can remain formally open while becoming practically difficult for one role to use.

MIP would treat this as an IA-box prompt rather than a judgment. Transparency may be partial if the review channel is written down. Justification remains under-specified unless the organization can explain why the burdens and timing are distributed as they are. Time-boundedness is a risk if review windows are vague. Reversibility is a risk if delayed review increases burden for one side. M-score should not be assigned from the vignette alone. AHP would flag a likely artefact risk: a polished example may make the organization look guilty even though the vignette lacks full evidence.

The forbidden claims are therefore explicit. The example does not prove exploitation, immaturity, negligence, retaliation, coercion, or legal wrongdoing. It does not diagnose the manager or the employee. It does not rank dignity. It does not validate PMS, PMS-CONFLICT, or MIP.

The non-capture note is equally important. A legal analysis might ask whether the review procedure satisfies labor obligations. A care or organizational ethics reading might ask whether dependency and mentorship duties are being handled responsibly. A phenomenological reading might ask how it feels to carry exposure under formal permission. PMS makes the structure readable, but it does not exhaust these questions.

The article takeaway is this: **asymmetry lock-in begins to matter when what is formally revisable becomes practically uneven, temporally burdened, and costly to interrupt.**

### Example 2 — Care, obligation, and the danger of over-reading non-exit

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** In the examples section before “Non-capture and rival readings,” where it can prepare the later rival-reading discussion; it can also be cross-referenced back to “PMS drift-pattern status” as a counterweight to over-reading non-exit.

A person remains in a demanding caregiving arrangement for a family member whose condition requires regular attention. Friends sometimes describe the situation as “trapping,” because the caregiver has little free time, postpones plans, and cannot easily leave for extended periods. The caregiver also sometimes says that leaving is “not really an option.”

This could look like asymmetry lock-in: one person is dependent, another carries responsibility, exit is difficult, and time intensifies the structure. But the example is intentionally bounded so that the article does not over-read non-exit as drift or coercion. The caregiver may experience real burden and still understand the arrangement as meaningful care. The difficulty of leaving may arise from love, responsibility, practical necessity, lack of services, family expectation, material constraint, or several of these at once. None of those possibilities should be collapsed into a single PMS finding.

What the example demonstrates is the importance of **non-capture**. PMS Core can make the structure visible, but it must not force closure. **Δ** distinguishes dependency, care, burden, obligation, and exit. **∇** marks pressures toward staying, resting, asking for help, or reorganizing care. **□** is crucial: the frame may be family care, moral obligation, public welfare failure, gendered expectation, economic constraint, or emotional attachment. Each frame changes what the non-exit means. **Λ** might become relevant if support that should exist does not appear, such as absent institutional care, missing family help, or delayed respite. Yet Λ must be calibrated: absence is meaningful only when an expectation is established. **Α** may be relevant if the caregiving pattern stabilizes over time. **Ω** is present because dependency and responsibility are asymmetric. **Θ** is active because care trajectories accumulate. **Φ** may recontextualize “personal sacrifice” as “unsupported care structure,” but this reframing must not erase the caregiver’s own interpretation. **Χ** requires reflective distance from both romanticizing care and condemning it as entrapment. **Σ** remains conditional: integration may be possible, partial, costly, or impossible. **Ψ** may become relevant if the caregiver has self-bound to a role, promise, or identity, but it cannot be inferred simply from remaining.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant only with caution. **DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in** may become a risk if distance, respite, or exit are unequally available and temporally harden. But the example must not be treated as a CONFLICT finding. Caregiving is not automatically conflict. Dependency is not automatically domination. Non-exit is not automatically immaturity or coercion. Binding collision may become relevant if commitments become jointly non-viable, but that is not shown here.

MIP is especially useful in this example because it blocks premature responsibility scoring. A-awareness may be conceptually strong if the burdens are visible, but actor-level A remains unknown. M-score cannot be assigned without knowing the caregiver’s knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and attempts at integration. The IA-box could ask whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible, but even that must be handled carefully. Some care asymmetries are not fully reversible; the question is then not whether asymmetry exists, but whether it is made less humiliating, less isolating, less unsupported, and less falsely individualized.

AHP would warn that this example has high misuse risk if written too dramatically. It could become sentimental, accusatory, or covertly diagnostic. It should therefore remain structurally modest.

Forbidden claims include: the caregiver is trapped; the dependent person is a burden in a dignity-reducing sense; the family is guilty; the situation is pathological; the caregiver is immature for staying; the caregiver is morally superior for staying; non-exit proves coercion; difficulty proves tragedy. None of these follows.

The non-capture note is central. Care ethics may preserve features that PMS does not fully capture: attentiveness, responsiveness, love, obligation, grief, fatigue, and the moral texture of dependency. Political theory may ask whether public systems offload care. Legal analysis may ask about entitlement to support. Phenomenology may ask how burden and meaning coexist.

The article takeaway is this: **asymmetry lock-in analysis must be able to see exit difficulty without automatically condemning staying, pathologizing dependency, or converting care into drift.**

### Example 3 — A public interpretation that hardens too quickly

Another person says they did not know the situation was being experienced that way and believed there were open channels for raising concerns.

A short excerpt from the discussion is later circulated publicly. It says that the situation shows “asymmetry lock-in.” The phrase travels quickly. Readers who do not know the context begin treating the phrase as if it proves that one person trapped another, that the higher-status person acted coercively, or that the lower-status person had no real agency. Others reject the phrase as exaggerated and insist that formal channels existed. The original structural question is replaced by a public contest over guilt, innocence, credibility, and status.

This example demonstrates why the article needs PMS-CONFLICT, MIP, and AHP after PMS Core. PMS Core can map the operators in the underlying case, but the public circulation of the phrase introduces a new risk: the analysis itself can become part of the asymmetry. A scene-bound structural term can harden into a reputational object. The vignette is not introduced as evidence that such a public hardening occurred in the actual case; it is a bounded demonstration of how an otherwise careful structural phrase can change function when it leaves its original frame.

The PMS Core hooks are layered. **Δ** distinguishes the original disagreement, the private interpretation, the public excerpt, and the later audience reaction. **∇** appears as pressure to interpret, defend, accuse, deny, or align. **□** shifts: what began as a private role-context frame becomes a public reputational frame. **Λ** may appear as missing context, absent reply, unprovided evidence, or failed opportunity for clarification. **Α** becomes relevant if the public narrative stabilizes into a repeatable role-script. **Ω** is intensified because status, exposure, and audience access become uneven. **Θ** matters because public records persist and revision becomes harder over time. **Φ** is central because recontextualization occurs: a structural term is moved from private analysis to public interpretation. **Χ** is endangered because reflective distance collapses under public pressure. **Σ** is not achieved merely because audiences settle on a coherent story. **Ψ** is not established; public attribution is not self-binding.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay makes visible **exposure capture** as a risk, although in the original abstract case it was not applicable. Here, once publicness is introduced, P and MG become relevant. Recontextualization under scaled exposure can turn interpretation into enforcement affordance. The phrase “asymmetry lock-in” may become a reputational marker rather than a reversible analytic description. **Dignity collapse drift** also becomes a risk if language shifts toward shaming, ranking, humiliation, or person-near verdict. Still, these are risks, not findings. Public circulation does not automatically prove misuse; it makes the boundary problem visible.

MIP would treat this as a transmission failure risk. The IA-box cannot be skipped. Was the asymmetry transparent? Was the public claim justified? Was the interpretation time-bound and reversible? Did affected parties have voice? Were formal and real options distinguished? M-score should not be assigned to the public participants based on the vignette. The D-module would remain highly guarded: public dignity risk is elevated, but dignity must not be scored. AHP would likely mark this as an excerpt-risk case: the analytic artefact has been detached from its boundary language.

Forbidden claims are especially important here. The example does not prove that the original higher-status person did wrong. It does not prove that the lower-status person misrepresented anything. It does not prove that the public audience is malicious. It does not prove legal coercion, reputational harm, institutional negligence, or clinical injury. It does not validate or invalidate the phrase “asymmetry lock-in.” It shows how a structurally careful term can become unsafe when detached from scope, evidence, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice.

The non-capture note is broad. Media theory may better explain circulation. Legal analysis may be needed if defamation, confidentiality, or institutional process is implicated. Phenomenology may better capture the experience of exposure. Political theory may better analyze status and public contestability. PMS-CONFLICT shows the structural risk of exposure capture, but it does not exhaust the public meaning of the event.

The article takeaway is this: **the language of asymmetry lock-in must remain scene-bound and reversible, because once it travels publicly, the analysis itself may become part of the asymmetry it was designed to clarify.**

### Non-capture and rival readings

Non-capture is not an afterthought in this case. It is one of the main structural results. The completed YAMLs preserve several non-capture zones, and the article must render them as legitimate rather than deficient.

The first non-capture zone is **moral remainder**. Even a careful PMS-CONFLICT-MIP reading may not capture what remains morally painful, tragic, or irreducibly unresolved in a concrete asymmetry. A role may face loss under every available continuation. A choice may be responsible and still costly. A dependency may be morally real without being reducible to operator structure. PMS can map frames and residues, but it does not eliminate moral remainder.

The second zone is **material, bodily, economic, institutional, or safety constraint**. A person may be unable to leave because of money, illness, bodily risk, legal status, caregiving duty, threat, disability, housing, documentation, institutional dependency, or safety concerns. PMS can mark exit asymmetry, but it cannot replace empirical investigation into material constraints. MIP’s P dimension explicitly remains under-specified for this reason.

The third zone is **legal meaning**. Consent, coercion, liability, negligence, rights, duty, and legal responsibility require legal frames. A PMS reading may make structural asymmetry visible, but it cannot determine legality. Legal analysis may preserve distinctions that PMS does not decide: formal consent versus valid consent, duty of care, coercive control, procedural fairness, evidentiary standards, and remedy.

The fourth zone is **phenomenological lived experience**. Dependency, fear, shame, guilt, care, exhaustion, ambivalence, and vulnerability are not reducible to structural mapping. PMS does not infer subjective experience. MIP does not diagnose. A future concrete case would need affected-side perspective if safe and appropriate. Without that, lived experience remains non-captured.

The fifth zone is **care ethics and dependency ethics**. These readings may preserve the moral reality of dependency without treating it as defect or domination. A person may remain because care binds them. A dependency may be constitutive of obligation rather than a failure of freedom. PMS can map dependency and exit asymmetry, but care ethics may better describe the normativity of responsiveness, attentiveness, and relational obligation.

The sixth zone is **domination theory**. Domination theory may better capture arbitrary power, contestability, dependency on another’s will, or structural subjection. PMS-CONFLICT can identify asymmetrically priced Χ, but domination theory may provide a more specific normative criterion for when asymmetry becomes objectionable.

The seventh zone is **coercion theory**. Coercion requires its own thresholds: threat, pressure, voluntariness, alternatives, consent, and context. PMS must not infer coercion from non-exit. But if coercion is later evidenced, PMS alone would not be sufficient to decide its full normative or legal status.

The eighth zone is **systems theory and institutional path-dependence**. Some lock-ins are not dyadic. They emerge from feedback loops, infrastructures, incentives, bureaucratic routines, policy layers, and distributed agency. PMS can map attractor structure, but systems theory may better describe complex causal feedback.

The ninth zone is **tragedy**. Some situations are not solved by better integration. A tragic reading may preserve the fact that all available continuations carry loss. PMS-CONFLICT marks terminality as a possible upper-bound but not a finding. Tragedy remains open.

The tenth zone is **ordinary commitment**. Not all difficulty of leaving is bad. Commitments often bind precisely by making exit costly. Promises, care, duty, contracts, and long-term projects can reduce local options while enabling longer-horizon agency. PMS must not pathologize commitment.

The three examples sharpen these non-capture zones rather than replacing them. The organizational review example shows how formal exit and real exit can diverge without proving wrongdoing. The caregiving example prevents the analysis from treating non-exit as drift by default. The public interpretation example shows how the analysis language itself can become part of a new exposure structure. Together they demonstrate why examples must remain illustrative. They are not evidence for the case. They are calibration devices.

These rival readings do not defeat PMS. They discipline it. They show what PMS may not capture fully and where comparison would be required. The YAMLs do not rank rival readings. They preserve them as epistemically open. A future article could strengthen discrimination by comparing what PMS, care ethics, domination theory, legal theory, phenomenology, and systems theory each clarify and each fail to decide.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS Core reading would be weakened if the case turned out to involve no meaningful asymmetry of position, exposure, dependency, responsibility, or exit. It would also be weakened if the difficulty of revision, interruption, or leaving were merely apparent, temporary, trivial, or equally distributed. If there is no differentiated field, Δ loses force. If there is no directional pressure, ∇ is under-specified. If no frame-specific expectation is absent, Λ should not be activated. If no repetition or stabilization exists, Α remains weak. If no temporal hardening occurs, Θ loses centrality.

The PMS reading would also be weakened if rival formalizations explained the decisive feature with greater precision. For example, if a case is primarily about legal consent, legal analysis may be more decisive. If it is primarily about lived fear, phenomenology may be more adequate. If it is primarily about care, care ethics may better preserve its normativity. If it is primarily about arbitrary power, domination theory may be more discriminating.

The reading becomes under-discriminating if it cannot distinguish asymmetry lock-in from ordinary commitment, temporary delay, caregiving dependency, institutional inertia, coercion, domination, tragic responsibility, legal duty, or material constraint. Under-discrimination is a central risk because the abstract case is broad. A PMS formula that covers too much without thresholds becomes less useful.

The reading becomes over-assimilative if it treats rupture, resistance, disagreement, silence, non-fit, absence, or rival framing as PMS-internal deficiency. The YAMLs explicitly forbid this. Non-fit may indicate that PMS is not the best grammar. Resistance may be meaningful refusal. Rupture may mark a real boundary. Silence may be safety. Absence may be structurally meaningful, but it may also be unavailable data.

The add-on reading would be weakened if exit, distance, revision, or interruption were not asymmetrically priced. PMS-CONFLICT’s strongest contribution is the Ω/Θ/Χ relation. If a concrete case showed that all parties had comparable distance, comparable exit, comparable exposure, and comparable revision capacity, the CONFLICT overlay would become weaker. If there is no incompatibility, no binding collision, no stabilization, and no costly distance, then PMS Core may be enough.

The CONFLICT terminal reading would be falsified or blocked unless a concrete case showed failed integration under sustained Ω/Θ/Ψ with costly or asymmetric Χ. The present case does not show this. Terminality remains weak. Any claim that the case is terminal would be an overclaim.

The MIP reading would be weakened if someone tried to treat it as person scoring. Its validity depends on keeping A as case-structure readability, M as non-assignable, IA-box as risk check rather than verdict, and D as qualitative guardrail. If the MIP output were used in HR, legal, clinical, forensic, public shaming, governance authorization, or sanctioning contexts, the transmission boundary would fail.

The AHP reading would be weakened if its attack points were treated as case findings rather than artefact vulnerabilities. AHP does not say the case has a defect. It says the analysis artefact has possible misreading surfaces. If AHP is used to strengthen authority rather than precision, it fails.

Operationalization requirements remain extensive and unfulfilled. A stronger concrete analysis would require at least the following: a scene boundary; role map; timeline; description of relevant actions and omissions; explicit frame; evidence of asymmetry; exposure distribution; dependency structure; responsibility structure; formal and real exit options; revision pathways; interruption possibilities; publicness level; institutional setting; material constraints; affected-side perspective; available alternatives; review points; evidence of recurrence or stabilization; and rival-model comparison.

For legal claims, legal evidence and jurisdiction-specific analysis would be required. For clinical claims, clinical evaluation would be required, but this stack does not authorize such use. For empirical claims, data would be required. For implementation claims, a separate implementation design and validation framework would be required. For governance claims, downstream governance criteria would be required. None of these is fulfilled here.

The correct final condition is therefore: structurally legible, conceptually strong, empirically unfulfilled, non-diagnostic, non-legal, non-governance, non-final, and reversible.

### Misuse boundary

The misuse boundary must be substantial because this case is especially easy to misuse.

The case must not be used for diagnosis. No person may be labeled avoidant, dependent, coercive, immature, pathological, manipulative, weak, rigid, unstable, or deficient based on this analysis. PMS drift and CONFLICT drift are not clinical categories.

The case must not be used for person ranking. It does not rank maturity, dignity, responsibility, awareness, coherence, agency, or moral worth. MIP A-band is not an actor score. M-score is not assignable. D₀ is untouched. D₁/D₂ are not dignity levels.

The case must not be used for legal or forensic conclusion. It does not establish coercion, consent failure, liability, negligence, duty, abuse, culpability, or admissible evidence. It may show what structural questions legal analysis would need to ask, but it cannot answer them.

The case must not be used for metaphysical proof. It does not prove what responsibility, freedom, dependency, dignity, asymmetry, or conflict really are in themselves. PMS is a structural grammar, not a metaphysical closure machine.

The case must not be used for empirical proof. It does not establish that any event occurred. It does not prove that exit was blocked, revision failed, or asymmetry hardened. It only maps what such a structure would involve.

The case must not be used for implementation validation. YAML completeness is not implementation. The article is not a procedure, protocol, algorithm, risk engine, or decision system.

The case must not be used for PMS Base validation. A strong fit does not validate PMS. It demonstrates a disciplined application under stated boundaries. That is all.

The add-on must not be treated as an operator. PMS-CONFLICT reduced signatures remain overlay shorthand. Asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, dignity collapse, binding collision, and terminal regime are not PMS operators. They do not override Δ–Ψ.

Drift must not become verdict. AD_A>>E, AD_Sigma_low, DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in, binding collision drift, exposure capture drift, dignity collapse drift, and Σ-incompatibility clash are structural risk categories. They are not findings unless evidence supports them. They are never person-types.

There must be no closure-by-authority. The fact that the analysis is long, formal, multi-layered, and YAML-derived does not make it authoritative in the sense of deciding the case. AHP explicitly warns that completeness can overstate evidential grounding. The article must therefore carry its own limits visibly.

The most compact misuse boundary is:

This case may be used to understand how asymmetry, time, distance, exit, responsibility, and non-capture interact structurally; it must not be used to diagnose persons, assign guilt, rank dignity, prove coercion, authorize governance, validate PMS, or close a morally difficult situation by analytic authority.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is that **Asymmetry lock-in** is highly legible within the PMS stack, but only as a bounded conceptual case-type. PMS Core identifies the main structural grammar: differentiated positions and pressures become framed; possible non-events of revision, interruption, recognition, or exit may matter; stabilization may form; asymmetry becomes central; temporality hardens the field; recontextualization, distance, and integration become calibration problems; self-binding remains weak unless concretely shown. The most compact PMS Core formula remains **Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ**, with **Φ, Χ, and Σ** as calibration structures and **Ψ** as a weak watch condition rather than a finding.

PMS-CONFLICT then sharpens what PMS Core alone does not isolate: the possibility that **distance, exit, delay, revision, or interruption become asymmetrically priced under Ω and Θ**. Its strongest contribution is the overlay handle **DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in**, but this remains an overlay shorthand, not a PMS operator and not a verdict. The CONFLICT layer does not prove terminality, binding collision, exposure capture, or dignity collapse. It makes those risks visible while keeping them subordinate to PMS Base and to the case’s abstract status.

MIP adds a maturity-in-practice boundary without turning the case into a maturity assessment. Its main result is that awareness is conceptually strong, coherence is useful but risky, responsibility is thematically relevant but not assignable, power-to-act is central but under-specified, and dignity remains qualitative and guardrail-bound. The IA-box yields conceptual asymmetry risk, especially around time-boundedness and reversibility, but not a verdict. M-score is not assignable; non-assignment is not a low score. D₀ remains untouched, and the D-module is not activated as a person profile.

AHP adds second-order artefact hardening. It confirms that the MIP analysis is high-precision as a private working note, but it also identifies the central danger: the analysis is so complete and coherent that it may look more evidentially grounded than the abstract case permits. AHP therefore strengthens the boundary language without changing the result. It does not rescore A, M, IA, or D. It does not upgrade transmission. It hardens the artefact against misuse.

The examples contribute three forms of calibration. The organizational review example shows why formal exit is not the same as real exit, and why asymmetrically priced review or interruption matters. The caregiving example prevents over-reading non-exit as coercion, immaturity, or drift, and preserves care ethics and dependency ethics as rival or complementary grammars. The public interpretation example shows that the language of asymmetry lock-in can itself become part of a new exposure structure if detached from scope, evidence, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. Together, the examples demonstrate the analysis without replacing it. They show why PMS-readable structure is useful, and why examples must not become evidence.

What remains unclaimed is just as important as what is claimed. The article does not claim empirical occurrence, legal coercion, clinical diagnosis, metaphysical truth, implementation validity, governance authority, PMS Base validation, person ranking, guilt, maturity failure, or dignity ranking. It does not claim that resistance, refusal, silence, rupture, non-fit, absence, or rival framing are drift. It does not claim that a coherent YAML analysis is sufficient. It does not claim that PMS captures everything morally decisive.

The boundary discipline is therefore the article’s central methodological result. PMS maps the closure-demand without satisfying it by force. PMS-CONFLICT sharpens conflict-specific visibility without becoming base grammar. MIP checks maturity-in-practice language without scoring persons. AHP hardens the output without making it a decision basis. Each layer adds structure, but each layer also narrows what may responsibly be claimed.

The final methodological takeaway is this: **Asymmetry lock-in is a strong AXIOM case not because it can be solved, but because it shows how structural legibility, overlay sharpening, maturity-in-practice discipline, and artefact hardening can coexist without collapsing into closure.** The case shows where closure is tempting, where it is not warranted, and where Λ-residue must remain visible. In that sense, the completed stack does not resolve the moral difficulty; it preserves the conditions under which the difficulty can be read without being converted into diagnosis, verdict, authority, or false completion.
