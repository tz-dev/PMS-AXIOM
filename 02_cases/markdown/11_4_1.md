### Why this case matters

This case matters because it stages a distinct modern closure-demand: a highly technical computational workflow is asked to become governable without being allowed to validate itself. The philosophical problem is not simply whether a quantum-computing workflow can be made efficient, reproducible, or technically correct. The structural problem is how iteration, repetition, traceability, and review can be arranged so that the workflow remains inspectable and bounded rather than self-authorizing.

The closure-demand appears in a deceptively simple form: if a workflow repeats, logs, converges, produces records, and appears internally coherent, what prevents that internal structure from being treated as sufficient warrant? In an ordinary computational context, the answer might seem to lie in testing, verification, external review, or implementation standards. In this case, however, the analysis is not allowed to assume those mechanisms are already specified. The case material names iteration depth, repetition limits, audit traces, monitoring, review, and structural constraints, but it does not give a concrete circuit, institution, validation protocol, or accountability architecture. That means the analysis must hold open the difference between the existence of governance vocabulary and the existence of actual governance.

The case belongs in AXIOM because it is a clean example of closure-demand cartography. It does not ask AXIOM to solve quantum computation. It asks AXIOM to map where closure is being demanded, what kind of frame would be required for that closure, which residues remain visible, and where add-on overlays sharpen the case without redefining PMS. The problem is structurally difficult because it combines several temptation points at once: technical opacity, repetition, possible measurement-like records, audit traces, stabilization language, and governance claims. Each of these can appear disciplined while still failing to supply independent validation.

The case also matters because it exposes a boundary between structural governance and technical correctness. A workflow may be structurally well-described and still technically invalid. It may produce detailed traces and still lack meaningful review. It may respect an iteration bound and still be poorly framed. It may pass a PMS-QC overlay check and still fail under quantum information theory, formal verification, hardware calibration, empirical benchmarking, legal review, or institutional governance. The YAML analyses therefore treat the case as an audit-oriented structural artefact, not as a success verdict.

The philosophical closure-demand is sharpened by the phrase “without treating the workflow as self-validating.” That phrase prevents the case from becoming a mere technical optimization problem. Iteration depth is not only a runtime parameter. Repetition limits are not only efficiency limits. Audit traces are not only logs. Structural constraints are not only engineering controls. Each becomes philosophically significant because it can be mistaken for its own warrant. A workflow that records itself may appear accountable. A workflow that repeats under a limit may appear disciplined. A workflow that is reviewed according to internal criteria may appear governed. The case asks how those appearances are structurally separated from actual external validation.

This is why the case is not solved metaphysically, empirically, clinically, legally, diagnostically, or implementationally. It does not ask whether quantum computation has a particular metaphysical status. It does not infer anything about persons, developers, reviewers, users, institutions, or agents. It does not determine whether an audit trace is legally admissible. It does not certify a hardware run or algorithm. It does not establish convergence. It does not prove that PMS or PMS-QC captures quantum computation. Instead, it asks what must be made explicit before a QC workflow can even be structurally discussed as governed rather than merely executed.

The most important philosophical closure-demand is therefore this: the workflow can be structurally tempted toward being treated as “reviewed enough” by virtue of being traceable, repeated, and internally coherent. The PMS Core analysis resists that demand through Χ, the operator of reflective distance. The PMS-QC overlay sharpens the same point by treating QC macro signatures as documentation and audit shorthand only. The PMS-QC-EXT overlay sharpens it again by showing that paper-specific refinements such as QPE ladders, Grover centering, attractor schedules, and measurement-domain refinements are mostly not activated by the case material. That negative result is part of the value of the case: a structurally disciplined overlay should sometimes say “not enough has been specified.”

### Scene and frame structure

The case is not a concrete praxis scene. This matters. A concrete scene would include named or at least structurally specified roles, a workflow implementation, a time sequence, a trace format, an event history, an authority relation, and a disputed outcome. Here, the case material gives only the general situation: governance of a quantum-computing workflow through iteration depth, repetition limits, audit traces, structural constraints, monitoring, and review. The analysis therefore operates at the level of a conceptual scene packet, not a fully scene-bound adjudication.

The primary frame, □, is a QC workflow governance frame. Inside the frame are the structural relations that must be specified for governance to be meaningful: what counts as a workflow run; what counts as an iteration; what counts as repetition; what counts as a stopping point; what counts as an audit trace; what counts as monitoring; what counts as review; what distinguishes trace production from trace review; and what prevents internal workflow structure from serving as final validation.

Also inside the frame are bounded concepts of iteration depth and repetition limit. Iteration is not treated as a neutral technical word. In PMS terms, iteration has Θ structure: it unfolds over time, accumulates, repeats, and can become path-dependent. In PMS-QC terms, QITERATE is active as an overlay shorthand, but the value of k, the repeated block, and the maximum permitted k_max remain under-specified. Therefore the frame can identify the need for iteration governance without validating any actual iteration schedule.

Audit trace is likewise inside the frame, but only as a structural record or review object. The case does not establish the physical semantics of a trace, the completeness of the logging mechanism, the basis or register of a measurement, the validity of the sampling procedure, or the reliability of the recorded data. PMS-QC treats audit trace as measurement-adjacent only in a structural sense. PMS-QC-EXT weakly invokes MEASUREMENT_PROJECTION_MODEL to discipline audit-branching questions: what was selected, what remained unrealized, what was expected but absent, and which frame made those distinctions meaningful. This is not a physical measurement claim.

Outside the frame are physical correctness, quantum-mechanical adequacy, hardware noise behavior, convergence guarantees, complexity claims, statistical validity, legal admissibility, governance legitimacy, institutional authority, and deployment readiness. These remain external validation domains. PMS and PMS-QC can help structure what would need to be asked; they do not answer those questions by themselves.

The protected constraints are non-self-validation, explicit framing, reversibility, Χ, non-capture, and dignity-in-practice. Non-self-validation means that neither a completed run, nor repeated iteration, nor traceability, nor a smooth operator chain, nor a QC macro signature may count as sufficient warrant. Explicit framing means the workflow must not rely on implicit standards of review or hidden assumptions about what the trace proves. Reversibility means that structural readings must remain open to revision if concrete workflow data, rival formalisms, or implementation evidence changes the case. Dignity-in-practice matters not because the workflow has dignity, but because any human-facing or institutionally applied analysis must not become a tribunal, person-ranking device, or coercive closure machine.

Roles are under-specified. The Core YAML names possible responsibility conditions only conditionally: a reviewer, governance regime, institution, policy, accountable procedure, downstream user, or exposed role would need to be identified before Responsibility_R or Ψ could be strongly activated. In the absence of such roles, Ω remains active but calibration-sensitive. It can identify possible asymmetry between execution and review, opacity and authority, or trace production and trace interpretation; it cannot assign responsibility, fault, or governance status.

Temporality, Θ, is central. The case is temporal from the start because iteration depth, repetition limits, monitoring, audit traces, review windows, stopping conditions, and stabilization policies all unfold across time. A single run is not the same as a repeated trajectory. Trace production is not the same as delayed review. A review that can still interrupt the workflow is not the same as a retrospective record that only rationalizes what already happened. The reversibility window depends on these temporal differences. If review occurs too late to affect action, or if the trace is produced only after a decision has hardened, the governance structure changes.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions remain possible but not established. Technical opacity may produce asymmetry between those who can inspect the workflow and those who rely on its outputs. Authority asymmetry may arise if one role can declare a workflow valid while another bears risk. Resource asymmetry may arise if review capacity is limited or delegated. But without a concrete scene, these remain calibration points, not findings.

The result of this scene analysis is therefore deliberately modest: the case has enough structure to justify a PMS Core reading and QC overlay application, but not enough structure to justify a concrete governance conclusion. What follows from the non-concrete status is not analytic weakness; it is boundary discipline. AXIOM can map the frame requirements, residues, and risk points, but it cannot fill in the absent scene.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading treats the case as a structurally bounded governance problem. It does not merely list operators; it asks how each operator contributes to the possibility of reviewable, non-self-validating workflow governance. The operators are considered in PMS order: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ.

**Δ — Difference** is active and foundational. The case depends on several distinctions: workflow execution versus workflow review; iteration versus termination; repetition versus validation; audit trace versus audit judgment; internal output versus external acceptability; logging versus justification; convergence versus governance adequacy. Without these distinctions, the workflow could collapse into a single self-confirming process. The main calibration issue for Δ is that the presence of a trace must not be confused with the validation of what the trace records. Overclaim occurs when distinction becomes verdict: for example, when “trace exists” is treated as “review has occurred,” or when “workflow completed” is treated as “workflow is acceptable.”

Δ also protects the distinction between PMS readability and external adequacy. A workflow can be legible to PMS without being technically correct. A QC macro signature can be coherent without being physically meaningful. A governance frame can be named without being institutionally valid. The first operator therefore already carries a boundary discipline: the analysis must keep apart what the case makes structurally distinguishable and what it actually proves.

**∇ — Impulse** is weak-to-active as structural directedness. The workflow is not treated as desiring or intending anything. Rather, ∇ names the directional pressure toward continued computation, optimization, repetition, convergence, or result production. In a quantum-computing workflow, this may appear as a pressure to run again, deepen iteration, refine outputs, or continue until a threshold is reached. The calibration issue is anthropomorphism: ∇ must remain procedural directedness, not psychological impulse. Overclaim occurs if the workflow is described as wanting, choosing, or committing in a mental sense.

∇ also marks a governance risk. Directional workflow pressure can shape its own frame. If the goal is output production, convergence, or optimization, then the workflow may implicitly treat continued execution as success. A governance frame must therefore constrain ∇ rather than merely document it. Repetition pressure is not itself illegitimate, but it must not become the source of its own warrant.

**□ — Frame** is active and central. Governance requires a declared frame. The frame defines what counts as a run, what counts as an iteration, what counts as a repetition limit, what counts as a trace, what counts as review, and what counts as external validation. It also defines which constraints are protected and which claims remain out of scope. The dependency risk is that ∇ may shape □ rather than being constrained by it: optimization pressure may generate its own implicit frame, after which governance becomes merely internal. Overclaim occurs when the frame is assumed rather than specified, or when a technical execution frame is silently treated as a governance-review frame.

The frame must also define what expected events are meaningful. Without □, later claims about missing review, absent halt, or insufficient trace are ungrounded. Λ requires expectations, and expectations require a frame. This is why the case cannot simply declare “missing governance” from the abstract material. It can say that governance requires specified expectations; it cannot say which expectations have been violated unless the frame supplies them.

**Λ — Non-Event** is active but must be tightly calibrated. Λ marks meaningful absence within a frame: missing trace, absent review, untriggered halt, absent validation, delayed feedback, unresolved expected occurrence, or failure of an expected governance step. But absence is not automatically failure. It becomes Λ only when the frame defines what was expected. In this case, Λ is especially important because audit governance depends not only on what happened but also on what should have happened and did not.

The main calibration issue is that under-specification must not be treated as a drift finding. The case material itself does not specify the workflow, trace format, or review procedure. Those absences are analytically relevant, but they are not evidence that an actual governance system failed. They are missing scene data. Overclaim occurs if every missing detail in the case material is treated as drift, immaturity, or failure. PMS preserves Λ without converting it into condemnation.

**Α — Attractor** is active as possible recurrence and stabilization. Iteration and repetition can stabilize into patterns: repeated workflow runs, repeated audit routines, repeated reliance on trace logs, repeated non-review, or repeated acceptance of internally coherent output. In PMS-QC language, attractor-like language must remain cautious because no amplitude amplification, QDIFFUSION, stabilizer routine, or convergence schedule is supplied. The calibration issue is recurrence versus proof. A repeated pattern may be legitimate if framed, bounded, traced, and reviewed. Overclaim occurs when recurrence is treated as reliability, or when repetition is treated as convergence without technical evidence.

Α matters because governance failures can become routine without appearing dramatic. A workflow may repeatedly produce traces that are stored but not interpreted. A review body may repeatedly accept internal outputs because no external rejection criterion exists. A repetition limit may repeatedly be met without anyone asking whether the limit is justified. These are possible attractor risks, not established findings. The operator makes recurrence visible; it does not judge it.

**Ω — Asymmetry** is active but calibration-sensitive. The case may involve several asymmetries: between workflow execution and human review; between technical opacity and governance authority; between those who can inspect the trace and those who bear downstream exposure; between internal computational process and external accountability; between a system capable of producing outputs and a regime capable of rejecting them. Ω must not become blame. It marks directional imbalance in capacity, exposure, obligation, authority, or review access. Overclaim occurs if the analysis assigns fault, institutional failure, or moral responsibility without a concrete scene.

Ω is especially important in QC contexts because technical opacity can be structurally relevant even when no person has acted wrongly. If only a small number of roles can understand or inspect the workflow, review capacity becomes asymmetrical. If downstream users rely on outputs without access to trace semantics, exposure becomes asymmetrical. If a governance body can declare acceptance without bearing error costs, authority and exposure diverge. These are structural possibilities, not legal or moral conclusions.

**Θ — Temporality** is active and central. Iteration depth is inherently temporal. Repetition limits are temporal. Audit trace production and review timing are temporal. Monitoring unfolds over time. Stopping conditions depend on sequence and horizon. A trace reviewed before decision, during execution, after execution, or after deployment has different structural significance. The main calibration issue is that Θ must not be reduced to a number of iterations. k matters, but so do cadence, delay, review window, cumulative history, and reversibility. Overclaim occurs when iteration depth is treated as bounded merely because it is countable, or when audit trace is treated as a static artefact rather than part of a temporal review process.

Θ also structures lock-in. If a workflow is repeatedly accepted before review criteria are clarified, a path can form. If trace formats become standardized before their meaning is validated, later revision becomes costly. If review occurs only after decisions are operationalized, the reversibility window narrows. The case therefore treats temporality not as background but as a central governance operator.

**Φ — Recontextualization** is active but bounded. The workflow’s outputs and traces can be recontextualized from the execution frame into a governance-review frame. A record that is internally meaningful to the workflow may become audit material under a different frame. A successful run may be re-read as insufficient because review criteria were not met. But Φ has a misuse risk: it can become post-hoc rationalization. Recontextualization must not allow failure, missing trace, or inadequate review to be redescribed as success merely by changing language. Overclaim occurs if every anomaly is absorbed into a new frame that preserves the workflow’s authority.

Φ is necessary because governance itself is a recontextualizing operation. It takes something produced in a technical process and asks what it means under constraint, review, accountability, and non-self-validation. But the operator must be governed by Χ. Without distance, Φ becomes a laundering mechanism: “the workflow succeeded” becomes “the trace shows governance,” and “the trace exists” becomes “review happened.” The case explicitly blocks that slide.

**Χ — Distance** is decisive. The case is fundamentally about not treating the workflow as self-validating. Χ separates execution from validation, trace production from trace review, internal coherence from external acceptability, and PMS structural fit from real-world adequacy. The strongest Χ test is whether a review structure can say no to the workflow despite traceability, repetition, internal coherence, or attractive results. In this case, Χ is also the main protection against PMS overreach: the analysis itself must not become a closure machine. Overclaim occurs if the workflow, its audit trace, the PMS Core chain, the PMS-QC signature, or the QC-EXT policy check is treated as sufficient warrant.

Χ must be more than a rhetorical pause. It requires an actual separation of roles, criteria, frames, or procedures. A workflow that logs itself but cannot be rejected by anything outside its own output loop has weak Χ. A review body that can only confirm internal success has weak Χ. A structural analysis that says “the chain is coherent, therefore governance is adequate” has suspended Χ. In this case, Χ carries the central anti-circularity discipline.

**Σ — Integration** is weak-to-active and conditional. Governance would require integration among iteration-depth rules, repetition limits, audit traces, monitoring, review criteria, stopping conditions, and accountability structures. But the case material merely names these concerns; it does not show that they are coordinated. A pile of controls is not integration. Σ requires that controls constrain one another in a coherent reviewable procedure. The main calibration issue is avoiding false integration: audit vocabulary, trace coherence, or PMS coherence must not be treated as proof of governance adequacy. Overclaim occurs if Σ is asserted before the procedure, checkpoints, trace semantics, review authority, and stopping rules are specified.

A stronger Σ claim would require evidence that iteration bounds affect stopping rules, that traces contain what reviewers need, that reviewers have criteria and authority, that monitoring can trigger action, and that frame shifts are logged rather than silently absorbed. The current case can identify these requirements, but it cannot show they are met.

**Ψ — Self-Binding** is weak-to-active only conditionally. In this case Ψ cannot mean machine subjectivity, consciousness, or inner commitment. It can only mean durable governance self-binding: a policy, institution, procedure, or review regime binds itself across time to limits, review obligations, non-self-validation, and stable constraint enforcement. The main Ψ question is what prevents the governance regime from suspending its own limits when repeated workflow output appears useful, efficient, attractive, or internally coherent. Overclaim occurs if the workflow itself is described as self-binding without an explicitly scoped structural or institutional meaning.

Ψ remains conditional because the case does not specify who or what binds itself. A policy might bind review procedure. An institution might bind itself to audit thresholds. A workflow controller might enforce a maximum iteration depth. But those are not supplied. Therefore Ψ functions as a requirement and analytic test, not as an established result.

The dependency risks are significant. Δ can energize premature valid/invalid distinctions. ∇ can shape the frame rather than submit to it. □ can produce expectations that convert every absence into failure. Λ can stabilize into Α if non-review becomes routine. Α can generate Ω if stabilized technical routines become opaque or authoritative. Ω accumulates over Θ if review is delayed or inaccessible. Θ can enable Φ as retrospective rationalization rather than genuine review. Φ can undermine Χ if reframing merely protects the workflow. Χ can remain empty if it never integrates into procedure. Σ can be overclaimed if coherence is treated as adequacy. Ψ can be falsely attributed to the workflow rather than to explicit governance structures.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The Core YAML emphasizes that later operators cannot be used cheaply. This is especially important because the case strongly invites smooth operator mapping. It is easy to say that a workflow has distinctions, direction, frame, trace, iteration, review, integration, and self-binding. But PMS dependency discipline asks whether each later operator is warranted by what has been specified.

Σ and Ψ remain conditional because their prerequisites are only partially present. Σ depends on Χ and Φ. That means integration is not just the presence of multiple controls. It requires reflective distance and recontextualization disciplined enough to integrate conflicting or separate elements into a coherent whole. If audit traces, repetition limits, monitoring rules, and review language exist separately, Σ is not yet established. It becomes stronger only if the case specifies how those elements coordinate, constrain one another, and remain reviewable.

Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. That means self-binding cannot be claimed merely because the workflow has a rule, loop, limit, or trace. A governance regime must bind itself across time to integrated constraints while preserving distance from its own outputs. In this case, that would require explicit policy or institutional mechanisms that prevent the governance structure from suspending its own limits under pressure of utility, efficiency, or attractive results. Without such mechanisms, Ψ remains weak, analytic, or under-specified.

Coherence is not proof. This is one of the central lessons of the case. The PMS Core analysis is coherent. The PMS-QC macro signature is coherent. The QC-EXT policy check is coherent. But coherence does not establish empirical validity, physical adequacy, implementation sufficiency, legal legitimacy, or governance success. Indeed, the YAML marks coherence risk as high precisely because the case fits PMS smoothly. A too-elegant structural mapping may under-discriminate if it fails to distinguish audit trace from validation, repetition from reliable governance, governance vocabulary from actual governance, or PMS coherence from external adequacy.

Non-capture is not failure. If quantum-mechanical correctness, hardware calibration, formal verification, statistical validity, legal admissibility, or institutional authority remains outside PMS, this does not mean PMS has failed. It means PMS has correctly preserved its boundary. Rival formalisms may be better for specific tasks. A structural grammar of praxis should not pretend to replace circuit semantics, quantum process theory, formal verification, complexity theory, empirical benchmarking, or legal analysis.

PMS must not become a closure machine. The case explicitly concerns a workflow that must not validate itself. The same rule applies to the analysis. PMS cannot be used to say: because the workflow maps cleanly onto Δ–Ψ, governance is complete. PMS-QC cannot say: because the macro signature is well formed, the quantum workflow is valid. PMS-QC-EXT cannot say: because policy checks pass, deployment is safe. Each layer must preserve Χ and non-capture.

Invalid dependency shortcuts include: Δ/∇ directly to Ψ, where a salient distinction and closure pressure become binding governance without frame, residue, distance, or integration; □ directly to Σ, where declaring a frame is treated as integrated governance; Λ directly to drift, where missing information is treated as failure; Α directly to reliability, where repetition is treated as correctness; Ω directly to blame, where asymmetry becomes moral accusation; Θ directly to boundedness, where countability is treated as governance; Φ directly to adequacy, where reframing becomes rationalization; and Χ directly to action, where reflective distance is claimed but never operationalized.

The dependency discipline therefore keeps the case reversible. It allows the analysis to say that PMS Core makes the governance structure visible; PMS-QC makes QC-specific annotation and audit constraints visible; PMS-QC-EXT shows which paper-specific refinements are weak, absent, or boundary-relevant. But none of these layers may skip the evidence required for stronger claims.

### Derived structures

The derived structures are formula-derived PMS constructs. They are not primitive axioms, independent layers, diagnoses, scores, or person evaluations. In this case, each derived structure must be read through its PMS formula and its evidential status.

**Awareness_A** is active but formal only. Its formula is Θ, □, Δ: sustained, framed differentiation across time. In the case, Awareness_A appears as the structural requirement to distinguish run state, iteration depth, trace status, review status, limit status, halt status, and validation status across the workflow’s temporal history. This is not phenomenological awareness. It does not imply consciousness in the workflow. It simply means that a governed workflow must preserve relevant distinctions over time within a declared frame.

Awareness_A is important because auditability depends on sustained differentiation. If a workflow cannot distinguish run status from review status, or trace status from validation status, it cannot be structurally audited in the relevant sense. Yet Awareness_A does not establish Action_E, Responsibility_R, or Ψ. A system can preserve distinctions without acting on them, owning them, or binding itself to them.

**Coherence_C** is weak-to-active, but carefully bounded. Its formula is Θ, Λ, □, ∇: temporally stabilized structuring of directional pressure and expectation within a frame. The case can support Coherence_C insofar as workflow-directedness, governance expectations, expected review events, possible non-events, and temporal sequence can form a coherent governance narrative. But this coherence is not validation. It does not prove that the workflow is correct, that the trace is sufficient, or that governance has occurred. Coherence_C is therefore useful but dangerous if over-read.

The analysis explicitly marks coherence risk as high. The case can sound coherent precisely because PMS makes it legible. That makes Coherence_C a double-edged derived structure. It helps identify how the case holds together, but it also risks smoothing away the very gaps the analysis is meant to preserve. The correct use of Coherence_C is therefore restrained: it describes structural organization, not adequacy.

**Responsibility_R** is weak and conditional. Its formula is Ψ, Φ, Θ, Ω: self-binding orientation toward asymmetry extended across time and recontextualization. The case implies the problem of responsibility but does not specify responsible actors, offices, institutions, review procedures, or authority relations. Responsibility_R would become stronger only if the case identified who has obligation, who has review authority, who can halt the workflow, who bears exposure, how obligations persist over time, and how recontextualization can revise or contest responsibility assignments. Without those details, Responsibility_R remains structurally possible but not established.

This weak status is not a deficiency. It is a boundary safeguard. The case concerns governance, but governance language alone does not identify responsibility. Responsibility requires more than a workflow, trace, or policy phrase. It requires role structure, asymmetry, temporality, review, and durable binding. The analysis therefore does not assign responsibility; it identifies what a responsibility claim would require.

**Action_E** is under-specified. Its formula is Σ, Θ, ∇: integrated realization of directedness across time. The case concerns conditions for governed action, but it does not specify a concrete intervention, halt, review decision, deployment, rejection, escalation, or revised workflow. Action_E cannot be inferred from audit vocabulary or conceptual governance aspirations. It would require integrated controls enacted over time under a directed workflow pressure. The case does not supply that.

Action_E is particularly important because it prevents audit from substituting for governance. A workflow may be highly monitored and still not produce governed action. A review process may be well described and still lack the authority to halt or revise. Therefore the under-specified status of Action_E protects the analysis from mistaking awareness or coherence for enactment.

**Dignity_in_Practice_D** is an application constraint, not an ontological dignity claim. Its formula is Ψ, Χ, Ω: self-bound reflective restraint and protection under asymmetry. In this case, D functions as a misuse guard. It requires that asymmetry be handled without shaming, person-ranking, tribunal posture, irreversible labeling, or coercive closure. Since the case does not provide a concrete human exposure scene, D does not produce a substantive human-facing finding. It constrains the analysis method: if the artefact were later used in governance, it must not become a way to rank persons, institutions, teams, or users.

Dignity-in-Practice also matters because QC workflows can be embedded in institutions. If this structural artefact later informs review practices, it must not become a proxy for blame, competence ranking, or legitimacy hierarchy. D constrains the application of the analysis, not the ontology of the workflow.

**SELF_FIXPOINT** is analytic-only and not established. Its full formula sequence is Ψ ∘ Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ. The case does not warrant a self-fixpoint claim. It does not establish artificial selfhood, machine subjectivity, or a complete self-binding system. At most, SELF_FIXPOINT functions as an analytic test: could a governance regime bind repeated workflow patterns into a stable, reflexively modulated, externally accountable procedure? The answer remains conditional. The case names the need for such a structure; it does not demonstrate its existence.

The derived-structure discipline therefore mirrors the operator discipline. Awareness_A and Coherence_C are the most available structures, but both must remain non-phenomenological and non-validating. Responsibility_R and Action_E are weak or under-specified. Dignity-in-Practice_D is an application constraint. SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only. None of these structures becomes a primitive. None authorizes scoring, diagnosis, legal judgment, implementation validation, or PMS Base validation.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS drift-pattern analysis remains deliberately cautious. The YAML artefacts do not classify an established drift. They identify possible structural risks that would become relevant if a concrete workflow showed certain patterns. This distinction is essential: a risk is not a finding, under-specification is not failure, and non-assignment is not a low score.

The first drift-pattern candidate is **AD_A>>E**, excessive distance between Awareness and Enactment. In the completed YAML analysis, this is marked as possible but not entailed. The case could drift toward AD_A>>E if audit awareness expands while concrete governed action remains suppressed, delayed, or absent. That would mean the workflow produces more monitoring, more trace material, more review vocabulary, and more evaluative distance, but no corresponding integrated capacity to interrupt, revise, halt, or reject the workflow.

In this case, however, such drift is not established. The case material names audit traces, monitoring, review, iteration depth, and repetition limits, but it does not show that awareness has become over-expanded. It also does not show that action has been suppressed. The YAML therefore marks the relevant axis effects as under-specified rather than active: the awareness axis is under-specified, and the action axis is under-specified. This is the right result. A case about audit depth should not automatically be read as audit inflation. Review may be necessary. Traceability may be appropriate. Delay may be justified. PMS only marks the drift risk that would arise if the awareness apparatus substituted for enacted governance.

The second drift-pattern candidate is **AD_Sigma_low**, fragmented integration. This too is marked as possible but not entailed. The risk would appear if iteration limits, repetition controls, audit traces, monitoring routines, review criteria, stopping conditions, and accountability structures existed as disconnected pieces. A system could have many governance artefacts without those artefacts constraining one another. It could log without review, review without stopping power, stop without criteria, or impose limits without trace semantics. That would be a low-Σ configuration: many components, little integration.

Again, the YAML does not classify AD_Sigma_low as present. It marks the coherence axis and responsibility axis as under-specified. This is not a failure of the case; it is a disciplined refusal to infer fragmentation from missing detail. The case material is too abstract to show whether controls are integrated or disconnected. Therefore AD_Sigma_low remains a structural risk, not a finding.

These drift-pattern statuses also support the broader coherence-risk warning. The case fits PMS extremely well: workflow iteration maps to Θ, audit trace to Δ/Λ/□ relations, governance frame to □, non-self-validation to Χ, integration to Σ, and durable constraint to Ψ. But that smooth fit can itself become under-discriminating. The analysis could appear to have solved the problem merely because it can name the relevant operators. PMS therefore treats the drift catalogue as a guardrail against over-smooth reading. Drift labels must not become a verdict engine. They mark possible structural distortions that require concrete evidence.

The most important point is that **non-capture is preserved inside the drift analysis itself**. If the PMS reading cannot determine whether an audit trace is technically meaningful, that is not PMS drift. If no concrete review architecture is supplied, that is under-specification, not immaturity. If a rival formalism models iteration governance more sharply, that is not a defect in the case. If a workflow resists PMS translation, that resistance must not automatically be redescribed as incomplete integration, recontextualization failure, or drift. The drift-pattern section is therefore not a hidden scoring layer. It is a set of conditional warnings about how a structural analysis could be misused if it became too confident.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant add-ons here are **PMS-QC** and **PMS-QC-EXT**. Both are overlays or application profiles only. PMS.yaml remains the only PMS Base reference. Neither QC layer redefines PMS operators, changes PMS dependencies, adds new PMS operators, or revises the Core pass. Their reduced signatures are overlay shorthand for annotation, audit, documentation, comparison, and policy-layer inspection.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer because the case explicitly concerns a quantum-computing workflow. PMS Core can already describe the general governance structure: framed iteration, meaningful absence, recurrence, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and conditional self-binding. But PMS Core does not isolate QC-specific annotation questions such as macro-level workflow rendering, measurement-adjacent audit traces, iteration-depth policy checks, or the distinction between structural QC notation and physical QC validity. PMS-QC is justified because it makes those QC-specific boundaries visible.

The PMS-QC overlay adds several things beyond the Core pass. It introduces a QC frame vocabulary, macro language, policy checks, and non-guarantee clauses. These are not PMS operators. They are overlay handles. The most important active QC macro is **QFRAME**, because the workflow must be declared inside a problem/governance frame. In this case, QFRAME is active as `problem: qc_workflow_governance`. The frame is not a physical theory; it is the structural declaration that the workflow is being treated as governable through iteration, trace, monitoring, and review.

The second active QC macro is **QITERATE**. It is active because iteration depth and repetition limits are the center of the case. However, QITERATE is also under-specified because no value of k is supplied, no repeated block is identified, and no maximum k_max is declared. PMS-QC can therefore say that iteration governance is structurally relevant, but it cannot say whether the iteration depth is bounded appropriately.

The third relevant macro is **QMEASURE**, but its status is weak. Audit traces are measurement-adjacent in a structural sense because they record selected outcomes or reviewable states while leaving alternatives, absences, and non-selected paths relevant. But no measurement basis, register, sampling rule, physical measurement operation, or trace-validity semantics are supplied. QMEASURE therefore cannot be treated as established physical measurement. It functions only as a cautious overlay shorthand for audit-trace interpretation.

The fourth active macro is **QSTABILIZE**, but only as policy-constraint shorthand. It marks the need for iteration-depth bounds, trace preservation, non-self-validation, and external review. It does not imply physical error correction, stabilizer-code semantics, convergence, safety, or durable governance success. The case can say that stabilization is structurally required; it cannot say that stabilization has been implemented.

Other PMS-QC macros remain weak or not applicable. **QPREP** is weak because no register initialization, superposition preparation, entanglement preparation, or input-state preparation is supplied. **QORACLE_CALL** is weak because the case might involve opaque or oracle-like operations, but no actual oracle, controlled-U, marked-state operation, or query structure is specified. **QDIFFUSION** is not applicable because no Grover-like diffusion or amplitude amplification is given. **QFT_ALIGN** is not applicable because no Fourier-domain alignment, phase estimation, inverse QFT, or basis-shift procedure is supplied.

The PMS-QC reduced signature for the case is therefore deliberately skeletal:

`QFRAME(problem:qc_workflow_governance) -> QITERATE(k:unspecified, block:under_specified_qc_workflow) -> QMEASURE(frame:under_specified_audit_trace) -> QSTABILIZE(policy:non_self_validation, bounds:k_max_unspecified, review:external_required)`

This signature is useful because it makes the missing pieces visible. It says: the case has a problem/governance frame, an under-specified iterative block, an under-specified audit or measurement-like record, and a policy-stabilization requirement. It does not say that the workflow is physically valid, technically correct, convergent, safe, deployable, or legally auditable.

The **measurement_requires_meas_frame** policy is risk-level because the audit or measurement frame is under-specified. PMS-QC therefore adds a valuable audit vocabulary. It can ask whether iteration is bounded, whether measurement-like records have a declared frame, whether Ω-heavy structures are framed, whether Σ boundaries are protected, and whether long temporal chains have sufficient stabilization checkpoints. But all of these remain structural checks. Passing them would not prove physical validity, and in this case several cannot even be fully checked because the concrete workflow is absent.

The second overlay, **PMS-QC-EXT**, is even more cautious. It is an optional paper-specific refinement layer. It is not a generic QC standard, not a canonical PMS layer, and not a replacement for PMS-QC base semantics. In this case, it is weakly triggered because the case concerns iteration depth, audit trace, and review. However, the trigger is not algorithm-specific. ### Layer interaction

The layer interaction is clean because each layer has a distinct role. PMS Core supplies the canonical Δ–Ψ operator grammar. PMS-QC supplies a QC-specific annotation and policy overlay. PMS-QC-EXT supplies optional paper-specific refinement checks. None of the overlays redefines the Core operators or their dependencies.

What remains PMS Core is the fundamental structural reading: the case is about frame-bound iteration governance under non-self-validation. Δ distinguishes trace from validation and execution from review. ∇ marks workflow-directedness. □ declares the governance frame. Λ preserves missing or absent expected review events. Α marks repeated or stabilized patterns. Ω marks possible asymmetry in opacity, authority, exposure, or review access. Θ marks iteration depth, sequence, monitoring, and review timing. Φ marks recontextualization of workflow outputs and traces into governance review. Χ prevents self-validation. Σ remains conditional on integrated controls. Ψ remains conditional on durable governance self-binding.

PMS-QC sharpens what is quantum-workflow-specific. It does not change what Δ, Θ, Χ, or Ψ mean. Instead, it gives overlay handles such as QFRAME, QITERATE, QMEASURE, and QSTABILIZE. These make it easier to render the workflow as a macro-level audit trace. They also highlight missing parameters: k, k_max, repeated block, measurement or audit frame, trace semantics, and stabilization rule. PMS Core can say that iteration and audit require governance; PMS-QC can show which QC overlay fields remain under-specified.

PMS-QC-EXT sharpens the boundary between generic QC annotation and paper-specific algorithmic refinement. It asks whether QPE, Grover, attractor schedule, measurement projection, and basis-reframe policies are warranted. In this case, it mostly answers no. That negative answer prevents overlay overreach. It shows that paper-specific templates must not be silently promoted into base QC analysis.

The overlays must not be allowed to redefine PMS operators. QITERATE is not Θ. It is an overlay macro that expands through Θ-like iteration structure while remaining subordinate to PMS. QSTABILIZE is not Ψ. It is a policy shorthand that may point toward Ψ-like constraint stability, but it does not establish self-binding. QMEASURE is not Δ/Λ/□ as PMS Base; it is a QC macro that can be rendered through distinctions, unrealized alternatives, and frame. MEASUREMENT_PROJECTION_MODEL is not a physical theory of measurement; it is an EXT modelling refinement for audit-branching discipline.

Overlay language could overreach in several ways. PMS-QC could make the case look more implementation-ready than it is. A macro program can look like a workflow even when the repeated block is unspecified. A policy check can look like governance even when no review authority exists. A measurement-adjacent trace can look like a physical measurement even when no basis, register, or sampling rule is supplied. PMS-QC-EXT could overreach by making weak applicability look like algorithm-specific activation. The completed YAML analyses prevent this by repeatedly marking weak, under-specified, not applicable, non-guaranteeing, and overlay-only statuses.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. PMS Core preserves Λ around missing review, absent trace, untriggered halt, absent validation, and unresolved governance expectations. PMS-QC preserves under-specification around k, k_max, repeated block, measurement frame, oracle operations, and stabilization frequency. PMS-QC-EXT preserves non-applicability of QPE and Grover refinements and weak status of measurement projection and attractor-cycle integrity. The layers therefore interact by increasing specificity while also increasing boundary discipline.

### Example 1 — A trace exists, but review has not occurred

**Type:** micro
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Scene and frame structure,” before the PMS Core structural reading.

A workflow run ends and produces a detailed audit trace. The trace records the number of iterations, the time of the run, selected intermediate markers, and the final output state as represented by the workflow’s logging layer. A reviewer later opens the trace and sees that the workflow completed without internal interruption. Nothing in the vignette says that the trace is false, incomplete, or misleading. The point is more basic: the existence of the trace is not yet the same as review.

This example demonstrates the central distinction between **trace production** and **trace interpretation**. A workflow may log what happened, but the log does not by itself decide whether the iteration depth was appropriate, whether repetition limits were meaningful, whether the stopping condition was adequate, or whether the output should be accepted. The audit trace is structurally important because it creates material for later inspection, but it is not the inspection itself.

The PMS Core hooks are straightforward. **Δ** distinguishes trace from validation, execution from review, and completion from acceptance. **□** is required because the trace becomes meaningful only inside a declared audit or governance frame: what was expected to be logged, what counts as sufficient trace detail, and who can interpret it. **Λ** may appear if an expected review step, halt trigger, or validation event is absent. **Θ** matters because the trace is produced at one time and reviewed at another; review after the fact is not identical to review that can still interrupt the workflow. **Χ** is the decisive operator: it prevents the workflow’s own trace from becoming its own warrant.

The QC overlay is relevant only as annotation. PMS-QC may render the scene through a weak **QMEASURE-like** audit record, but no physical measurement claim follows. There is no specified basis, register, sampling rule, or quantum measurement operation. PMS-QC-EXT may weakly invoke measurement-domain audit discipline, but only to ask what was selected, what remained unrealized, and which frame made the record meaningful. MIP and AHP are not triggered.

The forbidden claims are therefore clear. The example does not show that the quantum workflow is correct, safe, convergent, legally auditable, physically valid, or implementation-ready. It does not show that review failed. It only shows that review has not been established by trace existence alone.

The non-capture note is equally important: whether the trace is technically adequate depends on QC-specific, engineering, institutional, and possibly legal criteria outside PMS. The article takeaway is that **logging is a precondition for review, not a substitute for review**.

### Example 2 — A bounded loop without an integrated governance procedure

**Type:** compact
**Recommended insertion point:** inside “PMS Core structural reading,” after the discussion of Χ, Σ, and Ψ.

A workflow specification states that a quantum-computing routine may repeat a given computational block no more than fifty times. Each run produces an audit record, and the record is stored in a review folder. At first glance, this looks like governance: there is a repetition limit, a trace, and a place where review could occur. Yet the vignette leaves several details unspecified. It does not say what the repeated block is, why fifty is the relevant upper bound, what happens if the limit is reached, who reviews the record, whether review can reject the output, or whether the workflow can continue under a new label after hitting the limit.

This example demonstrates why **Σ** cannot be inferred from the mere presence of multiple controls. Iteration depth, repetition limits, trace storage, and review vocabulary may coexist without forming an integrated governance procedure. The controls must constrain one another. A limit that is not connected to a stopping rule is weak. A trace that is not connected to review authority is weak. A review folder that has no rejection criterion is weak. A repetition rule that can be silently bypassed by reframing the run as a new workflow is weak.

The PMS Core hooks show the structure. **Δ** distinguishes limit, trace, review, halt, and validation. **∇** names the workflow pressure toward continued execution or repeated optimization. **□** defines the governance frame in which “fifty iterations” is supposed to matter. **Λ** appears where expected governance events are absent: no review decision, no halt rule, no explicit rejection path, no stated reason for the threshold. **Α** may form if repeated reliance on the same limit-and-log routine becomes a stable pattern. **Ω** may arise if the people or systems exposed to outputs cannot inspect or contest the limit-setting procedure. **Θ** is central because iteration depth is a temporal and cumulative structure, not just a number. **Φ** becomes risky if the workflow can be recontextualized after the fact to avoid the limit. **Χ** requires separation between “the workflow stayed inside its own loop rule” and “the workflow has been externally validated.” **Σ** remains weak unless the controls are coordinated. **Ψ** remains weak unless the governance regime binds itself across time to enforce the constraint rather than waive it when results look useful.

The QC overlay sharpens the same point. PMS-QC would mark **QITERATE** as active but under-specified: k is bounded in the vignette, but the block, k_max justification, and stopping semantics remain unclear. **QSTABILIZE** is only policy shorthand unless an actual invariant, checkpoint, or review rule is supplied. PMS-QC-EXT adds a useful negative guard: this is not automatically a QPE ladder, a Grover fixed-point refinement, or an attractor schedule. The only EXT-relevant issue is a weak audit-policy question: if a Σ checkpoint is later claimed, any reframing after that point must be explicit and Ψ-bound.

MIP and AHP are still not active. This is not a governance evaluation report, and no analysis-artifact hardening layer has been invoked. The example does not score the workflow or assess institutional maturity.

The forbidden claims are that the workflow is governed, safe, valid, compliant, or technically adequate merely because it has a loop limit and audit folder. The non-capture note is that real adequacy would require QC-specific justification, software assurance, review authority, and possibly legal or institutional validation. The article takeaway is that **bounded repetition is not yet integrated governance**.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The completed YAML analyses preserve multiple non-capture zones. PMS does not capture quantum-computing technical correctness. PMS-QC does not replace quantum mechanics, quantum information theory, quantum process theory, circuit semantics, formal verification, complexity theory, hardware calibration, or empirical benchmarking. PMS-QC-EXT does not replace algorithm-specific formalisms or physical measurement theory. Legal, institutional, and governance legitimacy also remain outside the PMS/QC overlay stack unless separately supplied.

One rival reading treats the case as a technical verification problem. On this view, the central question is not praxeological governance but whether the workflow satisfies formal correctness properties, circuit equivalence, error bounds, sampling guarantees, or algorithm-specific validity. This rival reading preserves details that PMS does not supply: mathematical proof, formal semantics, circuit-level specification, and technical correctness criteria.

A second rival reading treats the case as software assurance or systems engineering. Here the focus would be version control, logging architecture, reproducibility, configuration management, testing, verification, validation, and failure recovery. PMS-QC can annotate macro-level governance structure, but it does not replace engineering assurance. This rival reading preserves implementation granularity and operational responsibility.

A third rival reading treats the case as epistemology of validation. The key issue would be circularity: when does a system’s own output, trace, or repeated success count as evidence? This reading overlaps strongly with the PMS emphasis on Χ, but it may formulate the problem in terms of justification, regress, evidence, warrant, or anti-circularity rather than Δ–Ψ operator chains. It preserves epistemic vocabulary that PMS intentionally does not reduce to a final theory.

A fourth rival reading treats the case as institutional accountability. The relevant questions would concern who authorizes the workflow, who reviews it, who can halt it, who bears downstream risk, who owns trace interpretation, and which governance body has legitimate authority. PMS marks these as Ω, Θ, Σ, and Ψ calibration points, but it does not supply the institutional design. This rival reading preserves organizational and procedural specificity.

A fifth rival reading treats the case as AI-safety or automation-governance. It would focus on delegation, oversight, human-in-the-loop structures, escalation paths, monitoring, audit trails, safety cases, and deployment constraints. PMS-QC can support annotation and audit scaffolding, but it does not validate an AI-control architecture. This rival reading preserves safety-engineering demands that the structural analysis cannot satisfy by itself.

A sixth rival reading treats the case through formal QC models such as ZX-calculus, categorical quantum mechanics, quantum process theory, type systems, or formal verification. PMS-QC explicitly preserves these rival formalisms. They may be superior for physical, algorithmic, or semantic precision. PMS-QC’s contribution is structural documentation and governance annotation, not replacement of QC theory.

A seventh anti-capture reading would argue that the very attempt to render quantum workflow governance through PMS-QC risks over-assimilation. This reading preserves the possibility that the workflow’s most important features are not captured by praxeological operators or macro signatures. The YAML analyses accept this possibility. They do not treat resistance, mismatch, missing evidence, or rival formalization as drift by default.

These rival readings are not defects. They are part of the case’s boundary discipline. AXIOM does not show its strength by eliminating them. It shows its strength by making visible what PMS and its overlays can map, and by preserving what they cannot.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would be weakened if the case turned out to be only a narrow mathematical parameter-setting problem with no governance frame, no review condition, no asymmetry, no temporality beyond a static parameter, and no non-self-validation issue. In that situation, PMS would be overbuilt for the case. It would be mapping a governance problem that the material does not actually contain.

The Χ-centered reading would be weakened if the concrete workflow already had a fully external validation structure, independent review authority, clear rejection criteria, and non-self-validation safeguards, and the only issue were runtime optimization. Χ would still matter as a general guardrail, but it would not be the central unresolved operator.

The Ω reading would be weakened if no directional imbalance exists: no asymmetry of review access, authority, opacity, exposure, capacity, obligation, or downstream risk. If every relevant role had equal access, equal capacity, equal exposure, and equal authority, Ω would be weak. The present case does not supply enough information to establish that, so Ω remains active but calibration-sensitive.

The Λ reading would be weakened if the case specified no expected governance event. Λ requires an expectation within a frame. If there is no expected trace, no review, no halt, no validation step, no trigger, and no missing evidence, then absence cannot be treated as structurally meaningful. The current case implies such expectations through governance vocabulary, but a concrete scene would need to define them.

The Α reading would be weakened if the workflow is a single non-repeated event with no recurrent pattern, no iteration, no stabilized trace routine, no repeated review structure, and no path dependence. Since the case explicitly names iteration depth and repetition limits, Α remains relevant, but concrete recurrence is not demonstrated.

The Θ reading would be weakened if the case were reducible to a static parameter with no sequence, persistence, delay, review window, cumulative history, or temporal governance. That is unlikely given the title, but the exact temporal structure remains under-specified.

The Φ reading would be weakened if the case never moves outputs, traces, or workflow states between frames. If there is no execution frame, review frame, audit frame, or governance reinterpretation, then recontextualization would be weak. In the current case, Φ is relevant because audit and review imply frame movement, but the mechanism is not supplied.

The Σ reading would be weakened if controls exist only as separate artefacts and do not constrain one another. If iteration limits, audit traces, monitoring, review criteria, and stopping conditions are merely listed rather than integrated, Σ remains weak. A strong Σ claim would require evidence of coordinated governance procedure.

The Ψ reading would be weakened if no durable self-binding rule, institution, policy, or accountability mechanism exists. If the workflow or governance regime can revise, suspend, or ignore its constraints whenever convenient, Ψ is not established. If the workflow itself is described as self-binding without a structural or institutional meaning, the analysis overclaims.

The PMS-QC reading would be weakened if the case did not actually concern a QC workflow. If “QC” were merely a metaphor, the QC overlay would be unjustified. It would also be weakened if no macro-level workflow structure could be identified: no frame, no iteration, no measurement-adjacent record, no policy-stabilization concern. Conversely, it would be overextended if the skeletal macro program were treated as a real circuit, implementation, or validation architecture.

The PMS-QC-EXT reading would be weakened if its optional refinements were used as active algorithmic claims without evidence. It would become over-assimilative if QPE_ORACLE_LADDER, GROVER_CENTERING, qpe_exact, grover_fixed_point, or attractor_alignment were imported merely because the case is about quantum computing. The completed EXT analysis avoids this by marking those structures not applicable.

The reading would become under-discriminating if it failed to distinguish audit trace from validation, repetition from reliability, coherence from adequacy, governance vocabulary from actual governance, structural reviewability from technical verification, and macro signature from implementation. These are the key discrimination tests. The analysis partially satisfies them by explicitly marking boundaries, but stronger satisfaction would require concrete evidence.

The reading would become over-assimilative if missing evidence, rival formalization, disagreement, rupture, under-specification, absent implementation details, or unresolved trace semantics were automatically redescribed as PMS drift, incomplete integration, or maturity failure. The YAML analyses explicitly reject that move.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. To operationalize the case empirically or technically, one would need a specified workflow, algorithm family, circuit or hybrid process, input/output structure, iteration parameter k, maximum iteration k_max, repeated block definition, stopping rule, trace format, measurement or audit frame, review authority, rejection criteria, trace semantics, monitoring schedule, validation protocol, hardware or simulation context, and independent technical benchmarks. To operationalize it institutionally, one would need roles, accountability assignments, escalation paths, contest channels, documentation requirements, and legal or governance criteria. To operationalize it comparatively, one would need rival models and explicit criteria for superior discrimination. None of this is supplied by the case material.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It says nothing about the psychology, competence, intent, maturity, virtue, or pathology of any person, developer, reviewer, user, institution, or team. PMS operators and QC macro signatures are structural descriptors, not clinical or psychological categories.

It must not be used for person ranking. The analysis does not rank agents, institutions, workflows, developers, reviewers, or users. Ω marks structural asymmetry; it does not assign moral superiority or infer blame. Dignity-in-Practice constrains the analysis precisely by blocking tribunal posture and irreversible labels.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusion. Audit trace language in the YAML does not establish admissibility, liability, compliance, negligence, chain-of-custody adequacy, or evidentiary sufficiency. A structurally meaningful trace is not automatically a legally valid trace.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. PMS-QC and PMS-QC-EXT do not make claims about the ontology of quantum states, measurement collapse, machine subjectivity, consciousness, selfhood, or agency. Self-binding language in the case means governance constraint fidelity, not inner commitment.

It must not be used as empirical proof. The analysis does not establish correctness, performance, convergence, sampling validity, error bounds, reproducibility, hardware fidelity, or physical adequacy. Those require domain-specific evidence and methods.

It must not be used as implementation validation. A YAML artefact, operator chain, macro program, policy check, or audit signature is not an implementation. It does not certify software, hardware, circuits, orchestration systems, or deployment pipelines.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. The case shows an application of PMS_1.3 and overlays; it does not prove PMS completeness, minimality, uniqueness, or superiority.

It must not treat add-ons as operators. QFRAME, QITERATE, QMEASURE, QSTABILIZE, MEASUREMENT_PROJECTION_MODEL, QPE_ORACLE_LADDER, GROVER_CENTERING, ATTRACTOR_FIXED_POINT, and related QC constructs are overlay handles or macros. They do not belong to the PMS Base operator inventory. The PMS operator inventory remains Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ.

It must not treat drift as verdict. AD_A>>E, AD_Sigma_low, max_iteration_depth risk, measurement frame risk, unstable asymmetry guard, basis_reframe_guard, and attractor_cycle_integrity are conditional structural risks. They are not findings of failure unless concrete evidence warrants them.

It must not produce closure by authority. The strongest misuse would be to say that because PMS Core, PMS-QC, and PMS-QC-EXT have rendered the workflow legible, the governance problem is solved. That would violate the central lesson of the case. The workflow must not validate itself, and the analysis must not validate itself either.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of this case is a bounded structural rendering of QC workflow governance under non-self-validation. PMS Core shows the underlying praxis grammar: distinctions must be preserved, execution pressure must be framed, expected governance events and absences must be made legible, repetition must not become proof, asymmetry must be calibrated without blame, temporality must be treated as central, recontextualization must not become rationalization, and reflective distance must separate workflow operation from validation. Σ and Ψ remain conditional because integrated controls and durable governance self-binding are not supplied by the case material.

PMS-QC adds a disciplined QC overlay. It shows that the case can be rendered as a problem/governance frame, an under-specified iteration, a weak measurement-adjacent audit record, and a policy-stabilization requirement. But it also shows that this rendering is skeletal. QITERATE is active but lacks k, k_max, and repeated block details. QMEASURE is weak because audit trace is not physical measurement. QSTABILIZE is policy shorthand, not proof of stability. PMS-QC therefore sharpens the case by exposing the missing audit and iteration fields rather than validating the workflow.

PMS-QC-EXT adds a further boundary check. Its main contribution is negative and discriminative. The case does not warrant QPE ladder refinement, Grover centering, QPE exact chains, Grover fixed-point chains, or spectral attractor alignment. MEASUREMENT_PROJECTION_MODEL is only weakly relevant as audit-branching discipline. ATTRACTOR_FIXED_POINT remains a weak future-refinement possibility rather than an active convergence claim. basis_reframe_guard is useful as a post-Σ audit guard, and attractor_cycle_integrity remains a risk-level policy. This means the extension layer does not inflate the case into algorithm-specific sophistication. It protects the case from unwarranted specificity.

The examples contribute by making these boundaries readable. The first example shows that a trace can exist without review having occurred. It demonstrates the central Δ/Χ distinction between logging and validation. The second example shows that a repetition limit and audit folder do not yet amount to integrated governance. It demonstrates why Σ cannot be inferred from the presence of multiple controls and why Ψ cannot be inferred from a rule-like surface. The third example shows why QC-EXT mostly says “not applicable.” It demonstrates that a disciplined overlay can refuse QPE, Grover, and attractor-schedule readings when the case material does not warrant them. Together, the examples do not prove the analysis; they illustrate why its restraint matters.

What remains unclaimed is extensive and important. The article does not claim empirical correctness, physical validity, quantum-mechanical adequacy, convergence, safety, legal sufficiency, implementation readiness, institutional legitimacy, diagnostic relevance, person evaluation, metaphysical truth, or PMS Base validation. It does not claim that the workflow is governed. It does not claim that the audit trace is adequate. It does not claim that a specific review procedure exists. It does not claim that PMS-QC replaces formal QC methods or that PMS-QC-EXT supplies algorithmic identity. It does not even claim that drift is present. It identifies conditional risks and under-specified requirements.

The boundary discipline is the substance of the case. PMS Core keeps the workflow from becoming self-validating. PMS-QC keeps macro annotation from becoming physical or implementation validation. PMS-QC-EXT keeps paper-specific refinements from being silently activated. Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological, not ontological. Drift remains risk, not verdict. Non-capture remains possible. Rival readings remain open. Coherence remains insufficient. Absence remains under-specified unless framed as an expected non-event.

The final methodological takeaway is that AXIOM can make a highly technical governance problem structurally legible without pretending to solve it. The case shows how a workflow can be mapped as a chain of closure-demands: iteration calls for boundedness, traceability calls for review, review calls for authority, authority calls for integration, and integration calls for durable self-binding. But the analysis also shows where each demand must stop. The workflow does not validate itself. The trace does not validate the workflow. The QC overlay does not validate the trace. The EXT layer does not validate the QC overlay. PMS does not validate itself. That chain of restraint is the point: structural clarity is useful only when it keeps Λ-residue visible and refuses closure by its own authority.
