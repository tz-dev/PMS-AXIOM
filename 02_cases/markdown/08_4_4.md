### Why this case matters

“Legitimacy Without Authority” matters because it isolates a recurrent philosophical closure-demand: the demand to decide whether legitimacy can stand when the authority normally expected to ground, certify, ratify, authorize, or settle it is absent. The case is structurally difficult because it does not merely ask whether a specific authority is valid. It asks what happens when legitimacy is claimed, granted, or contested without a recognized authority to stabilize the frame of recognition.

This is a closure-demand because the situation invites an immediate settling move. One can be tempted to say: if no recognized authority exists, then legitimacy is missing. One can also be tempted to say the opposite: if legitimacy appears without authority, then authority was never necessary in the first place. Both moves close too quickly. They convert an absent authority into either a deficit or a liberation without first specifying the operative frame. The PMS analysis refuses that shortcut. It asks: absent relative to which frame? Recognized by whom? Required for what? Claimed by which role? Granted under what conditions? Contested from which position? With what exposure, what reversibility, and what unresolved remainder?

This case belongs in AXIOM because AXIOM is not a doctrine of final answers. It is a cartographic and indexing paper: it maps closure-demands, operator chains, add-on justifications, residue structures, and misuse boundaries. “Legitimacy Without Authority” is a strong AXIOM case because the temptation toward closure is unusually high while the material is deliberately abstract. The case therefore tests whether the stack can preserve structural legibility without pretending to solve legitimacy metaphysically, empirically, legally, clinically, or diagnostically.

The difficulty is also that legitimacy is a concept that easily attracts other registers. It can become legal validity, moral justification, institutional standing, social recognition, theological authorization, democratic mandate, anti-authoritarian refusal, charismatic acceptance, or existential commitment. Without careful boundary discipline, these registers can collapse into each other. PMS Core therefore begins by separating legitimacy from authority, authority from recognition, recognition from legal standing, legal standing from moral truth, moral truth from PMS readability, and PMS readability from validity.

The case is not being solved metaphysically because no theory of legitimacy “in itself” is supplied or warranted. It is not being solved empirically because no evidence, scene, institution, event, or actor is supplied. It is not being solved clinically or diagnostically because no person, psychology, motive, pathology, maturity failure, or competence structure is being assessed. It is not being solved legally because no jurisdiction, authority chain, procedural standing, evidence standard, or enforceable rule is given. It is not being solved by implementation because no governance procedure, computational architecture, policy system, or applied decision mechanism is being specified.

Its philosophical value lies elsewhere. It reveals a structural question: how does a legitimacy claim behave when the expected ratifying authority is missing, disputed, or bracketed? PMS Core can map this as a configuration of difference, impulse, frame, non-event, possible attractor, possible asymmetry, weak temporality, recontextualization, distance, weak integration, and weak self-binding. PMS-CRITIQUE can then ask whether that configuration amounts to critique, judgment, commentary, narrative repair, or merely threshold readability. MIP can ask whether the case is even mature enough for maturity analysis. AHP can ask whether the analysis itself is precise enough to be reused.

The answer is deliberately restrained: this is a powerful conceptual case, but not a concrete praxis scene. Its strength lies in making the limits visible.

The case also matters because “authority” can become a false shortcut. In many practical and philosophical settings, invoking authority appears to settle legitimacy: an office authorizes, a rule permits, a role decides, a procedure ratifies. But the case reverses this expectation. It asks how legitimacy is claimed or contested when the usual settling structure is missing. That does not mean authority is irrelevant. Nor does it mean authority is oppressive by default. It means the absence of recognized authority must be treated as a structured problem rather than a ready-made answer.

A further reason for including this case is that it tests whether the analytic stack can remain non-tribunal under pressure. Legitimacy language often pulls toward judgment: legitimate or illegitimate, authorized or unauthorized, valid or invalid. AXIOM must not replicate that pull. It must show how the closure-demand is built, where the operator chain becomes strong, where it becomes weak, and where rival frameworks must remain alive.

### Claim status and scope discipline

The case is typed as a philosophical structural case and, more specifically, as a conceptual domain projection. It concerns a class of possible legitimacy disputes rather than one concrete event. The claim being made is therefore not “this legitimacy claim is valid” or “this authority is absent in reality.” The claim is: under PMS and its subordinate overlays, the supplied case material can be structurally mapped as a frame-dependent legitimacy problem involving claimed, granted, or contested legitimacy in the absence of recognized authority.

The claim status is conceptual PMS projection. The case is not a PMS-internal proof, not a concrete scene analysis, and not a validation of PMS Base. It uses PMS as the Base grammar for structural reading, PMS-CRITIQUE as an overlay lens, MIP as a downstream working-note boundary check, and AHP as second-order hardening of the analysis artifact. None of these layers is allowed to expand into an authority claim.

The concrete-scene status is explicitly negative. The case does not contain a scene packet in the ordinary sense. It does not tell us who claims legitimacy, who grants it, who contests it, what recognized authority is absent, why that authority matters, what institutional or social frame is operative, what consequences follow, what harms are at stake, or whether the process unfolds over time. Because these elements are absent, the analysis must not assign responsibility, blame, maturity, legal standing, empirical status, diagnosis, or action.

The empirical boundary is strict. No empirical finding is made. The analysis does not establish that such situations occur, how often they occur, what actors do in them, what institutions recognize them, or what outcomes they produce. If empirical use were later intended, the case would require concrete evidence, observable indicators, actor roles, frame definitions, sequence, consequence data, and comparison against rival explanations.

The metaphysical boundary is equally strict. The analysis does not decide whether legitimacy exists independently of authority, whether authority is metaphysically necessary for legitimacy, whether recognition grounds legitimacy, or whether legitimacy is a moral property, institutional status, social fact, or existential relation. PMS does not answer that kind of question here. It only maps how the closure-demand behaves when those questions are structurally present.

The diagnostic boundary is non-negotiable. No actor is diagnosed. No maturity failure, personality pattern, psychological weakness, clinical category, or moral defect is inferred. Resistance, refusal, rupture, disagreement, rival framing, absence, and non-fit are not automatically translated into drift, under-integration, immaturity, recontextualization failure, or defective calibration.

The legal and forensic boundary is also explicit. The analysis does not decide legal authority, jurisdiction, validity, culpability, liability, standing, admissibility, or evidence weight. A legal reading would require a legal frame, competent authority, relevant law, procedural facts, and a concrete dispute. None is supplied. Therefore the case cannot support legal conclusion or forensic use.

The implementation boundary blocks applied overreach. The YAML representation, the PMS case analysis, the PMS-CRITIQUE overlay, the MIP working note, and the AHP hardening pass are not governance systems, decision procedures, software implementations, institutional protocols, or enforcement tools. They are structured analytical artefacts. Operational use would require calibration, external evaluation, accountability, role-specific review, and concrete evidence.

The PMS Base validation boundary is central. The case does not validate PMS. It may show that PMS can render this case structurally legible, but legibility is not proof of completeness, correctness, superiority, or exhaustive capture. PMS coherence is insufficient by itself. A coherent PMS derivation still needs discriminative force: it must distinguish legitimacy without authority from legal invalidity, moral illegitimacy, anti-authoritarian legitimacy, institutional vacuum, coercive pseudo-legitimacy, mere disagreement, symbolic refusal, and non-capture.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. The MIP layer confirms this rather than correcting it. A-score and M-score are not assigned. IA classification is under-specified and explicitly non-classified. The D-module remains off. AHP hardens the analysis artifact but does not supply missing scene data. The result is a bounded, reversible, non-diagnostic, non-tribunal working analysis.

The scope discipline is therefore not ornamental. It is part of the case result. A case about legitimacy without authority is especially vulnerable to being converted into an authority claim by the analysis itself. If PMS, PMS-CRITIQUE, MIP, or AHP were treated as the new ratifying authority, the stack would reproduce the closure problem it is supposed to map. The claim status must therefore remain weaker than the temptation created by the case.

### Scene and frame structure

Because the case is not a concrete scene, its “scene” must be rendered as a conceptual scene structure. The boundary is the class of situations in which legitimacy is claimed, granted, or contested while recognized authority is absent. This is not yet an event. It is a structural configuration.

The frame operator □ is decisive. Legitimacy and authority only become meaningful within frames: legal frames, institutional frames, communal frames, moral frames, theological frames, political frames, recognition frames, anti-authoritarian frames, or philosophical frames. The case does not specify which of these is operative. Therefore PMS must treat frame identification as a calibration requirement, not as a solved condition.

Inside the frame are the structural terms supplied by the case: legitimacy, claim, grant, contestation, absence, and recognized authority. These are not fully defined by the material. The AHP analysis correctly identifies this as the main precision weakness of the analysis artifact: “legitimacy,” “recognized authority,” “claim,” “grant,” and “contestation” remain intentionally under-defined. That under-definition is partly necessary because the case is abstract, but it becomes a reuse risk if later readers silently import their own definitions.

Outside the frame are all stronger claims: the truth of legitimacy, legal standing, moral justification, empirical occurrence, psychological motive, authority validity, enforcement, diagnosis, and implementation. These cannot be smuggled in through the language of structural readability.

The protected constraints are reversibility, distance, non-person-ranking, Dignity-in-Practice as application discipline, non-capture openness, and layer separation. PMS remains Base. PMS-CRITIQUE remains overlay. MIP remains downstream working-note analysis. AHP remains second-order hardening. No layer may redefine the previous one.

The roles are abstract only. The PMS and MIP analyses identify possible conceptual roles: a legitimacy claimant, a legitimacy granter or recognizer, a legitimacy contester, an absent or unrecognized authority, and the analyst. These are not persons. They are role placeholders. No one is assigned awareness, responsibility, power-to-act, maturity, blame, or dignity profile.

Temporality Θ is weak. A legitimacy dispute could unfold over time: claims may persist, recognition may be delayed, contestation may intensify, absence may harden, and authority may emerge or remain unavailable. But the supplied case material does not include chronology. There is no sequence, review window, accumulation, residue trail, institutional memory, or durable commitment. Θ is therefore analytically relevant but not established.

The reversibility window exists primarily at the analysis level. The reading remains revisable because later concrete material could change the frame, weaken operators, activate new risks, or displace PMS in favor of a rival formalization. Case-internal reversibility cannot be assessed. No appeal, exit, review, pause, repair, or correction path is supplied.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are possible but low-resolution. Legitimacy without recognized authority may create uneven exposure: one role may bear the cost of claiming legitimacy without recognition; another may gain power by granting recognition; another may bear burden by contesting; an absent authority may leave affected parties without procedural settlement. But none of this is concrete. Ω must remain weak or low-resolution until roles and costs are specified.

The case therefore sits in a paradoxical position: it has enough structure to be analytically meaningful, but not enough scene material to be adjudicated. This is not a deficiency in the case. It is the point of the case. “Legitimacy Without Authority” tests whether the analysis can remain useful while refusing to become the authority it lacks.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core analysis proceeds through the eleven operators in order. This order matters because later operators cannot be used cheaply. The case is most strongly supported at the level of Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Φ, and Χ. It is weaker at Α, Ω, Θ, Σ, and Ψ because those require recurrence, concrete asymmetry, temporal development, integration, or self-binding that the case material does not supply.

**Δ — Difference** is active and foundational. The case begins with distinctions: legitimacy versus authority, recognized versus unrecognized authority, claim versus grant, grant versus contestation, structural readability versus validity. The legitimacy/authority distinction is the main entry point. If legitimacy were simply identical to recognized authority, the case would collapse. If absence of recognized authority automatically meant illegitimacy, the case would also collapse. PMS therefore holds the distinction open.

The calibration requirement is to specify which distinction is doing work in a given reading. A legal reading may distinguish authority from validity; a moral reading may distinguish legitimacy from institutional power; a recognition reading may distinguish formal mandate from social uptake. PMS can host these distinctions but cannot silently choose one. Overclaim would occur if Δ were treated as proof that legitimacy and authority are completely separable in all frames, or conversely if the distinction were erased by authority-default assumptions.

**∇ — Impulse** is active as directional pressure. The case generates moral-practical tension: pressure to justify legitimacy, accept it, refuse it, contest it, seek authority, reject authority, or reframe the problem. This is not psychological motive. PMS does not infer desire, will, resentment, bad faith, courage, maturity, or pathology. ∇ is only the structural pressure created by the difference.

The calibration issue is to keep impulse as directional tension rather than hidden diagnosis. It is legitimate to say that the case produces a demand for response. It is not legitimate to infer that a claimant must be seeking power, that a contester must be resisting maturity, or that a granter must be conferring valid recognition. ∇ names pressure, not interiority.

**□ — Frame** is active and central. Legitimacy without authority cannot be interpreted without a frame. In a legal frame, recognized authority may be indispensable. In an anti-authoritarian frame, its absence may be precisely what allows legitimacy. In a communal-recognition frame, legitimacy may arise from relevant participants rather than formal office. In a theological frame, authority may be recognized differently. In a philosophical frame, the case may remain a conceptual boundary problem.

The key calibration issue is that □ must be named before Λ can be evaluated. Authority absence means different things in different frames. Overclaim occurs when the analysis silently uses one frame while pretending to speak for all frames. PMS must not smuggle in a legal frame, anti-authoritarian frame, or moral frame as the default frame of legitimacy.

**Λ — Non-Event** is active as structured absence. The absence of recognized authority is the strongest structural feature of the case. However, PMS treats it as frame-relative. A non-event is not mere nothingness; it is an absence that matters because something was expected within a frame. If a frame expects recognized authority to ratify legitimacy, then the absence is a meaningful non-event. If a frame does not require recognized authority, then the same absence may not be a deficit at all.

Calibration for Λ therefore requires specifying the expected event: recognition by whom, under which frame, for what purpose? Was an authority expected to authorize, adjudicate, ratify, witness, certify, permit, recognize, or enforce? Without that specification, Λ remains structurally visible but not normatively decisive. Overclaim would occur if authority absence were automatically interpreted as failure, invalidity, immaturity, or incompleteness.

**Α — Attractor** is weak. The case mentions legitimacy being claimed, granted, or contested, but it does not show recurrence. There is no repeated pattern, stable script, institutional routine, habitual recognition loop, or path-dependent legitimacy practice. Α could become relevant if, for example, legitimacy without authority repeatedly stabilized into a recognizable pattern or if contestation became a default script. But that is not supplied.

This is one of the first points at which the analysis must slow down. A conceptual class of situations is not yet a recurrent pattern. The fact that an issue can be described in general terms does not mean that an attractor has formed. Overclaim would occur if one treated a conceptual case-type as though it already contained stabilized praxis.

**Ω — Asymmetry** is relevant but low-resolution. Legitimacy without recognized authority may create asymmetries of exposure, vulnerability, power, obligation, or recognition. A claimant may be exposed because legitimacy lacks recognized ratification. A granter may hold recognition power without formal authority. A contester may bear challenge costs. Affected parties may lack procedural recourse. But the case gives no parties, no direction, and no cost distribution.

Ω is therefore analytically important but not assignable. The PMS reading may name possible asymmetry fields, but it must not moralize them. It cannot decide who is vulnerable, who holds power, who bears responsibility, or who is exposed. Overclaim would occur if PMS assigned victim and perpetrator roles, inferred domination, or converted possible exposure into legal or moral finding.

**Θ — Temporality** is weak. The case could unfold across time: authority absence could persist, legitimacy claims could harden, contestation could recur, recognition could be delayed, or non-recognition could become an attractor. Yet no temporal sequence is supplied. Θ cannot be treated as active in the strong sense. It remains a conditional dependency needed for later structures such as Awareness_A, Coherence_C, Responsibility_R, Action_E, and SELF_FIXPOINT.

This weak temporality is important because many stronger conclusions would require time. Responsibility requires persistence and relation to consequence. Integration requires development. Self-binding requires commitment across time. Drift requires stabilization. The current case gives none of that. Strong temporal claims would be invented.

**Φ — Recontextualization** is active conceptually. The case itself reframes legitimacy apart from recognized authority. It asks what legitimacy means when authority cannot be presumed as the settling condition. PMS-CRITIQUE later sharpens this as critique-relevant recontextualization: legitimacy is moved from a simple authority-grounded frame into a frame where authority may be absent, refused, bracketed, or contested.

Calibration requires naming the source frame and target frame. Is the source frame “legitimacy requires recognized authority”? Is the target frame “legitimacy may arise without formal authority”? Or is the target frame only “authority absence must be analyzed before judgment”? These are different. Overclaim would occur if every rival reading were simply absorbed as PMS recontextualization, or if reframing were mistaken for resolution.

**Χ — Distance** is active as application discipline and structurally important. The analysis must maintain distance from verdict, urgency, enforcement, closure, and person attribution. Χ keeps legitimacy claim, recognized authority, legal validity, moral truth, PMS readability, critique threshold, and maturity analysis separated. It also prevents the analysis from becoming a tribunal.

However, case-internal Χ is not demonstrated, because no concrete actors pause, reflect, revise, or preserve stop-capability in a scene. The analyst’s distance is not the same as distance enacted within the case. Overclaim would occur if analytic distance were treated as proof that the case itself has enacted critique discipline.

**Σ — Integration** is weak. A stronger case might integrate rival legitimacy frames into a coherent structure: legal validity, moral justification, recognition, authority absence, contestation, and practical consequences might be coordinated without erasure. But the current material does not show such synthesis. PMS can prepare the integration question, but not answer it.

Calibration for Σ is especially important because PMS coherence may become too smooth. The fact that PMS can organize the case is not evidence that the legitimacy problem has been integrated. Overclaim occurs when structural readability is mistaken for successful integration of the legitimacy problem.

**Ψ — Self-Binding** is weak and analytic-only. Self-binding would require an actor, institution, community, or modeled agent binding itself over time to an integrated legitimacy structure. The case supplies none. No claimant owns a commitment, no authority binds a recognition procedure, no contester binds a review process, and no institution binds itself to follow-up. Ψ remains a later conditional possibility.

Overclaim would occur if legitimacy itself, authority itself, or the abstract case were treated as a self-binding subject. PMS must not invent a self where the case supplies only a conceptual relation.

The resulting PMS Core formula is best rendered as a bounded and conditional chain:

**Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ? ∘ Ω? ∘ Α? ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ**

This means: framed moral-practical tension around the legitimacy/authority distinction, structured by the absence of recognized authority, with possible but weak pattern, asymmetry, and temporality, conceptually recontextualized and held under reflective distance. Σ and Ψ are not included in the primary formula because they are not established as case results.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The central discipline of the PMS Core reading is that later operators cannot be used as shortcuts. The case is tempting precisely because it invites quick movement from Δ and ∇ to closure: legitimacy is distinguished from authority, moral pressure appears, and a reader may want to settle the matter. PMS blocks this.

Λ depends on □. The absence of recognized authority only has structural meaning within a frame that expects recognized authority. If no such expectation is specified, Λ cannot be treated as failure. This is one of the most important calibration points in the case. Authority absence is not automatically illegitimacy.

Α requires recurrence. A conceptual class of situations is not yet an attractor. To activate Α strongly, the analysis would need repeated claims, grants, contestations, recognitions, silences, refusals, or authority absences that stabilize into a pattern. Without that, attractor language remains weak.

Ω depends on Α in the default PMS dependency path, and in this case Α is weak. That does not make Ω irrelevant, but it makes it low-resolution. PMS may name possible asymmetry fields but cannot assign direction or weight. It would be invalid to infer blame, vulnerability, authority abuse, domination, or responsibility without scene data.

Θ depends on Ω and Α. Since both are weak, Θ remains weak. Temporality may matter in later operationalization, but the current case gives no sequence, duration, or trajectory. Strong temporal claims would be invented.

Φ depends on Θ, Ω, and □. In the case, Φ is active mainly at the conceptual level because the problem is explicitly reframed. But its dependencies remain under-specified. This means recontextualization is readable but not fully grounded as a concrete scene process. PMS must not use Φ to absorb all rival readings.

Χ depends on Φ, Θ, and □. Χ is required as application discipline, even where case-internal distance is not shown. This distinction matters. The analyst must maintain distance, but the case itself has not demonstrated practical stop-capability.

Σ depends on Χ and Φ. Because Φ is conceptual and Χ is application-level, Σ cannot be claimed as achieved. Integration would require more than a coherent essay or YAML structure. It would require actual coordination of rival frames without erasing non-capture.

Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. Since Σ and Θ are weak, Ψ is not available as a case result. Self-binding remains analytic-only.

This dependency discipline prevents PMS from becoming a closure machine. PMS can make the case legible, but legibility is not closure. Coherence is not proof. A structurally coherent PMS account may still be under-discriminating if it fails to distinguish rival legitimacy grammars. It may be over-assimilative if it absorbs disagreement, rupture, refusal, or non-fit as merely incomplete integration. It may become authority-like if it treats its own operator chain as final settlement.

Non-capture is not failure. It may be precisely the correct result. Some legitimacy disputes may be better handled by legal theory, recognition theory, political theology, democratic theory, discourse ethics, anti-authoritarian critique, rhetoric, deconstruction, institutional analysis, or moral philosophy. PMS does not lose because rival readings remain. In AXIOM terms, preserving Λ-residue and rival formalization is part of the case result.

Invalid dependency shortcuts include: treating absence of authority as illegitimacy without □; treating repeated conceptual mention as Α; treating possible exposure as Ω verdict; treating conceptual reframing as Σ; treating analysis discipline as case-internal Χ; treating PMS coherence as legitimacy; treating critique readability as correction; treating MIP template completion as maturity assessment; treating AHP precision as scoring upgrade.

The dependency discipline also protects the case from a subtler error: making “absence” do too much work. In this case, authority absence is meaningful, but it is not sovereign. It must not be allowed to decide every downstream structure. Absence may create pressure, reveal a frame, or expose the need for recognition. It may also simply mark that the case has not yet specified its authority relation. PMS therefore treats Λ as strong but bounded.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS drift-pattern reading remains deliberately conservative. The case activates drift watch-zones, not drift findings. This distinction is central: the analysis does not say that the case is drifting, deficient, immature, under-integrated, evasive, or unstable. It says only that certain drift forms would become relevant if later concrete material showed the required operator configuration.

The first PMS drift pattern is **AD_A>>E**, excessive distance between Awareness and Enactment. In PMS terms, this risk concerns configurations in which awareness, framing, analysis, or recontextualization expands while concrete enactment remains suppressed, deferred, or chronically delayed. The case has an abstract structure that could invite this drift: legitimacy is discussed, reframed, contested, and held at distance, but no action, settlement, repair, recognition procedure, or refusal is supplied. However, this is not enough to classify AD_A>>E as present. The case is not a concrete scene, and the absence of enactment is not a failure inside the case. It is simply a limit of the supplied material.

The axis effects therefore remain under-specified. The awareness axis is not over-expanded in a demonstrated praxis scene; it is only analytically developed in the case artifact. The action axis is not suppressed or chronically delayed by any actor; it is simply absent because the case material supplies no scene of action. It would be a misuse of PMS to treat abstraction itself as pathological over-awareness.

The second PMS drift pattern is **AD_Sigma_low**, fragmented integration or under-integration. This risk becomes relevant when recontextualization outpaces integration: frames shift, explanations multiply, or rival perspectives proliferate without any coherent synthesis, coordination, or bounded closure. In this case, the risk is visible because legitimacy can be framed morally, legally, politically, communally, theologically, anti-authoritarianly, or institutionally. PMS can name these rival frames. But naming rival frames does not show failed integration. Rival framing may be the correct result. Non-integration may indicate genuine non-capture rather than immaturity or drift.

The axis effects again remain under-specified. The coherence axis is not demonstrably volatile; there is no temporal sequence of frame shifts. The responsibility axis is not inconsistent; no responsibility-bearing role is supplied. Therefore AD_Sigma_low is a watch-zone only.

The broader coherence risk is high because the case fits PMS too easily at the abstract level. Legitimacy versus authority gives Δ; moral difficulty gives ∇; the problem demands □; absence of recognized authority gives Λ; legitimacy apart from authority gives Φ; non-tribunal restraint gives Χ. This fit can make the analysis appear more complete than it is. The risk is not that PMS fails to read the case; the risk is that PMS reads it so cleanly that readers mistake structural coherence for case adequacy.

The under-discrimination risk follows from the same fact. The PMS reading must still distinguish among very different configurations: principled legitimacy without authority, coercive pseudo-legitimacy, legal invalidity, moral legitimacy without institutional recognition, charismatic substitution, institutional vacuum, symbolic refusal, anti-authoritarian legitimacy, and simple frame conflict. If the PMS reading cannot discriminate among these, it remains too broad even if internally coherent.

The drift-pattern result is therefore cautious: **drift risks are present as analytic guardrails, not as findings**. Under-specification is not failure. Non-assignment is not low score. Watch-zones are not diagnoses.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant add-on is **PMS-CRITIQUE**. It is used as an overlay/application profile only. It does not redefine PMS operators, does not alter PMS dependencies, does not add new PMS Base structures, and does not rewrite the PMS Core pass. Its reduced signatures are overlay shorthand for critique readability. PMS remains the only Base operator grammar.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer because the case does not merely concern legitimacy as a structural problem. It also concerns how legitimacy becomes contestable. The moment legitimacy is claimed, granted, or contested without recognized authority, the situation approaches critique: someone may interrupt a frame, challenge a claim, refuse authority, demand justification, or recontextualize legitimacy itself. PMS Core can map this structurally, but PMS-CRITIQUE adds more precise threshold distinctions: reaction, minimal critique, productive critique, stable critique, judgment drift, narrative repair drift, commentary drift, publicness, reach, scale, and follow-up binding.

The key PMS-CRITIQUE result is that the case reaches only a **weak conceptual version of minimal critique**. It does not reach productive critique or stable critique.

**CRITIQUE_MIN** uses the reduced overlay signature Δ + □ + Χ. In this case, Δ is visible as the mismatch between legitimacy-claiming, legitimacy-granting, legitimacy-contestation, and absent recognized authority. □ is visible as the frame in which authority is expected, refused, bracketed, or contested. Χ is maintained by the application because the analysis remains reversible, non-tribunal, and non-sanctioning. But the case does not supply a concrete critique episode. No actor interrupts a legitimacy claim in a scene. No one demonstrates practical stop-capability. No protected revision window is enacted. Therefore CRITIQUE_MIN is present only as overlay-level threshold readability, not as demonstrated practical critique.

This distinction matters. PMS-CRITIQUE does not say that every mismatch is critique. Reaction without Χ is not weaker critique; it is structurally different. Judgment without Φ and Σ is not critique in the strict sense; it may be evaluation or verdict. Silence and paralysis under mismatch are not low-grade critique; they are distinct stabilizations. PMS readability of a mismatch is not critique unless the mismatch remains framed and interruptible.

**CRITIQUE_PROD** requires Δ, □, Χ, Φ, and Σ. Productive critique crosses from interruption into correction. It requires not only that a mismatch be visible and interruptible, but that it be recontextualized into an actionable context and integrated into a coordinatable corrective step. The case does not reach this threshold. Φ is active as conceptual recontextualization: legitimacy is made readable apart from recognized authority. But Σ is weak. There is no corrective step, coordination unit, decision, repair path, implementation unit, or shared action path. The case remains closer to bounded interruption and conceptual reframing than to correction. This is not a failure of the case; it is a boundary of what the supplied material warrants.

**CRITIQUE_STABLE** requires Δ, □, Χ, Φ, Σ, and Ψ. Stable critique binds correction into a trajectory without coercing others. Follow-up becomes owned over time, reviewable, and restrained by Dignity-in-Practice. The case does not reach this threshold. No actor binds itself to follow-up. No institution creates review intervals. No claimant, granter, or contester owns a correction path. Ψ remains unavailable. The overlay must therefore not convert the abstract legitimacy problem into a demand that any party accept a particular critique, authority claim, legitimacy theory, or PMS-CRITIQUE formalization.

The add-on also clarifies scale. The case is best placed at the horizontal scale tag **generic structural critique without actor binding**. It is not dyadic micro-critique, not small-group critique, not organizational critique, not institutional deployment, and not public mass critique. The publicness overlay **P** is not applicable in the base case. Publicness is a non-operator overlay modulator in PMS-CRITIQUE; it can amplify Ω, Θ, and Α when public exposure, audience dynamics, platform circulation, or durable reputational residue are present. None of these are supplied in the source case. P does not create critique, correction, or legitimacy.

The most relevant CRITIQUE drift risks are not active findings but bounded risks.

**D2 — Judgment masquerading as critique** is a risk because legitimacy contestation can become verdict-like if □ + Δ are used to label positions without Φ/Σ coordination. In this case, the risk would be overclaiming that legitimacy without authority is already valid or already invalid merely because PMS can read it. The overlay controls this by refusing truth adjudication, legal judgment, moral ranking, person-ranking, and legitimacy tribunal logic.

**D3 — Narrative repair masquerading as critique** is a risk because Φ is active while Σ is weak. Reframing legitimacy without authority could become explanation expansion without correction if every rival frame is absorbed as another PMS-readable recontextualization. But the case does not show repeated reframing or failed correction. The risk is analytic and preventive, not diagnostic.

**D4 — Commentary or analysis paralysis** is a risk because Χ is strong as application discipline while Σ is absent. The analysis may remain indefinitely reflective without ever specifying whether a concrete case requires action, further evidence, rival formalization, or abstention. In the current case, this risk is controlled by explicitly marking the case as non-concrete, withholding action and responsibility conclusions, and stating operationalization requirements.

The compact drift risks C2, C4, C5, and C9 mirror these: judgment drift, narrative reset drift, commentary drift, and integration bypass. Again, these are not findings. They are guardrails that prevent the overlay from becoming too smooth.

The final PMS-CRITIQUE signature is:

**weak CRITIQUE_MIN with Φ-active / Σ-weak: Δ + □ + Χ as overlay-stabilized framed differentiation, plus Φ as conceptual recontextualization, with bounded risks D2, D3, and D4; no CRITIQUE_PROD, no CRITIQUE_STABLE, no P amplification.**

This signature is overlay shorthand only. It does not alter PMS operator dependencies.

### Layer interaction

The PMS Core layer remains the structural base. It supplies the operator grammar: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. It also supplies the derived structures, dependency logic, application gate, and non-capture discipline. Nothing in PMS-CRITIQUE, MIP, or AHP changes those operators or their dependencies.

PMS Core says: the case is a frame-dependent legitimacy problem structured by the distinction between legitimacy and authority, moral-practical pressure, the frame-dependence of recognition, the structured absence of recognized authority, possible low-resolution asymmetry, conceptual recontextualization, and required reflective distance. It also says that recurrence, temporality, integration, and self-binding are not established.

PMS-CRITIQUE sharpens a different question: when does this legitimacy/authority mismatch become critique? Its contribution is not new ontology or new PMS operators. It adds threshold discipline. It shows that the case is not reaction-only, because mismatch, frame, and distance are present at the overlay level. But it is not productive critique, because correction is absent. It is not stable critique, because follow-up binding is absent. PMS-CRITIQUE therefore gives a more precise account of critique reach without revising the PMS Core reading.

MIP then shifts the analysis to maturity-in-practice boundary checking. It asks whether the case supports A-score, M-score, IA-box classification, D-module activation, or transmission. The answer is no. This “no” is not a negative score and not a finding of immaturity; it is a boundary result. MIP does not intensify the case into governance. Instead, it confirms that the case is too abstract for maturity scoring. A-score is not assigned because no enacted awareness exists. M-score is not assigned because no actor, power, predictability, alternatives, consequence chain, or integration attempt is supplied. IA classification is under-specified and remains a non-classification result, not weak IA or low IA. D remains off because no D1/D2 dignity-in-practice enactments are present. Dignity-in-Practice remains a guardrail on the analysis, not a profile of the case.

AHP then analyzes the MIP output as an analysis artifact. It does not change the case. It does not change A, M, IA, or D. It does not activate D. It does not upgrade transmission. It asks whether the analysis artifact is precise enough and where it remains vulnerable. It rates the Precision Heuristic as high, five out of six, but identifies term definition as the main attack surface. Its hardening function is second-order: improve future iteration by defining terms, clarifying the MIP boundary, tightening IA non-classification, marking D as guardrail-only, rephrasing M ceiling language, separating internal reuse from external evaluative transmission, and linking rival readings to term definitions. AHP therefore increases the inspectability of the article; it does not increase the certainty of the case.

The interaction among layers is therefore disciplined:

PMS Core maps the case.
PMS-CRITIQUE clarifies critique threshold reach.
MIP refuses unwarranted maturity evaluation.
AHP hardens the analysis artifact.

The overlay language could overreach if CRITIQUE_MIN were treated as actual critique, if drift risks were treated as drift findings, if MIP template completeness were mistaken for maturity assessment, or if AHP precision were mistaken for validation. The article must preserve these boundaries.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. PMS preserves Λ as authority absence and frame-relative non-event. PMS-CRITIQUE preserves the lack of Σ and Ψ. MIP preserves no-score and no-classification status. AHP preserves attack surfaces rather than hiding them. The stack does not close the legitimacy problem by authority; it shows where closure is unavailable.

The layer interaction also prevents a common error in multi-layer analysis: escalation by accumulation. Adding PMS-CRITIQUE, MIP, and AHP does not make the case more certain. It makes its boundaries more inspectable. PMS-CRITIQUE does not prove critique. MIP does not prove maturity. AHP does not prove the analysis valid. Each layer adds a specific visibility function while preserving the prior layer’s limits.

The following examples are therefore not evidence for the original abstract case. They are controlled demonstration scenes. Their function is to show how the same operator and overlay boundaries would behave if concrete material were later supplied. They must not be imported back into the source case as proof of recurrence, authority failure, legitimacy, critique success, responsibility, publicness, or maturity.

### Example 1 — The Informal Mediator

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Scene and frame structure”

A neighborhood group faces a recurring dispute over shared courtyard use. There is no formal association, no elected board, and no recognized authority empowered to decide binding rules. One resident begins to coordinate discussions, keeps notes, and proposes a shared calendar. Several neighbors treat this person’s proposals as legitimate because the process is useful, transparent enough for practical coordination, and open to revision. Others object: the resident was never elected, has no mandate, and should not be treated as an authority.

The vignette is bounded. It does not establish whether the resident’s role is actually legitimate. It does not decide whether the objectors are right. It does not claim that informal coordination is better or worse than formal authority. It only provides a readable scene in which legitimacy is claimed, partly granted, and contested without recognized authority.

What the example demonstrates is the frame-dependence of authority absence. In a formal legal or institutional frame, the resident has no recognized authority. In a practical coordination frame, the resident’s role may still acquire limited legitimacy because others use the calendar, respond to the notes, and treat the process as provisionally helpful. In a democratic-procedural frame, the same arrangement may appear deficient because there was no authorization procedure. The point is not to select one frame as correct. The point is to show why PMS must ask which frame makes authority absence relevant before treating that absence as a failure.

The PMS Core hooks are clear. **Δ** appears in the distinction between legitimacy and authority: the resident may be treated as practically legitimate by some without being formally authorized. **∇** appears in the pressure to settle the dispute: the group needs coordination, and the absence of authority intensifies the demand for closure. **□** is decisive because different frames alter the meaning of the same conduct. **Λ** appears as the missing authorization event: no election, no mandate, no recognized appointment. But Λ is not automatically a defect; it is a structured absence only relative to frames that expected such authorization. **Ω** is possible but not yet a verdict: the note-taking resident may acquire influence, and dissenters may face coordination costs if the calendar becomes de facto binding. **Θ** would become stronger only if the arrangement persisted and hardened over time. **Φ** is visible when the group shifts from “who has authority?” to “what makes this coordination acceptable?” **Χ** is required so that the analysis does not become either a celebration of informal legitimacy or a condemnation of unauthorized authority. **Σ** and **Ψ** remain conditional unless the group later integrates a shared procedure and binds itself to it.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay adds a useful distinction. The objectors’ challenge may be **weak CRITIQUE_MIN** if it remains framed and interruptible: they identify a mismatch, locate it in the frame of authorization, and preserve the possibility of revision. It is not yet productive critique unless it produces a coordinatable correction, such as a rotating facilitation process or an agreed review meeting. It is not stable critique unless someone or some group binds itself to follow-up over time. The drift risks are only risks. The dispute could slide into judgment drift if “unauthorized” becomes a label that replaces coordination. It could slide into commentary drift if the group discusses legitimacy indefinitely but never decides whether any procedure is needed. Neither is a finding from the vignette alone.

MIP would remain limited. A-score and M-score should not be assigned unless the example supplies role-specific knowledge, power, alternatives, predictability, and consequences. The IA-box could name possible asymmetry, but it should not classify it without more evidence: Can dissenters opt out? Does the calendar affect access to the courtyard? Can the facilitator be replaced? Are affected parties heard? The D-module should remain off unless dignity-in-practice enactments appear. AHP would likely flag term precision: “legitimacy,” “coordination,” “authority,” and “mandate” would need working definitions before the vignette could support stronger claims.

Forbidden claims are therefore important. The example does not prove that informal leadership is legitimate. It does not prove that formal authority is necessary. It does not diagnose the resident as power-seeking or the objectors as immature. It does not establish legal standing, institutional authority, or moral superiority. It does not validate PMS.

The non-capture note is also central. A democratic theory may insist that procedural authorization is the decisive issue. A communitarian reading may emphasize recognition through practice. A legal reading may ask whether any enforceable right exists. PMS can map these frames, but it does not replace them.

The article takeaway is that legitimacy without authority is not a single condition. It is a frame-dependent pressure field. The same absence of recognized authority may be a defect, a neutral feature, or a condition of provisional coordination depending on which frame is operative and how asymmetry, reversibility, and follow-up are handled.

### Example 2 — The Emergency Repair Decision

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “PMS Core structural reading”

A small research lab discovers a water leak late at night. The official facilities manager is unreachable, and no written emergency protocol is available. A junior researcher contacts a plumber, authorizes a temporary repair, and sends a message to the lab mailing list explaining what happened. The next morning, some colleagues thank the researcher for preventing damage. Others object that the researcher had no authority to authorize costs or act on behalf of the lab.

This example is concrete enough to be readable but still bounded. It does not establish legal authority, financial liability, employment responsibility, or moral praise. It also does not decide whether the repair decision was legitimate. It only shows how legitimacy may be claimed, granted, and contested where recognized authority is absent at the moment of action.

PMS Core first separates the relevant distinctions. **Δ** marks the difference between formal authority and practical uptake: the researcher lacks recognized authorization but may be treated by some as having acted legitimately under emergency constraints. **∇** is strong because the leak creates pressure to act. Without recognized authority available, the closure-demand intensifies: someone must decide whether to wait, intervene, or escalate. **□** becomes the hinge. In an emergency-preservation frame, temporary action may appear legitimate because delay risks damage. In a budget-authority frame, the same action may appear unauthorized. In a procedural governance frame, the issue may be less the repair itself than the missing protocol. These frames cannot be silently collapsed.

**Λ** is the missing expected event: the facilities manager does not respond, the protocol is absent, the authorized channel is unavailable. But Λ must be carefully calibrated. The absence of authority matters because the frame expected an authority to decide. It is not yet proof that the researcher’s action was invalid or valid. **Α** might emerge if emergencies are repeatedly handled by whoever happens to be present, but the vignette does not yet show recurrence. **Ω** is relevant because costs and exposure are uneven: the junior researcher may bear employment risk, the lab may bear financial cost, and later reviewers may have authority to approve or criticize after the fact. Still, Ω should not be moralized. **Θ** is partly visible because timing matters: the leak created a narrow window, and later review may reinterpret the action. But a full temporal trajectory is not supplied unless we follow the aftermath.

**Φ** appears when the event is reframed after the fact. Was this unauthorized spending, emergency stewardship, procedural failure, or informal legitimacy under constraint? Each recontextualization changes what the action means. **Χ** is essential: the analysis must not rush into praise, blame, legality, or diagnosis. It must preserve the difference between a structural emergency, an authority absence, and the legitimacy of action under pressure. **Σ** remains conditional. The lab might later integrate the event into a new emergency procedure; until then, no integration is established. **Ψ** would appear only if the lab binds itself to a revised protocol, or if the researcher explicitly accepts a defined role for future emergencies. Neither follows automatically.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay helps distinguish critique from reaction. A colleague who says “you had no right to do that” may be reacting, judging, or critiquing depending on whether the objection is framed, interruptible, and oriented toward correction. If the objection identifies the mismatch between emergency action and authorization procedure while preserving review and revision, it may reach weak CRITIQUE_MIN. If the lab creates a temporary-spending rule, emergency contact chain, and review procedure, productive critique becomes possible because **Φ** and **Σ** are linked to correction. Stable critique would require follow-up binding: someone owns the protocol, updates it, and reviews it over time. The vignette does not yet show that.

MIP status remains cautious. It would be tempting to assign responsibility: the researcher acted, the manager was absent, the lab lacked a protocol. But the MIP analysis must not assign M-score without evidence of knowledge, power-control, predictability, access to alternatives, and serious integration attempts. The IA-box might later ask whether asymmetry was transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible: Did the researcher document the decision? Was the repair limited? Could the cost be reviewed? Were affected parties informed? But these questions require scene data. The D-module remains off unless the aftermath includes humiliation, shaming, standing-stripping, or dignity-protective conduct.

AHP would likely harden the analysis by demanding term definitions. “Emergency,” “authority,” “temporary repair,” “legitimate action,” and “recognized authorization” must be defined before the example is used in a stronger argument. AHP would also warn that full template coverage is not equivalent to a maturity verdict. The example may illustrate the structure, but it does not score the actor.

Forbidden claims include: the junior researcher was legally authorized; the junior researcher was morally superior; the colleagues were obstructive; the absent manager was responsible; the lab is dysfunctional; emergency action is always legitimate; procedural authority is always secondary. None of these follows.

The non-capture note matters because legal, organizational, ethical, and emergency-management frameworks may each preserve features PMS does not decide. A legal reading may focus on spending authority. An organizational reading may focus on protocol design. An ethical reading may focus on preventable damage. PMS maps the interaction of those frames but does not settle them.

The article takeaway is that legitimacy without authority often becomes clearest under temporal pressure. The absence of recognized authority does not dissolve the need for action, but it also does not automatically legitimate whoever acts. PMS preserves the pressure, the absence, the frame conflict, and the need for review without converting them into verdict.

### Example 3 — The Open Letter Without a Mandate

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Add-on / overlay reading”

A group of students publishes an open letter claiming to speak for “the concerned members” of a university department. The letter criticizes a proposed curriculum change and demands that the decision be paused. The group has no elected mandate, no formal representative status, and no recognized authority to speak for the department. Some readers treat the letter as legitimate because it articulates concerns that had not been heard in the official process. Others reject it as illegitimate because the authors claim a collective voice without authorization.

This example is intentionally high-risk and must remain guardrail-bound. It involves publicness-adjacent dynamics, representation, critique, and legitimacy without recognized authority. However, the vignette does not identify real persons, does not decide whether the letter is valid, and does not establish whether the students represent anyone beyond themselves. It is an illustration of the article’s structure, not evidence for a governance conclusion.

At the PMS Core level, **Δ** marks several distinctions: speaking as individuals versus speaking for a group; legitimacy versus recognized mandate; critique versus representation; public uptake versus formal authority. **∇** appears in the pressure to respond to the curriculum change. A decision is pending, and the letter attempts to interrupt it. **□** is crucial. In a formal governance frame, the lack of mandate may weaken the representative claim. In a public critique frame, the letter may not need formal authority to raise concerns. In a recognition frame, legitimacy may depend on whether affected members identify with the concerns. In an institutional process frame, the issue may be whether official channels failed to include relevant voices.

**Λ** appears as missing authorization: no election, no formal appointment, no recognized representative procedure. It may also appear as a missing response event if concerns were previously raised but not addressed. Yet PMS must keep these Λ-structures separate. Missing mandate is not the same as missing hearing. Missing hearing is not proof of legitimacy. Missing authorization is not proof of illegitimacy. Each absence is meaningful only within a frame. **Α** might become active if open letters become the default way to interrupt decisions, but the vignette alone does not establish that. **Ω** is relevant because public letters can redistribute exposure: authors may gain visibility, named decision-makers may face public pressure, quieter members may feel represented or misrepresented, and institutional actors may control formal response channels. But Ω remains a structure to inspect, not a verdict.

**Θ** is more salient here than in a purely abstract case because timing matters: the proposed decision is pending, the letter asks for a pause, and public circulation may create residue. Still, the example does not supply long-term trajectory. **Φ** appears when the curriculum issue is recontextualized from an internal governance matter into a public legitimacy dispute. **Χ** is essential to avoid fusing critique with righteousness or unauthorized speech with illegitimacy. **Σ** is not established unless the letter leads to a coordinated review, clarified representation procedure, or revised curriculum process. **Ψ** is not established unless authors, department, or institution bind themselves to follow-up.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay is especially useful. The letter may instantiate weak **CRITIQUE_MIN** if it identifies a mismatch, locates it in a frame, and remains interruptible: “the process is proceeding without adequate recognition of affected concerns; pause and review.” It is not automatically productive critique. Productive critique would require **Φ** and **Σ**: a reframed problem plus an actionable correction, such as a defined consultation window, a procedure for collecting views, or a concrete revision pathway. Stable critique would require **Ψ**: follow-up ownership, review dates, and non-coercive commitment to the process.

The example also shows why CRITIQUE drift risks must remain risks, not findings. **Judgment drift** could occur if the letter labels the curriculum process illegitimate without offering a correction path. **Narrative repair drift** could occur if the dispute becomes endless reframing about who is really represented while no process changes. **Commentary drift** could occur if the institution produces reflective statements without review or action. But none of these is established by the vignette alone. They become visible as possible analysis attack surfaces.

Publicness requires care. The original case analysis marked the publicness overlay P as not applicable because no public scene was supplied. In this example, P may become conditionally relevant because an open letter can amplify exposure, harden residue, and accelerate attractor formation. But P is still not a PMS operator. It is an overlay modulator. Its presence would not create critique or correction by itself. It would only increase the need to track Ω, Θ, and Α.

MIP remains constrained. No A-score or M-score should be assigned from the vignette. The students’ awareness, the institution’s responsibility, and the legitimacy of the process cannot be scored without concrete evidence. The IA-box could become relevant if the open letter creates asymmetry: Who can respond? Is the exposure transparent? Is the pause demand justified? Is it time-bound? Is the reputational effect reversible? But without full scene data, IA classification should remain cautious. The D-module should stay off unless the example includes humiliation, shaming, degradation, dignity-protective restraint, or public standing harm.

AHP would add hardening requirements. Terms such as “speak for,” “concerned members,” “mandate,” “legitimate critique,” and “public representation” need working definitions. AHP would also warn that an article-ready example must not let the completeness of the rendering look like a judgment on the students or institution. The example should remain internal to the article’s explanatory task.

Forbidden claims are numerous: the students legitimately represent the department; the students illegitimately usurp authority; the institution is oppressive; the letter is courageous; the letter is manipulative; the curriculum change is valid or invalid; public critique is correction; lack of mandate defeats critique; public uptake creates authority. None follows from the bounded vignette.

The non-capture note is strong. A democratic theory may prioritize mandate. A social-movement theory may prioritize voice where formal channels fail. A legal-institutional reading may ask who has standing. A discourse-ethical reading may ask whether affected parties can participate. PMS-CRITIQUE maps critique thresholds and drift risks, but it does not settle representation or legitimacy.

The article takeaway is that legitimacy without authority becomes especially delicate when critique enters public or semi-public space. A claim may be critique-readable without being representative, productive, stable, or legitimate. Conversely, lack of formal authority does not automatically invalidate the interruption. The analytic task is to preserve the distinction.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The PMS and overlay readings do not exhaust the case. Several rival readings remain open, and each preserves something that PMS may not fully capture.

A **normative reading** would ask whether legitimacy can be justified without recognized authority. This reading may focus on moral reasons, autonomy, consent, justice, obligation, or rightness. PMS can map the structural frame, but it does not decide the normative truth. A normative theory may preserve the force of legitimacy as moral justification more directly than PMS does.

A **recognition-theoretic reading** would treat legitimacy as arising from recognition by relevant participants rather than from formal authority. On this view, legitimacy without recognized authority may not be deficient. It may be ordinary, communal, democratic, relational, or emergent. PMS can model claim, grant, contestation, and asymmetry, but it does not itself determine whose recognition counts.

A **legal-political reading** would distinguish legitimacy, legality, authority, jurisdiction, standing, validity, mandate, and enforceability. PMS deliberately does not collapse these. If the intended case is legal or institutional, a legal-political framework may be necessary. PMS cannot decide whether an authority is legally recognized or whether a legitimacy claim has legal effect.

An **anti-authoritarian reading** may argue that legitimacy without authority is not a problem but a positive structure. The absence of recognized authority may mark liberation from domination, refusal of coercive recognition, or critique of institutional gatekeeping. PMS must not turn this absence into a Λ-defect by default. It must allow the possibility that the frame does not require authority.

A **theological or communal reading** might locate authority outside formal recognition. Recognition may be spiritual, traditional, ritual, communal, or symbolic rather than institutional. PMS can only map the frame if it is supplied; it cannot decide such authority structures internally.

A **discourse-ethical reading** might ask whether legitimacy arises from communicative procedures, participation, public reason, or non-coercive justification. PMS-CRITIQUE can map critique thresholds, but it does not provide a full discourse ethics.

A **rhetorical or language-practice reading** might focus on how the words “legitimacy” and “authority” function in practice. It may ask what speech acts are performed by claiming, granting, or contesting legitimacy. PMS can name frames and operators, but it may not capture the full pragmatic or rhetorical force of the language.

A **phenomenological reading** might focus on lived experience: what it feels like to be subject to legitimacy without authority, or to contest authority without recognition. PMS explicitly does not claim phenomenological access. Its awareness structures are structural, not experiential.

An **anti-capture reading** may hold that the case resists formalization altogether because legitimacy is precisely what is at stake in contesting the frame. If PMS becomes another authority-like structure, it risks reproducing the problem. This reading is not a defect. It is a valid non-capture warning.

The examples sharpen these rival openings. The informal mediator can be read through procedural authorization, communal recognition, or practical coordination. The emergency repair case can be read through organizational authority, ethical urgency, legal liability, or protocol failure. The open letter can be read through democratic mandate, social-movement voice, public critique, or institutional procedure. PMS and PMS-CRITIQUE can map the tensions, but they do not settle which rival grammar should govern the case.

PMS and PMS-CRITIQUE preserve these rival readings by refusing to treat them as calibration noise. Non-capture zones include normative truth, legal authority, institutional recognition, symbolic refusal, theological authorization, public rupture, and lived experience. The case may remain partly outside the stack, and that remainder is part of the result.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS Core reading would be weakened if later material showed that recognized authority is actually present and dispositive. If a concrete scene revealed a clear authority structure, legitimate jurisdiction, accepted recognition procedure, and no meaningful absence, then Λ would need to be weakened or removed. The case would no longer be primarily “legitimacy without authority.”

The PMS reading would also be weakened if legitimacy is not actually claimed, granted, or contested. If the material turned out to concern only descriptive authority absence without legitimacy pressure, ∇ and Φ would lose force. If no frame expects authority, Λ may no longer be a structured non-event. If no asymmetry of exposure, power, recognition, vulnerability, capacity, or obligation is involved, Ω should remain inactive or minimal.

The reading would become under-discriminating if it could not distinguish principled non-authority legitimacy from legal invalidity, coercive pseudo-legitimacy, mere disagreement, institutional vacuum, charismatic substitution, or anti-authoritarian refusal. It would become over-assimilative if every rival framing were absorbed into PMS as recontextualization, under-integration, drift, or maturity failure.

The PMS-CRITIQUE reading would be weakened if a concrete scene showed no critique threshold at all: no framed mismatch, no interruptibility, no challenge, no refusal, no demand for justification, no contestation, and no recontextualization. It would also need revision if a concrete scene showed actual productive critique: a corrective step, shared coordination, repair path, or decision. In that case CRITIQUE_PROD might become active. If a concrete actor bound follow-up over time, CRITIQUE_STABLE might become relevant. Conversely, if public exposure, audience amplification, or platform dynamics appeared, the P overlay might need activation.

The MIP reading would change only if concrete scene evidence were supplied. Stronger MIP claims would require roles, time windows, knowledge, power-control, predictability, access to alternatives, serious integration attempts, consequences, publicness conditions, and D1/D2 enactments if dignity-profile analysis were considered. Without these, A-score and M-score remain not assigned. The IA-box remains under-specified and non-classified. D remains off.

The AHP reading identifies hardening conditions for the analysis artifact. The main attack surface is term ambiguity. A future iteration should define “legitimacy,” “recognized authority,” “claim,” “grant,” and “contestation” as working placeholders, with scope, exclusions, and reversibility. It should also explicitly state that full MIP template coverage does not equal full maturity assessment when scene evidence is absent. The IA-box should say that under-specification is a non-classification result, not weak IA or low IA. D-language should be marked guardrail-only. M ceiling language should avoid the appearance of hidden low-M inference. Transmission language should separate internal iteration from external evaluative transmission. Rival readings should be linked to the definitions of legitimacy and authority they presuppose.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. For empirical or applied use, the following would be required: a concrete scene; actor roles; operative authority concept; who claims legitimacy; who grants it; who contests it; what frame makes authority relevant; what consequences follow; whether power and exposure are asymmetric; whether the pattern recurs; whether it unfolds over time; what non-events matter; whether recontextualization stabilizes or destabilizes the situation; whether any integration occurs; whether any self-binding commitment exists; and what external accountability governs use.

Until those conditions are met, the analysis remains a working structural article, not an applied assessment.

The examples do not change these conditions. They illustrate what later scene material might look like, but they do not supply evidence for the original abstract case. The informal mediator does not prove informal legitimacy. The emergency repair decision does not prove emergency authority. The open letter does not prove public representation. Each example clarifies a possible configuration and the kind of data that would be needed before stronger claims could be made.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It does not identify pathology, maturity failure, psychological motive, competence, personality, sincerity, or clinical risk.

It must not be used for person ranking. No claimant, granter, contester, authority, group, institution, or audience is ranked as higher, lower, mature, immature, dignified, undignified, legitimate, illegitimate, responsible, irresponsible, rational, irrational, strong, weak, or defective.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusion. It does not establish authority, jurisdiction, legality, culpability, standing, validity, liability, admissibility, enforceability, or evidence weight.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. It does not prove what legitimacy is, whether authority is necessary, whether recognition grounds normativity, or whether anti-authoritarian legitimacy is true.

It must not be used as empirical proof. It does not show that real-world legitimacy disputes have this structure, how often they occur, or what outcomes they produce.

It must not be used as implementation validation. The YAMLs and article are not software, governance procedures, decision systems, policy tools, or institutional mechanisms.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. PMS legibility is not proof that PMS is complete, uniquely sufficient, final, or superior to rival grammars.

It must not treat PMS-CRITIQUE as a new operator system. CRITIQUE reduced signatures are overlay shorthand only. P is an overlay modulator, not a PMS operator. Drift catalogues are guardrails, not verdict engines.

It must not treat drift as finding. Judgment drift, narrative repair drift, commentary drift, AD_A>>E, and AD_Sigma_low are risks or watch-zones only. They are not diagnoses, accusations, or evidence of failure.

It must not treat MIP non-assignment as low score. “Not assigned” means that the evidence required for scoring is absent. It does not mean low awareness, low responsibility, weak maturity, or failed praxis.

It must not treat AHP precision as validation. A high Precision Heuristic means that the analysis artifact is relatively well bounded and language-disciplined. It does not prove the case, score the case, resolve legitimacy, or upgrade transmission status.

It must not create closure by authority. The whole point of the case is to resist the temptation to solve legitimacy without authority by installing the analysis itself as authority. PMS, PMS-CRITIQUE, MIP, and AHP must not become the recognized authority whose absence generated the case.

The examples also carry misuse risks. The informal mediator must not become an argument for informal authority. The emergency repair case must not become an argument that urgency legitimates unauthorized action. The open letter must not become a verdict on public critique. Each example is structurally illustrative, not evidentially decisive.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of “Legitimacy Without Authority” is a bounded structural reading of a closure-demand that PMS can make legible but must not resolve by force. PMS Core identifies the strongest operator chain at the level of Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Φ, and Χ: legitimacy and authority are distinguished; moral-practical pressure appears; the meaning of authority absence depends on frame; recognized authority functions as a structured absence only where expected; legitimacy can be recontextualized apart from authority; and the analysis must maintain distance from verdict. The later operators remain weaker: Α lacks recurrence, Ω lacks concrete direction, Θ lacks sequence, Σ lacks integration, and Ψ lacks self-binding. The final PMS Core reading is therefore conditional rather than totalizing.

PMS-CRITIQUE adds a second result. The case is weakly readable as a minimal critique-threshold problem, but it does not become productive or stable critique. The mismatch between legitimacy and absent authority can be framed and held under distance, but no correction path is supplied and no follow-up is bound. Drift risks such as judgment drift, narrative repair drift, and commentary drift remain guardrails, not findings. The overlay clarifies critique reach without redefining PMS or converting legitimacy contestation into verdict.

MIP adds a third result by refusing overextension. The case is not mature enough for maturity scoring because it is not a concrete praxis scene. A-score and M-score are not assigned. IA classification remains under-specified and explicitly non-classified. The D-module remains off. Dignity-in-Practice remains a praxeological application constraint, not an ontological dignity claim or score. This refusal is not an absence of analysis; it is the correct MIP result for a case lacking actors, power, predictability, alternatives, consequences, and enactments.

AHP adds a fourth result by hardening the analysis artifact. It finds the analysis largely precise but identifies the main vulnerability: the key terms “legitimacy,” “recognized authority,” “claim,” “grant,” and “contestation” remain intentionally under-defined. It also warns against mistaking full template coverage for full maturity assessment, against converting IA under-specification into an implicit IA result, against reading D-language as a hidden D-profile, against treating the M ceiling rule as low-M inference, and against transmitting the analysis as evaluation. AHP improves the next iteration; it does not rescore the case.

The three examples contribute by showing how the same structural pattern changes across frames without converting the examples into evidence. The informal mediator shows that authority absence may be a defect in one frame and a condition of provisional coordination in another. The emergency repair decision shows that temporal pressure can make authority absence practically urgent without automatically legitimating whoever acts. The open letter shows how legitimacy without authority becomes especially delicate when critique enters public or semi-public space. Together, the examples demonstrate why the core case must remain frame-sensitive: authority absence is not one thing, critique readability is not correction, and public uptake is not legitimacy. These examples demonstrate the analysis; they do not replace it. They are not evidence that informal coordination, emergency action, or public critique is legitimate or illegitimate.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what has been mapped. The article does not claim empirical proof, metaphysical truth, legal validity, diagnostic insight, person ranking, implementation readiness, PMS Base validation, or add-on validation. It does not claim that legitimacy without authority is good, bad, possible, impossible, mature, immature, emancipatory, coercive, or defective. It does not claim that authority is necessary or unnecessary. It does not claim that resistance, refusal, disagreement, absence, or non-fit is drift. It preserves the possibility that rival frameworks may capture decisive aspects that PMS and PMS-CRITIQUE only mark as residues.

The boundary discipline of the case is therefore the case’s methodological center. PMS must not become a closure machine. PMS-CRITIQUE must not become a critique tribunal. MIP must not become scoring pressure. AHP must not become validation-by-precision. Each layer is useful only if it remains within its claim boundary.

The final methodological takeaway is that “Legitimacy Without Authority” is not solved by finding a substitute authority. It is clarified by refusing premature substitution. The stack shows where legitimacy claims become structurally readable, where critique thresholds begin, where maturity analysis must stop, where analysis language needs hardening, and where non-capture must remain visible. The result is not closure. The result is a disciplined map of why closure is demanded, why it is not yet warranted, and what would be required before any stronger claim could responsibly be made. In this sense, the case performs AXIOM’s central discipline: it preserves the pressure toward judgment while refusing to let that pressure become a verdict machine.
