### Why this case matters

Forecasting is one of the places where modern praxis most strongly demands closure while knowing that closure is structurally unavailable. Science, weather systems, risk management, economics, engineering, and organizational planning all work with incomplete information, changing conditions, model limitations, future horizons, and uneven consequences. Their outputs are needed because the future is not yet settled in practice, yet their use can create the practical impression that the future has already become settled enough to govern action. The case therefore sits at a difficult point: forecasting remains structurally necessary, but forecast-status can drift.

The central philosophical pressure is the pressure of closure. A forecast is supposed to remain probabilistic, bounded, revisable, and frame-dependent. It is not the future itself. A model output is not an event. A probability is not a command. A scenario is not a verdict. Nevertheless, forecast-use scenes often require present action, resource allocation, warning, restraint, prevention, staging, or coordination before the event is confirmed. Institutions may need to prepare; engineers may need to design safety margins; planners may need to reserve resources; publics may need to be warned; organizations may need to choose among costly alternatives. This produces a practical demand that the forecast perform more authority than it formally possesses.

The case names the structural transition from **forecast-as-bounded-input** to **forecast-as-settled-future-truth** as prophecy drift. The term does not mean supernatural prediction. It means that a probabilistic projection begins to function as if it had the status of a settled future. The problem is not that forecasting occurs. The problem is not even that forecasting leads to action. The problem is that the action, communication, or institutional interpretation may no longer preserve the distinction between probability and certainty, forecast and outcome, warning and command, anticipation and prophecy.

This case belongs in AXIOM because it tests the PMS grammar at a point where the temptation to overclaim is unusually strong. PMS must show that it can analyze a high-pressure future-oriented structure without becoming a closure machine itself. It must not simply identify a smooth formula and then treat that formula as proof. It must distinguish legitimate probabilistic reliance from prophecy drift, precaution from overreach, high-confidence but revisable projection from certainty, and urgent action from collapse of reflective distance.

The case is structurally difficult because the same surface behavior can belong to different configurations. A city evacuating before a storm may be acting prudently or may be converting uncertainty into coercive inevitability. An engineer recommending a bridge shutdown may be integrating risk responsibly or may be letting a model output become fate. A university imposing a hiring freeze after a revenue forecast may be preparing carefully or may be externalizing responsibility onto “the numbers.” PMS cannot decide these questions by tone, outcome, or internal coherence alone. It must preserve calibration.

The difficulty is intensified by asymmetry. Forecasts are rarely used in neutral spaces. Someone produces the forecast, someone interprets it, someone acts on it, and someone bears the consequences if it is wrong or if action is delayed. False positives and false negatives may fall on different parties. Decision authority and exposure may be unevenly distributed. Some parties may have contestation channels while others are subject to downstream consequences. Under time pressure, these imbalances can push a forecast toward authority. A model output may become a justification. A risk estimate may become a mandate. A probability may become a demand for compliance.

This case is not solved metaphysically because PMS does not need to decide whether the future is open, fixed, knowable, unknowable, fated, probabilistically real, or epistemically inaccessible in some ultimate sense. The question is not what the future is in itself. The question is how future-oriented structure is handled in praxis.

The case is not solved empirically because no concrete forecast is supplied. There is no dataset, no model, no calibration record, no forecast horizon, no scoring method, no communication chain, no action log, and no outcome. PMS therefore cannot determine whether any forecast was accurate, inaccurate, well-calibrated, misleading, justified, negligent, or effective.

The case is not solved clinically or diagnostically because no person or group is being assessed. Prophecy drift is not a mental state, pathology, personality type, cognitive defect, institutional illness, or moral failing. It is a conditional structural risk in forecast-use praxis.

The case is not solved legally or forensically because there is no legal standard, evidence record, causation chain, duty analysis, liability question, evidentiary threshold, or adjudicative frame. A forecast being treated as settled truth may matter legally in a concrete dispute, but this case does not supply such a dispute.

The case is also not solved by PMS coherence itself. A PMS reading can be internally elegant and still under-discriminate. The completed YAML analyses therefore require a disciplined refusal of closure by theory. The reading must remain reversible, bounded, non-diagnostic, non-tribunal, and open to rival formalizations.

### Scene and frame structure

The conceptual scene is a forecast-use scene. Its boundary is not “the future” as such and not “science” as such. It is the structural moment in which probabilistic projections under uncertainty are taken up in praxis. The scene includes model outputs, forecast communication, uncertainty-bearing projections, planning pressure, risk orientation, and possible decisions under asymmetric stakes and time pressure. It concerns how a forecast is framed, carried, recontextualized, and potentially converted into authority.

The central frame, □, is the forecast-use frame. This can occur in scientific analysis, risk management, weather forecasting, engineering safety, organizational planning, or economic projection. The case deliberately spans domains, but this breadth also limits the strength of the claims. Because no single concrete domain is specified, PMS cannot import domain-specific standards. It cannot say how weather uncertainty should be communicated, how an engineering safety margin should be calculated, how an economic forecast should be scored, or how an organization should set thresholds. It can only track the structural logic that would be shared across such frames.

Inside the frame are the distinctions and pressures that make forecasting a praxis object: forecast versus outcome, probability versus certainty, model versus world, projection versus decision, anticipation versus prophecy, warning versus mandate, and bounded input versus settled future truth. Also inside are practical pressures: preparation, avoidance, allocation, intervention, delay, communication, escalation, or revision.

Outside the frame are all claims requiring concrete evidence or external normativity. The analysis does not include a specific model, named actor, real institution, actual forecast event, actual failure, legal dispute, clinical subject, or operational architecture. It also excludes metaphysical settlement of future truth. The future is structurally relevant, but not metaphysically adjudicated.

The protected constraints are Distance, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. Distance means that the analysis must not fuse forecast with verdict. Reversibility means the reading must remain revisable and falsifiable. Dignity-in-Practice means asymmetry must not be handled through shaming, person-ranking, coercive labeling, or tribunal-like verdicts.

Roles are only abstractly present. There may be forecasters, model-builders, decision-makers, recipients, exposed parties, organizations, publics, or affected groups, but the case does not instantiate them. This means role analysis is possible only as a map of what would need to be identified, not as a finding about actual roles.

Temporality, Θ, is structurally central. Forecasts operate through horizons, deadlines, update cadence, lag, anticipation, delay, and consequences. A forecast points toward what has not yet occurred. It may become stale, be revised, be confirmed, fail, or be overtaken by changed conditions. The temporality of the scene is not merely chronological; it is praxeological. Action may be demanded before confirmation. Consequences may become irreversible before uncertainty resolves.

The reversibility window is one of the most important missing concrete features. A concrete case would need to identify how long action remains reversible, when delay becomes costly, when update windows close, and how durable the consequences of action or inaction are. Without this, PMS can identify temporal pressure but cannot determine whether action, restraint, or delay is structurally preferable.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are explicitly named in the case material but not concretely mapped. There are asymmetric stakes and time pressure. This warrants Ω as a major operator. However, the direction and distribution of asymmetry remain unspecified. A stronger analysis would need to ask who bears false-positive costs, who bears false-negative costs, who can contest the forecast, who controls interpretation, who benefits from closure, and who is subject to irreversible consequences.

Because the case is not concrete, all later claims must remain case-type-bound. The scene is enough to generate a PMS and ANTICIPATION reading, but not enough to close the reading.

### PMS Core structural reading

#### Δ — Difference

Δ is the root structural operator in the case. Forecasting begins by differentiating possible futures from present conditions, model output from the world, probability from certainty, forecast from outcome, and bounded input from settled truth. The core case depends on preserving these distinctions. If a forecast is already treated as indistinguishable from the future, prophecy drift has effectively occurred; if the distinction remains alive, the forecast can remain a bounded input.

The operator is active and foundational. Its role is not to decide whether a forecast is good, accurate, useful, or justified. It simply provides the structural contrasts without which the case cannot be analyzed. The key calibration question is which distinction carries the case: probability versus certainty, scenario versus settled future, forecast versus command, or input versus verdict.

The main overclaim risk is to treat every use of a forecast as a collapse of Δ. A decision can rely on probabilistic information without confusing it with certainty. PMS must therefore distinguish reliance from fusion. If no stable distinction between probabilistic forecast and settled-future treatment can be shown, the prophecy-drift reading loses its entry point.

#### ∇ — Impulse

∇ appears as the directional pressure generated by forecast differences. A forecast does not merely sit in a frame; it orients possible action. It may push toward preparation, allocation, intervention, avoidance, escalation, communication, delay, or revision. This pressure is not automatically subjective desire. It can be institutional, practical, explanatory, risk-related, economic, engineering-based, or organizational.

The operator is active because forecasting is practically directional. A forecast that generates no orientation or tension would not produce the case problem. The calibration question is what kind of pressure is present and whether that pressure remains downstream of uncertainty or becomes self-authorizing.

The overclaim risk is to treat impulse as mandate. A forecast may create a practical gradient, but PMS cannot infer that action is correct, required, moral, legal, or optimal. ∇ activates the scene; it does not resolve it.

#### □ — Frame

□ is the forecast-use frame. It determines what counts as relevant, what thresholds matter, who can interpret, what uncertainty means, and how the forecast enters praxis. Forecast meaning depends on whether the frame is scientific inquiry, risk management, weather warning, engineering safety, organizational planning, or economic projection.

The operator is active and major. It allows PMS to ask where the forecast begins and where it is received. Prophecy drift is often a frame problem: an output generated as a probabilistic input in one frame is received as settled future truth in another. The source frame might be modeling; the target frame might be decision closure, authority, enforcement, or institutional justification.

Calibration requires naming the source and target frames. Because the case is abstract, the frame remains generic. This is sufficient for pre-analysis but insufficient for concrete adjudication. The overclaim risk is to assume that any frame shift is drift. Recontextualization can be legitimate; a forecast may be adapted for planning without becoming prophecy.

#### Λ — Non-Event

Λ is active but calibration-dependent. Forecasting structurally involves expected events, expected non-events, delays, absences, missing confirmations, absent warnings, absent updates, and uncertainty that remains unresolved. A forecast can be wrong because an expected event does not occur. It can also matter because a warning is absent, a correction is delayed, or uncertainty disclosure fails to appear.

In this case, Λ is crucial because prophecy drift often closes what Λ should keep open. A probabilistic projection concerns something that may or may not happen. The forecasted event is not yet present. The non-occurrence remains structurally possible. If the forecast is treated as settled future truth, Λ is effectively compressed into certainty.

Calibration for Λ is delicate. PMS must specify what expected occurrence, non-occurrence, warning, update, correction, or uncertainty disclosure is at issue. It must not treat every silence, absence, delay, disagreement, non-fit, or unresolved condition as meaningful drift. Absence is meaningful only within a frame of expectation.

The overclaim risk is high: PMS could become too good at finding meaning in absence. The case explicitly requires preserving possible non-capture. An absent update may be irrelevant. A delayed confirmation may be normal. A non-event may falsify the forecast, confirm caution, or remain ambiguous depending on the frame.

#### Α — Attractor

Α is weak-to-active and mostly conditional. The case does not show recurrence, stabilization, path dependence, or routine. It describes a possible structural transition, not a repeated institutional pattern. Α would become stronger if forecast-as-truth use recurred, stabilized into organizational routine, shaped role scripts, or produced path-dependent closure.

The operator matters because major drift patterns depend on stabilization. A single urgent decision or overconfident communication is not enough to establish an attractor. To claim Α strongly, one would need evidence of repeated forecast-to-prophecy transitions, routinized threshold bypassing, repeated closure practices, or institutional reinforcement.

The overclaim risk is to treat any plausible pattern as already stabilized. PMS must not infer an attractor merely because the abstract case is conceptually elegant. Α remains weak unless recurrence is shown.

#### Ω — Asymmetry

Ω is active as a case concern but dependency-calibrated as weak because its PMS dependency through Α is not concretely evidenced. The case material explicitly names asymmetric stakes and time pressure. That warrants Ω as a major analytic operator. However, because no concrete actors, roles, cost distributions, or recurring patterns are supplied, Ω cannot be fully mapped.

The case-specific role of Ω is to mark uneven exposure, capacity, vulnerability, authority, obligation, contestability, and error cost. Forecasts under asymmetry are structurally dangerous because closure may benefit one position while exposing another. The person or institution that treats the forecast as settled truth may not be the one that bears the main cost of error.

Calibration for Ω requires identifying who produces the forecast, who interprets it, who acts, who is exposed, who can contest, who benefits from closure, and who bears false-positive or false-negative costs. Without that map, Ω remains structurally relevant but under-specified.

The overclaim risk is moralization. Ω does not prove abuse, blame, exploitation, injustice, liability, or moral hierarchy. It identifies structural imbalance. Whether that imbalance is legitimate, illegitimate, unjust, necessary, or lawful requires external analysis.

#### Θ — Temporality

Θ is strongly case-relevant but also dependency-calibrated as weak where it depends on Α. Forecasting is inherently temporal. It projects horizons, anticipates trajectories, depends on update cadence, operates under deadlines, and may impose consequences before outcomes are known. The case cannot be understood without temporality.

The role of Θ is not merely that forecasts concern the future. It structures the pressure of acting before resolution. Forecasts can decay, update, be confirmed, fail, or be recontextualized. Time pressure can compress Distance. Reversibility windows can close. Delayed action can create costs. Premature action can create irreversible consequences.

Calibration requires asking: What is the horizon? What is the deadline? How often can the forecast update? What is the lag between forecast and action? How long do consequences last? When does reversibility close? These are not given by the abstract case.

The overclaim risk is to treat time pressure as sufficient justification for closure. Θ explains why closure pressure arises; it does not authorize prophecy drift. Urgency may justify action in some external frame, but it does not turn probability into certainty.

#### Φ — Recontextualization

Φ is the central Core operator. The case is fundamentally about a structure being moved into a new frame. A probabilistic forecast generated or presented as bounded input becomes treated as settled future truth, decision closure, authority, or prophecy-like certainty.

This is not merely error. It is frame transformation. The forecast’s status changes. It moves from “a projection under uncertainty that should inform judgment” to “the future as if already known enough to settle action, interpretation, or authority.” That movement is the heart of prophecy drift.

Calibration for Φ requires identifying source and target frames. The source frame is forecast-as-bounded-probabilistic-input. The target frame is forecast-as-settled-future-truth. A concrete case would need evidence that such movement occurred in communication, decision procedures, institutional framing, enforcement, or interpretation.

The overclaim risk is to call every adaptation drift. Recontextualization can be legitimate. A weather forecast may be translated into a warning. An engineering projection may inform safety design. A risk model may shape preparedness. These are not automatically prophecy drift if uncertainty, revisability, thresholds, and frame limits remain visible.

#### Χ — Distance

Χ is both a PMS application guardrail and a case-level safeguard. It prevents immediate fusion between forecast and truth, probability and command, model output and verdict. The completed YAML treats Χ as active because the analysis itself must preserve Distance, and because prophecy drift marks a possible loss of Distance in the scene.

Χ is not inaction. This is critical. Reflective distance does not mean refusal to act, denial of risk, passive skepticism, or paralysis. It means the forecast remains structurally separated from final authority. A high-stakes action can preserve Χ if uncertainty is explicit, thresholds are bounded, revision is possible, and the forecast is not treated as destiny.

Calibration for Χ asks whether actors preserve the separation between model output and future truth. Is uncertainty communicated? Are actions reversible where possible? Are thresholds explicit? Is contestation possible? Are updates allowed? Or does the forecast become verdict-like?

The overclaim risk is twofold. First, PMS could wrongly condemn urgent action as distance collapse. Second, PMS could suspend its own Distance and force the conclusion that prophecy drift occurred. The case avoids both by keeping the reading reversible and conditional.

#### Σ — Integration

Σ is weak-to-active. Forecast use requires integration if it is to become responsible praxis: uncertainty, rival models, thresholds, temporal horizons, stakes, non-model knowledge, update procedures, and action constraints must be coordinated without suppressing conflict. Prophecy drift is a risk where recontextualization outruns integration.

The role of Σ is not to make everything smooth. It is to coordinate disparate elements without false closure. A coherent narrative may be a failure of integration if it erases uncertainty. Conversely, a messy but explicit decision process may show stronger Σ if it keeps tensions visible.

Calibration asks whether uncertainty ranges, alternatives, thresholds, stakes, timing, revisions, and constraints are actually integrated. The abstract case does not show this, so Σ remains weak as a finding and strong as a requirement.

The overclaim risk is treating coherence as proof. PMS can generate an elegant structural formula, but that does not prove the forecast use was integrated, true, ethical, legal, or effective. Σ must remain discriminative, not decorative.

#### Ψ — Self-Binding

Ψ is weak and conditional. The abstract case does not show explicit self-binding by an anticipator, institution, forecaster, or decision-maker. It does not supply commitments to revision, accountability, uncertainty disclosure, restraint, or consequence-handling across time.

Ψ becomes relevant as a possible requirement for responsible forecast praxis. A concrete forecast-use scene might show Ψ if actors bind themselves to update procedures, accountability under error, transparency about uncertainty, restraint under asymmetry, or refusal to externalize obligation onto others.

The overclaim risk is severe. PMS must not attribute selfhood, agency, maturity, responsibility, or identity to an abstract process, model, or organization without evidence. Ψ does not turn forecasting into a self-model. It also does not allow the analyst to impose a responsibility verdict. In this case, Ψ remains conditional and mostly analytic.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The Core YAML is especially careful about dependency logic because the case tempts the analyst to jump directly to high-level claims. Later operators cannot be used cheaply. Ω depends on Α in the PMS dependency table. Θ depends on Ω and Α. Φ depends on Θ, Ω, and □. Χ depends on Φ, Θ, and □. Σ depends on Χ and Φ. Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. If earlier structures are weak or under-specified, later claims must reflect that weakness.

This does not mean an operator cannot be case-relevant when a dependency is weak. Ω is clearly relevant because the case names asymmetric stakes. Θ is clearly relevant because forecasting is temporal. But dependency discipline prevents the analyst from treating these as fully evidenced concrete structures. The case may activate operator relevance while still requiring weak or conditional dependency status.

Σ remains conditional because the case does not show actual integration. It says forecasts may become prophecy-like under pressure, but it does not show whether uncertainty, alternative models, thresholds, stakes, revision procedures, and action constraints were integrated. A concrete scene could strengthen or weaken the Σ reading.

Ψ remains even more conditional. Responsibility_R, Dignity-in-Practice as fully enacted, and any self-binding chain require explicit self-binding. The abstract case does not provide that. PMS must therefore refuse to convert future-oriented seriousness into responsibility verdict.

Coherence is not proof. This is one of the controlling lessons of the case. The PMS formula can be coherent: Δ generates distinctions; ∇ generates action pressure; □ frames it; Λ preserves possible non-event; Ω and Θ intensify exposure and temporal pressure; Φ marks recontextualization; Χ and Σ act as safeguards; Ψ remains conditional. But the elegance of that structure does not prove that it captures everything important. It does not prove empirical accuracy, legal relevance, moral adequacy, or practical wisdom.

Non-capture is not failure. PMS may not capture probabilistic scoring rules, weather-model calibration, economic uncertainty, engineering safety factors, institutional incentives, political legitimacy, affective fear, lived vulnerability, or legal standards. These are not defects in the case analysis unless PMS pretends to exhaust them. The completed YAML explicitly preserves non-capture zones.

PMS must not become a closure machine. This is particularly important because the case is about closure. A theory analyzing prophecy drift could itself drift into prophecy if it treats its own structural reading as final truth. The completed YAML avoids this by marking the reading as reversible, bounded, falsifiable, and open to rival formalizations.

Invalid dependency shortcuts would include claiming AD_A>>E without Α stabilization, claiming Responsibility_R without Ψ, treating Ω as proof of injustice, treating Θ as action authorization, treating Φ as drift whenever a forecast is adapted, treating Χ as inaction, treating Σ as truth, or treating SELF_FIXPOINT as active merely because forecasts involve long-term commitments.

The PMS Core case treats drift patterns as possible structural risks, not findings. That distinction controls the whole section. A drift pattern is not a diagnosis, not a score, not a moral verdict, not a clinical classification, not an institutional accusation, and not a person type. AD_A>>E would require a stronger showing that awareness expands under asymmetric recontextualization while integrated enactment is structurally suppressed or chronically delayed.

AD_Sigma_low, Under-integration, is also possible but not entailed. Its primary tension is Φ against Σ: recontextualization outruns integration. This is directly relevant to prophecy drift because a forecast may be moved into settled-future status before uncertainty, alternatives, stakes, temporal revision, and action constraints are integrated. Still, the completed YAML does not claim volatile coherence or inconsistent responsibility as findings. The axis effects remain under-specified for both coherence and responsibility.

Under-specification is not failure. It is a mark of discipline. The case is abstract; therefore some structures must remain possible rather than active findings. Non-assignment is not a low score. It is a refusal to turn the PMS grammar into a tribunal.

The associated coherence risk is high. The PMS reading fits the case smoothly: probability becomes prophecy through Φ under Ω and Θ, while Χ and Σ are needed safeguards. Because the fit is smooth, the danger of under-discrimination increases. PMS must not absorb every forecast failure, every disagreement, every resistance, every absence, every delay, or every rival framing into drift. That would be exactly the closure machine the case warns against.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant add-on is **PMS-ANTICIPATION**. It is applied as an overlay/application profile only. PMS.yaml remains the only PMS Base reference. The overlay does not redefine PMS operators, change dependencies, introduce new PMS operators, or rewrite the Core pass. Its reduced signatures are overlay shorthand only.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer here because the case specifically concerns future-oriented praxis. Core PMS identifies the operators involved in prophecy drift, but ANTICIPATION sharpens the question of how possible future events and non-events are carried under irreversibility and asymmetry without being closed into future truth. The overlay makes visible the difference between anticipation and prognosis, the role of Λ as open possibility or non-event, the risk of probability becoming authorization, and the demand that future-oriented stance bind the anticipator to restraint rather than bind others to compliance.

The overlay’s central definition is that anticipation is the structurally disciplined handling of possible future events and non-events under Θ and Ω, without closure claims. This is exactly the pressure point in the forecasting case. A forecast may inform anticipation, but it becomes structurally dangerous when it supplies authority.

The overlay adds several constraints beyond the Core reading. It insists that anticipation is not prediction, not command, not future-truth claim, and not action mandate. It distinguishes prognosis as a future-truth posture from anticipation as a present structural stance. It adds viability criteria: a configuration is viable if it remains coherent under Λ and responsibility-bearing under Ω and Θ without collapsing Χ. It also adds post-confirmation discipline: if a forecast proves right, that confirmation must reopen Distance rather than become epistemic superiority, entitlement, or retroactive inevitability.

The overlay’s minimal reduced signature, ANT_min, is active. It consists of Δ, □, Λ, Θ, and Χ as reduced shorthand. It means that a bounded anticipatory object is held in a frame, remains open as event or non-event, is temporally loaded, and is constrained by Distance. In this case, ANT_min applies because a probabilistic forecast is bounded, framed, future-oriented, unresolved, and vulnerable to closure.

The full viable anticipatory configuration, ANT_viable_full, is weak. It uses all eleven PMS operators, but as a viability standard rather than an established scene finding. A mature anticipatory configuration would discipline impulse, carry openness, recognize pattern stabilization, handle asymmetry across time, adapt, preserve Distance, integrate without closure, and bind the anticipator. The abstract case does not evidence Α, Σ, or Ψ strongly enough for a full viability finding.

ANT_to_planning_constraint is active as a non-operator overlay handle. It is not a PMS operator, not a new dependency route, and not an action mandate. It states that downstream planning or preparedness may emerge, but only under Χ, Dignity-in-Practice, and reversibility. This is important because the case explicitly includes organizational planning and risk management. The overlay does not forbid planning, preparedness, warning, staging, or even urgent action where an external frame justifies them. It constrains the PMS-ANTICIPATION reading so that probability does not become compulsion while Λ remains open.

The overlay identifies several risk markers. Certainty replacing Λ is the core prophecy-drift risk. Actionism neutralizing Θ appears where time pressure produces must-act compulsion and suppresses reversibility analysis. Χ suspension appears where model output, probability, urgency, or expertise becomes verdict-like authority. Ω instrumentalization appears where asymmetry is used to enforce compliance. Ψ externalization appears where the anticipator’s own restraint is replaced by demands that others accept, comply, or act.

The most directly relevant ANTICIPATION drift risk is probability-induced action compulsion. The forecast’s probabilistic form may be rhetorically transformed into “we must act because…” The overlay does not deny that action may sometimes be necessary. It says anticipation itself cannot authorize that action. Action requires separate justification, frame-specific thresholds, and preserved constraints.

Another relevant risk is preemptive authoritarian drift or Ω-inflation. This remains a risk, not a finding. It would occur if asymmetric stakes or authority positions turned forecast anticipation into enforced compliance. Moralization of uncertainty is also a risk: uncertainty becomes a normative warrant, and pressure is framed as virtue. Preventive escalation is a risk where irreversible commitments are justified before Λ is resolved.

The overlay also introduces post-confirmation discipline. If a forecast later proves right, the danger does not disappear. It may intensify. Confirmation can produce Ψ-overbinding, where identity becomes tied to correctness; Ω-inflation, where validation becomes superiority; Χ-erosion, where being right reduces reflective distance; and retroactive Λ-denial, where uncertainty is rewritten as inevitability. The overlay’s rule is that confirmation calls for renewed Distance, not accelerated entitlement.

Responsibility in ANTICIPATION is not “being right.” Responsibility is binding oneself to restraint, revision, uncertainty disclosure, and consequence-handling under Ω and Θ. In this case, responsibility remains weak because no concrete anticipator or capacity map is supplied. But the overlay sharpens the principle: anticipation may bind the anticipator; it must not bind others by converting probability into command.

Dignity-in-Practice is active as an overlay constraint. Anticipation is PMS-valid only if it preserves D under Ω, especially after confirmation. The overlay explicitly excludes fear production, coercion generation, and replacement of justification by anticipatory stance. It does not allow forecast probability to become a dignity-eroding instrument.

The overlay remains subordinate to PMS Base throughout. Its constructs—ANT_min, ANT_viable_full, ANT_to_planning_constraint, viability markers, prognosis contrast, red flags, post-confirmation discipline—are not new PMS operators. They are application handles for reading anticipation-specific structures through PMS.

Together they produce a disciplined reading: forecasting without prophecy is not the refusal to forecast, and not the refusal to act; it is the refusal to let forecast-status become future truth without the necessary structural constraints.

### Example 1 — The storm cone and the evacuation memo

A regional weather office issues a probabilistic storm forecast. The forecast cone shows a substantial chance that a coastal district may experience severe winds and flooding within forty-eight hours, but the model output remains explicitly uncertain: the storm may shift north, weaken, or arrive later than expected. Emergency planners receive the forecast and prepare several options. One internal memo, however, begins to describe the projected landfall as “the incoming event” rather than “a possible event.” A second memo says that “delay is irresponsible” and urges immediate full evacuation of all exposed zones. The actual case is not a legal dispute, and no outcome has yet occurred. The vignette concerns the structural movement from weather forecast as bounded probabilistic input to weather forecast as settled future truth.

The example demonstrates the central case problem without deciding whether evacuation is correct. A high-stakes weather forecast may properly orient preparation. It may justify readiness, communication, resource staging, and threshold planning. None of that is prophecy drift by itself. The drift risk appears only when the forecast’s uncertainty is no longer carried and the projected event begins to function as if it were already settled. The difference is not between “act” and “do nothing.” The difference is between action under maintained uncertainty and action justified by the conversion of probability into future-truth status.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. Δ distinguishes forecast from event, probability from certainty, and warning from command. ∇ appears as the pressure to prepare, protect, communicate, or evacuate. □ is the emergency-planning frame, which differs from the meteorological modeling frame. Λ is central because the storm may not strike the district, may arrive differently, or may fail to reach the projected intensity. Α remains weak unless such forecast-to-certainty memos become a repeated institutional pattern. Ω is present because exposure is uneven: officials decide, residents bear disruption or danger, and some groups may have less capacity to evacuate. Θ is strong because the window is closing, updates matter, and consequences may become irreversible. Φ is the key risk: the forecast is recontextualized from probabilistic input into the “incoming event.” Χ is the safeguard that keeps probability from becoming verdict. Σ would require integration of uncertainty, evacuation capacity, timing, alternative routes, false-positive costs, false-negative costs, and update procedures. Ψ would require self-binding by the planners to revision, transparency, accountability, and restraint; it cannot be presumed from the vignette.

The ANTICIPATION overlay makes the example sharper. ANT_min is active: a bounded possible event is held within a frame under Θ and Χ while Λ remains open. ANT_viable_full is only weak unless the planners demonstrate actual integration and self-binding. The planning constraint is active: preparedness may follow, but it must not convert the forecast into compulsion while uncertainty remains. The main overlay risks are certainty replacing Λ, probability-induced action compulsion, Χ suspension under urgency, and Ω instrumentalization if authorities use the forecast to silence contestation rather than to coordinate protection.

The MIP / AHP / QC status remains limited. No independent MIP or AHP layer is supplied by the case stack. QC is relevant only as boundary discipline: the example must not conclude that officials were right, wrong, negligent, authoritarian, cowardly, or irrational. It can show where stronger evidence would be needed.

The forbidden claims are crucial. The example does not claim that evacuation is unjustified. It does not claim that forecast-based emergency action is prophecy. It does not claim that a later direct hit would prove the memo was structurally valid, nor that a miss would prove bad faith. It does not make a legal claim about duty of care, a diagnostic claim about panic, or a metaphysical claim about future certainty.

The non-capture note is equally important. Meteorological model skill, emergency law, evacuation logistics, disability access, transportation capacity, public trust, and actual storm behavior are not captured by the PMS reading. These may determine what should be done in the concrete case. PMS only tracks the structural danger of closing uncertainty into authority.

The article takeaway is that disciplined forecasting does not mean refusing early action. It means preserving the difference between forecast and future while acting, if necessary, through explicit thresholds, reversibility where possible, and continued update discipline. The storm forecast becomes prophecy only when the possible event is no longer carried as possible.

### Example 2 — The bridge-fatigue model and the maintenance shutdown

An engineering team monitors a bridge using sensors that estimate fatigue accumulation in several load-bearing components. A model projects a rising probability of structural failure within the next three years if traffic load remains unchanged and maintenance is deferred. The model does not predict a collapse on a specific date. It produces a risk envelope under assumptions about traffic, weather, materials, and inspection intervals. A city infrastructure committee receives the report. Some members treat the model as a bounded input for maintenance planning. Others begin to speak as if “the bridge will fail” unless a shutdown is imposed immediately. The vignette focuses on the structural status of the forecast in engineering praxis, not on whether a shutdown is technically correct.

This example demonstrates the need to distinguish prudent precaution from prophecy drift. Engineering often requires action before certainty. Waiting for certainty in safety-critical systems may be reckless in non-PMS terms. A conservative maintenance decision may be entirely legitimate. PMS does not contest that. The structural question is different: does the probabilistic forecast remain a risk input integrated with inspection data, safety margins, reversibility windows, traffic alternatives, and material evidence, or does it become a settled-future statement that bypasses integration?

The PMS Core hooks show why later operators cannot be used cheaply. Δ marks risk projection versus actual failure, model estimate versus material condition, and maintenance recommendation versus future truth. ∇ is the pressure toward inspection, repair, closure, reinforcement, or monitoring. □ is the engineering safety and municipal decision frame. Λ is present because the projected failure may not occur, may occur differently, or may become less likely after maintenance, load reduction, or new inspection data. Α is weak unless the city repeatedly turns model outputs into automatic shutdown narratives. Ω is present because the costs of action and inaction fall unevenly: commuters, local businesses, maintenance crews, public officials, and vulnerable users may be differently affected. Θ is central because fatigue accumulates over time, inspection intervals matter, and delay can narrow options. Φ appears if the model is recontextualized from risk input into the claim that failure is settled. Χ is the discipline that keeps the model from becoming fate. Σ is the major calibration point: a responsible engineering decision would integrate sensor uncertainty, physical inspection, redundancy, traffic load, maintenance feasibility, public safety, and reversibility. Ψ remains conditional unless decision-makers bind themselves to review, update, transparency, and accountability.

The example is especially useful for clarifying Σ. Integration is not the same as moderation. A fully integrated decision may still choose a temporary shutdown. Conversely, a decision that avoids shutdown may be poorly integrated if it ignores risk. PMS does not privilege action or restraint. It asks whether the future-oriented projection is being integrated without false certainty. This protects the analysis from becoming anti-precautionary.

The ANTICIPATION overlay adds the distinction between prognosis and anticipation. The fatigue model may supply prognosis-like content: projected risk under defined assumptions. It may inform anticipation. But prognosis becomes structurally invalid as anticipation if it supplies authority by closing Λ, suspending Χ, or eroding Dignity-in-Practice. ANT_min is active because the possible failure is bounded, framed, temporally loaded, and not yet resolved. ANT_viable_full remains weak because the vignette does not prove institutional self-binding or recurring pattern stabilization. The planning constraint is active: downstream maintenance planning is permitted but constrained by Χ, D, and reversibility.

The overlay also highlights post-confirmation discipline. Suppose later inspection finds severe corrosion. That confirmation would not retroactively prove that all prior uncertainty was illusion. It would require renewed Distance: the model was useful in this case, but not thereby transformed into a general authority beyond calibration. If the bridge later proves safe, that also does not prove the model was useless or the planners irrational. Forecast confirmation and disconfirmation both require disciplined interpretation.

MIP / AHP / QC status remains limited to QC boundaries. There is no separate ranking, scoring, or priority matrix here. AHP-like tradeoffs might later be introduced in a concrete infrastructure decision, but they are not part of this case artefact. QC prevents the example from becoming an engineering recommendation.

Forbidden claims include: the bridge should be closed; the bridge should remain open; engineers are alarmist; officials are negligent; the model is valid; the model is invalid; shutdown is legally required; or failure is metaphysically imminent. None follows from the PMS analysis.

The non-capture note is substantial. Material science, civil engineering standards, inspection regimes, public-safety law, budget constraints, redundancy analysis, and acceptable-risk thresholds are outside the PMS reading. Those domains may be decisive in real action. PMS contributes only the structural grammar of forecast handling.

The article takeaway is that precaution is not prophecy when it preserves uncertainty, integrates relevant constraints, and remains revisable. Engineering forecast use becomes structurally risky when a model’s projection stops being a bounded input and starts functioning as the future itself.

### Example 3 — The revenue forecast and the organizational hiring freeze

A university department receives an internal financial forecast projecting a significant revenue shortfall for the next academic year. The projection depends on enrollment assumptions, grant renewal uncertainty, energy prices, and central administration allocations. The forecast is circulated to department leadership as a planning input. Within a week, the projection is repeated in meetings as “the coming deficit.” Several proposed hires are informally suspended. Junior staff are told that “the numbers make this unavoidable,” although the forecast has not yet been updated after late enrollment data and no formal budget decision has been made. The example is not a labor-law case, not an accusation, and not a claim about actual finances. It is a bounded organizational-planning vignette about how a forecast can acquire settled-future status.

This example demonstrates the role of Ω and Dignity-in-Practice. Organizational forecasts often affect people who did not produce them and cannot contest their assumptions. A revenue model may be technically uncertain, but its downstream effects may be immediate: hiring slows, opportunities disappear, morale shifts, and some staff become more exposed than others. The risk is not merely that the forecast may be wrong. The risk is that the forecast becomes an authority object that reorganizes roles before its uncertainty has been carried.

PMS Core begins with Δ: projected deficit versus actual deficit, model assumption versus budget fact, planning input versus employment decision, and financial caution versus inevitability. ∇ appears as pressure to save, freeze, delay, justify, or reorganize. □ is the organizational planning frame, which differs from the financial modeling frame and from the employment-governance frame. Λ is active because the deficit may not occur, may be smaller, may be offset, or may depend on missing data. Α is weak unless the department or university regularly converts forecasts into informal pre-decisions. Ω is major because authority and exposure are uneven: leadership interprets the forecast, while prospective hires, junior staff, and dependent programs may bear the effects. Θ is central because budget cycles, hiring timelines, enrollment updates, and irreversible opportunity losses unfold over time. Φ is the drift point: “projected shortfall” becomes “the coming deficit.” Χ is needed to prevent the forecast from becoming a verdict. Σ would require integration of uncertainty, alternative budget scenarios, timing, governance procedures, staff exposure, and revision conditions. Ψ would require leadership to bind itself to transparent update practices, accountability for assumptions, and restraint in communicating inevitability.

The example is useful because prophecy drift here may occur without dramatic language. No one needs to say “prophecy.” The drift can happen through ordinary administrative phrases: “the numbers say,” “we have no choice,” “the deficit is coming,” or “delay would be irresponsible.” These phrases may collapse Λ, suspend Χ, and externalize responsibility onto the forecast. PMS does not claim that such phrases always do this. It marks the calibration question: are they shorthand within a still-revisable planning process, or are they functioning as authority?

The ANTICIPATION overlay clarifies this distinction. The forecast may support anticipation: the department can prepare scenarios, identify reversible commitments, protect vulnerable positions, and plan update points. ANT_min is active because a possible future financial state is bounded, framed, temporally relevant, and not yet settled. ANT_viable_full remains weak unless leadership demonstrates integration and self-binding. The planning constraint is active: preparedness is allowed, but probability must not become compulsion while Λ persists. The main overlay risks are Ψ externalization, Ω instrumentalization, and certainty replacing Λ. The phrase “the numbers make this unavoidable” is structurally risky because it may shift binding away from decision-makers and onto the forecast as if the forecast itself commanded action.

This example also shows why Dignity-in-Practice is not metaphysical dignity. It does not say staff have dignity because PMS grants it. It constrains the handling of asymmetry: do not use a forecast to make exposed people carry uncertainty as if it were already settled fact. Dignity-in-Practice would require communication that preserves uncertainty, avoids humiliation or fatalism, and keeps contestation or revision possible where roles are affected.

MIP / AHP / QC status remains boundary-only. A future decision process might use ranking or prioritization methods to compare budget options, but this example does not. QC prevents the vignette from becoming a claim that the hiring freeze is wrong, illegal, or irrational. It also prevents the opposite claim that leadership is simply prudent. The example demonstrates structural risk, not verdict.

Forbidden claims include: the university is acting unlawfully; leadership is dishonest; staff are harmed in a legally cognizable way; the forecast is inaccurate; the hiring freeze is unjustified; or the deficit will not occur. The vignette also does not diagnose anxiety, cowardice, authoritarianism, or bad faith.

The non-capture note is broad. Budget rules, enrollment analytics, grant accounting, labor agreements, governance procedures, institutional politics, and actual financial constraints are not captured by PMS. A rival organizational-sociology reading might better explain why forecasts become authority. A governance reading might better evaluate procedural legitimacy. A financial analysis might determine whether the forecast is sound.

The article takeaway is that organizational forecasting becomes structurally dangerous when it lets decision-makers speak as if responsibility has migrated into the model. Forecasting without prophecy requires the opposite: the more uncertain and asymmetrically consequential the projection is, the more explicitly the anticipator must preserve Distance, integration, and self-binding restraint.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The YAML analyses preserve several non-capture zones and rival readings. These are not defects. They are necessary because PMS and PMS-ANTICIPATION are structural grammars, not total theories of forecasting.

An epistemological rival reading would focus on evidence, uncertainty, probability, prediction, model validity, calibration, scoring rules, confidence intervals, Bayesian updating, and truth conditions. It preserves something PMS does not fully capture: the internal standards by which forecasts are made, evaluated, improved, or rejected. PMS can distinguish probability from authority, but it does not calculate probability.

A Bayesian decision-theoretic reading would focus on expected value, priors, updating, decision thresholds, costs of false positives and false negatives, and rational action under uncertainty. It preserves the action-guiding logic of probabilistic decision-making. PMS must not treat every decision under uncertainty as prophecy drift merely because the decision uses probabilistic projections.

A risk-governance reading would focus on accountability, institutional roles, burden distribution, transparency, legitimacy, and public decision processes. It preserves governance questions that PMS can mark structurally but not settle normatively. PMS can identify Ω and Dignity-in-Practice constraints, but it cannot by itself design or validate governance.

An engineering-safety reading would focus on safety margins, tolerances, failure modes, redundancy, conservative design, and acceptable risk. It preserves the fact that some domains require action before certainty and may legitimately build for worst-case scenarios. PMS must not confuse conservative safety design with prophecy drift unless the forecast’s status actually changes into settled truth or coercive authority.

An organizational-sociology reading would focus on institutional pressure, authority structures, accountability avoidance, routinization, internal incentives, and closure practices. It preserves the social mechanisms by which prophecy drift might become an attractor. PMS marks Α only weakly here because no concrete organizational recurrence is supplied; sociology might be needed to establish it.

A language-practice or communication reading would focus on how forecast language moves between technical and public contexts: probability words, warning levels, confidence statements, uncertainty visualization, managerial summaries, and media translation. This preserves the pragmatic pathway through which Φ may occur.

A phenomenological reading would focus on lived anticipation, anxiety, dread, hope, urgency, waiting, and felt futurity. PMS explicitly does not analyze inner experience as such. ANTICIPATION also does not claim to capture affective futurity.

An ethical or precautionary reading would focus on when it is morally required to act under uncertainty, when precaution is justified, and how harms should be weighed. PMS avoids moral verdicts. It can make structural constraints visible, but it does not produce ethical obligation by itself.

A legal-forensic reading would focus on foreseeability, duty, causation, negligence, standard of care, evidentiary sufficiency, and liability. PMS excludes legal conclusions unless a legal frame and evidence are supplied.

An anti-capture reading would resist the translation of forecasting into PMS terms altogether, arguing that model practice, political authority, or lived vulnerability is distorted by operator grammar. The completed YAML leaves this possibility open. Non-capture is preserved where PMS coherence would weaken what is decisive in the case.

The examples reinforce these rival openings rather than closing them. The storm vignette leaves meteorology, emergency law, evacuation logistics, and public trust outside PMS capture. The bridge vignette leaves engineering standards, material science, inspection protocols, and acceptable-risk thresholds outside PMS capture. The revenue-forecast vignette leaves accounting rules, labor agreements, governance procedures, and organizational politics outside PMS capture. This is not a weakness of the article. It is the condition under which PMS remains a bounded structural grammar rather than an imperial explanatory system.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS Core reading is weakened if the forecast remains explicitly probabilistic, revisable, bounded, and separated from future truth in communication and action. If uncertainty ranges are preserved, thresholds are clear, alternatives are considered, and updates are allowed, the prophecy-drift reading becomes weaker.

It is also weakened if decision procedures show strong Σ integration: uncertainty, rival forecasts, temporal windows, stakes, non-model knowledge, action constraints, and revision conditions are coordinated. In that case, forecast use may be prudent, even urgent, without becoming prophecy.

The PMS reading is weakened if no frame shift can be shown. Φ is central. Without evidence that the forecast moved from bounded input to settled future truth, the case becomes general forecast-use rather than prophecy drift.

It is weakened if no asymmetry can be concretely identified. The abstract case names asymmetric stakes, but a concrete case would need to show who is exposed, who acts, who controls interpretation, and who bears costs. Without that, Ω remains under-specified.

It is weakened if no recurrence or stabilization appears. Α-dependent drift patterns cannot be claimed from a single scene or abstract possibility. Stabilized prophecy drift requires repeated patterning or path dependence.

It is weakened if no self-binding structure is present. Responsibility_R and full ANTICIPATION viability require Ψ. If no actor or institution binds itself to revision, accountability, uncertainty disclosure, and restraint, then Ψ remains weak.

The ANTICIPATION overlay is weakened if the case is better treated as ordinary prognosis, statistical decision-making, engineering precaution, or scenario planning without closure drift. It is also weakened if action under uncertainty is justified by external criteria while preserving uncertainty and Dignity-in-Practice.

The reading becomes under-discriminating if it treats all forecast reliance as prophecy, all urgency as Distance collapse, all prevention as coercion, all asymmetry as abuse, all coherence as closure, all delay as AD_A>>E, or all disagreement as failed integration.

The reading becomes over-assimilative if every absence becomes Λ, every reframing becomes Φ drift, every pattern becomes Α, every future-oriented statement becomes prognosis, or every action under uncertainty becomes probability-induced compulsion.

Operationalization would require much more than the abstract case supplies. For Δ, one would need operational evidence distinguishing forecast, model output, probability, outcome, decision threshold, and settled-future treatment. For ∇, one would need records showing how the forecast oriented action pressure. For □, one would need explicit domain and decision frames. For Λ, one would need specified expected events, non-events, updates, warnings, or absences. For Α, one would need evidence of recurrence or stabilization. For Ω, one would need a distribution map of authority, exposure, vulnerability, capacity, obligation, and cost. For Θ, one would need horizon, deadline, cadence, lag, consequence duration, and reversibility window. For Φ, one would need traceable movement from probabilistic input to future-truth status. For Χ, one would need evidence of maintained or lost Distance. For Σ, one would need evidence of integration. For Ψ, one would need evidence of self-binding commitments.

The overlay adds its own operational requirements: identify what might not happen, where closure pressure appears, who carries exposure, where action pressure rises, whether probability is being used rhetorically, who is bound by anticipation, and whether binding is internal or externalized.

None of these operationalization requirements is fulfilled by the abstract material. The three examples illustrate what such evidence might look like in bounded vignettes, but they do not supply evidence for the original abstract case. Therefore the final status remains valid with limits.

### Misuse boundary

The case must not be used diagnostically. It does not identify a person, institution, expert community, planner, scientist, model-builder, manager, or public as prophetic, immature, irrational, coercive, or irresponsible.

It must not be used for person ranking. PMS operators and ANTICIPATION markers do not rank intelligence, dignity, moral worth, rationality, maturity, or epistemic superiority.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusions. Nothing here establishes negligence, causation, culpability, liability, foreseeability, evidentiary sufficiency, or standard of care.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. The case does not prove anything about the ultimate nature of the future, probability, time, causality, fate, or possibility.

It must not be used as empirical proof. The analysis does not validate or invalidate any forecast, model, domain method, or decision outcome.

It must not be used as implementation validation. The YAML and this article are not forecasting systems, decision engines, risk tools, governance architectures, or AI implementations.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. The fact that PMS can render the case does not prove PMS completeness, uniqueness, finality, or superiority.

It must not treat the add-on as an operator. PMS-ANTICIPATION constructs such as ANT_min, ANT_viable_full, prognosis contrast, viability markers, post-confirmation discipline, or red flags are overlay handles, not PMS Base operators.

It must not treat drift as verdict. Drift risks are conditional markers. They do not establish that anyone did anything wrong.

It must not produce closure by authority. The analysis itself must not become a prophecy about forecasting. Its strongest conclusion is structural: forecasting without prophecy requires carrying future-oriented uncertainty under time and asymmetry without converting probability into settled truth or command.

It must not treat the examples as evidence. The examples demonstrate the architecture; they do not prove that such drift occurs in any real case. They are bounded vignettes, not empirical findings, legal hypotheticals with conclusions, or diagnostic scenes.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of the case is that forecasting becomes structurally dangerous not when it is probabilistic, not when it informs action, and not even when stakes are high, but when its status changes. The core transition is Φ: a bounded projection is recontextualized into settled future truth. Around that transition, Δ preserves the distinction between forecast and future; ∇ marks the pressure toward response; □ locates the frame; Λ keeps possible event and non-event open; Α remains weak unless recurrence is shown; Ω marks asymmetry without moralizing it; Θ marks temporal pressure without authorizing closure; Χ preserves Distance; Σ remains required but not proven; and Ψ remains conditional unless self-binding is evidenced.

The ANTICIPATION overlay sharpens the same result by naming the future-oriented discipline more precisely. Forecasting without prophecy is not refusal to forecast and not refusal to prepare. It is disciplined anticipation: carrying possible events and non-events under irreversibility and asymmetry without letting probability become authority. ANT_min is active as an overlay shorthand because the case involves bounded possibility, frame, Λ, Θ, and Χ. Full anticipatory viability remains weak because recurrence, integration, and self-binding are not concretely evidenced. Downstream planning is possible, but only under Distance, Dignity-in-Practice, and reversibility.

The examples contribute by making the abstract structure readable across three domains. The storm example shows how emergency warning can support action without automatically becoming prophecy, and how urgency can pressure Λ and Χ. The bridge example shows why precaution is not itself drift and why Σ must not be confused with moderation or inaction. The revenue-forecast example shows how ordinary organizational language can allow responsibility to migrate into “the numbers,” especially under Ω. The examples demonstrate the architecture; they do not replace it, validate it empirically, or prove any real-world claim.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is claimed. The article does not establish forecast accuracy, model validity, decision correctness, moral fault, legal liability, clinical meaning, metaphysical truth, implementation viability, or PMS Base validation. It does not say that urgent action is wrong, that restraint is always superior, that prognosis is always illegitimate, or that uncertainty forbids decision. It does not claim that all resistance, non-fit, disagreement, delay, rupture, or absence is drift. It does not rank persons or institutions.

The boundary discipline is therefore not an accessory to the case; it is part of the case’s logic. A theory analyzing prophecy drift must not become prophetic about its own analysis. PMS coherence is not proof. Risk is not finding. Under-specification is not failure. Non-assignment is not a low score. Non-capture remains possible. Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological rather than ontological. Add-on terms remain overlay handles rather than PMS operators.

The final methodological takeaway is that forecasting without prophecy requires a disciplined separation between future-oriented usefulness and future-truth authority. Forecasts may orient, warn, prepare, and inform. They may support planning and even urgent action in external frames. But within the PMS and PMS-ANTICIPATION reading, they remain structurally viable only when they preserve uncertainty, maintain Distance, integrate constraints, handle asymmetry without coercion, and bind the anticipator to restraint rather than binding others to compliance. This is a methodological boundary claim, not a general policy rule, moral command, legal standard, or substitute for domain-specific decision criteria.
