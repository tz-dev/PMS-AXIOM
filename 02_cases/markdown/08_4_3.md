### Why this case matters

Epistemic injustice matters because it names a morally difficult field in which people may be wronged not merely as recipients of material harm, legal disadvantage, or interpersonal disrespect, but in their practical standing as knowers, interpreters, and credible participants in shared understanding. The case therefore does not begin with a simple action, a single rule violation, or a measurable institutional failure. It begins with a more elusive closure-demand: how should a situation be understood when someone’s contribution to knowledge, interpretation, testimony, or shared intelligibility is structurally discounted, misread, excluded, or not taken up?

This makes the case important for AXIOM because it is a paradigmatic example of a classical closure-pressure that cannot be responsibly resolved by simply declaring one side right, one side wrong, one participant mature, one participant immature, one frame legitimate, or one interpretation final. Epistemic injustice is not only about what is said. It is about whether a contribution is allowed to count as knowledge, whether a speaker is received as a credible participant, whether available interpretive resources are sufficient for a lived or situated experience to become intelligible, and whether the frame of shared understanding is already structured in a way that distributes uptake unequally.

The philosophical difficulty lies in the fact that the wrong, if present, may occur partly through the conditions of recognizability themselves. A person may speak, but not be heard as a knower. A person may interpret, but not be recognized as an interpreter. A person may participate, but only within a frame that has already reduced the range of intelligible contributions. The closure-demand is therefore not merely “what happened?” or “who is right?” but “what counts as uptake, credibility, interpretation, and participation in this frame?” This is structurally demanding because the frame that is supposed to adjudicate the case may itself be part of what is at issue.

For PMS-AXIOM, this makes the case especially useful. AXIOM is not trying to solve epistemic injustice metaphysically, empirically, legally, clinically, or diagnostically. It is not trying to prove that a particular wrong occurred. It is not trying to generate a doctrine of personhood, testimony, oppression, hermeneutics, or law. Instead, it maps the closure-demand: a morally charged concept asks for recognition, explanation, and possible correction, but the available material is abstract and non-scenic. PMS can make the operator structure visible; CRITIQUE can show where critique-readability begins and where it does not yet become productive or stable critique; MIP can prevent the analysis from turning into premature scoring or governance use; AHP can identify where the analysis artefact itself needs hardening.

The difficulty is also that epistemic injustice is highly susceptible to over-assimilation. Once the phrase is introduced, it may become tempting to redescribe every disagreement as injustice, every non-response as exclusion, every refusal as non-uptake, every rival framing as suppression, and every demand for evidence as illegitimate credibility discounting. The completed analyses explicitly resist that move. They preserve the possibility that a given case may instead be ordinary disagreement, evidentiary caution, conceptual mismatch, legitimate refusal, silence, non-capture, or a rival formalization outside PMS. This is not a weakening of the analysis; it is part of its discipline.

The case therefore belongs in AXIOM because it shows what a structural cartography can do when a concept has high moral force but low scene-specific evidence. AXIOM can represent the closure-demand without satisfying it by authority. It can show where asymmetry, non-uptake, recontextualization, and dignity-in-practice become relevant, while also showing where the analysis must stop. It can keep visible the difference between critique-readability and validated critique, between structural projection and empirical proof, between possible dignity stress and D-module activation, between coherence and adequacy, between formal completion and evidential completion.

That final distinction is central. The completed YAMLs are formally complete, but the case itself is not concretely complete. The analysis is strong because it does not hide that difference. It shows the performance of PMS, CRITIQUE, MIP, and AHP precisely by refusing to let their formal power become a substitute for scene evidence, accountability, or rival readings.

### Scene and frame structure

The case does not present a concrete scene. It presents a conceptual scene: morally difficult situations in which people may be wronged in their capacity as knowers, interpreters, or credible participants in shared understanding. This means the “scene” is not an event but a structured domain of possible events. The analysis therefore treats the scene boundary as abstract: a shared-understanding field in which credibility, interpretation, uptake, and participation become relevant.

The frame, in PMS terms, is the credibility-and-interpretation frame. This frame defines what counts as knowledge, testimony, interpretation, contribution, relevance, and credible participation. It is the frame within which a person may be recognized as a knower or discounted as unreliable, heard as an interpreter or reduced to an object of interpretation, included as a participant or kept outside shared intelligibility. The frame is not neutral merely because it is shared. It may itself distribute relevance, authority, credibility, and uptake.

Inside the frame are questions such as: who is allowed to count as a knower; what forms of expression are treated as credible; which interpretive resources are available; who can name an experience intelligibly; what counts as evidence; what counts as response; what counts as non-response; what counts as disagreement; and what counts as exclusion. These are PMS-readable because they involve difference, role space, expectations, asymmetry, temporal persistence, and possible recontextualization.

Outside the frame are claims that the YAMLs explicitly refuse. The analysis does not decide metaphysical personhood. It does not define epistemic agency in final philosophical terms. It does not adjudicate legal rights or duties. It does not measure credibility empirically. It does not decide whether a given refusal to believe someone is wrongful. It does not convert silence into harm by default. It does not prescribe institutional correction. It does not produce a universal theory of injustice.

The protected constraints are central. First, the analysis must remain reversible: any reading is revisable if a rival explanation discriminates better or if concrete evidence undermines the operator mapping. Second, it must maintain Distance, Χ: it must not fuse with accusation, sanction, urgency, or closure. Third, it must protect Dignity-in-Practice: asymmetry may be examined, but not through shaming, humiliation, public labeling, person-ranking, or ontological evaluation. Fourth, it must preserve non-capture: some aspects of lived epistemic harm, historical oppression, refusal, legal context, or phenomenological experience may exceed the PMS grammar.

The roles are conceptual rather than concrete. The completed MIP analysis names several role positions without assigning them to real actors. The first is the affected epistemic participant: the role of a person or group whose knowing, interpretation, or credibility may be discounted. The second is the frame-holder or listener: the role that grants, withholds, filters, or structures uptake. The third is the shared-understanding field: the relational, institutional, discursive, or cultural context in which credibility and interpretation become meaningful. The fourth is the analyst role: the formalizing observer who must not turn the structural analysis into a verdict.

Temporality, Θ, remains conditional. Epistemic injustice often appears temporally: repeated credibility deficits, delayed recognition, accumulated interpretive exclusion, inherited hermeneutical gaps, recurring non-uptake, or historical stabilization of roles. But the abstract case material does not provide duration, sequence, recurrence, or trajectory. Therefore temporality is relevant but weak. A later concrete case could activate Θ more strongly by showing repetition, accumulation, records, review delays, institutional memory, public archive, or long-term role lock-in.

The reversibility window is analytic rather than scene-level. The analysis itself remains reversible because no concrete scene has been adjudicated. The case does not yet contain a real appeal path, response protocol, correction procedure, review interval, or exit option. In a later applied scene, reversibility would require concrete mechanisms: reply rights, frame clarification, evidence review, safe participation channels, correction opportunities, and protections against irreversible labeling.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are conceptually central but evidentially under-specified. The core asymmetry concerns credibility, interpretive authority, participation standing, and power to define the frame of shared understanding. Affected participants may bear the cost of non-uptake; frame-holders may possess the ability to grant or withhold recognition; institutions may stabilize certain credibility norms; audiences may amplify or erase contributions. Yet without a concrete scene, these remain possible asymmetry structures, not findings about actual actors.

This is the central consequence of the non-concrete scene status: the case can be structurally legible without being adjudicated. PMS may show where a case of epistemic injustice would need to be specified. CRITIQUE may show that the concept is critique-readable at a weak minimal threshold. MIP may show why scoring is not justified. AHP may show how the analysis artefact should be hardened. But none of these layers turns the abstract material into a concrete finding.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading treats epistemic injustice as a possible structural configuration in which differences of epistemic role and standing are framed by shared-understanding conditions, possibly marked by non-uptake, possibly stabilized over time, asymmetrically distributed, recontextualized as epistemic wronging, and held under reflective distance. The primary formula given in the YAMLs is:

Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ

This formula is not a proof. It is a structural projection with conditional middle operators. It intentionally stops short of treating Σ and Ψ as fully active case results, because integration and self-binding are not established by the abstract case material.

**Δ — Difference** is active and foundational. The case becomes PMS-legible because distinctions are drawn between knower and excluded knower, interpreter and interpreted object, credible participant and discounted participant, recognized contribution and dismissed contribution, shared understanding and exclusion from shared understanding. Without Δ, the case would remain an undifferentiated moral concern. Δ makes the relevant contrasts visible.

The calibration issue for Δ is that these distinctions must remain structural, not ontological. PMS is not sorting persons into higher and lower epistemic types. It is not saying that some persons are knowers by essence and others are not. It is mapping the role-difference that becomes relevant when credibility, interpretation, and participation are at issue. Overclaim would occur if Δ were used to rank persons, essentialize groups, or treat epistemic standing as a fixed metaphysical property rather than a frame-bound role relation.

**∇ — Impulse** is weak to active. The case implies directional pressure toward uptake, recognition, interpretation, correction, testimony, or participation. A person whose contribution is discounted may seek recognition; a frame may produce pressure to resolve whether a claim counts; an analyst may feel pressure to reframe the situation as injustice. This directedness is present conceptually, but no concrete affect, motive, demand, or action sequence is supplied.

The calibration issue is that ∇ must not be psychologized. The analysis does not infer resentment, desire, defensiveness, outrage, fragility, courage, or motive. It marks structural directional pressure only. Overclaim would occur if the abstract case were treated as evidence of someone’s inner state or if critique-pressure were mistaken for enacted protest.

**□ — Frame** is active and central. Epistemic injustice is unintelligible without a frame that determines credibility, relevance, interpretation, and participation. The frame decides what counts as knowledge, whose speech is heard as testimony, whose interpretation is available to shared understanding, and which absences matter. In this case, □ is the credibility-and-interpretation frame.

The calibration issue for □ is that frame selection must be explicit. A situation may be framed as disagreement, misunderstanding, lack of evidence, social exclusion, legal discrimination, hermeneutical failure, testimonial injustice, institutional procedure, or legitimate refusal. PMS cannot silently select the epistemic-injustice frame and then treat the result as self-evident. Overclaim would occur if every communication failure were automatically reclassified as epistemic injustice without testing whether the frame actually improves discrimination.

**Λ — Non-Event** is weak to active. Epistemic injustice often involves structured absences: not being heard, not being believed, not being interpreted, not receiving uptake, not having concepts available, not being recognized as a participant, not receiving a response where one is expected. These are PMS-readable as non-events only if there is a frame-based expectation that something should have occurred.

The calibration issue for Λ is especially important. Absence is not automatically injustice. Silence, refusal, non-response, evidentiary caution, conceptual mismatch, disagreement, or non-participation may be legitimate, ambiguous, resistant, or outside PMS capture. Λ requires a specified expected occurrence within a frame. Overclaim would occur if every silence or non-fit were treated as structured non-uptake, or if absence were automatically redescribed as drift, failed integration, or maturity failure.

**Α — Attractor** is weak. Epistemic injustice may involve recurrent credibility deficits, stabilized non-uptake, routine interpretive exclusion, habitual dismissal, or institutional scripts. But recurrence is not supplied by the abstract material. The case concept suggests the possibility of attractor formation; it does not establish it.

The calibration issue is evidence of pattern stabilization. One abstract concept does not prove a repeated script. A concrete case would need repeated events, records, observable routines, role scripts, institutional habits, or path dependence. Overclaim would occur if the analysis treated epistemic injustice as necessarily recurrent in this case without scene evidence.

**Ω — Asymmetry** is active as a conceptual operator and central to the case. Epistemic injustice concerns uneven standing in credibility, interpretation, exposure, participation, and authority. Some participants may depend on others for uptake. Some frames may privilege credentialed, dominant, institutional, or familiar forms of expression. Some actors may define what counts as relevant or intelligible. This creates directional imbalance.

The calibration issue for Ω is that asymmetry is structural, not moralistic by itself. PMS may mark that one role can grant or withhold credibility, while another bears the cost of exclusion. But it does not automatically assign guilt, bad faith, domination, oppression, or superiority. Overclaim would occur if Ω were treated as a ready-made moral verdict or if asymmetry alone were enough to establish injustice without further discrimination.

There is also a dependency issue: in PMS, Ω depends on Α. Since Α is weak, Ω is conceptually central but dependency-weak in a strict structural sense. This means that the analysis may identify asymmetry as the case’s thematic center, but it cannot fully establish a stabilized asymmetrical structure without recurrence or role-pattern evidence. This is not a failure; it is dependency hygiene.

**Θ — Temporality** is weak to active. Epistemic injustice can unfold over time through delayed recognition, accumulated credibility loss, repeated non-uptake, inherited interpretive gaps, institutional memory, public records, or long-term exclusion. Temporality would matter strongly in any concrete scene where the wrong is not momentary but cumulative or durable.

The calibration issue is that the abstract case does not provide sequence, duration, repetition, delay, or trajectory. Θ must therefore remain conditional. Overclaim would occur if the analysis treated epistemic injustice as historically or institutionally established in this case without temporal evidence. The correct formulation is: Θ is relevant where recurrence, accumulation, delay, or persistence is specified; here it remains weak.

**Φ — Recontextualization** is active. The case explicitly asks how morally difficult situations should be understood. It therefore invites a shift from one frame to another: from disagreement to epistemic wronging, from communication failure to credibility asymmetry, from lack of understanding to interpretive exclusion, from isolated non-response to structured non-uptake. Φ is the operator that makes this shift legible.

The calibration issue is that recontextualization must not become capture. A rival reading may be valid, sharper, or outside PMS. The fact that a situation can be recontextualized as epistemic injustice does not mean that it must be. Overclaim would occur if Φ absorbed all rival framings into PMS as incomplete understanding, resistance, immaturity, or calibration residue. The YAML analyses therefore insist that recontextualization is valid only if it improves discrimination and preserves non-capture.

**Χ — Distance** is active as an application constraint and central to the validity of the case analysis. Χ prevents the reading from becoming a tribunal. It requires reflective pause, reversibility, non-fusion with accusation, and resistance to closure-by-authority. In a later concrete scene, Χ might also appear as protected interruption, suspension of immediate credibility judgment, or a procedure for revisable response. Here, however, it is primarily the analysis’s own discipline.

The calibration issue is that Χ must not become avoidance or false neutrality. It does not mean refusing to name possible injustice. It means not collapsing structural legibility into verdict, sanction, or person-label. Overclaim would occur if the analysis suspended Χ in order to declare guilt, maturity failure, legal responsibility, or metaphysical truth. Conversely, underclaim would occur if Χ were used to neutralize all accountability in a later concrete scene. In this case, Χ holds the reading open.

**Σ — Integration** is weak. The case may require integration of testimony, credibility, interpretive resources, absence, disagreement, asymmetry, and rival frames. But no concrete integration occurs in the material. There is no repair process, no institutional response, no synthesis of accounts, no corrective procedure, and no shared understanding achieved. Therefore Σ remains weak.

The calibration issue is that coherence is not proof. PMS can generate a coherent structural reading, but that does not make the reading adequate, true, or exhaustive. Integration must not erase dissent, refusal, rupture, or non-capture. Overclaim would occur if the article treated the PMS rendering itself as integrated resolution. The YAMLs explicitly refuse that: Σ is possible as future integration, but not established.

**Ψ — Self-Binding** is weak as case structure and active only as analytic discipline. Ψ would become relevant if an agent, institution, community, or analytic procedure bound itself over time to norms of credible uptake, interpretive fairness, response, repair, or review. The case material does not supply such commitments. Therefore Ψ is not established as case-level follow-up binding.

The calibration issue is that self-binding must not be invented. PMS cannot assign stable normativity or responsibility-as-self-commitment without a temporal commitment structure. Overclaim would occur if the analysis treated the concept of epistemic injustice itself as a self-binding agent, or if it demanded that another party accept the PMS frame, confess error, or enter a repair path. In the completed YAMLs, Ψ remains primarily the self-binding of the analysis: the artefact binds itself to non-overclaim, non-coercion, reversibility, non-capture, and dignity-in-practice.

Taken together, the PMS Core reading is strong but deliberately bounded. Δ, □, Ω, Φ, and Χ carry the case most strongly. Λ, Θ, Α, Σ, and Ψ remain weaker or conditional because the case is abstract. This layered activation pattern is the core of the analysis. The case is structurally readable, but not concretely adjudicated.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The dependency discipline is crucial because epistemic injustice is exactly the kind of case in which later operators can be used too cheaply. The moral seriousness of the topic creates pressure to move quickly from difference to judgment, from frame to verdict, from asymmetry to blame, from non-uptake to injustice, from recontextualization to truth, from coherence to closure, and from conceptual critique to obligation. PMS blocks that shortcut.

The first dependency risk is Ω without sufficient Α. The case concept strongly implies asymmetry, but strict PMS dependency treats Ω as downstream of stabilized patterning. Since recurrence and attractor formation are not evidenced, Ω is conceptually central but not fully established as a concrete stabilized asymmetry. The analysis may say that epistemic injustice is a case about credibility and interpretive asymmetry. It may not say that any specific asymmetrical pattern occurred.

The second risk is Θ without sequence. Temporality becomes significant if the wrong repeats, accumulates, delays recognition, or stabilizes over time. The abstract case does not supply that. Therefore any strong claim about historical persistence, institutional memory, inherited interpretive gaps, or cumulative harm would exceed the evidence. Θ must remain weak until scene data provide sequence, duration, recurrence, or trajectory.

The third risk is Λ without a specified expectation. Non-events matter only inside frames. Not every absence is a non-event. Not every silence is exclusion. Not every refusal is injustice. Not every missing response is moral failure. Λ requires a defined expectation of uptake, recognition, reply, interpretive resource, credibility assignment, or correction. Without that expectation, the analysis must not treat absence as structured harm.

The fourth risk is Φ as over-assimilation. Recontextualization can make the case visible, but it can also become a capture-machine. If every rival reading is redescribed as incomplete PMS calibration, the analysis violates its own non-capture rule. PMS must allow that a rival theory may be more adequate, that a legal frame may be required, that a phenomenological account may preserve lived experience better, or that refusal may be dignified resistance rather than incomplete integration.

The fifth risk is Χ as pseudo-neutralization. Distance is necessary, but it can be misused. If Χ becomes a way to endlessly postpone naming asymmetry, it becomes a shield against accountability. If Χ is suspended, the analysis becomes a verdict machine. The discipline is therefore double: maintain distance without erasing possible wrong; name structure without turning it into judgment.

The sixth risk is Σ as smoothing. Integration is tempting because the YAML system can generate a coherent reading. But a smooth structural reading can be under-discriminating. It may make the case feel resolved while leaving open whether the situation is injustice, disagreement, evidence caution, conceptual mismatch, refusal, or non-capture. Therefore Σ remains weak and must not be treated as proof of adequacy.

The seventh risk is Ψ as coercive binding. Self-binding is especially dangerous if it becomes a demand that another party accept the frame or repair path. In the CRITIQUE overlay, this is explicitly blocked: Ψ must bind self or own follow-up, not become a Ψ→Other demand. In the PMS Core reading, Ψ cannot be activated unless a role, institution, or analytic procedure actually binds itself across time. The current case does not supply that.

This is why later operators cannot be used cheaply. PMS ordering is not decorative. It prevents the analysis from smuggling conclusion into structure. A fully mature chain would require Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, and Ψ to be meaningfully grounded. Here, the chain is partially grounded and partially conditional. That partiality is the responsible result.

Coherence is not proof. The YAML analyses explicitly identify high coherence risk: epistemic injustice fits PMS vocabulary very well. It involves frame, asymmetry, non-uptake, recontextualization, distance, dignity, and possible responsibility. That fit is analytically useful, but it can also become too smooth. A coherent PMS derivation may still fail to discriminate. It may cover too much. It may make disagreement, silence, refusal, and non-capture look like variants of the same structure. The analysis therefore marks coherence risk as high and mitigates it through weak dependencies, falsifier conditions, rival readings, and operationalization requirements.

Non-capture is not failure. It is a necessary boundary. PMS does not need to capture all of epistemic injustice in order to be useful. It may map structural relations while leaving lived humiliation, historical oppression, political struggle, hermeneutical loss, legal evidence, speech-act uptake, and resistant refusal to other grammars. The case is stronger because it says this plainly.

PMS must not become a closure machine. AXIOM exists to map closure-demands, not satisfy them by authority. In this case, closure would be invalid if it declared epistemic injustice proven, if it assigned blame, if it ranked persons, if it diagnosed listeners, if it activated D, if it scored maturity, if it treated critique-readability as validated critique, or if it treated AHP hardening as case truth. The correct output is a bounded structural article that preserves where closure is possible and where Λ-residue must remain visible.

Invalid dependency shortcuts would include: moving from Δ directly to Ψ by declaring that a perceived mismatch proves responsibility; moving from □ directly to moral verdict by treating the epistemic-injustice frame as self-validating; moving from Λ to drift by treating every absence as failed integration; moving from Ω to guilt by treating asymmetry as wrongdoing by itself; moving from Φ to proof by treating recontextualization as truth; moving from Χ to paralysis by refusing any structural claim; moving from Σ to adequacy by treating coherence as enough; and moving from Ψ to coercion by binding others into the analysis.

The completed YAMLs avoid these shortcuts. That is the main reason the case analysis is structurally strong.

### Derived structures

The derived structures are not independent primitives. They are formula-derived PMS constructs. They do not replace the operators and do not add new base axioms. In this case, each derived structure is either weak, conditional, application-constraining, or analytic-only. This is not a low score. It reflects the fact that the case is abstract and non-concrete.

**Awareness_A** is derived from Θ + □ + Δ. It represents sustained, framed differentiation across time. In this case, Awareness_A is weak to active at the level of conceptual analysis. The frame differentiates knowers, interpreters, credible participants, discounted participants, uptake, and non-uptake. That satisfies the □ and Δ components. But the Θ component remains weak because no concrete temporal sequence or persistence is supplied.

Awareness_A therefore appears as analyst-level structural awareness, not as phenomenological consciousness and not as a maturity score. The case analysis is aware of the relevant distinctions; it does not prove that any actor in a concrete scene possessed such awareness. Overclaim would occur if Awareness_A were treated as evidence that participants understood the injustice, or as evidence that a group lacked awareness.

**Coherence_C** is derived from Θ + Λ + □ + ∇. It represents temporally stabilized structuring of impulse and expectation within a frame. In this case, Coherence_C is weak. There is a frame of credibility and interpretation; there is directional pressure toward uptake and recognition; there are possible non-events such as non-uptake or absent interpretive resources; and temporality is conceptually relevant. But the case lacks enough scene detail to show a stable expectation structure unfolding over time.

This weakness is important. The PMS article itself may be coherent, but that is not the same as case coherence. The analysis must not confuse a coherent formal rendering with coherent praxis. Coherence_C remains weak because no actual sequence of expectation, absence, response, delay, or correction is supplied.

**Responsibility_R** is derived from Ψ + Φ + Θ + Ω. It represents self-binding orientation toward asymmetry extended across time and recontextualization. In this case, Responsibility_R is weak. Ω and Φ are relevant: the case concerns asymmetry and recontextualization. Θ is conditional, and Ψ is not established. No agent, institution, listener, or analyst-role within a concrete scene is shown binding itself to obligations across time.

Responsibility_R therefore cannot be used to assign blame, guilt, liability, accountability, forensic responsibility, or moral rank. The CRITIQUE overlay asks whether a framed mismatch can be interrupted without collapsing into reaction, judgment, public pillory, or coercive follow-up binding. The case is critique-readable because there is a conceptual mismatch between epistemic standing and credibility framing: persons may be knowers, interpreters, or credible participants, while a frame may discount, misread, silence, exclude, or fail to uptake that role. No correction pathway appears. No follow-up ownership exists. Therefore the case is not a completed critique. It is critique-readable, not critique-validated.

### Example 1 — The seminar contribution that does not yet count

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Scene and frame structure”

A graduate seminar is discussing a canonical text on testimony. One participant offers an interpretation that connects the text’s argument to a form of exclusion she has encountered in academic discussion: some statements are not rejected explicitly, but are never treated as contributions around which the conversation has to reorganize. The seminar leader responds politely, thanks her for the comment, and returns to the planned interpretive question. Other participants continue as if the comment were an illustrative aside rather than a claim about the structure of the discussion itself.

The vignette is intentionally bounded. It does not establish that epistemic injustice occurred. It does not prove that the seminar leader acted wrongly, that the participant was harmed, that the institution is exclusionary, or that the other participants lacked moral awareness. It only supplies a scene in which the question becomes structurally legible: a contribution is made, the frame of relevance does not shift, and the contribution may have been treated as personal illustration rather than interpretive participation.

What this example demonstrates is the difference between **non-uptake as possible structure** and **non-uptake as established wrong**. The relevant issue is not whether someone “felt ignored” or whether the seminar leader had bad intentions. The PMS question is narrower: within this frame, was the contribution expected to count as an interpretation of the shared object, and did the frame actually allow it to count that way? If the contribution was merely acknowledged but not integrated into the discussion’s relevance structure, then a possible Λ appears: an expected uptake event may not have occurred. But that remains conditional. The comment might also have been unclear, outside the session’s scope, insufficiently connected to the text, or simply one of many contributions that could not all redirect the discussion. The example does not decide among these readings.

The PMS Core hooks are visible. **Δ** marks the difference between “comment as personal aside” and “comment as interpretive contribution.” **∇** appears as pressure toward uptake: the participant’s contribution implicitly asks the seminar to reconsider what counts as relevant interpretation. **□** is the seminar frame: a setting with norms about textual relevance, authority, turn-taking, and legitimate interpretive moves. **Λ** is possible if the expected event—recognition of the comment as a frame-relevant contribution—does not occur. **Α** remains weak unless the same pattern repeats over multiple sessions or across participants. **Ω** is present as a possible asymmetry: the seminar leader has more authority to define relevance, while the participant has less control over whether her contribution reshapes the discussion. **Θ** would become stronger if the pattern accumulated over time. **Φ** appears if the scene is recontextualized from “a comment that did not fit” to “a possible case of epistemic non-uptake.” **Χ** is required so the analysis does not become an accusation. **Σ** is weak because no repair or revised discussion structure is shown. **Ψ** is not established because no one binds themselves to future uptake norms.

The CRITIQUE overlay helps by showing that the scene is at most **weakly critique-readable**. The mismatch between epistemic standing and the seminar’s relevance frame can be interrupted and examined: this is the CRITIQUE_MIN zone. But the example does not reach productive critique. No corrective step occurs. The seminar leader does not reopen the discussion, establish a review norm, or invite clarification in a way that changes the frame. It also does not reach stable critique, because there is no follow-up binding.

MIP remains non-scoring. No A-band or M-band can be assigned. The leader’s knowledge, alternatives, role obligations, time pressure, and actual interpretive options are not sufficiently specified. The IA-box would be only a question: were the frame rules transparent, justified, time-bounded, and reversible? AHP would flag the example as useful but sensitive: a reader might mistake critique-readability for proof that the seminar leader committed epistemic injustice. That is exactly what the example must prevent.

The forbidden claims are therefore strict. The example may not claim discrimination, bad faith, immaturity, guilt, institutional pathology, or legal relevance. It may not diagnose the participant as wounded or the leader as dismissive. It may not treat polite acknowledgment as proof of non-uptake, nor non-integration as proof of injustice.

The non-capture note is equally important. The participant’s lived experience, the affective texture of being heard or not heard, and the broader history of seminar norms may exceed PMS capture. A phenomenological or political reading may preserve aspects that the structural analysis only marks abstractly.

The article takeaway is that epistemic injustice becomes visible only when the frame of uptake is examined, but visibility is not verdict. A PMS reading can identify where the question should be asked without deciding the answer in advance.

### Example 2 — The review meeting and the missing category

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “PMS Core structural reading” or after “Add-on / overlay reading”

A research team is reviewing a classification scheme for field reports. The scheme contains categories for “confirmed incident,” “unconfirmed incident,” and “irrelevant report.” A field worker objects that several reports from local participants do not fit these categories. They describe recurring conditions that are not yet incidents, but that local participants recognize as early warning signs. The project lead replies that the scheme cannot include categories that lack formal verification. The reports are therefore placed under “unconfirmed” or “irrelevant,” and the review proceeds.

This vignette is bounded as a structural illustration. It does not claim that the project lead is wrong, that the field worker is right, or that the classification scheme is unjust. It does not establish that the local participants possess superior knowledge, nor that formal verification is oppressive. It shows a situation in which epistemic and interpretive standing may become structurally difficult: some participants appear to have a category of relevance that the official frame does not yet recognize.

The example demonstrates the relation between **hermeneutical non-capture** and **frame-bound non-uptake**. The key issue is not merely that one person disagrees with a classification scheme. The issue is that the available frame may lack a category for something that matters to some participants. If the missing category prevents certain forms of situated knowledge from becoming intelligible within the review process, then epistemic injustice becomes a possible reading. But it remains possible only. The project lead may have legitimate methodological constraints. The reports may be too ambiguous. The category may be too broad, too risky, or too difficult to validate. PMS must preserve these alternatives.

The PMS Core hooks are especially clear. **Δ** marks the difference between recognized report types and the proposed missing category. **∇** appears as pressure to make the reports usable: the field worker pushes toward recognition; the project lead pushes toward methodological closure. **□** is the classification frame, including evidence rules, verification standards, and decision purposes. **Λ** appears as a possible non-event: the expected category or interpretive resource does not arrive. It is not simply silence; it is a missing structural place within the frame. **Α** would become active if the scheme repeatedly absorbs local early-warning reports into “unconfirmed” or “irrelevant,” thereby stabilizing a pattern. **Ω** is visible because the project lead or institution controls the categories, while field participants bear the cost of being unreadable within them. **Θ** becomes relevant if the pattern accumulates across reporting cycles. **Φ** appears when the scene is recontextualized from “insufficient evidence” to “possible interpretive exclusion.” **Χ** prevents the analysis from declaring the scheme illegitimate too quickly. **Σ** would require integration of formal verification needs and local interpretive categories. **Ψ** would require a future binding: for example, the team commits to reviewing the scheme after a trial period.

The CRITIQUE overlay sharpens the threshold. The field worker’s objection creates a critique-readable mismatch: the frame’s categories do not accommodate a kind of situated report. CRITIQUE_MIN is plausible because Δ, □, and Χ can be maintained: there is a mismatch, a frame, and a reflective pause is possible. CRITIQUE_PROD is not yet reached unless the team creates a corrective step, such as a provisional category, a review protocol, or an explicit test for whether early-warning reports have predictive value. CRITIQUE_STABLE is not reached unless the team binds itself to follow-up: a review date, responsibility assignment, audit trace, or revision condition.

This example also shows why drift risks remain risks, not findings. Judgment drift would occur if “epistemic injustice” were used simply to label the project lead’s refusal as morally defective. Narrative repair drift would occur if the team produces a sympathetic explanation of local knowledge but still changes nothing. Commentary drift would occur if the issue is discussed extensively but no review mechanism is created. Silence-attractor risk would become relevant only if similar objections repeatedly disappear without acknowledgment.

MIP status remains guarded. No A-score or M-score should be assigned. The project lead’s constraints, knowledge, authority, alternatives, and obligations are not fully specified. The IA-box could become useful in a later concrete analysis: were the asymmetrical category rules transparent, justified, time-bounded, and reversible? At present, those remain questions. AHP would recommend hardening the example by requiring future-scene thresholds before calling the frame unjust or mature.

Forbidden claims include legal liability, methodological misconduct, epistemic superiority, bad faith, or proof that local participants were wronged. The example must not become an argument against verification standards as such. It must not imply that every unrecognized category is valid, or that formal categories are inherently oppressive.

The non-capture note is substantial. A hermeneutical reading may better describe the missing category. An empirical reading may be needed to test whether the early-warning reports are reliable. A political reading may be needed if the categories systematically privilege institutional knowledge over local knowledge. PMS can map the structural tension, but it cannot decide all of these questions.

The article takeaway is that epistemic injustice can appear where a frame lacks room for a form of knowing, but PMS must distinguish missing category, methodological caution, and actual exclusion. The example shows why Σ and Ψ cannot be claimed until the frame produces correction and follow-up binding.

### Example 3 — The public memo that should not become a verdict

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions” or before “Misuse boundary”

An organization drafts an internal memo after receiving complaints that some contributors’ expertise is not being taken seriously in cross-functional meetings. The memo states that “epistemic injustice may be structurally relevant where credible participation is repeatedly discounted.” It recommends reviewing meeting procedures, clarifying what counts as expertise in different contexts, and creating a reversible process for participants to request that a contribution be reconsidered. Before the memo is finalized, someone suggests naming the teams where the problem is “most severe” and ranking managers by “epistemic maturity.”

This example is designed as a guardrail case. It shows how a structurally useful concept can become dangerous when moved toward governance, publicness, or ranking. The memo’s initial formulation is cautious: it says epistemic injustice may be structurally relevant. It proposes review procedures rather than verdicts. It keeps the focus on frames, criteria, and reversibility. The suggested ranking, by contrast, would turn a conceptual and procedural analysis into person evaluation. That is precisely the move the completed YAML analyses prohibit.

The example demonstrates the difference between **structural use** and **misuse by escalation**. The organization may legitimately ask whether meeting frames distribute credibility unevenly. It may ask whether expertise criteria are explicit. It may create procedures for reconsideration. But it may not use PMS, CRITIQUE, MIP, or AHP to rank managers, diagnose teams, assign moral maturity, or declare epistemic guilt without evidence and due process. Even with evidence, PMS would still not be a person-ranking tool.

The PMS Core hooks are distributed across the memo’s possible uses. **Δ** appears in distinctions between recognized and discounted expertise. **∇** appears as pressure to respond to complaints. **□** is the meeting and organizational decision frame: what counts as expertise, relevance, contribution, and uptake. **Λ** may appear where expected reconsideration or acknowledgment does not occur. **Α** would require repeated patterns across meetings, not isolated dissatisfaction. **Ω** is central because managers, chairs, or dominant roles may control uptake, agenda, and recognition. **Θ** matters if patterns persist across review cycles or become part of organizational culture. **Φ** appears when complaints are recontextualized as possible epistemic wronging rather than interpersonal friction. **Χ** is essential: the memo must preserve distance and avoid verdict-fusion. **Σ** would appear only if procedures integrate complaints, role duties, meeting design, and review mechanisms into a coherent process. **Ψ** would appear only if the organization binds itself to a non-coercive follow-up process, such as periodic review, documented criteria, or accountable revision.

The CRITIQUE overlay makes the escalation visible. The memo, if written carefully, may reach weak CRITIQUE_MIN: it identifies a framed mismatch and maintains distance. It may even move toward CRITIQUE_PROD if it creates a concrete corrective step, such as a meeting review protocol. It approaches CRITIQUE_STABLE only if the organization binds itself to follow-up in a transparent, non-punitive way. But the proposed ranking of managers would be critique drift. It risks judgment masquerading as critique, moralization drift, and possibly public pillory if circulated. It would convert critique-readability into verdict-readiness.

MIP is directly relevant here, but still bounded. This is the kind of context where a downstream guardrail reading matters because the artefact may influence institutional practice. Yet the MIP response should not be to assign A-bands or M-bands to managers. It should ask whether the memo’s use is transparent, justified, time-bounded, reversible, and dignity-protective. It should check publicness, role clarity, power-to-act, and transmission status. The D-module should remain off unless a very specific, safe, role-bound and time-windowed dignity analysis is warranted. Even then, D0 remains untouched and no dignity score is assigned.

AHP is also relevant. It would flag several attack surfaces: the memo may look more evidentially complete than it is; “epistemic injustice” may be misread as validated accusation; critique-readability may be mistaken for proof; ranking language may create person-level harm; and transmission beyond the intended audience may turn a working note into a public verdict. AHP would recommend hardening the memo’s scope, adding explicit non-ranking language, defining review thresholds, and repeating that the document is not a decision basis unless further evidence and process are supplied.

The forbidden claims are extensive. The memo must not claim that named managers are epistemically unjust, immature, deficient, oppressive, or morally ranked. It must not treat complaints as proof without review. It must not treat structural explanation as exoneration either. It must not use “maturity” as a hidden score. It must not turn Dignity-in-Practice into a dignity rating. It must not treat AHP attack points as defects in people.

The non-capture note is important because an organizational memo may not capture the lived experience of contributors who were discounted. A procedural review may be necessary but insufficient. Conversely, some complaints may reflect disagreement, role confusion, unclear expertise boundaries, or evidence standards rather than injustice. PMS and MIP can structure the review, but they cannot replace affected perspectives, institutional context, or fair process.

The article takeaway is that the stack is most valuable when it prevents premature escalation. PMS makes the structural issue legible, CRITIQUE distinguishes critique-readability from completed critique, MIP prevents scoring and governance overreach, and AHP hardens the artefact against misuse. The example prepares the later conclusion by showing that the point of the analysis is not to name culprits, but to keep closure, correction, and dignity-protection properly bounded.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The YAML analyses preserve multiple rival readings. These readings are not defects. They mark the fact that epistemic injustice is not exhausted by PMS or CRITIQUE. They also protect the case from becoming an internal-language exercise that treats all resistance as incomplete uptake.

An **epistemological reading** may treat the case primarily as a problem in knowledge, testimony, credibility, justification, and epistemic agency. This reading preserves the question of what it means to wrong someone specifically as a knower. PMS can map credibility asymmetry and participation frames, but it does not replace epistemology’s detailed accounts of testimony, warrant, evidence, rational belief, or epistemic norms.

A **moral-philosophical reading** may treat epistemic injustice as a wrong done to persons in their epistemic capacity. This preserves the ethical force of the concept: the wrong is not merely a failed information transfer but a harm or disrespect tied to standing. PMS can represent Dignity-in-Practice as a constraint under asymmetry, but it does not prove a moral theory of wronging or dignity.

A **political-structural reading** may focus on social power, oppression, institutional recognition, marginalization, or structural inequality. This preserves historical and social patterns that a purely operator-based analysis may under-specify. PMS can represent Ω, Α, Θ, and □, but it does not by itself supply empirical sociology, political history, or theory of oppression.

A **hermeneutical reading** may focus on shared interpretive resources: whether people have available concepts to make their experience intelligible to themselves or others. This preserves the language of interpretive gaps, collective meaning-making, and the social distribution of intelligibility. PMS can map Λ as missing interpretive resources and Φ as recontextualization, but it does not replace hermeneutical theory.

A **speech-act or language-practice reading** may focus on uptake, illocution, perlocution, discursive authority, or the conditions under which speech acts succeed or fail. This preserves fine-grained language-practice dynamics that PMS only abstracts structurally. PMS can treat non-uptake as Λ, but it does not fully analyze speech-act mechanics.

A **phenomenological reading** may preserve the lived experience of being disbelieved, silenced, humiliated, misread, or rendered unintelligible. PMS explicitly does not claim phenomenological completeness. It can mark structural non-uptake and dignity risk, but it does not describe the inner experience of epistemic harm.

A **legal-forensic reading** may become relevant if a concrete scene involves discrimination, evidence, testimony, procedure, admissibility, liability, or rights. The current case does not support such a reading, but it remains a possible rival formalization for future scenes. PMS cannot substitute for jurisdiction-specific law or forensic evidence.

A **sociological or empirical reading** may ask how credibility distributions operate in real institutions, professions, publics, or datasets. This preserves the need for evidence and method. PMS may provide a schema for what to observe, but it does not generate empirical findings.

An **anti-capture or resistance-centered reading** may argue that some refusals of uptake, non-participation, silence, or resistance should not be integrated into the dominant frame. This is especially important. PMS and CRITIQUE might be tempted to interpret non-fit as under-integration or non-event. The YAMLs explicitly refuse that automatic translation. Resistance may be dignity-protective. Silence may be refusal. Non-capture may be the right result.

Each rival reading preserves something the PMS stack may not fully capture. PMS preserves structural grammar. CRITIQUE preserves critique-threshold discipline. MIP preserves use-boundary discipline. AHP preserves artefact-hardening discipline. None of these replaces the other grammars. The case remains open to comparison, supplementation, or rejection.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would be weakened if no credibility, interpretive, or participation asymmetry can be specified. If a concrete scene shows equal standing, transparent criteria, genuine mutual uptake, and no asymmetrical frame control, Ω would no longer carry the case strongly. The reading would also be weakened if the alleged wrong is better explained as ordinary disagreement, evidentiary insufficiency, conceptual mismatch, misunderstanding, or legitimate non-uptake.

The PMS reading would be weakened if no frame □ governs credibility or interpretation. If the case lacks any identifiable context in which participation, testimony, or interpretation are structured, then the analysis loses its central frame. It would also be weakened if the frame selected by the analyst is not the frame operating in the scene, or if a rival frame explains the case with better discrimination.

The PMS reading would be weakened if Λ cannot be specified. A concrete case would need to show what expected uptake, response, recognition, interpretive resource, or credibility assignment did not occur. If no such expected occurrence exists, non-event language should be removed. Absence without expectation is not enough.

The PMS reading would be weakened if recurrence or temporal trajectory is claimed without evidence. Α and Θ require pattern, repetition, stabilization, delay, accumulation, or persistence. If a later analysis claims institutional or historical structure without showing such evidence, it overextends the case.

The PMS reading would become under-discriminating if it cannot distinguish epistemic injustice from disagreement, caution, refusal, silence, lack of evidence, communication breakdown, or non-capture. This is one of the strongest falsifier conditions. A structural reading that covers everything explains too little.

The PMS reading would become over-assimilative if every resistance, rupture, absence, disagreement, or rival framing were translated into PMS drift, incomplete integration, incomplete calibration, recontextualization residue, or maturity failure. Such a move would violate the non-capture rule.

The CRITIQUE overlay would be weakened if the case lacks a framed mismatch that can be interrupted under Χ. CRITIQUE_MIN requires Δ, □, and Χ. If no mismatch exists, or if the frame is not specified, or if distance is not maintained, even minimal critique-readability fails. It would also be weakened if the case is better understood as reaction, judgment, silence, or commentary rather than critique.

CRITIQUE_PROD would require Σ. The overlay would overclaim if it treated recontextualization as productive critique without a corrective step. To upgrade the case, a future scene would need concrete correction: a response procedure, repair act, reframed rule, reviewed evidence path, revised participation channel, or other integrated change. Without that, productive critique remains unestablished.

CRITIQUE_STABLE would require Ψ. A future scene would need non-coercive follow-up binding: assigned review, audit loop, repeated correction, role-owned responsibility, or self-bound commitment across time. Without that, stable critique is not reached. Any attempt to force another party into accepting the critique would be coercive Ψ→Other misuse.

The MIP reading would be weakened if it assigned bands or responsibility without scene evidence. No A-band can be assigned because there is no enactment. No M-band can be assigned because knowledge, control, predictability, alternatives, and integration attempts are unknown. The IA-box cannot be adjudicated because transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility are under-specified. The D-module cannot be activated because no safe, concrete, role-bound, time-windowed communication space exists.

Operationalization would require substantial additional material. A future applied case would need concrete roles, specific scene boundary, actual frame, expected uptake, evidence of non-uptake, timeline, publicness level, asymmetry distribution, available alternatives, review path, correction channel, affected-party perspective, counterparty perspective, and external accountability. If legal, clinical, computational, institutional, or public use were contemplated, additional domain-specific validation would be required.

AHP identifies further hardening requirements for the analysis artefact itself. A next iteration should state even more explicitly that full field completion is not evidential completeness. It should define critique-readability as structural analyzability only, not validated accusation. It should add if/then IA thresholds. It should add a compact C-section counter-reading test. It should reinforce D-language boundaries. It should mirror “conceptual use only; not a decision basis” in any exported summary. It should make role, context, publicness, evidence, time window, and response channels hard prerequisites before any scoring, D-module activation, IA adjudication, or transmission upgrade.

The final operationalization status remains unfulfilled. The case is structurally analyzable, weakly critique-readable, MIP-guarded, and AHP-hardened as an artefact. It is not yet empirical, applied, legal, clinical, computational, or governance-ready.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used as a diagnostic tool. It cannot identify psychological traits, deficits, motives, maturity levels, pathologies, resentment, bad faith, fragility, arrogance, or cognitive competence. It does not diagnose those who are wronged, those who listen, those who fail to listen, institutions, groups, or publics.

It must not be used for person ranking. No participant is more or less dignified, mature, credible, enlightened, responsible, or morally worthy because of this analysis. PMS operators do not rank persons. MIP bands are not assigned. Dignity-in-Practice is not a dignity score. AHP attack points are not vulnerabilities in people.

It must not be used as a legal or forensic conclusion. The analysis does not establish discrimination, liability, culpability, evidentiary breach, procedural failure, rights violation, or damages. It does not decide what a court, tribunal, HR body, ethics board, or regulator should conclude.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. It does not prove personhood, dignity, consciousness, epistemic agency, moral worth, truth, injustice, or ontology. It treats dignity-in-practice as praxeological constraint, not ontological ranking.

It must not be used as empirical proof. No actual distribution of credibility, uptake, exclusion, institutional practice, or social pattern is established. The case lacks data, observations, interviews, records, and method.

It must not be used as implementation validation. The YAML format is not a system. It is not a policy engine, moderation tool, institutional workflow, compliance checklist, clinical protocol, or AI safety implementation. Any operational use would require calibration and accountability.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. A successful PMS rendering of one case does not prove PMS completeness, optimality, superiority, or finality. Rival readings remain open.

It must not treat add-ons as operators. CRITIQUE reduced signatures are overlay shorthand only. Publicness P is a modulator, not a PMS operator. MIP and AHP do not alter PMS. Drift catalogues do not become base grammar.

It must not treat drift as verdict. Drift risks are conditional structural warnings, not findings about persons, groups, institutions, or theories. Judgment drift, narrative repair drift, commentary drift, silence attractor risk, AD_A>>E, and AD_Sigma_low are not accusations.

It must not produce closure by authority. The article may map where closure is demanded and where it fails. It may not close the case because PMS, CRITIQUE, MIP, or AHP says so. The correct boundary is structural: epistemic injustice is conceptually legible under PMS and weakly critique-readable under CRITIQUE, but the case remains non-concrete, non-scored, non-adjudicated, and open to rival formalizations.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is that **Epistemic Injustice** is structurally legible but not adjudicated. PMS Core renders the case as a frame-bound configuration of epistemic difference, credibility-and-interpretation framing, possible non-uptake, possible patterning, asymmetry, conditional temporality, recontextualization, and required reflective distance. The strongest operators are Δ, □, Ω, Φ, and Χ. The weaker or conditional operators—Λ, Α, Θ, Σ, and Ψ—are not weaknesses in the model; they mark the absence of concrete scene evidence. The case can therefore be mapped, but it cannot be closed.

The CRITIQUE overlay sharpens this result. It shows that the case reaches only weak minimal critique-readability: **CRITIQUE_MIN_weak: Δ + □ + Χ**, with Φ active as conceptual recontextualization, Σ weak, Ψ not established, and publicness not applicable. This is a meaningful result because it prevents two opposite errors. It prevents under-reading the case as merely abstract moral language, and it prevents over-reading it as validated critique, completed correction, or stable follow-up binding. The case is critique-readable; it is not critique-completed.

MIP and AHP then discipline the use of the artefact. MIP confirms that no A-band, M-band, IA verdict, D-profile, or D-module activation is warranted. The case is a conceptual working note, not a decision basis. AHP confirms that the analysis artefact is precise and well-guarded, but still susceptible to predictable attack surfaces: formal completeness may look like evidential completeness; critique-readability may be misread as accusation; coherence may substitute for discrimination; dignity language may be quoted without its guardrails; and transmission beyond conceptual use may create false authority. These are not new findings about the case. They are hardening requirements for the analysis.

The three examples contribute by showing how the layered analysis behaves when moved toward more concrete material while remaining below adjudication. The seminar example demonstrates that possible non-uptake becomes visible only inside a frame of expected contribution, but visibility is not verdict. The classification-meeting example shows how a missing category can make hermeneutical non-capture and frame-bound non-uptake structurally legible, while still preserving methodological caution and rival readings. The organizational memo example shows why the stack’s guardrails matter most when analysis might become governance: the move from structural review to person-ranking is precisely the escalation that PMS, CRITIQUE, MIP, and AHP must block. Together, the examples test the article’s boundaries: each makes the structure more readable, but none supplies empirical proof, legal relevance, diagnosis, person ranking, or implementation validation. They demonstrate the analysis; they do not replace it, and they do not serve as evidence that epistemic injustice occurred.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is claimed. The article does not prove empirical injustice, does not diagnose anyone, does not rank persons, does not establish legal or forensic responsibility, does not activate a clinical or institutional protocol, does not validate PMS Base, and does not claim final capture of epistemic injustice. It does not say that every disagreement is epistemic injustice, that every silence is non-uptake, that every evidentiary caution is exclusion, or that every rival frame is drift. Resistance, refusal, non-fit, rupture, absence, and disagreement remain open to other readings.

The boundary discipline is therefore the main methodological achievement. PMS provides structural readability without metaphysical closure. CRITIQUE provides critique-threshold discipline without accusation. MIP provides governance restraint without scoring. AHP provides artefact hardening without rescoring. Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological: it constrains how asymmetry is handled, but it does not become an ontological rank or a dignity score. Drift remains risk, not finding. Coherence remains useful, not sufficient. Non-capture remains possible, not embarrassing.

The final methodological takeaway is that AXIOM can make a morally forceful concept structurally inspectable without turning it into a verdict machine. In this case, the stack shows where epistemic injustice becomes formally visible, where critique can begin, where correction would need to be specified, where follow-up binding is absent, and where operationalization must stop. The analysis does not solve epistemic injustice by authority. It makes the closure-demand explicit, preserves the Λ-residue, and keeps open the rival readings that a responsible structural cartography must not erase.
