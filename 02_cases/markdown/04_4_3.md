### Why this case matters

Moral luck matters because it exposes a powerful closure demand at the boundary between action, outcome, control, contingency, and responsibility. The classical pressure is familiar: two agents may act similarly, yet one causes harm and another narrowly avoids it; one is judged more severely because the outcome materialized, while another escapes comparable judgment because the harmful result did not occur. The closure demand asks whether responsibility should follow action, outcome, control, harm, risk, intention, character, institutional frame, or later evaluative need.

In AXIOM terms, the case belongs in the PMS-core layer because it is a paradigmatic closure problem. It does not first require a domain add-on. The structural difficulty is already visible through PMS core: difference, frame, non-event, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and possible self-binding are all directly implicated. The problem is not that PMS lacks access to the case. The problem is that PMS must not make the case too easy.

Moral luck is difficult because it tempts closure at several levels at once. It invites metaphysical closure: whether agents truly deserve praise or blame under contingency. It invites legal closure: whether harmful outcome should alter liability or sanction. It invites psychological closure: whether the unlucky agent is worse, guilty, negligent, careless, or morally defective. It invites institutional closure: whether systems should encode outcomes as proof of responsibility. It invites narrative closure: whether harm retroactively reveals the “real” character of an action.

The PMS reading refuses these collapses. It treats moral luck as a structural problem of attribution under outcome variance, not as a doctrine of moral desert. PMS core can map how outcome variance becomes legible, how attribution pressure forms, how frames stabilize relevance, how counterfactual non-events remain unavailable, how asymmetry shapes exposure, how time hardens reputations or precedents, how later frames re-read earlier conduct, and how distance prevents the analysis from becoming a verdict. But this mapping does not settle ultimate responsibility. It shows where closure is demanded, where closure is structurally available, and where Λ-residue must remain visible.

The case is also important because it tests whether PMS can preserve tragedy. A weak structural model would either dissolve moral luck into a clean attribution rule or declare it insoluble and stop. PMS does neither. It allows structured legibility while preserving possible non-capture. It can say: here are the operator pressures; here are the dependency risks; here are the conditions under which responsibility could be structurally modeled; here is why the absent counterfactual cannot be simply filled; here is why the unlucky outcome must not become a proxy for person-worth. PMS therefore performs well precisely by refusing to solve the case by authority.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading proceeds through all eleven operators in order. The point is not to force all operators to equal strength, but to show where each appears, where it remains weak, and where overextension would violate PMS discipline.

**Δ — Difference** is active and foundational. Moral luck becomes visible only once differences are drawn: action versus outcome, control versus contingency, intention versus consequence, harm versus near-miss, evaluation versus legal conclusion, responsibility versus blame. Δ makes the case legible by separating what might otherwise be fused. The major calibration demand is to preserve these distinctions without turning them into settled conclusions. If the analysis lets harm overwrite control, or lets outcome replace action, Δ collapses. Overclaim would occur if the mere presence of difference were treated as proof that PMS captures the case exhaustively.

**∇ — Impulse** is weak but relevant. The case generates pressure toward assessment: if harm occurred, someone wants to know whether the agent is more responsible; if harm did not occur, someone may still ask whether the lucky escape matters. This is directional tension, not psychological desire. The analysis must not infer guilt, shame, blame-seeking, vindictiveness, remorse, or motive. ∇ marks the closure drive: the urge to align outcome and evaluation. It remains weak because no concrete actor or institution is shown exerting that pressure.

**□ — Frame** is active. Moral luck depends on the frame in which responsibility is being asked. A legal frame, a relational frame, a professional-duty frame, a moral-philosophical frame, and a narrative frame will not treat outcome variance in the same way. In this case, the frame is conceptual moral evaluation under contingency. Calibration requires frame declaration: are we asking about blame, liability, regret, standing, repair, desert, or structural responsibility? Overclaim arises when the moral-philosophical frame is silently replaced by law, psychology, metaphysics, or implementation.

**Λ — Non-Event** is one of the strongest operators in the case. Moral luck is saturated with structured absence. The decisive counterfactual does not arrive. The full comparison between lucky and unlucky trajectories cannot be observed in a clean form. Harm may fail to occur despite risky action; harm may occur through contingency; an avoided harm may still haunt evaluation; an expected alignment between control and responsibility may fail. Λ must therefore be carried as absence, not patched. Its calibration point is crucial: the absent counterfactual must not become hidden guilt, hidden innocence, or hidden failure. Nor may it be redescribed automatically as incomplete integration.

**Α — Attractor** is weak. The case title may evoke recurrent patterns in moral discourse: repeated comparisons between equally risky agents with unequal outcomes, habitual outcome-weighting, or institutional tendencies to treat harm as proof of fault. **Ω — Asymmetry** is active but under-specified. Moral luck involves several possible asymmetries: agent control versus outcome exposure, actor versus affected party, evaluator versus evaluated, prospective actor versus retrospective judge, institution versus individual, harm-bearer versus non-exposed observer. Ω matters because outcome variance is not evenly distributed. Harm is borne by someone; attribution is often imposed by someone else; reputational cost may fall on the evaluated agent; closure pressure may come from affected parties or institutions. The main calibration rule is to separate asymmetry of outcome from asymmetry of agency. Unequal consequences do not automatically prove unequal responsibility. **Φ — Recontextualization** is active. Moral luck often operates through retrospective re-reading. A prior action is placed into a later outcome-frame: what looked merely risky becomes harmful; what might have been condemned escapes attention because no harm occurred; what was initially framed as ordinary conduct becomes negligence or tragedy after consequences emerge. ### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The core dependency problem is that later PMS operators are structurally expensive. They cannot be used cheaply merely because the theme of moral luck semantically evokes responsibility. In particular, Σ and Ψ must remain conditional.

The invalid shortcut would be **Δ/∇ → Ψ**: a salient difference plus closure pressure becomes immediate binding. In moral luck this would look like: harm happened, therefore responsibility hardens; harm did not happen, therefore responsibility disappears; outcome diverged, therefore moral standing changes. Such shortcuts suppress □, erase Λ, ignore Ω, flatten Θ, bypass Φ, suspend Χ, and treat Ψ as available without integration.

Another invalid shortcut would be **outcome → essence**. This occurs when the unlucky outcome is treated as revealing the agent’s true worth, character, or moral type. PMS blocks this by maintaining Δ distinctions and Dignity-in-Practice. Outcome exposure may change the structure of attribution; it does not authorize person-ranking.

A third shortcut is **coherence → proof**. Because PMS can produce a clean operator chain, the analysis may appear stronger than it is. The YAML explicitly marks high coherence risk: the PMS reading may become too smooth unless weak operators, falsifiers, rival readings, and non-capture zones remain active. Coherence is a condition of disciplined analysis, not evidence of final capture.

Non-capture is not failure. A moral-luck case may preserve tensions that PMS should not erase: ultimate desert, phenomenological guilt or regret, legal causation, theological fate, brute contingency, tragic remainder, or anti-formalist resistance. Preserving these zones is not a weakness of the case artefact. It is a sign that the artefact has not become a closure machine.

PMS must therefore remain cartographic. It maps the demands: where action and outcome diverge, where frames seek closure, where Λ remains, where asymmetry distributes exposure, where temporality enables retrospective re-reading, where integration is tempting, and where self-binding would require more evidence. It does not decide the metaphysics of responsibility.

### Example 1 — The near-miss and the realized harm

**Type:** compact
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Why this case matters,” as the first intuitive illustration of the closure demand.

Two drivers each glance down at a message for a few seconds while passing through a quiet residential street. In one case, no one is present; the car drifts slightly, the driver corrects course, and the incident leaves no trace beyond a moment of private alarm. In the other case, a child steps from behind a parked van during the same interval. The driver strikes the child. The actions appear structurally similar at the level of distraction, risk, and loss of attention, yet the outcomes diverge radically.

The example demonstrates the basic moral-luck pressure: evaluation does not remain indifferent to outcome, even where the control structure appears similar. The realized harm generates stronger closure pressure than the near-miss. That does not mean the harmed case automatically proves deeper guilt, nor that the near-miss case proves innocence. It shows how outcome variance produces a demand for attribution.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ** marks the difference between near-miss and harm, action and outcome, control and contingency. **∇** appears as the pressure to judge, explain, blame, excuse, or distinguish. **□** is the attribution frame: moral, legal, relational, institutional, or safety-oriented. **Λ** is central because the decisive counterfactual remains absent. We cannot replay the first case with the child present or the second case with the street empty. **Ω** appears in the different exposure of driver, child, family, observers, and institutions. **Θ** enters because the later outcome reorders how the earlier action is interpreted. **Φ** appears when the same glance at a phone is recontextualized after harm. **Χ** is required to prevent immediate collapse into person-ranking. **Σ** remains cautious: the case invites integration, but no integration should erase the counterfactual residue. **Ψ** remains conditional unless a concrete responsibility frame supplies role-duty, self-binding, or formal obligation.

No add-on is active here. PMS Core is sufficient to show the closure demand. MIP, AHP, QC, and QC-EXT are not invoked because the vignette is illustrative, not a governance artefact, audit protocol, or claim-hardening exercise.

Forbidden claims are clear. The example does not prove legal liability, diagnose either driver, rank their moral worth, establish metaphysical desert, or validate PMS. It also does not claim that outcomes should never matter. The non-capture note is equally important: the grief of the affected family, the driver’s remorse, and the lived tragedy of the harm are not exhausted by PMS mapping.

The article takeaway is that moral luck begins where outcome variance places pressure on responsibility closure while Λ counterfactual residue remains structurally unavailable.

### Example 2 — The institutional record and the lucky absence of damage

**Type:** compact
**Recommended insertion point:** inside the “Scene and frame structure” or “PMS Core structural reading” section, especially under Ω, Θ, and Φ.

Two technicians skip the same required inspection step in two different facilities. In the first facility, the machine continues operating normally. The omission is discovered during a routine review, noted as a procedural lapse, and corrected through retraining. In the second facility, the same omitted inspection precedes a mechanical failure that damages equipment and interrupts operations. The second omission enters an incident report, triggers a formal review, and becomes part of a durable institutional record.

This example demonstrates how moral luck becomes amplified by institutional temporality. The issue is not only that one outcome is worse. It is that the harmful outcome changes the frame in which the omission is read. What was a correctable lapse in one case becomes an incident-bearing event in the other. The second technician may face a longer record trail, more severe reputational exposure, and stronger pressure toward responsibility assignment, even if the omitted step was structurally similar.

The PMS Core hooks are again ordered. **Δ** distinguishes omission, outcome, record, and consequence. **∇** appears as pressure to close the incident: identify what failed and who owns the failure. **□** determines whether the relevant frame is safety review, disciplinary procedure, maintenance governance, or operational learning. **Λ** appears in the missing comparison: what would have happened in the first facility under different background conditions, and whether the second failure would have occurred despite inspection. **Α** may become weakly relevant if the institution repeatedly treats damaging outcomes as stronger evidence than non-damaging omissions. **Ω** is significant because record-setting authority and cost-bearing exposure are unevenly distributed. **Θ** is central because the record persists; the unlucky case may harden over time. **Φ** appears when the omission is re-read through the later failure. **Χ** is required so the incident does not become a proxy for the technician’s personhood. **Σ** may be needed to integrate omission, system design, training, maintenance load, supervision, and machine condition. **Ψ** becomes legitimate only if the frame establishes role-duty, accepted responsibility, policy binding, or procedural ownership.

No add-on is required. The example does not activate LOGIC, MIP, AHP, QC, or QC-EXT. If later converted into an institutional audit case, MIP or QC could become relevant, but this article example does not perform that escalation.

Forbidden claims include treating the second technician as morally worse, treating the first as innocent by nature, inferring intent, assigning legal liability, or declaring a final root cause. The example must also not be used to excuse the omission simply because one case was lucky.

The non-capture note is that institutional practice may require action before all uncertainty is resolved. PMS can map the closure pressure and residue, but it does not determine the correct policy response.

The article takeaway is that moral luck often becomes durable through Θ and Ω: outcomes do not merely happen; they enter frames, records, and asymmetrical attribution systems.

### Example 3 — The apology that arrives before the outcome is known

**Type:** compact
**Recommended insertion point:** after the discussion of Responsibility_R and Ψ, to clarify why self-binding remains conditional.

A project lead approves a risky deployment after a review that appears incomplete but not reckless within the team’s ordinary practice. Before any harm occurs, the lead notices the gap, sends a message to the team acknowledging the incomplete review, pauses the deployment, and proposes a revised approval procedure. In one possible continuation, the pause prevents a failure that would otherwise have occurred. In another, the same acknowledgment comes too late: the deployment has already caused downstream disruption before the pause takes effect.

The example demonstrates why responsibility in moral luck cannot be read from outcome alone. The lead’s acknowledgment and procedural correction introduce possible **Ψ** material: not guilt as inner state, not legal admission, but a form of self-binding to a responsibility structure. Yet the meaning of that self-binding changes depending on temporal placement, frame, and outcome. If the acknowledgment precedes harm, it may be read as preventive responsibility. If harm has already occurred, it may be read as repair, admission, mitigation, or insufficient action. The same gesture becomes differently legible under different temporal and outcome conditions.

The PMS Core hooks are especially useful here. **Δ** distinguishes risk, harm, acknowledgment, prevention, repair, and responsibility. **∇** appears as pressure to stabilize what the acknowledgment means. **□** determines whether the relevant frame is internal learning, public accountability, professional duty, legal exposure, or relational repair. **Λ** remains because the counterfactual is incomplete: would harm have occurred without the pause, would the revised procedure have prevented it, and what was knowable at the time? **Ω** appears between lead, team, downstream users, and any authority that can interpret the message. **Θ** is decisive because timing changes the structure: before harm, after harm, before discovery, after discovery. **Φ** appears when the same message is recontextualized as prevention, confession, repair, or liability signal. **Χ** prevents immediate conversion of the acknowledgment into either absolution or condemnation. **Σ** is needed to integrate action, timing, knowledge, role, procedure, and consequence. **Ψ** is possible but conditional: it requires a concrete commitment structure, not merely the topic of responsibility.

No add-on is active. MIP, AHP, QC, and QC-EXT are not used. A governance version of this example might later require MIP if the acknowledgement became part of an institutional accountability artefact, but the present example remains PMS Core.

Forbidden claims include treating the apology as proof of guilt, treating it as proof of innocence, inferring psychological sincerity, determining legal admissibility, or ranking the lead’s moral worth. The example also must not imply that self-binding erases harm.

The non-capture note is that remorse, trust, repair, and affected-party experience may exceed PMS structural mapping. PMS can show how the acknowledgment functions within responsibility structure, but not exhaust its lived or ethical meaning.

The article takeaway is that Ψ can matter in moral luck, but only when self-binding is concretely supplied. Responsibility_R is not activated merely because an outcome is bad; it depends on asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and actual commitment structure.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The case preserves several rival readings, and these are not defects.

A **control-condition reading** holds that responsibility tracks only what was under the agent’s control. This reading preserves the intuition that luck should not alter responsibility. PMS can map the frame, asymmetry, and non-event pressures around this position, but it does not refute it.

An **outcome-sensitive reading** holds that consequences legitimately alter moral evaluation. This preserves the intuition that actual harm matters, not merely risk structure. PMS can show how outcome frames recontextualize action, but it does not decide that outcome-sensitive judgment is correct.

A **virtue or character reading** treats moral luck as revealing dispositions rather than only action/outcome structure. PMS resists trait inference unless a concrete scene warrants it, but the rival reading may preserve a normative vocabulary that PMS intentionally does not adopt.

A **legal-causal reading** treats the operative issue as causation, liability, negligence, evidence, or sanction. PMS does not replace legal doctrine. It can map structural conditions under which legal frames create closure, but legal validity would require independent legal criteria.

A **phenomenological reading** may focus on guilt, regret, shame, remorse, or lived moral remainder. PMS does not describe inner experience. It can preserve this as non-capture, not as a missing PMS field.

A **tragic-praxis reading** holds that the tension is not a problem to be resolved but an irreducible feature of moral life. This is highly compatible with the PMS boundary discipline: structural maturity and responsibility do not eliminate tragedy. PMS may map tragic conditions, but it must not pretend tragedy has disappeared.

An **anti-capture reading** holds that moral luck exposes a limit of formal operator grammars, including PMS. This remains open. PMS translation does not defeat rival formalizations unless explicit comparative criteria are supplied and applied. The completed YAML explicitly preserves non-capture zones and rival readings rather than treating them as drift or failure.
