### Why this case matters

Dirty hands / forced choice matters because it presses directly on a classical philosophical closure-demand: the wish to know whether morally difficult action can be made clean, justified, integrated, absolved, condemned, or finally explained. The case does not simply ask what an agent should do under conflicting obligations. It asks how such a situation should be structurally understood when loss, guilt, responsibility, conflict, or irreversibility may remain significant even after every available action, interpretation, or reconciliation has been exhausted.

The central difficulty is that the case places pressure on several closure mechanisms at once. It invites a moral closure: which act was right? It invites a responsibility closure: who bears the burden? It invites an interpretive closure: how should the situation be described? It invites an integrative closure: can the competing demands be held together? It invites a temporal closure: can later repair, explanation, or acceptance settle what happened? And it invites a metaphysical closure: is there some ultimate structure that makes the remainder disappear?

The completed PMS analyses refuse these closures as automatic. They do not deny that action may be required. They do not deny that responsibility may persist. They do not deny that guilt or moral residue may be meaningful. But they also do not convert any of these into person ranking, diagnosis, culpability, maturity failure, or final metaphysical insight. Instead, the case is treated as a structural problem of praxis under constraint: a field of differentiated alternatives, directional pressure, framing, structured absence, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, contested integration, and possible self-binding.

This is why the case belongs in AXIOM. AXIOM is a cartographic and indexing project, not a verdict machine. It maps closure-demands, shows where PMS Core is sufficient, where an overlay adds precision, where MIP is justified as a downstream guardrail, and where AHP is useful for hardening the analysis artefact. Dirty hands / forced choice is exemplary because it tests the whole discipline of that stack. It is easy to overclaim here. It is easy to treat moral seriousness as proof of guilt, non-resolution as proof of failure, conflict as proof of pathology, responsibility as legal culpability, tragedy as metaphysical depth, or PMS coherence as a solution. The case forces the model to demonstrate restraint.

The philosophical closure-demand is especially strong because dirty-hands cases typically resist the comforting separation between “the correct act” and “the remaining loss.” A choice may be necessary and still leave residue. A person or role may be responsible and still not be simply blameworthy. A frame may clarify the structure and still not eliminate tragedy. A coherent analysis may be possible and still not be morally sufficient. This is the decisive point: in this case, structural legibility is not the same as moral purification.

The case is therefore not solved metaphysically. PMS does not say what guilt “really is,” whether moral contamination exists, whether tragedy has ontological status, or whether an act carries metaphysical stain. It is not solved empirically, because no event, actor, data, history, decision path, or outcome trace has been provided. It is not solved clinically or diagnostically, because no mental state, symptom, disorder, trait, maturity deficit, or person-type is inferred. It is not solved legally or forensically, because responsibility in the PMS and MIP sense is not liability, culpability, duress, excuse, justification, intent, admissibility, or legal fault. It is not solved by implementation, because the analysis does not produce a decision procedure, intervention script, governance protocol, enforcement rule, or automated classification.

The case matters because it shows the value of AXIOM’s boundary discipline. PMS can make the form of the problem visible; CONFLICT can sharpen the possibility of stabilized incompatibility; MIP can identify where maturity, responsibility, power-to-act, IA, and D-guardrails would matter if the case became concrete; AHP can harden the analysis against overreading. But none of these layers is allowed to claim what the case material does not warrant. The result is not closure by authority, but disciplined visibility of where closure is possible, where it remains conditional, and where Λ-residue must remain visible.

### Scene and frame structure

The case is not a concrete scene; it is a conceptual scene packet. That distinction matters. A concrete scene would have actors, roles, actions, constraints, alternatives, harms, time sequence, evidence, and publicness conditions. Here, the scene is an abstract philosophical field: a morally difficult choice in which loss, conflict, guilt, responsibility, or irreversibility may remain significant even when no available action, interpretation, or reconciliation fully resolves the situation.

The scene boundary is therefore conceptual. Inside the frame are the structural features explicitly supplied by the case material: morally difficult choice, loss, conflict, guilt, responsibility, irreversibility, unavailable full resolution, and the question of interpretation. Outside the frame are empirical details, legal standards, clinical claims, psychological states, moral rankings, metaphysical doctrines, and implementation procedures.

The PMS frame □ is a moral-praxeological frame. It is not a moral theory in the sense of deciding which act is right. It is a frame that makes relevant the structure of choosing under constrained alternatives, bearing responsibility across time, and encountering residue where clean resolution is unavailable. Within this frame, dirty hands / forced choice is not treated as a solved dilemma but as a pressure field: action may be necessary, yet the structure may still preserve loss or guilt as Λ-residue.

The frame contains several protected constraints. The analysis must remain reversible. It must maintain Χ, reflective distance. It must preserve Dignity-in-Practice as a praxeological constraint, not as ontological ranking. It must not convert structural asymmetry into blame. It must not treat non-resolution as failure. It must not treat coherence as sufficient. It must leave rival readings open. It must not reframe resistance, rupture, non-fit, disagreement, or missing evidence as drift merely because PMS can describe a structure.

The roles are abstract rather than concrete. The possible responsibility-bearing agent is only a structural placeholder: someone or some role-position that may have to act, refuse, choose, decide, or interpret under loss and constraint. Affected parties are likewise abstract: possible bearers of harm, vulnerability, cost, or unresolved consequence. No concrete institution is given, but the possibility of institutional or public reuse becomes relevant in the CONFLICT and MIP layers as a transmission risk. The analyst is also a role: the structural interpreter who must not let model fluency become closure by authority.

Temporality Θ is central. Irreversibility implies that the case cannot be reduced to a momentary choice. The relevant structure extends before and after the decision: expectations, alternatives, delays, consequences, guilt, responsibility, and possible repair all unfold across time. However, no concrete temporal sequence is provided. We do not know the timing of the decision, the duration of consequences, the speed of lock-in, the availability of revision, or whether there were review points. The reversibility window exists in the analysis, not in the case material. The PMS application remains reversible because it withholds verdicts. That means the analysis must distinguish two things: practical irreversibility within a possible dirty-hands situation, and analytic reversibility in how the case is interpreted. Exposure and asymmetry conditions are also abstract. If this language were moved into public, institutional, legal, or person-near contexts, exposure capture could arise. The result is a carefully bounded conceptual frame. The case is inside AXIOM because it exhibits a closure-demand of high philosophical intensity, but the frame prevents closure from becoming verdict. ### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading treats dirty hands / forced choice as a framed difference-field under structured absence, asymmetry, and temporality, requiring recontextualization and distance while leaving integration and self-binding unresolved. The recommended formula in the completed Core YAML is:

**Χ[Φ(Λ + Ω + Θ within □ over Δ)] → Σ? / Ψ?**

This is not a dependency chain replacing PMS Base. It is a case formula: a compact way of saying that the case requires reflective distance around recontextualization of non-resolution, asymmetry, and irreversibility inside a moral frame over differentiated alternatives, while Σ and Ψ remain question marks.

The operators must be read in PMS order.

**Δ — Difference.**
Δ is active and foundational. The case begins with distinctions: possible actions versus unavailable clean actions; loss versus non-loss; responsibility versus non-responsibility; guilt versus absence of guilt; conflict versus reconciliation; reversible versus irreversible consequence; interpretation versus non-resolution. Without such distinctions, there would be no forced choice and no dirty-hands pressure. Δ makes the field legible.

The calibration issue is that the distinctions are abstract. No concrete alternatives are named. We do not know whether the available options are genuinely incompatible, morally comparable, legally permitted, practically feasible, or different only under one interpretive frame. Pushing Δ too far would mean treating the abstract presence of “choice” as evidence of a concrete decision structure. The proper reading is thinner: a difference-field is active, but its internal topology is not yet specified.

**∇ — Impulse.**
∇ is weak-to-active. The case implies directional pressure: something must be done, interpreted, borne, or resolved. Forced choice is not neutral contemplation; it contains action-pressure and closure-pressure. However, no motive, desire, urgency, institutional command, psychological drive, or enacted movement is given. Therefore ∇ must be structural rather than psychological.

The dependency risk is Δ → ∇ inflation. One might see differentiated alternatives and infer agency, intention, or decisive action. The YAML blocks that. Directionality exists as pressure in the structure, not as evidence of subjective impulse. Overclaim would occur if the analysis inferred that an actor wanted, avoided, intended, or chose in a particular way.

**□ — Frame.**
□ is active. The case only makes sense because a moral-praxeological frame is already in place. Loss, guilt, conflict, responsibility, irreversibility, and reconciliation matter only within a frame that defines them as relevant. The frame does not solve the case; it makes the closure-demand intelligible.

The calibration issue is plurality. Dirty-hands cases can be framed as tragedy, culpability, necessity, role conflict, political responsibility, existential burden, moral remainder, or legal necessity. The PMS frame must not collapse these rival frames into one. It must declare itself as structural and moral-praxeological, not as a comprehensive ethical doctrine. Overclaim would occur if □ were treated as the true frame rather than the working frame.

**Λ — Non-Event / structured absence.**
Λ is highly active. The case turns on what does not occur: no available action fully resolves the situation; no interpretation fully dissolves guilt or loss; no reconciliation fully repairs the conflict; no clean option appears; no final closure arrives. Λ is not mere lack of information. It is meaningful absence within the frame.

This operator is crucial because dirty-hands phenomena often involve residue even after action. The absence of full resolution is not a temporary defect waiting for better modeling. It may be part of the phenomenon. The calibration risk is twofold. First, Λ could be reduced to missing evidence, as if the problem would disappear with more facts. Second, Λ could be overdramatized into metaphysical tragedy. PMS must do neither. It marks structured absence and keeps its meaning open.

**Α — Attractor.**
Α is weak. The case material does not specify recurrence, repeated patterning, role-script stabilization, institutional routine, or habitual conflict formation. Dirty-hands discourse as a philosophical topic may stabilize around patterns such as guilt, necessity, sacrifice, or residue, but the case itself does not provide evidence of repeated praxis. Therefore Α cannot carry major downstream claims.

The dependency risk is important because Ω and Θ depend on Α in PMS Base. The Core YAML allows Ω and Θ as conceptually active but dependency-limited because the case lacks concrete attractor formation. Overclaim would occur if the analysis treated a single abstract case as a stabilized pattern or conflict attractor.

**Ω — Asymmetry.**
Ω is active and analytically major, but under-specified. Forced choice under responsibility and loss implies imbalance. Some party or role may have power-to-act; another may bear harm. One obligation may dominate another. One role may carry more exposure, vulnerability, dependency, or decision burden. Responsibility itself often implies asymmetry: someone is differently positioned relative to consequence.

But the exact gradient is not given. Ω could mean power, vulnerability, obligation, capacity, exposure, dependency, role burden, or liability. It could run between agent and affected party, institution and subject, public and private roles, or competing commitments within one role. The YAML therefore marks Ω as structural, not moralistic. It must not be converted into blame, superiority, guilt, authority, or victimhood without evidence.

**Θ — Temporality.**
Θ is active. Irreversibility directly activates temporality. So do remaining guilt, persisting loss, delayed responsibility, aftermath, and the possibility that no later reconciliation fully undoes the situation. Dirty hands is not exhausted by the choice moment. It concerns what persists after choice: consequence, memory, burden, responsibility, and possible non-repair.

The calibration issue is that no trajectory is supplied. We do not know what happened before or after the choice, how quickly consequences locked in, whether repair was attempted, whether revision was possible, or how long the burden persisted. Θ therefore supports temporal extension but not developmental interpretation. Overclaim would occur if temporality were treated as maturity, growth, decline, fate, or irreversible guilt without concrete sequence.

**Φ — Recontextualization.**
Φ is active. The case explicitly asks how such choices should be understood. That is a recontextualization question. The same forced-choice structure may be read as tragedy, necessity, guilt, culpability, sacrifice, role conflict, political office, existential responsibility, legal duress, or moral remainder. Φ is therefore central to the article.

But Φ must not become assimilation. Recontextualization is not a machine for absorbing rival readings into PMS. The YAMLs repeatedly warn that resistance, non-fit, rival framing, or residue must not automatically be redescribed as incomplete calibration or weak integration. The role of Φ is to make frame movement visible while preserving the possibility that some rival readings capture something PMS does not.

**Χ — Distance.**
Χ is active and required. The PMS entry condition demands distance, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. In this case, Χ prevents the analysis from becoming a tribunal. It interrupts the rush from moral seriousness to verdict. It blocks moves from guilt-language to diagnosis, from responsibility to legal culpability, from asymmetry to blame, from conflict to pathology, and from coherence to solution.

Χ is not emotional detachment or moral indifference. It does not erase loss. It does not deny responsibility. It is reflective restraint under pressure. The calibration risk is that distance could become avoidance, sanitization, or abstraction that neutralizes tragedy. The YAML avoids this by holding both sides: distance is required, but it must preserve rather than dissolve moral residue.

**Σ — Integration.**
Σ is weak-to-active but unresolved. Dirty-hands cases demand integration: competing obligations, harms, reasons, responsibilities, and interpretations must somehow be held together if action or judgment is to proceed. But the case explicitly allows that no integration fully resolves the situation. Therefore Σ is not an achieved result. It is the contested site of the case.

The greatest overclaim risk lies here. A coherent PMS mapping could be mistaken for successful integration. That would be false. Structural coherence of the analysis does not prove moral adequacy, practical reconciliation, empirical truth, legal validity, phenomenological completeness, or metaphysical resolution. In this case, Σ must remain marked with a question mark.

**Ψ — Self-Binding.**
Ψ is weak-to-active but not established. Responsibility may persist across time and may involve self-binding: a role or agent may bind itself to owning, repairing, remembering, or carrying a burden. But the case supplies no concrete actor, commitment, promise, role, confession, duty, identity, policy, or stable self-relation. Therefore Ψ is possible, not found.

Ψ applies most clearly to the analysis itself: the PMS application must self-bind to its guardrails. It must not violate Χ, reversibility, Dignity-in-Practice, non-capture, or claim boundaries. In the case material, however, Ψ remains conditional. Overclaim would occur if self-binding were treated as moral maturity, guilt acceptance, identity formation, or responsibility assignment without scene data.

Taken together, the PMS Core reading is strong but bounded. It can say that dirty hands / forced choice is structurally legible as a Λ–Ω–Θ case requiring Φ and Χ. It cannot say that Σ succeeds, that Ψ is established, that a person is guilty, that the conflict is terminal, or that moral residue has been resolved.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The dependency discipline is central because the case strongly tempts shortcutting. Dirty hands / forced choice invites a jump from Δ and ∇ directly to responsibility, guilt, or tragic self-binding. PMS does not allow that. Later operators cannot be used cheaply.

Σ cannot be asserted merely because there is a coherent interpretation. PMS requires Χ and Φ before Σ. In this case, Χ and Φ are active: the analysis does recontextualize and maintain distance. But Σ still remains unresolved because the case itself concerns possible non-integrability. The analysis may coordinate the structure; it may not claim that the moral conflict is integrated.

Ψ cannot be asserted merely because responsibility is thematically present. Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. Θ and Χ are active, but Σ is unresolved and no concrete self-binding structure is supplied. Therefore Ψ remains possible and analytic, not achieved. A responsibility-bearing self-relation may become relevant in a fuller scene, but it is not established by the abstract material.

Ω and Θ are also dependency-limited because Α is weak. PMS Base places Ω after Α and Θ after Ω and Α. The case conceptually activates asymmetry and temporality, but without recurrence or stabilizing attractor data these operators cannot support full concrete claims. They can carry the abstract reading; they cannot ground a scene-level finding about lock-in, pattern formation, or role asymmetry.

Coherence is not proof. This is the governing calibration rule. The analysis can be formally coherent and still under-discriminate the case. It can map Λ, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, and weak Σ in a way that looks elegant. That elegance is itself a risk. The completed YAMLs mark the coherence risk as high: PMS might become too smooth, translating all residue into recontextualization, weak integration, or drift. The proper discipline is to treat coherence as a structural hypothesis, not as moral, empirical, legal, metaphysical, or phenomenological adequacy.

Non-capture is not failure. The case may contain moral residue, tragic remainder, incommensurable obligations, first-person guilt, theological meaning, phenomenological anguish, or legal distinctions that PMS does not fully capture. That does not mean PMS failed in a simple sense. It means the boundary has been marked. A good PMS application preserves where it stops.

PMS must not become a closure machine. The model is not used here to replace dirty-hands difficulty with a formula. It is used to make visible where closure is demanded, where it is structurally possible, where it remains conditional, and where residue persists. If the analysis were to say, “the PMS formula solves the case,” it would violate its own discipline.

Invalid dependency shortcuts include: Δ/∇ → Ψ, where choice-pressure becomes responsibility binding without frame and integration; □ → legal culpability, where a moral frame becomes forensic judgment; Λ → failure, where non-resolution becomes immaturity; Ω → blame, where asymmetry becomes guilt; Θ → development, where temporality becomes maturity; Φ → capture, where rival readings are absorbed; Χ → detachment, where distance erases tragedy; Σ → closure, where coherence becomes adequacy; and Ψ → person essence, where self-binding becomes identity verdict.

The dependency discipline therefore preserves the most important result of the PMS Core pass: this case is structurally readable, but not structurally closable by PMS alone.

The proper result is non-assignment, not a low score and not a hidden criticism.

The second relevant PMS drift-pattern is **AD_Sigma_low**, fragmented integration. This is also possible but not established. Dirty-hands cases naturally involve competing frames and difficulty integrating responsibility, loss, action, guilt, and non-resolution. The case may resist integration because no available action or interpretation fully resolves it. But weak or absent integration is not automatically drift. It may be faithful to the phenomenon. If the case is genuinely tragic or incommensurable, then the failure of full Σ is not an analytic defect or maturity failure; it is part of what must remain visible.

The YAML therefore marks AD_Sigma_low as conditional. The coherence axis is under-specified, not declared volatile. The responsibility axis is under-specified, not declared inconsistent. A concrete AD_Sigma_low finding would require evidence of repeated recontextualization without consolidation, unstable trajectories, inconsistent responsibility handling, or recurrent inability to coordinate frames. None of that is supplied. The abstract case licenses the possibility of fragmented integration, but not the drift finding.

The related coherence risk is high. PMS can make this case look very clean: Λ, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, unresolved Σ, possible Ψ. The formula works. But the very smoothness of the formula creates under-discrimination risk. If the same PMS pattern can describe ordinary dilemma, tragic accident, legal necessity, existential guilt, psychological regret, political office, and moral contamination without differentiating them, the analysis becomes too general. The completed YAML therefore requires discrimination tests: dirty hands must be distinguished from ordinary solvable dilemmas, mere regret, tragic loss without agency, legal duress or necessity, culpability, and successful reconciliation.

Under-discrimination is not solved by adding more PMS vocabulary. It is solved by maintaining claim boundaries and specifying what is missing. In this case, what is missing is concrete scene data: actors, alternatives, consequences, knowledge, capacity, asymmetry direction, action trace, and responsibility structure. The drift-pattern result therefore remains restrained. The analysis can say: “this is a field in which awareness/action imbalance and low integration could become risks.” It cannot say: “the case exhibits these drifts.”

This is one of the most important performative successes of the YAML chain. The model does not punish non-resolution. It does not treat residue as immaturity. It does not translate conflict into pathology. It does not convert non-assignment into failure. It lets under-specification remain under-specification.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The active add-on is **PMS-CONFLICT**. It is applied as an overlay/application profile only. PMS.yaml remains the only PMS Base reference. The CONFLICT layer does not redefine Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, or Ψ. It does not change PMS dependencies. It does not rewrite the Core pass. Its reduced signatures are shorthand lenses and threshold markers, not new operator chains or proof structures.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer here because dirty hands / forced choice is not only a general case of non-resolution. It also presses on a specifically conflict-related question: when does unresolved moral difficulty become stabilized incompatibility under binding, time, cost, and exposure? PMS Core can show that Λ, Ω, Θ, Φ, and Χ are central and that Σ/Ψ remain conditional. PMS-CONFLICT adds a more precise vocabulary for possible binding collision, priced or asymmetric distance, exposure capture, terminal legibility without reintegration, dignity collapse risk, and downstream docking.

The CONFLICT overlay’s primary contribution is therefore not a stronger verdict. It is a sharper set of thresholds. It asks whether weak Σ is merely under-specification, whether competing obligations form a binding collision, whether distance or exit is asymmetrically priced, whether exposure converts interpretation into pressure, whether conflict stabilizes into an attractor, and whether the case reaches a terminal descriptive regime where internal reintegration no longer carries. This is important because dirty-hands language can otherwise move too quickly from “there is residue” to “there is terminal conflict,” or from “there are competing obligations” to “a binding collision has been found.” CONFLICT prevents that escalation by requiring additional structural markers: specified bindings, specified cost, specified exit or distance conditions, specified exposure channels, and some evidence of stabilization or failed shared Σ. In this case, those thresholds are mostly not crossed.

The overlay marks **CONFLICT terminal core** as weak. Its reduced signature involves Σ, Ψ, Ω, Θ, and Χ: integration under load, binding, asymmetry, temporality, and priced or asymmetric distance. The case approaches this signature conceptually because Σ is unresolved, Ω and Θ are active, Χ is required, and Ψ is possible. But it does not fully activate the signature because no concrete binding collision, priced Χ, asymmetric exit, exposure channel, or recurrence is specified. This is a threshold lens, not a finding of terminal conflict.

The overlay also marks **stabilized conflict attractor** as weak. Α_conflict would require conflict itself to stabilize as a repeatable orientation field under asymmetrical cost and temporal pressure. The abstract case does not show repeated conflict, role-script formation, path dependence, or recurrent incompatibility. It shows possible moral non-resolution, not stabilized conflict-pattern formation. Therefore conflict attractor language remains weak.

**DRIFT_binding_collision** is treated as a risk. Dirty-hands cases often suggest incompatible obligations: one binding may require action that violates another; one responsibility may be carried only at the cost of another. CONFLICT makes this visible more sharply than PMS Core. But the case does not name the bindings, their bearers, their shared frame, their incompatibility threshold, or the cost of carrying them together. Binding collision is therefore conceptually relevant but under-specified. It is not an active drift finding.

**DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in** is also a risk, not a finding. The Core pass already marks Ω and Θ as active. CONFLICT asks the more specific question: is Χ unevenly available? Can one role withdraw, delay, reframe, or exit at lower cost than another? Does reflective distance become a privilege rather than a shared stabilizing resource? The case does not answer. It gives no distribution of exit cost, liability, exposure, or reflection access. Therefore Χ remains an analytic guardrail rather than a case-internal lock-in mechanism.

**DRIFT_exposure_capture** is a transmission risk. The abstract case contains no public, institutional, archival, media, or audience-coupled scene. But dirty-hands language is highly vulnerable to hardening. If excerpted into public or institutional contexts, “dirty hands” can become guilt attribution, culpability language, person-near verdict, or sanction affordance. CONFLICT identifies this as a reuse risk rather than a case finding. That distinction is crucial: the risk belongs to transmission, not to the abstract material itself.

**DRIFT_dignity_collapse** is not applicable as a finding. The completed Core pass and CONFLICT overlay preserve Χ, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. The analysis does not shame, rank, diagnose, morally sentence, or legally attribute responsibility to any person. Dignity collapse remains a prohibited misuse pathway: it would arise if conflict legibility were converted into coercive pressure, humiliating classification, person-near guilt assignment, or sanctioning language under Ω/Θ load.

The CONFLICT layer also contains non-operator overlay handles. **Publicness P** is not an operator. It amplifies Ω, Θ, Α, and Φ by increasing exposure coupling and raising revision costs. In this case, P is weak because no public scene exists. **Misuse Gradient MG** is also not an operator. It marks predictable transmission risk when conflict language travels through excerpts, audience coupling, or enforcement affordances. Here MG is weak-to-mixed because the current analysis is private and descriptive, but the terminology is sensitive.

The overlay’s non-operator constructs remain carefully bounded. **Conflict attractor Α_conflict** is weak. **Binding collision** is weak. **Asymmetry lock-in** is weak. **Exposure capture** is weak as a transmission risk. **Dignity collapse** is not applicable internally. **Terminal regime positioning** is weak. None of these constructs becomes a PMS operator. None replaces PMS dependencies. None permits drift-as-verdict.

The overlay’s terminality language is especially important. PMS-CONFLICT defines terminality as stack-internal and grammar-internal. It does not mean conflict as such has no further analysis. It does not mean repair is impossible in the world. It means that within the CONFLICT descriptive pathway, once incompatibility stabilizes under sustained Θ/Ω/Ψ and costly or asymmetric Χ, the grammar yields no further internal reintegration layer; continuation would become downstream governance, institutionalization, decision, or evaluation. In this case, terminal positioning remains weak. The case raises the possibility of legibility without reintegration, but it does not evidence terminal conflict.

The CONFLICT final signature is therefore best rendered as:

**weak_CONFLICT_terminal_core / risk_DRIFT_binding_collision with active_Χ_guardrail and preserved_non_capture**

This is a reduced signature only. It is not a PMS dependency chain. It is not a terminal conflict finding. It is not a solution. It is an overlay statement that dirty hands / forced choice can be conflict-legible while remaining below the threshold for stabilized terminal conflict.

The MIP layer adds a further, downstream discipline. It is not a PMS operator layer and not a replacement for PMS Core or PMS-CONFLICT. Its task is to ask whether the abstract case permits maturity-in-practice profiling. The answer is deliberately negative in the scoring sense. MIP marks the case as a private structural working note only. It assigns no A-band, no M-band, no IA result, and no D-profile. It treats the case as conceptually rich but evidentially insufficient for enactment assessment.

MIP’s value is not in giving a score. Its value is in preventing score-like misuse. It distinguishes case-level awareness from actor awareness. It distinguishes responsibility as a conceptual pressure from responsibility as a structured assessment. It distinguishes power-to-act as thematically relevant from power-to-act as evidenced. It distinguishes Dignity-in-Practice as a guardrail from D as an activated module. This is especially important because dirty-hands language can easily produce the impression that someone must be mature, immature, guilty, heroic, contaminated, evasive, or responsible in a determinate way. MIP refuses that move.

The MIP output marks the A-score as not assigned because there is no concrete time-role-context. It marks the M-score as not assigned because knowledge, power/control, predictability, access to alternatives, and serious integration attempt are all unknown. It marks the IA-box as not assigned because transparency, justification, time-boundness, and reversibility cannot be assessed without a concrete asymmetry. It keeps the D-module inactive because no D1/D2 enactments are available and D0 remains untouched. MIP therefore adds guardrail precision, not case closure.

The AHP layer is still more specific: it is not a case analysis but an analysis-artifact hardening overlay. It treats the previous MIP output as the object of analysis. Its high Precision Heuristic means that the MIP artefact is precise, scope-aware, reversible, and careful about score hygiene. It does not validate the dirty-hands reading itself. It does not rescore A, M, IA, or D. It does not activate the D-module. It does not upgrade transmission status. It identifies attack surfaces in the analysis artefact: formal completeness may be mistaken for evidence; high PH may be mistaken for validation; threshold conditions are present but distributed; model-derived claims may overshadow sparse case material; D-language is excerpt-sensitive; IA-box decidability may be misunderstood; and private working-note status must be repeated.

Thus the full overlay stack adds increasingly specific boundaries. CONFLICT sharpens conflict thresholds. MIP blocks maturity and responsibility overclaim. AHP hardens the analysis against formal and transmission misuse. None of these layers increases authority to decide the case. They increase precision about what remains undecided.

### Layer interaction

The PMS Core layer remains the base. It supplies the operator grammar, dependency discipline, derived structures, entry condition, and non-capture rule. The Core pass tells us that the case is primarily a Λ–Ω–Θ field inside a moral-praxeological frame, requiring Φ and Χ, with Σ unresolved and Ψ possible but not established. That structure remains unchanged.

The CONFLICT overlay sharpens a narrower question: whether unresolved dirty-hands residue should be treated as conflict-terminal, binding-collision, exposure-capture, or asymmetry-lock-in material. Its answer is restrained. It says that these are relevant thresholds and risks, but mostly weak or under-specified in the abstract case. The overlay therefore adds precision by refusing premature escalation.

MIP adds a different kind of layer. It does not deepen PMS operators or CONFLICT signatures. It asks what maturity-in-practice analysis can responsibly say about awareness, coherence, responsibility, power-to-act, IA, and D when the case is abstract. The answer is again restrained: no A-band, no M-band, no IA result, no D-profile. MIP does not score the case. It identifies missing evidence and protects against misuse.

AHP adds one more layer, but it is second-order. It does not analyze dirty hands directly. It analyzes the quality of the MIP analysis artefact. It assigns a high Precision Heuristic because the MIP output is careful about scope, evidence, non-assignment, D boundaries, reversibility, and transmission. But high PH does not validate the case reading. It does not rescore. It does not activate D. It does not upgrade transmission. It hardens the analysis against overreading.

The interaction across layers is therefore not cumulative authority. It is cumulative discipline. PMS Core makes structure visible. CONFLICT tests conflict-specific thresholds. MIP blocks maturity and responsibility overclaim. AHP identifies attack surfaces in the analysis artefact. Each layer narrows misuse rather than expanding verdict power.

What remains PMS Core is the Δ–Ψ grammar, the dependency order, the derived-structure formulas, and the entry condition. What the add-on sharpens is conflict-legibility: binding collision, terminal threshold, priced Χ, exposure capture, dignity collapse, publicness, misuse gradient, downstream docking. What the add-on must not redefine is the PMS operator set or the meaning of operators. It must not treat reduced signatures as new dependencies. It must not use terminality as finality. It must not turn conflict into essence.

Overlay language could overreach in several ways. “Binding collision” could be mistaken for proof that duties are actually incompatible. “Terminal regime” could be mistaken for a claim that nothing more can be done. “Exposure capture” could be mistaken for accusation. “Dignity collapse” could be mistaken for a moral verdict. “Weak CONFLICT terminal core” could be mistaken for a diagnosis of tragic failure. The YAMLs block these readings by marking weak, risk, not applicable, or transmission-only statuses.

MIP language could also overreach. “Maturity-in-practice” can sound evaluative even when no score is assigned. The case therefore keeps MIP in under-specification mode. MIP does not say the possible agent is mature, immature, responsible, evasive, blameworthy, or protected from responsibility. It says the evidence required to assess such matters is absent.

AHP language could overreach as well. “High precision” can sound like correctness. The AHP output explicitly blocks that reading. A high Precision Heuristic means the artefact is well-scoped and guarded; it does not mean the case is proven, solved, validated, or operationally usable.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. PMS preserves Λ. CONFLICT preserves non-terminality and non-capture. MIP preserves non-assignment. AHP preserves the distinction between artefact precision and case truth. The result is a layered analysis that does not become more coercive as it becomes more detailed.

### Example 1 — The rescue choice that leaves a remainder

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after the PMS Core discussion of Λ, Ω, and Θ.

A rescue coordinator receives two simultaneous distress signals during a severe storm. One signal comes from a small vessel closer to shore; the other comes from a larger vessel farther out, carrying more people but requiring longer travel time. The available team cannot reach both in time. Weather conditions are worsening, communication is intermittent, and every delay changes the likely outcome. The coordinator must choose a route, knowing that the choice may leave some people without help.

This vignette is not a legal case, not an empirical claim, and not a moral verdict about the coordinator. It is a bounded illustration of dirty hands / forced choice: a situation where action is required, but no available action fully resolves the loss. Even if the coordinator chooses the route that appears best under the information available, the remaining loss does not automatically disappear. The example demonstrates the difference between structural legibility and moral purification.

In PMS Core terms, **Δ** appears in the distinction between the two possible rescue paths, between reachable and unreachable lives, and between action and delay. **∇** appears as pressure to decide under deteriorating conditions. **□** is the emergency-response frame: the relevant question is not abstract moral purity, but action under constrained rescue capacity. **Λ** is central because the expected clean event — saving everyone, or making a choice without remainder — does not occur. The absent rescue is not just missing information; it is a meaningful non-event inside the frame. **Ω** appears in unequal exposure: the coordinator has decision power, while those in danger bear the direct consequences. **Θ** appears because the decision unfolds under time pressure, worsening conditions, and irreversible aftermath.

The example also shows why **Φ**, **Χ**, **Σ**, and **Ψ** must be handled carefully. The event could later be recontextualized as tragedy, necessity, failure, courage, negligence, or institutional under-resourcing. That is a Φ problem. Χ is required so that the analysis does not collapse into a quick verdict. Σ may be attempted through an after-action review, but no integration can be presumed to erase the loss. Ψ might become relevant if the coordinator or institution takes responsibility for review, communication, or future preparedness, but the vignette alone does not establish a stable self-binding structure.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant only as a weak or threshold lens. There may be a possible binding collision: save the nearer vessel, or prioritize the larger number farther out. But the vignette does not prove a terminal conflict regime, stabilized incompatibility, or asymmetry lock-in. It merely illustrates why CONFLICT can ask sharper questions about binding, time, cost, and exposure.

MIP would not assign an A-band, M-band, IA result, or D-profile from this vignette alone. A maturity-in-practice assessment would require concrete data: what the coordinator knew, what alternatives were real, what protocols applied, what resources were available, what was predictable, and whether serious integration or review occurred. AHP, if applied, would harden the later analysis artefact; it would not validate the choice itself.

The forbidden claims are clear. The example does not show that the coordinator is guilty, innocent, mature, immature, negligent, heroic, or morally contaminated. It does not establish legal liability, clinical state, metaphysical tragedy, or policy validity. It does not validate PMS.

The non-capture zone remains important. A person affected by the loss may experience grief or anger that is not exhausted by operator mapping. A theological, deontological, consequentialist, or tragic-ethical account may preserve dimensions that PMS only marks structurally. The article takeaway is therefore restrained: the vignette shows how dirty hands can be structurally legible without becoming morally clean, legally settled, or fully integrated.

### Example 2 — The confidentiality conflict in a care institution

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** in the PMS-CONFLICT overlay section, after the discussion of binding collision and weak terminal-core status.

A senior member of a care institution receives confidential information from one resident suggesting that another resident may soon be harmed. The information is incomplete. Revealing it may violate confidentiality and damage trust in the care relationship. Keeping it confidential may leave another person exposed to risk. The institution has general policies, but they do not clearly settle this specific configuration. The senior member must decide whether to disclose, escalate, delay, seek more information, or intervene indirectly.

This vignette is useful because it shows why PMS Core alone is not always the final analytic layer, while also showing why the add-on must remain restrained. PMS Core can map the case as a framed difference-field under structured absence, asymmetry, temporality, and contested integration. PMS-CONFLICT adds a more specific question: are two bindings colliding in a way that no shared Σ can integrate without unacceptable cost?

The possible bindings are visible but not automatically settled. One binding concerns confidentiality: the care role depends on trust, privacy, and non-exposure. Another binding concerns protection: the institution may have a responsibility to prevent foreseeable harm. These bindings may collide, but the example does not by itself prove that they are absolutely incompatible. More facts would be needed: the severity and likelihood of harm, the resident’s capacity, policy thresholds, available protective alternatives, consent possibilities, supervisory channels, and whether disclosure could be limited.

The PMS Core hooks are clear. **Δ** distinguishes confidentiality, disclosure, delay, and protection routes. **∇** is the pressure to act before harm occurs. **□** is the care-institution frame, with confidentiality and protection as relevant constraints. **Λ** appears in missing information: unclear likelihood, absent consent, absent policy clarity, and no clean option. **Ω** appears because the staff member and institution have role power, while residents may bear exposure and consequence. **Θ** appears because delay, disclosure, trust erosion, and harm risk unfold over time. **Φ** appears when the same act is reframed: disclosure may be seen as betrayal in one frame and protection in another. **Χ** is necessary to prevent urgency from becoming coercive certainty. **Σ** is attempted through policy interpretation and practical coordination, but may remain incomplete. **Ψ** may appear only if the institution binds itself to a decision, review process, or revised protocol.

The CONFLICT hooks are stronger here than in a purely abstract example, but still bounded. The vignette illustrates **risk_DRIFT_binding_collision**: two commitments may become jointly non-viable. It also illustrates possible **asymmetry lock-in** if one role can externalize risk while another bears exposure. It may contain **exposure capture** if the disclosure becomes a durable record or public accusation. Yet none of these is automatically a finding. The vignette must not be read as proof of terminal conflict, dignity collapse, negligence, or institutional guilt.

The example also shows why the CONFLICT layer is a threshold discipline rather than a stronger moral language. If the staff member discloses, the analysis must not immediately call the disclosure betrayal. If the staff member does not disclose, the analysis must not immediately call the non-disclosure abandonment. If the staff member delays, the analysis must not immediately call the delay avoidance. Each of those readings would require evidence about knowledge, policy, feasible alternatives, time pressure, probability of harm, available consultation, and the reversibility or irreversibility of each path. CONFLICT makes the incompatibility legible; it does not decide which binding wins.

MIP would be relevant only if the vignette were expanded into a concrete case. It would ask what the staff member knew, what alternatives were realistically available, what was predictable, what authority and resources existed, and whether serious integration attempts occurred. It would also check IA conditions: transparency, justification, time-boundness, and reversibility. The D-module should remain off unless concrete D1/D2 enactments are visible and the communication space is safe, role-bound, and time-windowed. AHP would only harden the later analysis artefact: it might flag whether the analysis separates observation from inference, whether “confidentiality” and “protection” are defined with thresholds, and whether the language avoids converting institutional difficulty into person-near fault.

Forbidden claims include: that disclosure is legally required; that non-disclosure is culpable; that the staff member is mature or immature; that the resident who gave information is reliable or unreliable; that institutional policy is valid or invalid; or that PMS-CONFLICT has solved the care dilemma. Those would require facts and external frameworks.

The non-capture note is essential. The lived trust relation, fear, vulnerability, and institutional history may matter in ways the structural example does not fully capture. Legal duties, professional ethics, and trauma-informed practice would require separate frameworks. The article takeaway is that CONFLICT sharpens the visibility of binding collision without converting it into verdict. It shows what would need to be specified before stronger claims could be made.

### Example 3 — When “dirty hands” becomes a public label

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** in the misuse-boundary section, or at the end of the PMS-CONFLICT overlay section before the layer interaction discussion.

A policy committee privately discusses a difficult decision that produced serious loss despite constrained options. In the internal review, one member describes the case as involving “dirty hands”: not to accuse a person, but to mark that the decision may have been necessary while still leaving moral residue. Later, a fragment of that internal phrase appears in a public summary without the surrounding boundaries. The phrase is then quoted as if the committee had admitted guilt, moral corruption, or legal wrongdoing.

This example demonstrates a transmission risk rather than a new first-order case finding. The original phrase may have been structurally careful inside a private review frame. Once excerpted publicly, however, the same phrase can change function. It can move from philosophical description to reputational pressure, from non-resolution to accusation, from structural residue to person-near verdict. That shift is precisely why the completed analyses treat publicness, misuse gradient, exposure capture, and Dignity-in-Practice as serious boundary concerns.

In PMS Core terms, **□** shifts. Inside the internal frame, “dirty hands” may have meant: a difficult forced choice left Λ-residue despite action. In the public frame, the same phrase may be received as confession, blame, or evidence. **Φ** is therefore central: the phrase is recontextualized. **Ω** changes because public audiences, media dynamics, institutions, and affected parties may have unequal power to impose meanings or bear consequences. **Θ** matters because public records persist; an excerpt can harden over time. **Λ** appears because the full context is absent. **Χ** is required but endangered: public pressure may reduce reflective distance. **Σ** may be replaced by slogan-level coherence, and **Ψ** may harden into institutional memory or reputational label.

The CONFLICT overlay is directly relevant. This is a potential **exposure capture** pattern: recontextualization under scaled exposure converts interpretation into enforcement affordance. It may also create a **misuse gradient** increase, because the phrase travels without mode declaration, publicness boundary, reversibility clause, or non-person guardrail. But even here, the example must remain careful. It does not prove that exposure capture has occurred in every public reading. It illustrates a pathway by which it can occur.

MIP would treat this as a transmission and publicness problem, not as a maturity score. It would ask whether the analysis is still private, whether it has become public or media-amplified, whether identifiable persons are exposed, whether contest and correction channels exist, and whether the phrase creates shame or irreversible labeling. It would not assign an A-band, M-band, IA result, or D-profile without concrete evidence. AHP would be relevant as an analysis-artifact hardening layer: it would flag attack surfaces such as excerpt sensitivity, validation overread, and formal-completeness risk.

Forbidden claims are especially important here. The public excerpt does not prove legal guilt. It does not prove moral corruption. It does not prove that the committee, a member, or any affected party has a defect, diagnosis, or maturity failure. It does not prove that the original analysis was invalid. It shows that transmission can change the function of language under publicness and asymmetry.

The non-capture note cuts both ways. Public outrage may include genuine harm, grief, distrust, or political accountability that the structural analysis does not settle. At the same time, structural terminology may be misused to intensify accusation beyond what the original frame warranted. PMS and CONFLICT do not decide which public response is justified; they make visible the shift in frame, exposure, and risk.

The article takeaway is that dirty-hands language is not safe merely because it is analytically careful at the point of production. Under Ω, Θ, Φ, and publicness amplification, even bounded language can harden into verdict. This is why the case requires misuse boundaries, transmission discipline, and AHP-style artefact hardening before any public or institutional reuse.

### Non-capture and rival readings

Non-capture is central to this case. The YAMLs explicitly refuse the idea that translating dirty hands / forced choice into PMS, CONFLICT, MIP, or AHP exhausts the phenomenon. Several rival readings remain open, and each preserves something the structural stack may not fully capture.

A **tragic ethics reading** may preserve the possibility that some moral situations involve unavoidable loss that cannot be repaired by coherence. PMS can map Λ-residue, weak Σ, and Θ aftermath, but tragic ethics may insist that the remainder is not merely structural absence; it is the substance of the ethical situation. This reading resists any implication that a clearer map reduces tragedy.

A **deontological reading** may preserve the claim that some actions remain wrong even when necessary, useful, or unavoidable. PMS can describe competing frames and responsibility structures, but it does not decide whether an act violates an absolute duty. If dirty hands means doing wrong for the sake of necessity, that normative structure lies beyond PMS’s descriptive claim.

A **consequentialist reading** may preserve the focus on minimizing loss under constrained alternatives. PMS can map action-pressure and residue, but it does not compute outcomes or justify trade-offs. If the case is fundamentally about choosing the least harmful available act, PMS does not replace the evaluative calculus.

A **virtue-ethical reading** may preserve questions of character, practical wisdom, courage, corruption, or moral damage. PMS does not infer traits or character. It can describe self-binding and responsibility only structurally. If the issue is whether forced choice forms or deforms character, that requires a different framework and evidence.

An **existential reading** may preserve responsibility without full justification. It may say that one must choose and bear the choice even when no system can authorize it. PMS can map Ψ as possible self-binding, but without concrete actor evidence it cannot render existential ownership. The existential remainder may exceed operator description.

A **political-theological reading** may preserve questions of office, sovereignty, exception, sacrifice, and legitimacy. Dirty hands has a political tradition in which office-holders may act under exception or necessity. PMS-CONFLICT can make terminality and downstream governance boundaries visible, but it does not itself analyze sovereignty, theological exception, or political legitimacy.

A **phenomenological reading** may preserve lived guilt, anguish, burden, or moral remainder as first-person experience. PMS explicitly does not model qualia, consciousness, or inner experience. MIP likewise refuses to infer inner guilt from abstract guilt-language. If first-person experience is central, the PMS stack only partially captures the case.

A **legal-forensic reading** may preserve distinctions among culpability, justification, excuse, duress, necessity, negligence, intent, liability, and admissibility. The present analysis cannot make those distinctions because no legal frame or facts are supplied. Legal analysis would not be a defect of PMS; it would be a different mode.

An **anti-capture reading** may argue that dirty hands is precisely the kind of case that resists formal mapping. The YAMLs leave that open. PMS may produce useful structural visibility while still failing to capture the moral force of residue. The analysis does not treat this as failure. It treats it as a boundary.

These rival readings are not defects. They are part of the case’s philosophical seriousness. AXIOM’s role is to index them as open rather than to subordinate them to PMS. The structural reading gains value only if it preserves what it cannot settle.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would weaken if the case lacked meaningful frame, asymmetry, temporality, or structured absence. If dirty hands / forced choice were shown to be merely a semantic dispute about words, with no praxis stakes, no responsibility, no loss, no irreversibility, and no unavailable resolution, the Λ–Ω–Θ reading would be excessive. If a clean option were available and fully resolved the situation, the case would be closer to an ordinary dilemma than dirty hands.

The reading would also weaken if no asymmetry were identifiable. Ω is central but abstract. If a concrete version of the case involved no imbalance of exposure, obligation, power, vulnerability, capacity, dependency, or role burden, then the PMS Core analysis would need revision. Similarly, if there were no temporal persistence, no irreversibility, no aftermath, and no continuing responsibility, Θ would be overclaimed.

The reading becomes under-discriminating if the same formula fits too many cases without distinction. Ordinary regret, solvable conflict, tragic accident without agency, legal duress, psychological guilt, political sacrifice, and metaphysical contamination are not the same. A PMS reading must show what makes dirty hands distinct: unavailable clean resolution, meaningful residue, responsibility pressure, and temporality under asymmetry. If it cannot distinguish these, the formula is too smooth.

The reading becomes over-assimilative if all rival framings are translated into PMS categories. If phenomenological guilt becomes merely Λ, if deontological wrongness becomes merely frame conflict, if existential responsibility becomes merely Ψ, if legal culpability becomes merely Responsibility_R, the analysis would be absorbing rather than clarifying. Over-assimilation is one of the main falsifier risks.

The CONFLICT reading would weaken if there were no binding collision, no stabilized incompatibility, no priced or asymmetric Χ, no exposure channel, no recurrence, and no conflict attractor. In the present case, those are already missing; hence the CONFLICT reading remains weak or risk-level.
