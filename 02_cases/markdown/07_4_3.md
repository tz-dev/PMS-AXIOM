### Why this case matters

Trolley dilemmas matter because they concentrate a large philosophical closure-demand into a deceptively simple structure. Someone appears to face a forced choice. An action or omission appears to alter who is harmed and who is saved. A foreseeable consequence appears to generate pressure to assign responsibility. The case therefore asks not merely “what should be done?” but also “what kind of structure makes that question seem mandatory?” It brings together action, non-action, causation, harm, rescue, obligation, responsibility, temporal foreseeability, and the temptation to rank outcomes under pressure.

This is why the case belongs in AXIOM. AXIOM is not trying to settle classical problems by authority. It maps closure-demands: where a frame demands resolution, where the grammar can clarify structure, where a residue remains, and where an add-on is justified. Trolley dilemmas are exemplary because they make closure feel urgent. The dilemma seems to demand a yes-or-no response, an act-or-omit classification, a harm-minimization calculus, a responsibility assignment, and often a moral verdict. Yet the structure resists clean closure because different formalizations preserve different parts of the problem.

A consequentialist framing may treat branch outcomes as primary. A deontological framing may treat direct harm, prohibition, or the use of a person as decisive. A causation-based framing may distinguish redirecting harm from allowing harm. A role-duty framing may ask whether the chooser had a mandate, office, or obligation. A tragedy-based framing may refuse the premise that the situation yields a clean solution at all. These readings are not simply different opinions about the same solved structure. They preserve different closure demands, different residues, and different ways of locating what remains morally unresolved.

The structural difficulty lies in the fact that the same minimal material can be read through multiple non-equivalent frames. “Action” and “omission” are not neutral descriptors; they depend on the frame that makes a non-event meaningful. “Foreseeable consequence” may be conceptual, empirical, legal, probabilistic, stipulated, or retrospective. “Responsibility” may mean causal involvement, role accountability, moral blame, legal liability, repair obligation, tragic burden, or public answerability. “Saving” and “harming” may be counted as branch outcomes, but the act by which they are produced may still matter.

PMS Core can map the operator structure of this pressure, but it must not pretend that structural mapping is equivalent to solving the moral problem. The PMS-CONFLICT overlay can sharpen the incompatibility structure, but it must not turn weak dilemma-legibility into terminal conflict. MIP can review the maturity and guardrail quality of the analysis artefact, but it must not score a hypothetical chooser. AHP can harden the MIP output against ambiguity and misuse, but it must not rescore the analysis or validate the result.

The case is also important because it attracts misuse. Trolley dilemmas can be used as if they license ranking lives, shaming choosers, flattening victims into quantities, treating disagreement as irrationality, or turning philosophical thought experiments into decision procedures. A PMS reading must resist that. The analysis must remain reversible, non-diagnostic, non-tribunal, and non-person-ranking. It must preserve that the case is not a concrete scene. There is no actual switch, no actual person on the track, no real jurisdiction, no institutional mandate, no empirical probability distribution, no testimony, no affected-party voice, and no real-time decision record. The case is therefore structurally analyzable but not concretely decidable.

The case is not being solved metaphysically because PMS does not claim ontological truth about value, personhood, consciousness, dignity, or the commensurability of lives. It is not being solved empirically because no empirical data are supplied about actual behaviour, outcomes, probabilities, decision psychology, or population-level intuitions. It is not being solved clinically because no person, symptom, disorder, or treatment context is present. It is not being solved legally or forensically because no jurisdiction, duty, causation standard, mens rea, negligence threshold, excuse, justification, or evidence burden is supplied. It is not being solved diagnostically because no decision-maker is evaluated. The case is used to make the closure-demand visible, not to replace philosophical, legal, empirical, or practical judgment.

This is also why the case has AXIOM value. It shows how a familiar philosophical puzzle can be rendered without becoming a verdict machine. PMS Core identifies the operator structure that makes the dilemma legible. PMS-CONFLICT clarifies why incompatibility is visible without making the case terminal. MIP checks whether the analysis itself is handled with sufficient guardrail discipline. AHP then asks where the analysis artefact could still be misread. The point of the stack is therefore not cumulative certainty. It is cumulative boundary control.

### Scene and frame structure

The scene is not concrete. It is a conceptual scene: an abstract forced-choice configuration in which a chooser-position appears able to affect harm and rescue branches through action or omission. This distinction matters because a concrete scene would require much more. A concrete trolley variant would need a specified mechanism, timing, actors, available alternatives, uncertainty, possible intervention costs, role duties, and evidence of foreseeability. Without those details, the analysis cannot say what should be done or who should be held responsible.

The frame, □, is the forced-choice philosophical frame. It makes certain distinctions relevant: action versus omission, harmed versus saved, intervention versus non-intervention, foreseeable versus unforeseeable consequence, responsibility assigned versus responsibility not assigned. Within that frame, omission can become meaningful because the frame makes an expected or possible intervention salient. But the frame is not self-validating. It can be contested. A critic may reject the forced-choice frame as artificial, morally distorting, or incomplete. PMS must preserve that possibility.

Inside the frame are the abstract branches of the dilemma: possible action, possible omission, possible harm, possible rescue, possible causal redirection, possible responsibility-ascription, and possible rival interpretations of what the chooser-position is doing. Also inside the frame is the pressure to compare alternatives. The case makes branch difference legible and produces directional pressure toward resolution.

Outside the frame are all claims that would require further material: actual legal liability, actual moral truth, actual psychological motivation, actual empirical probability, actual institutional duty, actual personhood claims, actual consent or affected-party voice, and actual implementation standards. Also outside the frame is any claim that the case fully captures moral life. PMS can map the structure of a dilemma without claiming that a forced-choice abstraction adequately represents lived moral situations.

The protected constraints are Χ, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. Χ requires reflective distance: the analysis must not collapse into a verdict machine. Reversibility requires that operator assignments remain revisable if new facts or rival frames are supplied. Dignity-in-Practice requires restraint under asymmetry: no shaming, no ranking of persons, no humiliation, no ontological evaluation, no irreversible labels.

Roles are present only as abstract structural positions. The first is the chooser-position or decision-position, imagined as having some capacity to affect outcomes. The second is the affected-party position, exposed to harm or rescue without control over the abstract frame. The third is the analyst or observer, who has interpretive power over how the dilemma is formalized. The fourth is the downstream reader or user, who may misuse the analysis if it is treated as a decision rule. These are not persons. They are roles in the analysis.

Temporality, Θ, is central because the case depends on sequence. Action or omission precedes consequences. Foreseeability concerns future harm or rescue. Irreversibility matters because harm branches, once realized, cannot simply be undone. However, this temporality remains conceptual. The case does not supply actual time pressure, probability, delay, or causal evidence.

The reversibility window is strong for the analysis and weak or unknown for the imagined dilemma. The article can remain revisable because it is a structural working note. The hypothetical trolley scene, by contrast, may involve irreversible harm, but because no concrete timing is supplied, PMS cannot calibrate the actual window for revision. This distinction is important: analytic distance does not imply that a real agent would have reflective distance in a concrete emergency.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are visible but under-specified. The chooser-position appears to have consequence-shaping capacity. Affected parties appear exposed to harm or rescue. The analyst has interpretive authority over the artefact. Downstream users may have transmission power. Yet the exact asymmetry gradient is not concrete. Is the chooser obligated? Are the affected parties equally vulnerable? Is the action causal redirection or prevention? Is omission meaningful because of duty, capacity, or mere possibility? The YAML keeps Ω active but calibrated as conceptual rather than concrete.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading begins with **Δ, Difference**. Trolley dilemmas are made of distinctions. Action is differentiated from omission. Harm is differentiated from rescue. Direct intervention is differentiated from non-intervention. A branch in which one party is harmed is differentiated from a branch in which another party is harmed. A responsibility-ascribed branch is differentiated from a branch in which responsibility is denied or diffused. Without Δ, the dilemma has no structure. However, Δ must not be pushed too far. It would be an overclaim to assume that all these distinctions are clean, morally decisive, or legally stable. In many trolley variants, action and omission blur; causing and allowing are contested; harm and rescue may be framed differently depending on baseline assumptions. Δ opens the field, but it does not settle the field.

**∇, Impulse**, appears as directional pressure. Once the differences are visible, the case generates pressure to choose, prevent harm, reduce harm, redirect harm, justify non-action, or assign responsibility. This pressure is structural, not psychological. PMS does not infer fear, compassion, cruelty, courage, utilitarian commitment, deontological rigidity, or moral maturity. The case activates ∇ because the frame makes non-response feel consequential. But it would be an overclaim to read ∇ as an actual motive of an actual agent. No agent’s inner state is present.

**□, Frame**, is the forced-choice structure itself. It defines which differences matter and which possible branches are being compared. The frame may be moral-philosophical, legal, institutional, emergency-oriented, pedagogical, or purely formal; the supplied case does not specify which. This under-specification is not a defect in the analysis. It is part of the case. Trolley dilemmas are frame-sensitive because the same material can be re-read under different standards. PMS must therefore keep □ explicit. The invalid shortcut would be to treat the forced-choice frame as natural, complete, or exhaustive. The frame is an analytic construction, not an ontological container.

**Λ, Non-Event**, is strongly active because omission is central. Not switching, not intervening, not preventing, not rescuing, not refusing the frame, or not choosing an available alternative may all become meaningful non-events. But PMS defines Λ as structured absence: absence becomes meaningful only within a frame of expectation. Therefore, not every non-action automatically counts as responsibility-bearing omission. If the frame does not generate a duty, capacity, expectation, or salient possibility, Λ may weaken. The calibration issue is precise: what expected event failed to occur, and why did the frame make it expected? An overclaim would be to treat every absence as culpable omission or every omission as equivalent to action. Λ makes omission legible; it does not decide its moral or legal status.

**Α, Attractor**, is weak. The trolley dilemma is a recurrent philosophical problem-form. It has stabilized as a recognizable script: a forced choice, branching harms, action/omission distinction, and responsibility pressure. In that limited sense, Α is present. But this is not the same as a concrete behavioural attractor, institutional routine, or repeated decision pattern. No agent repeatedly chooses under trolley conditions. No institution is shown to reproduce the same conflict. Therefore Α remains weak or conditional. It would be overclaim to treat the abstract philosophical recurrence of the example as evidence of real-world patterned action.

**Ω, Asymmetry**, is active but conceptual. The chooser-position appears able to affect outcomes, while affected parties are exposed to harm or rescue. This creates a structural imbalance in capacity and exposure. There may also be asymmetry between those saved and those harmed, or between analyst and downstream reader. Yet Ω must be carefully calibrated. Is the asymmetry one of power, duty, knowledge, vulnerability, causal control, institutional role, or accidental position? The case does not specify. Therefore Ω cannot become blame, authority, superiority, inferiority, or person ranking. The overclaim would be to say that because a chooser-position has consequence-shaping capacity, it is already responsible, guilty, mature, or morally ranked.

**Θ, Temporality**, is active. Trolley dilemmas depend on temporal sequence: action or omission occurs before harm or rescue; consequences are foreseeable; later outcomes are linked to earlier choices; irreversibility matters. Θ also structures the responsibility question: when did the chooser know, when could action occur, when did the omission become meaningful, and when did harm become unavoidable? But the case supplies only conceptual temporality. It does not provide actual timing, probabilities, delays, or causal reliability. An overclaim would be to treat stipulated foreseeability as empirical predictability or legal foreseeability. Θ makes the trajectory visible; it does not validate the trajectory as fact.

**Φ, Recontextualization**, is central. Trolley dilemmas are famous because they change under reframing. The same branch structure may be read as action versus omission, causing versus allowing, rescue versus sacrifice, harm minimization versus prohibition, causal responsibility versus role responsibility, moral tragedy versus solvable calculation. Φ must remain active because the case is not reducible to one frame. At the same time, Φ must not become an assimilation machine. Rival readings are not automatically PMS drift, incomplete integration, or failed maturity. A deontological reading is not a defective consequentialist frame. A tragedy reading is not simply low Σ. A refusal of the trolley frame may preserve something that PMS does not capture. The overclaim would be to treat every rival formalization as merely another PMS recontextualization already contained by the model.

**Χ, Distance**, is active as an application condition. It is what prevents the analysis from becoming a tribunal. Χ suspends immediate closure, keeps the reading reversible, and protects against converting structural legibility into moral decision. In the case itself, however, Χ is not necessarily available to a concrete chooser, because no concrete scene exists. The analysis has reflective distance; the hypothetical scene may not. This distinction blocks a common misuse: treating the analyst’s distance as proof that an agent in a real emergency should have had the same distance. Χ must also not become detachment from harm. It is restraint against overclaim, not indifference.

**Σ, Integration**, is weak. Trolley dilemmas demand integration across competing frames: consequences, duties, omissions, causal pathways, affected parties, and responsibility. Yet the case material supplies no integrated resolution. PMS may show the demand for integration, but it cannot claim that integration has been achieved. A coherent PMS rendering is not enough. This is one of the most important YAML findings. The case strongly fits the PMS grammar, but that fluency is a risk. If Σ is pushed too far, PMS becomes a closure machine: it may smooth over morally relevant differences by producing an elegant operator chain. Therefore Σ remains conditional. It requires discriminative force, not merely coherence.

**Ψ, Self-Binding**, is weak and mostly analytic. Responsibility is mentioned, but PMS Responsibility_R requires self-binding orientation under Φ, Θ, and Ω. The case does not specify a concrete agent, promise, role, duty, institutional obligation, policy commitment, or stable normativity. Therefore Ψ cannot be fully activated for the hypothetical chooser. It is active only as discipline on the analysis itself: the article binds itself to the PMS entry condition, non-overclaim, and Dignity-in-Practice. An overclaim would be to infer selfhood, responsibility, identity, guilt, or mature commitment from the abstract dilemma-form.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The YAML analyses insist that later operators cannot be used cheaply. PMS has an operator order and dependency structure. Δ can open distinctions. ∇ can show directional pressure. □ can frame relevance. Λ can make absence meaningful. Α can stabilize recurrence. Ω and Θ can mark asymmetry and temporality. Φ can recontextualize. Χ can create reflective distance. Σ can integrate. Ψ can bind. But later operators depend on earlier structures and must not be invoked simply because the case feels important.

The most dangerous shortcut is **Δ/∇ → Ψ**. A difference becomes visible, pressure arises, and the analysis leaps directly to binding responsibility: “someone must own this.” In trolley dilemmas, this shortcut is tempting because harm is severe and branch outcomes are clear enough to create urgency. But PMS discipline blocks the leap. Before Ψ can be claimed, the analysis must establish frame, meaningful non-event, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, reflective distance, and integration. In this case, several of those structures are present only conceptually or weakly. Therefore Ψ cannot be treated as established.

A second shortcut is **Λ → blame**. Because omission is central, one might say that not acting is already a meaningful failure. PMS does not allow that. Λ is structured absence within a frame. The frame must specify why the absent event was expected. A non-event can be tragic, irrelevant, impossible, morally meaningful, legally meaningful, or non-capturable depending on the frame. Absence is not automatically failure.

A third shortcut is **Ω → authority or culpability**. Asymmetry is visible because one role appears able to affect others. But structural asymmetry does not decide moral blame. It also does not rank affected parties. Ω shows capacity, exposure, vulnerability, or obligation gradients; it does not produce a verdict.

A fourth shortcut is **Θ → certainty**. Foreseeable consequence trajectories are central to the dilemma, but conceptual foreseeability is not empirical probability. A thought experiment may stipulate that a branch outcome will occur. A real-world case would require evidence. PMS must not smuggle empirical certainty into the case by treating the philosophical structure as a factual model.

Σ remains conditional because integration would require more than formal elegance. It would require a discriminating synthesis of action/omission, causing/allowing, duty/consequence, harm/rescue, and responsibility frames. The YAML does not claim such a synthesis. It instead marks Σ as weak and warns that PMS coherence is insufficient by itself. A coherent derivation may still be morally inadequate, legally invalid, empirically unsupported, or philosophically under-discriminating.

Ψ remains conditional because self-binding requires a concrete self-relation or role commitment across time. The abstract chooser-position does not supply one. If a concrete variant introduced a train operator, physician, automated system, emergency official, or institutional agent with defined duties, Ψ could be recalibrated. Without that, responsibility-ascription remains a topic, not a derived PMS Responsibility_R.

Non-capture is not failure. This is a central AXIOM point. A PMS reading may reach a boundary where the case remains morally unresolved, tragically burdened, metaphysically contested, or legally under-specified. That boundary does not show that PMS failed in the sense of poor analysis. It shows that the analysis has preserved residue rather than laundering it into false closure. PMS must not become a closure machine. Its task here is to show where closure is structurally possible and where Λ-residue, rival formalisms, or non-capture must remain visible.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core analysis treats drift-patterns as possible structural risks, not as findings. This distinction is central. A trolley dilemma can invite distorted analysis because it creates high closure pressure: a difference is visible, harm is severe, and the frame appears to demand a decision. But pressure is not drift by itself. A drift-pattern would require a further structural configuration in which some operator relation becomes distorted, overextended, suppressed, or stabilized in a misleading way. The YAML therefore marks the relevant drift-patterns cautiously.

**AD_A>>E**, the pattern of excessive distance between awareness and enactment, is only a weak risk and not established. The case can certainly produce expanded awareness: the analyst can distinguish action, omission, consequence, responsibility, asymmetry, temporality, and rival frames at great length. At the same time, no concrete enactment is present. There is no agent deciding under pressure, no real intervention, no actual rescue, and no observable action sequence. This creates a risk that analysis expands while enactment remains abstract. Yet this is not a finding about anyone. The absence of concrete enactment follows from the case type. It does not show avoidance, paralysis, irresponsibility, excessive reflection, or failed maturity.

The YAML’s axis-effects discipline keeps this risk under control. The awareness axis is under-specified rather than simply over-expanded, because no actor’s actual awareness is assessed. The action axis is likewise under-specified rather than suppressed or chronically delayed, because no concrete action-context exists. This matters: a philosophical case can be abstract without thereby being a pathology of abstraction. PMS must not convert “not a concrete scene” into “failure to act.”

**AD_Sigma_low**, the pattern of fragmented integration, is also only a weak risk and not established. Trolley dilemmas invite many frames: consequence, duty, causation, omission, rescue, sacrifice, responsibility, tragedy, legal liability, and moral intuition. These frames can remain unresolved. But unresolved plurality is not automatically fragmented integration. Rival readings may be legitimate. Some may preserve moral differences that PMS Core does not settle. Others may mark genuine non-capture. Therefore the coherence axis remains under-specified rather than volatile, and the responsibility axis remains under-specified rather than inconsistent.

The central coherence risk is different: PMS may be too smooth. The case fits many operators well. Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Ω, Θ, Φ, and Χ all have clear roles. This fluency creates an under-discrimination risk. A fluent PMS formula may make materially different trolley variants look structurally identical: action versus omission, direct versus indirect harm, certainty versus probability, role duty versus accidental capacity, causal intervention versus foregone rescue, and tragedy versus calculable choice may be flattened into one operator chain. The YAML therefore marks coherence risk as high. Not because PMS fails to map the case, but because it maps it easily enough that the mapping must be held open to falsifiers and rival formalizations.

Under-specification is not failure. Non-assignment is not a low score. Weak Σ does not mean bad integration by an agent. Weak Ψ does not mean absence of responsibility in all possible versions. It means the supplied material does not justify stronger claims. That is exactly what a disciplined PMS reading should show.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant add-on is **PMS-CONFLICT**. It is used as an overlay/application profile only. It does not redefine PMS operators, does not alter PMS dependencies, does not add new base operators, and does not rewrite the Core pass. Its reduced signatures are shorthand lenses, not new proof chains. PMS.yaml remains the only PMS Base reference.

PMS Core alone makes the trolley-dilemma structure visible: difference, forced frame, omission, asymmetry, temporality, rival framing, distance, weak integration, and weak self-binding. PMS-CONFLICT is added because the case also has a specific incompatibility shape. The branches of the dilemma cannot be jointly realized. Harm allocation appears to force a non-compossible choice. Rival frames can each be locally coherent while failing to produce shared Σ. The overlay therefore makes visible a conflict-specific layer: weak dilemma-legibility as incompatibility under branch-structure. But it does not convert the case into terminal CONFLICT.

The overlay’s core distinction is between **conflict-legibility** and **terminal conflict-classification**. The trolley case is conflict-legible because the branch outcomes are structurally incompatible: one cannot simply realize all goods, avoid all harms, and preserve all frames at once. Yet the terminal PMS-CONFLICT threshold is not reached. The supplied material does not establish concrete Ψ binding, sustained Θ/Ω load, priced or asymmetric Χ, failed shared Σ under durable commitment, binding collision, institutional lock-in, or terminal reintegration exhaustion. A philosophical dilemma may remain unresolved without becoming terminal in the PMS-CONFLICT sense.

The **CONFLICT terminal core** reduced signature is therefore not applicable as an established case signature. That signature would require Σ under load with binding Ψ and priced or asymmetric distance Χ under Ω/Θ. The case lacks concrete binding and concrete distance-cost distribution. Ω and Θ are conceptually active, but Ω + Θ alone do not activate terminal CONFLICT.

The **stabilized conflict attractor** signature is weak. Trolley dilemmas recur as a philosophical problem-form. They are recognizable, repeatable, and culturally stabilized as a kind of dilemma script. In that limited sense, the overlay can read a weak Α_conflict-like pattern. But it is not a concrete conflict attractor. No repeated institutional conflict, role pattern, or behavioural loop is established. The weak attractor belongs to philosophical form, not to an actual conflict regime.

**Binding collision** is not applicable. The case raises responsibility-ascription, but it does not specify two or more concrete self-bindings that collide. There is no promise, role duty, professional mandate, law, institutional obligation, or durable commitment. Therefore PMS-CONFLICT may not claim a binding collision. To do so would smuggle in Ψ where the Core analysis has already marked Ψ as weak.

**Asymmetry lock-in** is also not applicable. The Core case has conceptual asymmetry: chooser capacity versus affected-party exposure. But asymmetry lock-in requires more. It requires that exit, reflection, delay, or withdrawal become asymmetrically priced under Ω and Θ. The case gives no concrete cost structure of that kind. Therefore PMS-CONFLICT cannot say that Χ is priced, blocked, institutionally unavailable, or coercively distributed.

**Exposure capture** is a risk, not an active state. The abstract case itself is private, descriptive, and reversible. But if the analysis is excerpted into public, institutional, legal, educational, or governance settings, a reversible structural reading could harden into pressure. The phrase “trolley dilemma” can travel easily. So can reduced signatures. The overlay therefore flags a transmission risk: interpretation may become enforcement affordance. A structural reading may be treated as a moral decision rule, a governance authority, or a maturity test. That risk belongs to transfer, not to the abstract case itself.

**Dignity collapse** is also a misuse risk, not an active finding. Trolley dilemmas tempt ranking: lives, victims, choosers, moral positions, or theories may be treated as comparable units. PMS-CONFLICT flags the risk that dignity-in-practice constraints fail under Ω/Θ pressure, especially if Χ is delegitimized and Ψ no longer binds restraint. But in the completed analysis, Dignity-in-Practice is preserved. The overlay blocks chooser-shaming, life-ranking, moral tribunal posture, and person-near verdicts.

The CONFLICT drift catalogue remains descriptive. **Σ-incompatibility clash** is a risk because rival ethical frames may each be locally coherent while no shared integration resolves them. But local coherence without shared Σ is not itself failure. It may reflect genuine moral plurality, non-capture, or tragic remainder. **Binding collision drift** is not applicable because concrete bindings are absent. **Asymmetry lock-in drift** is not applicable because concrete exit and reflection costs are absent. **Exposure capture drift** is a transfer risk. **Dignity collapse drift** is a misuse risk. None is a diagnosis, verdict, or person-type.

The overlay also supplies application-boundary scaling. In the current context, the mode is descriptive, publicness is private, and misuse gradient is low. If the same artefact were reused in public or institutional contexts, publicness and misuse-gradient classification would need to be redone. The overlay explicitly marks downstream docking as not triggered. Any move into governance, policy, legal decision, AI decision procedure, moral calculus, or institutional evaluation would be a mode shift beyond the descriptive overlay.

The final CONFLICT signature is therefore intentionally modest:

**CONFLICT_weak_dilemma_legibility = weak(CONFLICT_stabilized_pattern) + risk(DRIFT_exposure_capture) + risk(DRIFT_dignity_collapse), with CONFLICT_terminal_core, DRIFT_binding_collision, and DRIFT_asymmetry_lock_in not applicable as established case structures.**

This is the overlay’s contribution: it sharpens the distinction between abstract incompatibility and terminal conflict. It does not solve the dilemma.

The presence of the CONFLICT overlay also explains why a later MIP/AHP reading is useful. Once conflict-legibility, exposure capture, and dignity-collapse risk become visible, the question is no longer only whether the case is structurally mappable. The question also becomes whether the analysis artefact itself can travel safely: whether its terms invite scoring, public pressure, terminality inflation, or person-near use. PMS-CONFLICT therefore does not authorize MIP or AHP as stronger truth layers; it creates a reason to check the analysis for transmission discipline.

### Layer interaction

The Core reading remains PMS Core. Its operators, dependencies, derived structures, and boundaries are not redefined by any later layer. PMS-CONFLICT sharpens one aspect of the case: the incompatibility structure of branch outcomes and the risk of weak conflict-legibility being mistaken for terminal conflict. It does not add PMS operators. It does not change Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, or Ψ. It supplies reduced signatures and drift risks as overlay handles.

MIP then changes the object of attention. It does not ask whether the trolley dilemma is solved. It asks whether the analysis artefact is handled maturely: whether scope is clear, whether score hygiene is preserved, whether D is protected, whether IA risk is visible, whether transmission is bounded, and whether responsibility is not over-assigned. MIP is therefore downstream governance and guardrail reading, not PMS Core derivation.

At the MIP layer, the case remains an abstract dilemma-form. No A-score or M-score is assigned to a concrete actor. The A-reading concerns the analysis artefact’s awareness: its ability to distinguish material from inference, PMS Core from overlay, MIP from PMS, and private reflection from public transmission. The C-reading concerns artefact-level coherence: whether the layered analysis keeps its roles clear. The R-reading is cautious: responsibility-ascription is visible in the case, but actor responsibility cannot be assigned without knowledge, power, predictability, real alternatives, and role-specific context. The P-reading concerns the real power of the analyst and document: the power to frame, overclaim, transmit, or protect boundaries.

The MIP IA-box result is **IA_risk**, not a verdict. It marks asymmetry as visible but only partially transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible in the abstract frame. This is not a finding that anyone acted with inadmissible asymmetry. It is a guardrail signal: if the case becomes concrete, public, institutional, legal, or person-near, the IA-box must be redone. The D-module remains off. Dignity remains a protection signal only: D0 is untouched, no D-profile is produced, and no dignity score is assigned.

AHP then changes the object again. It does not evaluate the case or the actor. It evaluates the MIP output as an artefact: where is the language vulnerable, where could it be misread, what needs hardening before reuse? AHP is second-order hardening only. It cannot rescore A or M, cannot change IA, cannot activate D, and cannot make the artefact more transmittable by itself.

The AHP Precision Heuristic is high because the MIP artefact satisfies term definition, scope boundaries, conditions and thresholds, evidence linking, reversibility clause, and language hygiene. But high precision is not validation. AHP’s attack points identify vulnerabilities in the analysis artefact: “structurally transmittable” could be overread outside the working-note boundary; numeric-looking analytic band language could be mistaken for a hidden A-score; D-language could be excerpted as a dignity judgment; qualitative IA-box status could be carried into concrete contexts; layered PMS/CONFLICT/MIP coherence could look like framework confirmation; private publicness could collapse under reuse; and density could hide non-use boundaries. AHP’s hardening backlog addresses these vulnerabilities without adding new case claims.

The layers interact by increasing boundary visibility. PMS Core maps the structural dilemma. PMS-CONFLICT prevents weak incompatibility from being confused with terminal conflict. MIP prevents structural analysis from becoming person evaluation or governance authority. AHP prevents a careful MIP output from being misread through excerpting, density, or publicness collapse.

The add-on must not redefine the Core. PMS-CONFLICT language such as conflict attractor, binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, and dignity collapse remains non-operator overlay language. It may sharpen risk; it may not replace PMS dependencies. MIP language such as A-band, M-band, IA-box, and D-module remains MIP-layer language. It may review artefact handling; it may not feed back into PMS as if maturity were a PMS operator. AHP language such as Precision Heuristic, attack points, and hardening backlog remains artefact-hardening language. It may improve the next version of the analysis; it may not create new case claims.

Overlay language could overreach in several ways. “Conflict” could be mistaken for terminal conflict. “Responsibility” could be mistaken for blame. “IA-risk” could be mistaken for a verdict. “Dignity protection” could be mistaken for a D-profile. “High precision” could be mistaken for validation. “Structurally transmittable” could be mistaken for public readiness. The completed YAMLs explicitly block these overreaches.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. PMS Core leaves moral resolution open. PMS-CONFLICT leaves terminality unactivated. MIP leaves actor scores unassigned. AHP leaves hardening actions as next-iteration tasks, not fixes that settle the case. The layered result is therefore not closure-by-accumulation. It is disciplined non-closure with increasing clarity about what may and may not be claimed.

### Example 1 — The switch as frame-dependent action

A bounded version of the trolley case can be stated without deciding it. A track branches ahead. On one branch, several persons are exposed to harm; on another branch, one person is exposed to harm. A chooser-position is imagined near a switch. Pulling the switch would redirect the trolley. Not pulling the switch would leave the trolley on its current path. The vignette is deliberately abstract: no jurisdiction, no role duty, no actual probabilities, no institutional mandate, no testimony, no emergency protocol, and no identifiable persons are supplied.

This example demonstrates why the frame matters before any responsibility claim can be made. In one frame, the switch appears as an available action that changes the outcome. In another frame, pulling the switch may be described as direct intervention into harm. In another, not pulling the switch may be described as omission. In yet another, the whole setup may be rejected as an artificial forced-choice frame that excludes other possible responses. The point is not to decide between these readings. The point is to show that the case does not become structurally legible without a declared frame.

The PMS Core hooks are immediate. **Δ** marks the relevant distinctions: pulling or not pulling, redirected harm or non-redirected harm, harmed or saved, intervention or non-intervention. **∇** appears as the pressure to choose, prevent, justify, or assign responsibility. **□** is the forced-choice frame that makes the switch meaningful in the first place. **Λ** appears because not pulling can become a meaningful non-event only if the frame treats intervention as expected, available, or relevant. **Ω** appears as a conceptual asymmetry between a chooser-position with possible consequence-shaping capacity and affected parties exposed to harm. **Θ** appears in the sequence from possible action or omission to foreseeable consequence. **Φ** is active because the same switch can be recontextualized as rescue, sacrifice, causal redirection, omission, non-interference, or tragic exposure. **Χ** is required so that the analysis does not become a verdict. **Σ** remains weak because no integrated resolution is supplied. **Ψ** remains weak because no concrete self-binding role, duty, promise, or institutional obligation is specified.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay is relevant only weakly. The vignette is conflict-legible because the branches cannot both be realized: the imagined outcome-space contains incompatible harm distributions. But this does not activate terminal CONFLICT. No binding collision is established. No concrete role commitment collides with another. No priced or asymmetric distance structure is supplied. No terminal Σ-failure under sustained Θ/Ω/Ψ load is demonstrated. The overlay therefore helps name weak incompatibility without escalating it into a terminal conflict regime.

MIP remains artefact-bound here. No A-score or M-score is assigned to the chooser-position. The vignette lacks the factors required for responsibility scoring: concrete knowledge, power, predictability, real alternatives, and role-defined obligation. The IA-box would remain a risk check only: asymmetry is visible, but transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility are only partially available in the abstract frame. The D-module remains off. Dignity functions only as a guardrail: the example must not rank lives or shame the imagined chooser.

The forbidden claims are therefore clear. This example does not show that pulling is right, that not pulling is wrong, that the chooser is guilty, that one person counts less than several, that omission is equivalent to action, or that PMS decides the case. It does not establish legal liability, moral desert, clinical meaning, empirical probability, or implementation validity.

The non-capture note is equally important. A deontological reading may preserve the difference between doing and allowing. A consequentialist reading may preserve outcome comparison. A tragedy reading may preserve the remainder that no branch is clean. A refusal of the switch-frame may preserve the concern that the setup is morally artificial. PMS can map these frame pressures, but it does not erase their rivalry.

The article takeaway is that the switch example shows that trolley dilemmas are not simply “choice problems.” They are frame-dependent structures in which action, omission, harm, and responsibility become legible only under declared boundaries. PMS clarifies the frame-pressure; it does not convert the switch into a moral verdict.

### Example 2 — The operator, the bystander, and the missing role

Consider two versions of the same abstract trolley structure. In the first, the chooser-position is a random bystander near a switch. In the second, the chooser-position is a trained railway operator working inside an explicit safety role. The visible branch structure may look similar: action or omission appears to affect who is harmed and who is saved. Yet the responsibility structure cannot be assumed to be the same.

This example demonstrates why later PMS operators cannot be used cheaply. In both versions, **Δ** distinguishes action and omission, harmed and saved, intervention and non-intervention. **∇** creates pressure to respond. **□** frames the situation as one in which the switch or control mechanism matters. **Λ** can mark non-intervention as a possible non-event. **Ω** marks asymmetry between a decision-position and exposed parties. **Θ** marks the temporal sequence from response to consequence. But the operator version may introduce further structures that the bystander version lacks: role duty, training, mandate, institutional expectation, prior commitment, safety protocol, and documented responsibility channels. Those would matter for **Σ** and **Ψ**.

In the bystander version, Ψ remains weak. The bystander may have capacity, but capacity alone is not self-binding. The case does not yet show a role-owned obligation, a promise, a professional duty, or a stable commitment across time. Responsibility-ascription is structurally salient, but PMS Responsibility_R is not fully derived. In the operator version, Ψ might become stronger if the role includes explicit duty, accepted responsibility, procedural knowledge, and temporal obligation. Even then, the analysis would need concrete facts: what the operator knew, what the protocol required, what alternatives existed, what the timing allowed, and whether action or non-action was actually available.

This is the key dependency lesson. PMS does not permit a leap from “someone could affect the outcome” to “someone is fully responsible.” That would be a Δ/∇/Ω-to-Ψ shortcut. It bypasses frame declaration, omission calibration, temporality, recontextualization, reflective distance, and integration. A responsible PMS reading must ask what kind of role the chooser-position occupies and whether any self-binding structure is actually present.

The PMS-CONFLICT overlay also changes with the role structure. In the bystander version, conflict-legibility remains weak: there are incompatible branch outcomes, but no binding collision. In the operator version, a binding collision might become possible if concrete duties conflict: for example, a duty to minimize system harm collides with a duty not to redirect harm onto a protected zone. But that cannot be asserted from the abstract vignette alone. The overlay can say what would need to be shown; it cannot supply the missing role data.

MIP’s relevance becomes clearer here. It would still not score a person from the abstract example. But it can explain why high M is blocked in the bystander case unless knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and role obligations are established. In the operator case, MIP could become more relevant, but only after concrete scene material is supplied. The M-score would still describe shared structural responsibility, not guilt. The IA-box would need renewed calibration because institutional asymmetry, duty, and contest capacity may be stronger. The D-module would still not activate automatically; Dignity-in-Practice would remain a guardrail against shaming or irreversible labeling.

AHP would treat this example as a hardening reminder. If the article uses “chooser” too generally, readers may miss the role difference. AHP would likely flag a precision issue: the analysis should not let a bystander and an operator collapse into the same responsibility formula. The hardening action would be to specify that role data can strengthen Ψ only when supplied, and that abstract capacity is not enough.

The forbidden claims are specific. This example does not say the operator is liable, the bystander is innocent, the professional role always creates responsibility, or the absence of role always removes responsibility. It does not decide whether action is morally required. It does not rank the affected parties. It does not diagnose hesitation or refusal.

The non-capture note is that legal, professional, and institutional readings may preserve distinctions PMS Core cannot fill without data. A legal doctrine might treat duties differently from moral philosophy. A professional safety frame might specify obligations not visible in the generic trolley version. A tragedy reading might still say that even role-bound action leaves moral residue.

The article takeaway is that the bystander/operator contrast shows why PMS dependency discipline matters. Later structures such as Responsibility_R, Action_E, and Ψ cannot be activated merely because harm is foreseeable. Role, duty, knowledge, alternatives, and temporal commitment must be calibrated before stronger claims become legitimate.

### Example 3 — When a classroom example becomes a public rule

Imagine that a teacher uses the trolley dilemma in a private seminar. The purpose is reflection: students compare frames, examine action and omission, and discuss why responsibility-ascription remains difficult. In that setting, the example remains reversible. Interpretations can be revised. No real person is scored. No institution acts on the result. The analysis stays within a low-publicness, descriptive setting.

Now imagine that a simplified excerpt travels outside the seminar: “PMS-CONFLICT shows trolley cases are conflict structures; MIP confirms the mature handling is to preserve the greater number; AHP gives the analysis high precision.” This sentence is wrong, but it is a plausible misuse. It converts weak conflict-legibility into a decision rule, MIP guardrail review into moral maturity judgment, and AHP precision into validation. It turns a bounded analysis into public pressure.

This example demonstrates why transmission is part of the case architecture. PMS Core may be disciplined in the original article, and the CONFLICT overlay may be carefully bounded, but public reuse changes the risk environment. The same wording that is safe inside a private working note can become dangerous when detached from its boundaries. Publicness can harden interpretation into authority. A reversible structural reading can become an enforcement affordance.

The PMS Core hooks remain the same, but their misuse changes. **□** is silently changed: the frame shifts from philosophical reflection to public rule-like statement. **Φ** occurs without being declared. **Λ** is suppressed because the excerpt hides non-capture, rival readings, and weak Σ/Ψ. **Ω** increases because the speaker, institution, platform, or publication may gain authority over how the dilemma is received. **Θ** matters because the excerpt can persist as a record, teaching note, policy prompt, or public claim. **Χ** is weakened if readers are not invited to preserve distance and reversibility. **Σ** is falsely inflated if the excerpt implies a settled integration. **Ψ** is falsely implied if the excerpt makes the analysis sound binding.

PMS-CONFLICT is especially relevant here. The original overlay marks **exposure capture** and **dignity collapse** only as risks. In the public excerpt, those risks become more salient. Exposure capture occurs when a scene-bound interpretation hardens into pressure. Dignity-collapse risk appears if the excerpt encourages ranking lives, shaming students who resist the calculus, or presenting disagreement as immature. Yet even here the article should be careful: the example demonstrates transmission risk, not an established drift finding unless a concrete public event is documented.

MIP clarifies why the public move is not harmless. The original MIP status is a private structural working note. It does not assign A-score or M-score to a chooser. It does not activate the D-module. It does not authorize public D-readings. If the analysis is reused in a public or institutional setting, publicness must be recalibrated. The IA-box must be redone. Transmission status must be reviewed. A phrase like “structurally transmittable” cannot be detached from “only as a bounded working note.”

AHP is directly relevant. It already flags the main attack point: transmission ambiguity. “High Precision Heuristic” means the MIP output is well-structured as an analysis artefact. It does not mean the case is solved, the result is public-ready, or the analysis can be operationalized. In this example, AHP does not add a new moral claim about the seminar, the teacher, the students, or the trolley dilemma. It identifies a weakness in the artefact’s possible travel path: a careful private formulation can become misleading when compressed into a public rule-like sentence. AHP’s recommended hardening banner fits here: working note only; no rescore; no D-module activation; no verdict; no operational use.

The forbidden claims are therefore explicit. This example does not show that public teaching is wrong, that trolley dilemmas should not be used pedagogically, that one moral theory is immature, or that public discussion is a drift by itself. It does not claim empirical harm from classroom use. It does not say that AHP detects wrongdoing. It only shows how a bounded analysis can become unsafe when excerpted without its frame.

The non-capture note is that pedagogy, public philosophy, and governance each have their own norms. PMS, CONFLICT, MIP, and AHP can identify transmission boundaries, but they do not replace educational judgment, democratic deliberation, legal standards, or institutional review. A classroom may legitimately use trolley dilemmas to teach moral conflict, but the article’s layered analysis must not be converted into a public decision procedure.

The article takeaway is that the classroom-to-public-rule example shows why AHP belongs at the end of the stack. The danger is not only analytical error inside the case; it is also transmission error after the case. A careful structural reading can still be misused if its boundaries do not travel with it.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The PMS and overlay analyses preserve multiple rival readings. These are not defects. They show where different formalisms protect different aspects of the case.

A **consequentialist reading** preserves the importance of outcomes. It treats branch consequences as central: who is harmed, who is saved, how many are affected, and what total harm results. PMS can map this as Δ, ∇, □, Ω, and Θ, but PMS does not prove that outcome comparison is the morally decisive frame.

A **deontological reading** preserves the distinction between doing and permitting, using and allowing, direct harm and indirect consequence, prohibition and calculation. PMS can represent this as frame difference and Λ-sensitive omission structure, but it does not eliminate the deontological claim that some actions may be impermissible even when outcomes improve.

An **action/omission-asymmetry reading** preserves the possibility that action and omission are structurally and morally different. PMS can make omission legible through Λ, but it does not decide whether omission is equivalent to action, less culpable, more culpable, or differently framed.

A **causal-responsibility reading** preserves the importance of causal intervention. It asks whether the chooser redirects an existing threat, creates a new threat, allows an existing process, or fails to prevent harm. PMS can map temporal and causal branch structure through Θ and Δ, but actual causation standards require more than the abstract material supplies.

A **role-duty reading** preserves institutional and practical context. It asks who the chooser is: bystander, operator, doctor, official, engineer, parent, judge, AI system, or institution. PMS would need □, Ω, Θ, Σ, and Ψ recalibrated for such a case. Without role data, role-duty responsibility remains non-captured.

A **legal reading** preserves jurisdiction, evidence, liability, justification, excuse, and procedural standards. PMS can show why legal responsibility would require frame and evidence, but it cannot supply the legal doctrine.

A **phenomenological reading** preserves lived burden, fear, moral injury, hesitation, horror, and subjective experience. PMS does not claim phenomenology. It can map structural pressure without representing what it feels like to face the situation.

A **tragedy reading** preserves the possibility that the dilemma is not solvable in the normal sense. It may show unavoidable loss, residue, or moral remainder even after disciplined analysis. PMS can preserve Λ-residue and weak Σ, and PMS-CONFLICT can mark weak tragedy-legibility, but neither may inflate tragedy into terminal conflict without the required thresholds.

An **anti-capture reading** preserves the right to reject the trolley frame itself. The forced-choice structure may be artificial, manipulative, too clean, or ethically misleading. PMS must not redescribe such refusal as drift by default. A refusal may preserve something the frame excludes.

A **non-PMS reading** preserves methodological rivalry. Another grammar may discriminate morally relevant features more sharply than PMS: intention, double effect, rights, duties, consent, agency, sacredness, distributive justice, or legal categories. PMS translation is therefore not a victory condition over rival frameworks.

The examples reinforce these non-capture zones. The switch example shows that the frame is not self-validating. The bystander/operator contrast shows that responsibility cannot be derived from capacity alone. The classroom-to-public-rule example shows that even a disciplined analysis can become unsafe when detached from its boundaries. In each case, the example demonstrates the analysis; it does not replace it with evidence.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would weaken if there were no genuine forced choice. If the case did not involve differentiated branches, Δ would lose its entry point. If action or omission did not affect harm or rescue, ∇, Θ, and Ω would weaken. If no frame made omission meaningful, Λ would weaken. If responsibility-ascription were not actually at issue, Responsibility_R would remain irrelevant rather than weak. If there were no asymmetry of capacity, exposure, duty, or vulnerability, Ω would need removal or major reduction.

The PMS reading would also weaken if the case were purely semantic and not praxeological. If “trolley dilemmas” were being discussed only as a term in language use, the praxis structure would need reframing. If the case supplied a concrete legal standard that overrode the abstract structure, the legal frame would become primary. If empirical uncertainty changed the consequence structure, Θ and foreseeability would require recalibration. If a rival formalization discriminated the case more sharply, PMS would need to acknowledge its own under-discrimination.

The add-on reading would weaken if incompatibility were not structurally central. If all goods could be jointly realized, or if the apparent branches were false alternatives, PMS-CONFLICT would lose relevance. If no repeated dilemma-form were present, the weak conflict attractor would disappear. If the case introduced concrete bindings, priced distance, or institutional lock-in, the overlay might become stronger—but it would also require new calibration. Conversely, without such data, terminal CONFLICT remains invalid.

The reading becomes under-discriminating if materially different trolley variants all receive the same formula. A case involving certainty differs from one involving probability. A bystander differs from an official. A switch differs from pushing. Preventing harm differs from redirecting harm. A legal duty differs from accidental opportunity. A human emergency differs from an AI optimization scenario. If PMS ignores these differences, it has mapped too broadly.

The reading becomes over-assimilative if rival disagreement is absorbed as PMS drift. A deontological objection is not automatically failed integration. A refusal of the trolley frame is not automatically avoidance. A tragic reading is not automatically low Σ. A legal reading is not automatically a narrower PMS frame. Non-capture must remain possible.

Stronger claims would require concrete data. For PMS Core, stronger Responsibility_R would require a concrete agent or role, a self-binding commitment, an obligation frame, temporal sequence, asymmetry calibration, and recontextualization of responsibility. Stronger Action_E would require an actual enactment. Stronger Ω would require capacity, vulnerability, duty, or power data. Stronger Θ would require timing, probability, sequence, and irreversibility data. Stronger Λ would require a specified expected event and the frame that makes its absence meaningful.

For PMS-CONFLICT, stronger claims would require sustained incompatibility, concrete binding collision, priced or asymmetric Χ, repeated conflict pattern, exposure capture, or terminal Σ-failure under Θ/Ω/Ψ load. None is supplied.

For MIP, stronger A or M claims would require concrete roles, knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, serious integration attempts, and real consequences. A full D-module would require a concrete role/context/time window and safe communication conditions. Public transmission would require renewed publicness and misuse-gradient review.

For AHP, stronger hardening would require applying the recommended fixes to the artefact and then rechecking. AHP does not itself make the analysis public-ready. It only identifies attack points and hardening tasks.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. Empirical, comparative, computational, legal, clinical, institutional, and applied validation are all absent. If someone wanted to operationalize the case, they would need observable indicators, decision thresholds, validation criteria, accountability structures, external review, and explicit prohibitions against person-ranking and automated moral judgment. The completed analyses refuse operational authority.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. No person, group, philosopher, agent, reader, or moral position may be labelled as mature, immature, pathological, avoidant, deficient, cruel, courageous, integrated, fragmented, or irresponsible on the basis of this analysis.

It must not be used for person ranking. The analysis does not rank lives, victims, choosers, moral theories, philosophical camps, or affected parties. Dignity-in-Practice blocks exactly this move. D0 remains untouched and non-rankable in the MIP layer.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusions. The case supplies no jurisdiction, duty, evidence, causation standard, liability doctrine, excuse, justification, or burden of proof. PMS responsibility pressure is not legal responsibility.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. The analysis does not settle the value of persons, commensurability of lives, moral absolutes, consciousness, dignity, or ultimate responsibility.

It must not be used as empirical proof. It contains no data about actual decisions, outcomes, intuitions, psychological mechanisms, or probabilities.

It must not be used for implementation validation. It is not a decision rule, AI policy, triage system, governance protocol, emergency algorithm, or optimization formula. Formal YAML structure does not implement praxis.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. A successful mapping does not prove PMS completeness, correctness, uniqueness, or superiority.

It must not treat PMS-CONFLICT as a new operator system. Conflict attractor, binding collision, asymmetry lock-in, exposure capture, and dignity collapse are non-operator overlay constructs. Reduced signatures are shorthand only.

It must not treat drift as verdict. AD_A>>E, AD_Sigma_low, exposure capture, dignity collapse, Σ-incompatibility clash, and other drift patterns remain risks unless concrete evidence establishes them. Risk is not finding.

It must not use MIP as a person-evaluation layer. A-band, M-band, IA-box, and D-module language must remain artefact-bound here. The abstract chooser is not scored.

It must not use AHP as a validation stamp. High PH means the analysis artefact is precise; it does not mean the case is solved.

It must not close by authority. The article’s layered structure is not a ladder to final truth. Each layer increases discipline, not entitlement to closure.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is a disciplined, layered non-closure. PMS Core shows that trolley dilemmas are structurally readable as forced-choice configurations involving differentiated branches, directional pressure, a frame, meaningful non-events, conceptual asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, and the need for reflective distance. It also shows that Σ and Ψ remain weak because no concrete integration or self-binding structure is supplied. Responsibility is visible as a pressure in the case, but not fully derived as PMS Responsibility_R. Action is visible in ordinary-language terms, but not established as PMS Action_E. SELF_FIXPOINT is analytic-only and cannot be applied to an abstract chooser-position.

PMS-CONFLICT then adds a narrower clarification: the case is weakly conflict-legible because its branch outcomes are incompatible, but it is not terminally conflict-classified. The overlay makes visible weak stabilized dilemma-patterning and transfer risks such as exposure capture and dignity collapse, while leaving CONFLICT terminal core, binding collision, and asymmetry lock-in not applicable as established structures. This is not a reduction of trolley dilemmas to “conflict.” It is a boundary-sensitive overlay reading of one structural aspect: non-joint realizability under harm-branch pressure.

MIP adds guardrail discipline around the analysis artefact. It confirms that the case is not a person profile and that no concrete actor receives A-score, M-score, IA verdict, or D-profile. The MIP layer identifies conceptual IA risk, preserves the D-module as off, and treats Dignity-in-Practice as a protection signal rather than a dignity judgment. It shifts responsibility to where responsibility is actually available in this article: the handling of the analysis, the clarity of its boundaries, and the prevention of misuse in transmission.

AHP adds second-order hardening. It finds the MIP output precise as an artefact while identifying transmission vulnerabilities: “structurally transmittable” could be overread; numeric-looking analytic band language could look like scoring; D-language could be excerpted as dignity judgment; IA partiality could be carried into concrete contexts; layered coherence could be mistaken for validation; private publicness could collapse under reuse; and density could hide the non-use boundaries. AHP therefore contributes hardening tasks, not new case claims.

The three examples contribute by making these abstract boundaries readable. The switch example shows why the frame is not self-validating and why action, omission, and responsibility only become legible under declared boundaries. The bystander/operator example shows why role, duty, knowledge, alternatives, and self-binding matter before stronger responsibility claims can be made. The classroom-to-public-rule example shows why transmission is part of the case architecture: even a careful structural reading can become unsafe if excerpted as a moral rule, maturity standard, or validation stamp.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is claimed. The article does not decide whether one should pull the switch. It does not decide whether action and omission are morally equivalent. It does not decide whether lives are commensurable. It does not assign guilt, liability, diagnosis, maturity, or dignity status. It does not validate PMS, PMS-CONFLICT, MIP, or AHP. It does not produce an implementation standard. It does not claim that rival ethical theories are merely incomplete PMS readings. It does not claim that tragic residue is solved by structural legibility.

Boundary discipline is therefore the case’s main methodological result. Coherence is not proof. Risk is not finding. Non-assignment is not low score. Non-capture is not failure. Resistance, refusal, non-fit, disagreement, rupture, or absence are not automatically drift. Dignity-in-Practice remains praxeological rather than ontological. Add-on terms remain overlay handles rather than PMS operators. AHP hardens the analysis without rescoring it.

The final methodological takeaway is that trolley dilemmas are useful for AXIOM precisely because they tempt premature closure. A good analysis should not merely solve that temptation in one direction. It should make the temptation visible, show which structures generate it, identify where stronger claims would require more data or a different frame, and preserve the residue that disciplined modelling cannot erase. In this case, PMS and its downstream layers do not end the trolley problem. They show how to read it without turning structural legibility into a verdict.

That restraint is not a weakness of the article. It is the result the stack is meant to make visible. The dilemma remains philosophically alive; the analysis becomes more disciplined about what may be said, what may not be said, and what must remain open.
