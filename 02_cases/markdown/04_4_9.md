This case treats freedom in a non-metaphysical sense. Its object is not an individual person, not an institutional decision, not a legal case, and not an empirical event. The case asks how “freedom” can be rendered structurally when it is not treated as ultimate free will, metaphysical self-causation, indeterminism, or pure independence. The analysis therefore remains conceptual: it maps freedom as a praxeological configuration involving differentiated alternatives, directedness, situated frames, constraint, temporality, asymmetry, reflective distance, possible integration, and conditional self-binding.

The central PMS reading is that non-metaphysical freedom is not reducible to mere option-space, arbitrariness, randomness, voluntariness, autonomy, independence, or responsibility. It becomes structurally legible only when alternatives are differentiated inside a frame, when action-pressure or directedness is present, when constraints and asymmetries shape the real action-space, when temporality affects commitment, delay, reversibility, and lock-in, and when reflective distance prevents the analysis from collapsing into either metaphysical speculation or tribunal judgment.

No add-on is used because the case does not require a domain-specific overlay lens. PMS Core already supplies the relevant operator grammar: Δ for differentiation, ∇ for directedness, □ for situated framing, Λ for hidden or absent options, Α for stabilized constraint patterns, Ω for asymmetry, Θ for temporal extension, Φ for frame-sensitive reinterpretation, Χ for reflective distance, Σ for integration, and Ψ for responsibility-bearing or commitment-bearing self-binding. MIP, AHP, and QC are not activated because no downstream governance artefact, policy use, claim-hardening requirement, or quality-control audit context is supplied.

### Why this case matters

Freedom is one of the classical philosophical closure-demands because it invites a strong and often premature settlement. The demand usually appears in one of several compressed forms: either agents are free or they are not; freedom either requires metaphysical free will or collapses into determinism; choice either proves responsibility or is dissolved by constraint; autonomy either grounds freedom or becomes another name for disciplined self-control. Each version pressures the analysis toward closure before the operative frame has been declared.

The case belongs in AXIOM because it is structurally difficult in exactly the way PMS-AXIOM is designed to handle. “Freedom” is not one problem. It is a cluster of overlapping closure-demands: freedom as real alternative, freedom as lack of coercion, freedom as reflective distance, freedom as self-command, freedom as responsibility, freedom as institutional permission, freedom as autonomy, freedom as reasons-responsiveness, freedom as exit-capacity, and freedom as metaphysical independence. These are not identical structures. Treating them as one produces under-discrimination; treating them as wholly separate produces fragmentation. PMS Core offers a way to keep them distinct without forcing metaphysical closure.

The philosophical pressure is especially strong because freedom often functions as a bridge-term. It links action to responsibility, responsibility to blame, blame to legitimacy, legitimacy to institutions, and institutions to enforcement. That bridge is dangerous if not controlled. A claim that someone “was free” can become a blame claim. A claim that someone “was not free” can become a standing-stripping claim. A claim that a choice existed can hide asymmetry, coercion, dependency, cost, or lock-in. A claim that constraint existed can erase agency, commitment, or the possibility of differentiated response.

This is why the case is not solved metaphysically. PMS does not decide whether ultimate free will exists, whether determinism is true, or whether indeterminism would help. The case is also not solved empirically, because no observations, experiments, behavioral sequence, capacity assessment, or institutional record are supplied. It is not solved clinically or diagnostically, because PMS does not infer mental states, traits, pathology, maturity, competence, or incapacity. It is not solved legally or forensically, because no jurisdiction, standard of liability, evidentiary threshold, coercion test, consent doctrine, or capacity standard is present. It is not solved by implementation either; the YAML representation does not implement freedom in an agent architecture.

The value of the analysis is therefore cartographic. It shows how a freedom claim must be decomposed before it can be used responsibly. Which frame is active? What counts as an alternative? What constraints matter? Which asymmetries shape the option-space? Over what time horizon is freedom being assessed? Is the issue immediate action, delayed commitment, responsibility, exit, revision, coercion, or self-binding? Where are the hidden or missing options? Where is the demand for closure stronger than the available warrant?

Freedom matters here because it is a test of PMS discipline. If PMS turned freedom into a smooth formula and then treated that formula as a solution, the analysis would fail. The case only works if PMS preserves non-capture, rival readings, reversibility, and the difference between structural coherence and sufficient explanation.

It also does not deny them as metaphysical theses. It simply does not use them. In PMS terms, the relevant object is not metaphysical freedom but praxeological freedom: differentiated, framed, constrained, temporally extended, asymmetry-sensitive action-space.

The implementation boundary blocks a different overclaim. YAML form does not implement praxis. Finally, the PMS Base validation boundary remains closed. A coherent PMS reading of freedom does not validate PMS Base, prove the operator order, prove completeness, or defeat rival theories. The analysis shows that PMS can render this case structurally under the declared scope. It does not show that PMS is the only or final grammar for freedom.

### Scene and frame structure

The case has no concrete scene. This matters because PMS is praxeological: strong claims normally require a bounded scene in which roles, frames, expectations, asymmetries, temporal sequences, and costs can be inspected. Here, the “scene” is conceptual. The analysis therefore operates at the level of a philosophical frame: non-metaphysical freedom as situated action-space under constraint.

The operative frame, □, is the frame of structural-praxeological freedom. Inside the frame are alternatives, constraints, directed impulses, reason-responsive orientation, reflective distance, temporal commitment, capacity to differentiate options, self-binding under conditions, responsibility without metaphysical free will, and freedom under asymmetry. Also inside the frame are adjacent concepts that must not be collapsed: arbitrariness, randomness, option-space, autonomy, independence, voluntariness, agency, coercion, constraint, determinism, indeterminism, necessity, responsibility, and metaphysical free will.

Outside the frame are metaphysical proofs of free will, claims about ultimate causal independence, phenomenological reports of felt freedom, clinical judgments about capacity, legal findings of coercion or consent, and person-level moral verdicts. Also outside the frame is any attempt to use PMS as a tribunal for deciding who is truly free or unfree.

The protected constraints are the PMS entry discipline: distance, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. Freedom language is especially susceptible to coercive use. “You were free” may become a tool for blame. “You were not free” may become a tool for paternalism or standing-stripping. “You had options” may hide asymmetry and cost. “You were constrained” may erase agency. The frame must therefore keep freedom claims bounded, revisable, and non-ranking.

Temporality, Θ, is conceptually central. Freedom is not only the number of options at an instant. It includes delay, commitment, path-dependence, exit cost, action horizon, self-binding, and the possibility that a local limitation enables long-term agency. A promise reduces immediate option-space but may express responsibility-bearing freedom. A constraint may remove one action while preserving or enabling another. A temporary permission may not constitute durable freedom. A nominal option may be practically unavailable if it carries long-term retaliation, dependency, or irreversible cost.

Asymmetry, Ω, is also central, but it must be handled carefully. The case explicitly concerns freedom under asymmetry: unequal power, exposure, capacity, obligation, vulnerability, dependency, and coercive pressure. Yet because no concrete scene is supplied, Ω is case-explicit as a conceptual concern, not scene-evidenced as a specific imbalance. Full Ω analysis would require knowing who can impose constraints, who bears costs, who can contest, who controls exit, and who benefits from the framing.

The reversibility window remains open throughout. Since there is no concrete scene, no binding conclusion can be fixed. Any structural formula remains revisable if the frame changes, if the case is applied to a concrete situation, if rival theories discriminate better, or if hidden constraints become visible.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading begins with **Δ — Difference**. Freedom is only legible if differences can be maintained: free versus unfree, option versus constraint, agency versus randomness, voluntariness versus coercion, autonomy versus independence, responsibility versus blame, determinism versus metaphysical free will. Δ is therefore active and foundational. But Δ alone does not produce freedom. A list of distinctions is not yet an action-space. Overclaim would occur if the analysis treated conceptual differentiation itself as freedom.

**∇ — Impulse** is active because freedom is not merely a set of abstract alternatives. It involves directedness: urge, motivation, reason-responsive orientation, action pressure, or impulse requiring inhibition. The case explicitly includes inhibition of immediate impulse and responsiveness to reasons. ∇ helps distinguish living agency from static option-space. Yet ∇ must not be psychologized. PMS does not infer desire, intention, diagnosis, or inner experience. Overclaim would occur if structural directedness were treated as subjective will or metaphysical agency.

**□ — Frame** is active and decisive. Freedom is frame-bound. Legal freedom, relational freedom, technical freedom, institutional discretion, political liberty, moral autonomy, and metaphysical free will do not share the same criteria. A person may be legally permitted but materially constrained; institutionally authorized but socially dependent; locally unconstrained but temporally locked in. □ determines what counts as an option, a constraint, an exit, a commitment, or coercion. Overclaim occurs when “free” is used globally without declaring the frame.

**Λ — Non-Event** is relevant but weak or conditional. In this case, Λ appears as missing option, unavailable exit, withheld impulse, absent permission, absent clarification, delayed revision channel, or foregone alternative. Freedom often depends on what does not occur: the expected opportunity never arrives, the appeal path is missing, the viable alternative is absent, the hidden constraint is not disclosed. Yet because the case is not a concrete scene, no specific expected event is shown to have failed. Λ must remain calibrated. Overclaim would occur if every absence, non-fit, disagreement, or rival theory were automatically converted into a PMS residue.

**Α — Attractor** is weak-to-active conceptually. Freedom is shaped by stabilized patterns: habits, compliance routines, learned navigation scripts, default choices, institutional permissions, contracts, dependencies, and recurrent constraint structures. These can make some options practically salient and others invisible. But no actual recurrence is supplied. Α therefore cannot be treated as evidenced pattern formation. Overclaim would occur if conceptual possibility were mistaken for observed stabilization.

**Ω — Asymmetry** is conceptually central. Freedom under asymmetry is one of the case’s explicit concerns. Real option-space is shaped by power, capacity, exposure, dependency, vulnerability, obligation, and contestability. Nominal options may be hollow under asymmetry. Permission may be meaningless if exit is too costly or contest is impossible. However, because Α is weak in the concrete sense, Ω’s formal dependency through stabilized patterning remains weak or conceptual rather than scene-evidenced. Overclaim would occur if asymmetry were treated automatically as domination, blame, coercion, immaturity, pathology, or legal wrongdoing.

**Θ — Temporality** is central. Freedom unfolds across time: immediate impulse may be inhibited for longer-term agency; commitment may bind present choice; delay may preserve or destroy freedom; exit costs accumulate; responsibility extends beyond a single moment. The case strongly warrants Θ conceptually. Still, without a concrete sequence, promise, omission, or enacted trajectory, Θ is a structural requirement of the concept, not an empirical case finding. Overclaim would occur if PMS inferred actual persistence, identity continuity, legal accountability, or character stability.

**Φ — Recontextualization** is active because freedom changes meaning when moved between frames. A choice can be reframed as consent, coercion, compliance, self-binding, autonomy, risk management, or liability. Recontextualization is necessary to distinguish freedom from metaphysical free will and from mere option-space. It also helps preserve rival readings. But Φ is dangerous if overused. PMS must not absorb every disagreement or non-fit as “recontextualization residue.” Rival theories may remain genuinely external or better suited for a particular purpose.

**Χ — Distance** is central. Non-metaphysical freedom requires distance from immediate impulse, but the analysis itself also requires distance from closure. Χ prevents the case from turning into a verdict about persons or into a metaphysical solution. It distinguishes reflective inhibition from passivity, avoidance, suppression, or incapacity. Χ also blocks coercive use of freedom-language under Ω. Overclaim would occur if distance alone were treated as freedom, or if reflection were used to deny the importance of enactment.

**Σ — Integration** is active but insufficient by itself. Freedom must be integrated enough to avoid collapse into randomness, arbitrariness, fragmentation, or mere option plurality. Reasons, constraints, commitments, and action possibilities must be coordinated. However, PMS coherence is not proof. A coherent freedom account may still hide coercion, omit asymmetry, suppress alternatives, or confuse integration with conformity. Overclaim occurs when Σ becomes totalization: the smoothness of the PMS reading is mistaken for adequate capture.

**Ψ — Self-Binding** is active only conditionally. It is central where freedom involves responsibility, commitment, autonomy-like stability, promise, obligation, or long-term self-relation. But not every minimal free action requires full Ψ. If Ψ is made universal, freedom collapses into responsibility-bearing autonomy. PMS must distinguish a local option, a voluntary act, an integrated action, and a self-bound commitment. Overclaim occurs if Ψ is treated as metaphysical selfhood, moral maturity, person ranking, or a required condition for all freedom.

The full operator chain is therefore available as a conceptual model, but not all operators carry equal evidential status. Δ, ∇, □, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, and conditional Ψ carry major analytic weight. Λ and Α remain weaker because no concrete absence sequence or recurrence pattern is supplied.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

Later PMS operators cannot be used cheaply. This is the main dependency lesson of the case. The temptation in freedom debates is to jump from “there is a choice” to “there is responsibility,” or from “there is constraint” to “there is no freedom,” or from “there is reflection” to “there is autonomy.” PMS blocks those shortcuts.

A Δ/∇ reading makes alternatives and action pressure visible. But it does not yet establish freedom. A frame, □, must declare what kind of freedom is being discussed. Λ must preserve missing or hidden constraints. Α must not be asserted unless pattern stabilization is shown. Ω must be calibrated rather than moralized. Θ must specify the time horizon. Φ must track frame shifts without absorbing rivals. Χ must preserve distance from closure. Σ must integrate without totalizing. Ψ must only bind where commitment or responsibility is actually in scope.

Σ remains conditional because integration is not the same as freedom. A coerced action may be internally coherent. A constrained agent may integrate available reasons under pressure. A social system may produce coherent compliance while suppressing exit. Therefore Σ can help distinguish freedom from randomness, but it cannot prove freedom by itself.

Ψ remains conditional for similar reasons. Self-binding may express freedom when a commitment is legitimate, frame-declared, temporally understood, and asymmetry-aware. But self-binding may also be imposed, internalized under coercion, or hardened through unequal power. PMS therefore cannot treat Ψ as automatically positive. Ψ under Θ/Ω requires especially careful calibration.

Coherence is not proof. This case maps very smoothly onto PMS, which creates a risk. Because freedom includes difference, action, frames, constraint, temporality, asymmetry, distance, integration, and responsibility, nearly the entire PMS chain can be activated. That breadth is analytically useful, but it also creates over-assimilation risk. PMS must show discriminative force: it must distinguish freedom from option-space, voluntariness, arbitrariness, randomness, autonomy, coercion, responsibility, and metaphysical free will. If it merely redescribes all of them in PMS terms, the reading becomes too smooth.

Non-capture is not failure. If metaphysical free will remains outside PMS, that is not a defect of the case. If phenomenological openness is not captured, that is not drift. If legal doctrines require other standards, that is not incomplete PMS calibration. If a rival theory better captures political non-domination, legal voluntariness, or lived agency, that possibility must remain open.

Invalid dependency shortcuts include Δ/∇ → Ψ collapse, where a salient choice becomes responsibility without frame, asymmetry, time, or integration; Ω → unfreedom collapse, where asymmetry automatically means coercion; Χ → freedom collapse, where reflection alone becomes freedom; Σ → adequacy collapse, where coherence becomes proof; and Ψ → moral worth collapse, where self-binding becomes person-ranking.

The PMS reading must therefore remain a map, not a closure machine.

PMS does not claim subjective experience, consciousness, or felt openness. Awareness_A is structural only.

**Coherence_C** is active but insufficient. Its formula is C = Θ + Λ + □ + ∇. Freedom requires more than impulse. Still, coherent unfreedom remains possible. Coherence_C cannot establish freedom without asymmetry analysis, distance, integration, and enactment conditions.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The drift-pattern status is cautious. No PMS drift pattern is assigned as a finding, because no concrete scene is present. Drift patterns are treated only as conditional structural risks.

**AD_A>>E — excessive distance between awareness and enactment** is a watch-risk, not a finding. The case may over-expand awareness, reflection, or reasons-talk while under-specifying enactment. A philosophical account of freedom can become too reflective: it describes alternatives, constraints, frames, and commitments, but never shows how action occurs. However, the supplied material does not show chronic delay, suppressed action, evaluative over-distance, or a stabilized pattern of awareness replacing enactment. Therefore AD_A>>E remains under-specified.

The axis effects remain under-specified. The awareness axis is not shown to be over-expanded in a concrete scene, and the action axis is not shown to be suppressed or chronically delayed. Philosophical reflection alone is not drift.

**AD_Sigma_low — fragmented integration** is also only a watch-risk. The case includes many adjacent concepts and rival frames. That plurality could produce under-integration if freedom were repeatedly reframed without a coherent distinction among option-space, voluntariness, autonomy, responsibility, and metaphysical free will. But conceptual plurality is not itself fragmentation. Rival readings may be legitimate. Therefore AD_Sigma_low remains possible but not entailed.

The coherence and responsibility axes are under-specified rather than volatile or inconsistent. No evidence shows unstable integration or inconsistent responsibility assignment. The analysis therefore avoids treating under-specification as failure and avoids treating non-assignment as a low score.

The main coherence risk is different: PMS may fit too well. If the reading becomes too elegant, it may stop discriminating. The antidote is to preserve falsifier conditions, rival readings, non-capture zones, and the distinction between structural coherence and sufficient explanation.

### Example 1 — Nominal choice and practical exit-cost

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Scene and frame structure,” where the article explains why freedom must be frame-bound rather than treated as a global status.

A worker is offered a new scheduling arrangement. Formally, the arrangement is presented as voluntary: the worker may accept weekend shifts in exchange for higher pay, or decline and remain on the ordinary schedule. In a narrow permission frame, there appears to be a choice. No one physically blocks refusal, and no explicit threat is stated. But the worker depends on the higher pay to cover rent, has no realistic alternative employment in the near term, and knows that refusing may reduce future scheduling preference. The employer does not need to say, “You must accept.” The practical structure already narrows the action-space.

The example is bounded. It does not decide whether the arrangement is legal, coercive, exploitative, fair, unfair, valid, invalid, or morally blameworthy. It does not diagnose the worker as weak, dependent, irrational, or unfree. It does not accuse the employer of wrongdoing. It simply makes visible why “there was a choice” is not enough to establish non-metaphysical freedom in the PMS sense.

What the example demonstrates is the gap between **formal option-space** and **practical freedom under constraint**. A binary reading would say either the worker was free because refusal was possible, or not free because economic pressure was present. PMS keeps both reductions at a distance. The relevant question is not whether an abstract alternative existed, but how alternatives were framed, how costly exit was, how asymmetry shaped the situation, and over what temporal horizon the choice operated.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ** marks the visible distinction between accepting and refusing. **∇** appears as pressure toward decision: the worker must choose, the employer needs coverage, the situation pushes toward closure. **□** is crucial because the frame determines what kind of freedom is being assessed: contractual permission, economic feasibility, relational dependence, workplace discretion, or long-term agency. **Λ** appears in the missing or unavailable events: no realistic alternative job appears, no protected contest channel is evident, no clear assurance is given that refusal will not affect future treatment. These are not findings of illegality; they are structured absences relevant to the freedom claim.

**Α** may be weakly relevant if this scheduling arrangement is part of a repeated pattern. If workers regularly “choose” shifts under similar pressure, a constraint-navigation script may stabilize. But this cannot be inferred from a single vignette. **Ω** is active because the employer has scheduling power and the worker bears exposure. Yet Ω must not be moralized automatically. An asymmetry is not by itself coercion or wrongdoing. **Θ** is active because the worker’s freedom depends on rent deadlines, future scheduling consequences, accumulated dependency, and the cost of later reversal. **Φ** appears if the situation is recontextualized: what first looked like voluntary overtime may also be read as constrained economic choice, institutional dependency, or long-horizon self-protection. **Χ** prevents the analysis from rushing to either blame or exoneration. **Σ** would be needed to integrate pay, dependency, workplace rules, alternatives, and future consequences into a coherent freedom assessment. **Ψ** becomes relevant only if the worker’s acceptance is later treated as a binding commitment, role obligation, or responsibility-bearing choice.

There are no add-on hooks required here. PMS Core is sufficient. A LOGIC overlay would be unnecessary unless the example became about justification claims such as “voluntary means valid.” A CONFLICT overlay would be unnecessary unless the scene escalated into dispute. MIP, AHP, and QC remain inactive because the vignette is not an institutional audit, governance artefact, or claim-hardening exercise.

The forbidden claims are important. The example must not be used to declare that the worker was legally coerced, that the employer acted wrongly, that the worker lacked agency, or that the decision proves exploitation. It must also not be used in the opposite direction to say that formal choice fully proves freedom. Both moves exceed the example.

The non-capture note is that PMS does not replace labor law, political theory, economic analysis, or lived experience. A capability-theory reading may better describe material opportunity. A legal reading may better distinguish duress, consent, and enforceability. A phenomenological reading may better capture the worker’s felt experience of pressure or resolve. PMS only maps why the freedom claim cannot be reduced to the mere presence of alternatives.

The article takeaway is that non-metaphysical freedom is graded, framed, asymmetry-sensitive, and temporal. “Could have done otherwise” is structurally incomplete unless the frame, costs, dependencies, missing alternatives, and binding consequences are also made explicit.

### Example 2 — Self-binding as freedom and constraint

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** inside “PMS Core structural reading,” especially after the discussion of Θ, Σ, and Ψ.

A person commits to caring for an elderly parent three evenings a week. The commitment is not imposed by a court or contract. It emerges through family expectation, affection, history, limited resources, and the person’s own decision to become reliable. After several months, the commitment strongly constrains the person’s evenings. Invitations are declined, travel is harder, spontaneous plans become rare, and other family members begin to assume that this person will continue carrying the role.

At first glance, the example can be read in opposite ways. One reading says the person is less free because the commitment reduces option-space. Another says the person is exercising freedom precisely by binding themselves to a valued responsibility over time. PMS does not force either closure. It asks how the commitment is framed, how asymmetry operates, how temporality changes the action-space, and whether self-binding remains reflective, revisable, and dignity-constrained.

The bounded vignette does not decide whether the commitment is good, bad, fair, unfair, healthy, unhealthy, chosen, coerced, noble, exploitative, or legally relevant. It does not diagnose the caregiver. It does not rank the caregiver as more mature or more dignified. It does not judge the parent or family. The example only shows why freedom cannot be equated with maximal immediate options.

The PMS Core hooks clarify the structure. **Δ** distinguishes available evenings from committed evenings, spontaneous options from bound obligations, and local discretion from long-term reliability. **∇** appears in the directional pressures: care needs, family expectation, affection, guilt, concern, and the practical need for someone to act. PMS does not psychologize these; it treats them as structural directedness. **□** defines the frame: family care, moral commitment, relational obligation, household coordination, or personal promise. The freedom claim changes depending on which frame is active.

**Λ** appears where expected support is absent. Perhaps no public care resource is available, no sibling reliably shares the burden, no clear handover exists, or no explicit renegotiation moment has been created. These absences matter, but they do not automatically prove coercion or failed integration. **Α** may develop as the caregiving schedule stabilizes into a repeated script: “this is what Tuesdays and Thursdays are for.” **Ω** is active because exposure is uneven. The parent may be vulnerable; the caregiver may bear time-costs; other relatives may benefit from the arrangement without equal burden; institutions may be absent or under-resourced. Still, Ω is not a verdict. It marks directional imbalance, not guilt.

**Θ** is central. The meaning of the commitment changes across time. A single evening of help is different from a repeated duty. A repeated duty is different from an open-ended role. Long-term care can create lock-in, identity pressure, accumulated fatigue, and also meaningful continuity. **Φ** becomes important when the commitment is reframed: from voluntary help to expected duty, from family affection to unfair burden, from constraint to self-chosen responsibility, from private care to institutional gap. **Χ** is needed so the analysis does not fuse responsibility with blame or freedom with sacrifice. Reflective distance asks whether the commitment remains open to reconsideration, whether alternatives can be discussed, and whether the caregiver is being treated as a person rather than a role-function.

**Σ** would integrate the competing elements: care need, capacity, family distribution, time horizon, available alternatives, and the caregiver’s own commitments. **Ψ** is conditionally active because the case involves self-binding. The caregiver’s freedom may partly consist in binding themselves to a meaningful commitment. But Ψ can also become risky if the commitment hardens into identity capture: “you are the responsible one,” “you always do this,” or “you cannot now refuse.” PMS therefore treats Ψ as responsibility-bearing structure, not as automatic maturity or moral superiority.

No add-on is needed. PMS Core already distinguishes option-space, temporal commitment, asymmetry, integration, and self-binding. MIP might become relevant only if a care institution, family agreement, policy, or audit procedure were being evaluated. AHP/QC would be relevant only for hardening an analysis artefact or checking claim discipline. They are not active in this vignette.

Forbidden claims include any claim that the caregiver is legally obligated, psychologically dependent, morally superior, exploited, immature, guilty, or fully autonomous. The example must also avoid the simplistic claim that reduced options mean reduced freedom in every respect. A self-binding can restrict local option-space while enabling long-horizon agency and responsibility.

The non-capture note is that PMS does not capture the felt emotional weight of care, the ethical depth of familial obligation, the legal status of caregiving, or the social policy question of support infrastructure. Those may require ethical, phenomenological, legal, feminist, political, or care-theoretical readings.

The article takeaway is that freedom and constraint are not opposites in a simple binary. A commitment can be both a limitation and an expression of freedom, depending on frame, asymmetry, temporality, integration, and whether self-binding remains reflective and revisable.

### Example 3 — “You are free to leave” and hidden constraint

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** near “Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions,” where the article distinguishes PMS structural mapping from stronger empirical, legal, or governance claims.

A digital platform changes its terms. Users may either accept the new data-sharing conditions or stop using the service. The platform announces that users remain free because participation is optional. In a narrow formal frame, this is true: the accept button and the account-deletion path are both available. But many users rely on the platform for professional visibility, client contact, archived messages, community access, and identity continuity. Leaving is possible but costly. The platform also controls export tools, notice timing, interface design, and the practical visibility of alternatives.

This example is bounded and deliberately non-forensic. It does not claim the platform violates law, manipulates users, coerces consent, or acts immorally. It does not claim users lack agency. It does not provide empirical evidence about actual user behavior. It shows a structural problem: the statement “users are free to leave” may be true inside one frame and under-discriminating inside another.

What the example demonstrates is the importance of operationalization. A stronger analysis would require evidence: actual exit costs, data portability, dependency levels, availability of alternatives, notice periods, user roles, professional reliance, and contest channels. Without these, PMS can map the structure but cannot issue a finding. This is exactly the discipline required by the article: conceptual coherence does not authorize empirical, legal, or governance closure.

The PMS Core hooks show why. **Δ** distinguishes accepting, refusing, deleting, migrating, delaying, or contesting. **∇** appears as closure pressure: users must decide, the platform must stabilize terms, and the public statement demands recognition of optionality. **□** is decisive. If the frame is formal access, users have an exit. If the frame is practical dependency, the action-space may be narrower. If the frame is professional infrastructure, exit may carry long-horizon costs. The freedom claim depends on which □ is declared.

**Λ** is important because several expected supports may be absent: no easy export, no equivalent alternative, no meaningful negotiation channel, no clear explanation of consequences, no realistic time to adapt. These absences should be named without converting them into automatic wrongdoing. **Α** may appear if the platform has become an infrastructural attractor: repeated use, network effects, archives, professional norms, and habitual dependence stabilize participation. Unlike the prior conceptual case, this example suggests how Α could become empirically relevant, but actual evidence would still be required. **Ω** is active because the platform can set terms and users bear differentiated costs. Users are not equally exposed: casual users, freelancers, organizations, vulnerable communities, and creators may face different losses. **Θ** is central because dependence accumulates over time. An account created casually years earlier may become a professional archive or social infrastructure.

**Φ** appears when the same claim shifts frames: “optional service” becomes “professional dependency,” “consent” becomes “infrastructure lock-in,” “exit” becomes “loss of accumulated history.” **Χ** prevents the analysis from becoming a slogan either way. It blocks both “they accepted, so they were free” and “they depended on it, so they were not free.” **Σ** would be needed to integrate formal choice, practical cost, platform control, alternatives, temporal dependency, and user exposure. **Ψ** becomes relevant only if acceptance is later treated as binding consent, durable responsibility, or legitimate self-commitment. PMS would then ask whether the binding was frame-declared, asymmetry-aware, and revisable.

There are no active add-on hooks in this example. PMS Core suffices to map the freedom structure. MIP could become relevant if the platform’s policy artefact were being assessed for governance use. AHP could harden the analysis if claims about user consent or institutional responsibility were to be made. QC could be relevant if the article were evaluating the quality of an audit or compliance report. None of those layers is activated by the vignette itself.

Forbidden claims include legal conclusions about consent, regulatory compliance, manipulation, unfairness, or liability. The example also forbids empirical claims about user psychology, such as addiction, dependency disorder, irrationality, or incapacity. It must not rank users as less autonomous or platforms as guilty. It must not validate PMS as a better theory of digital freedom.

The non-capture note is substantial. Legal analysis may be required for consent and data rights. Political economy may better address platform dependency and market structure. Interface studies may examine dark patterns. Empirical research may measure actual exit behavior. PMS does not replace these. It only clarifies why the freedom claim must not be reduced to the existence of an exit button.

The article takeaway is that freedom can be formally available and practically thinned at the same time. PMS helps preserve that distinction without turning it into a verdict. The case remains a map of option-space, asymmetry, temporal lock-in, and missing supports—not a legal judgment, moral accusation, or empirical finding.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The YAML preserves several rival readings. They are not treated as errors. Each protects something PMS may not fully capture.

An **epistemological or compatibilist reasons-responsiveness reading** may treat freedom as the capacity to respond to reasons under appropriate conditions. PMS can map this structurally through ∇, Χ, □, Θ, and Σ, but it does not by itself supply a full epistemology of reasons or rational control.

A **negative liberty reading** treats freedom as non-interference. PMS can represent interference as constraint under □ and Ω, but it does not automatically decide which interferences count as liberty-reducing. Political theory may discriminate this more precisely.

A **positive liberty or autonomy reading** treats freedom as self-mastery, self-governance, or rational self-direction. PMS can model self-binding through Ψ and integration through Σ, but it must not collapse all freedom into autonomy. A person may have local freedom without full autonomy-like self-relation.

A **republican non-domination reading** focuses on arbitrary power and dependence, even where no actual interference occurs. PMS can represent this through Ω, Θ, Λ, and Χ, especially where hidden vulnerability or contest incapacity shapes option-space. But republican theory may preserve institutional and political distinctions beyond PMS Core.

A **capability-theoretic reading** treats freedom as real opportunity, not formal permission. PMS can map nominal versus real option-space through □, Ω, Θ, Λ, and Action_E. However, capability theory may offer richer criteria for material opportunity, welfare, and social conversion factors.

A **legal voluntariness reading** distinguishes consent, coercion, duress, capacity, and liability. PMS can clarify structural preconditions, but it does not supply legal thresholds or evidentiary standards.

A **phenomenological reading** preserves the lived experience of openness, deliberation, hesitation, or felt agency. PMS explicitly does not claim phenomenological capture. Χ and Awareness_A are structural, not experiential.

A **libertarian metaphysical reading** insists that genuine freedom requires ultimate self-causation or indeterminist agency. PMS does not refute that claim. It simply does not need it for the non-metaphysical case.

A **hard-determinist reading** may deny free will while preserving causal explanation. PMS does not settle determinism. It can still map responsibility-bearing structures without metaphysical free will, but that does not defeat determinist metaphysics.

A **decision-theoretic option-space reading** may formalize freedom as the set of available actions. PMS can use Δ and □ to represent option-space, but it adds asymmetry, temporality, hidden constraints, integration, and self-binding to prevent option-counting from exhausting freedom.

These rivals remain live. PMS translation does not defeat them. It only shows what PMS can render structurally under the declared non-metaphysical frame.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would weaken if it failed to discriminate freedom from adjacent concepts. If every option becomes freedom, the reading is too broad. If every constraint becomes unfreedom, it is too crude. If every self-binding becomes autonomy, it overclaims Ψ. If every asymmetry becomes domination, it overclaims Ω. If every reflective pause becomes freedom, it overclaims Χ. If every coherent structure becomes adequate freedom, it overclaims Σ.

The reading would also weaken if it suppressed non-capture. A PMS account that quietly treats metaphysical free will as solved, phenomenological freedom as captured, legal voluntariness as structurally settled, or empirical capacity as inferred would exceed its warrant.

The reading becomes under-discriminating if it cannot separate at least the following: option-space, impulse, voluntariness, agency, enacted freedom, autonomy-like freedom, responsibility-bearing freedom, randomness, arbitrariness, coercion, constraint, and metaphysical free will. The YAML proposes minimal distinctions: option-space as Δ + □; impulse as ∇; enacted freedom as Action_E under frame, distance, and integration; responsibility-bearing freedom as R = Ψ + Φ + Θ + Ω; and metaphysical free will as outside PMS warrant.

The reading becomes over-assimilative if resistance, rupture, disagreement, rival framing, missing evidence, or non-fit is automatically redescribed as PMS drift, incomplete integration, recontextualization residue, or maturity failure. Rival theories may be external to PMS rather than immature within it.

Concrete strengthening would require a scene. A stronger claim would need an actor or role, a declared frame, actual alternatives, specific constraints, asymmetry conditions, temporal sequence, evidence of impulse or directedness, evidence of reflective distance, enactment, exit costs, hidden or absent options, and criteria for distinguishing constraint from coercion. Responsibility claims would require evidence of commitment, authority, capability, obligation, handover, and repair horizon. Legal claims would require legal standards. Clinical claims would require clinical evidence and still would not be PMS claims. Computational claims would require architecture, implementation, and testing.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. The YAML can specify what indicators would be needed for Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, and Ψ, but the case material supplies none of the empirical, comparative, legal, clinical, computational, or applied data that would be required for stronger claims. Therefore the final status remains: valid as a conceptual PMS projection with limits.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It does not identify personality, maturity, pathology, competence, incapacity, impulse-control status, agency type, or psychological condition.

It must not be used for person ranking. PMS does not rank people as more or less free in worth, dignity, maturity, rationality, or moral standing. Dignity-in-Practice prevents that use.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusions. It does not decide coercion, consent, culpability, duress, negligence, liability, capacity, or guilt.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. It neither proves nor disproves ultimate free will, determinism, indeterminism, self-causation, or metaphysical agency.

It must not be used as empirical proof. No data are supplied. Conceptual coherence is not evidence.

It must not be used as implementation validation. YAML representation is not an agent architecture, freedom detector, decision system, or responsibility engine.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. A successful case mapping does not prove PMS complete, final, uniquely ordered, or superior to rivals.

It must not treat add-ons as operators. No add-on is active here; even if one were later used, it would remain subordinate to PMS Core.

It must not treat drift as verdict. AD_A>>E and AD_Sigma_low are only conditional watch-risks, not findings, scores, diagnoses, or moral labels.

It must not close by authority. PMS is a cartographic grammar here. It shows how closure-demands are structured, where closure is possible, and where Λ-residue must remain visible. It does not settle freedom by force of vocabulary.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is that non-metaphysical freedom can be rendered as a frame-bound, temporally extended, asymmetry-sensitive praxis structure rather than as metaphysical self-causation or a binary status. PMS Core does not ask whether freedom exists “in itself.” It asks how freedom-claims are structured: what alternatives are differentiated, what directedness is present, which frame defines the action-space, which constraints or missing options remain, how asymmetry shapes real availability, how temporality creates commitment and lock-in, how recontextualization changes the meaning of choice, how reflective distance prevents closure, how integration coordinates competing constraints, and where self-binding becomes relevant to responsibility.

The three examples contribute by making these distinctions readable without turning them into evidence. The scheduling example shows why formal choice is not enough when exit-costs and asymmetry shape practical action-space. The caregiving example shows why self-binding can be both constraint and expression of freedom, depending on frame, temporality, asymmetry, and revisability. The platform example shows why “free to leave” may be structurally under-discriminating unless hidden constraints, accumulated dependency, and operational evidence are examined. Each example demonstrates the PMS reading; none replaces the analysis, proves the theory, supplies empirical support, or licenses a verdict. Their function is illustrative and discriminative, not evidentiary or adjudicative.

What remains unclaimed is substantial. The case does not prove or disprove metaphysical free will. It does not claim empirical knowledge about actual agents. It does not diagnose persons, rank maturity, assign blame, decide coercion, determine consent, validate institutions, or implement a model of freedom. It does not defeat rival theories. It does not claim that PMS captures phenomenological openness, legal voluntariness, political liberty, material capability, or metaphysical agency in full.

The boundary discipline is therefore part of the result. PMS coherence is not enough. A smooth operator chain is not proof. Drift risks are not findings. Under-specification is not failure. Non-assignment is not a low score. Resistance, refusal, disagreement, rupture, absence, or non-fit must not automatically be redescribed as PMS drift or failed integration. Dignity-in-Practice remains a praxeological constraint on how asymmetry is handled, not an ontological ranking of persons.

The final methodological takeaway is that AXIOM can make freedom structurally legible without closing it by authority. PMS Core can show where freedom-talk demands frame declaration, where option-space is too thin, where asymmetry and temporality matter, where self-binding becomes responsibility-relevant, and where Λ-residue must remain visible. The resulting formula remains case-type-bound: it is a disciplined structural rendering of non-metaphysical freedom, not a final theory of freedom, not a metaphysical settlement, and not a verdict on any concrete agent, institution, or responsibility claim.
