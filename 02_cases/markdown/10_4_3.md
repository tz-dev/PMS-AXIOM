### Why this case matters

*Boundary Confusion* matters because it is a closure-demand case. Its surface term appears simple: a boundary is unclear, contested, or crossed. Yet the moment the term “boundary” enters analysis, a powerful demand for settlement appears. A boundary seems to ask: where is the line, who crossed it, who owns it, who may define it, who violated it, who must repair it, and who is responsible for the confusion? This closure-demand is philosophically strong because boundaries often carry normative, relational, institutional, bodily, spatial, linguistic, epistemic, and praxeological force at once.

A boundary can be a conceptual distinction, an interpersonal limit, a legal right, a role mandate, a privacy claim, a space, a responsibility distribution, an authority threshold, or a decision entitlement. These forms can overlap without being identical. A boundary may be unclear because the relevant role is ambiguous; contested because two parties use different frames; crossed because one position acts beyond its mandate; violated because a norm or rule has force; negotiated because exceptions have been accepted; or absent because no stable frame ever defined it. The same surface word can therefore name several structurally different situations.

The case belongs in AXIOM precisely because it tests whether a formal praxeological grammar can hold distinction without prematurely deciding the dispute. A weaker model would either resolve the case too quickly or leave it as mere ambiguity. It might say simply: “the boundary is unclear,” or “the boundary was crossed,” or “different parties frame it differently.” The AXIOM stack has to do more. It must identify what kind of structure makes the case legible while refusing to turn legibility into verdict.

PMS Core provides the first discipline. The case becomes readable through Difference and Frame, but later operators such as Non-Event, Attractor, Asymmetry, Temporality, Recontextualization, Integration, and Self-Binding cannot be activated merely because the word “boundary” invites them. PMS-SEX then tests whether sexual-praxeological structure is present; it finds that it is not. MIP tests whether maturity, responsibility, awareness, coherence, and power-to-act can be discussed without person scoring. AHP tests whether the analysis itself is precise enough not to be misread as stronger than its evidence.

The structural difficulty is therefore not simply that the case lacks data. It is that the case is conceptually fertile. It maps very easily onto many parts of the PMS grammar. Difference appears because a boundary differentiates. Frame appears because boundaries require contexts. Non-Event appears because missing clarification, missing refusal, or missing repair can matter. Attractor appears because repeated crossing can stabilize. Asymmetry appears because some positions may define, bear, or suffer boundaries differently. Temporality appears because repeated crossing implies duration. Recontextualization appears because parties may use rival frames. Distance appears because the analysis must not collapse into verdict. Integration appears because boundary conflicts invite synthesis. Self-Binding appears because responsibility and durable roles can become involved. The danger is not that PMS cannot read the case. The danger is that PMS can read it too smoothly.

For that reason, this case is not solved metaphysically. The analysis does not decide what a person “really is,” where personhood metaphysically begins or ends, what inner experience belongs to whom, or whether a boundary reflects essence. Nor is it solved empirically. No episode, evidence set, testimony, actor identity, chronology, institutional procedure, or observable enactment is supplied. It is not solved clinically or diagnostically, because no psychological trait, disorder, motive, competence, pathology, or risk status follows from the case. It is not solved legally or forensically, because no rights, liability, consent standard, evidentiary threshold, custody, ownership, jurisdiction, or culpability are established.

The case is also not solved sexually. No sexual scene, sexual access threshold, embodied cost, genital meaning, after-scene residue, or public sexual configuration is present. The PMS-SEX layer is therefore important precisely because it refuses activation. It does not add a hidden sexual reading. It prevents sexualization-by-overlay. It marks what would be required for a sexual-praxeological reading and then concludes that those requirements are absent from the supplied material.

Nor is the case solved by maturity language. MIP does not assign A-score or M-score. It does not decide that role_A, role_B, the analyst, or any hypothetical person is mature or immature. It treats awareness and coherence as strongest at the documentation level, responsibility as mainly methodological, and power-to-act as located in wording, review, and transmission control. Dignity remains guardrail-only. AHP then adds a second-order caution: the more complete the formal article becomes, the greater the risk that readers mistake structured restraint for evidentiary authority.

The case matters, then, as a disciplined anti-closure example. It shows how a model can identify a core structural reading without pretending to have exhausted the phenomenon. PMS Core gives a minimal signature; PMS-SEX refuses unwarranted sexualization; MIP refuses maturity verdicts; AHP marks the analysis itself as vulnerable to over-formal authority. The result is not a weaker article but a stronger one: the force of the case lies in the way it preserves the gap between formal readability and justified closure.

### Claim status and scope discipline

The YAML classifies *Boundary Confusion* as a conceptual domain projection. This is not an incidental limitation. It is the governing claim status of the case. A conceptual domain projection applies a formal grammar to a class of possible situations rather than to a documented episode. The relevant class consists of situations in which distinctions between one person’s role, responsibility, space, or decision and another’s become unclear, contested, or repeatedly crossed. The analysis therefore asks what structural conditions become visible when such cases are read through PMS and its later overlays. It does not ask who, in any concrete episode, is right or wrong.

The case’s claim status is *conceptual_pms_projection*. This means the PMS reading is authorized only as a structural projection over the case-type. It may identify the operators that would become relevant if certain conditions were supplied. It may say that Δ and □ are central, that Χ is required as application discipline, and that other operators remain weak or conditional. It may also say that rival readings and non-capture remain open. It may not say that PMS has proven the true nature of all boundary confusion.

The concrete-scene status is negative. The YAML explicitly marks the case as not a concrete scene. This has several consequences. First, role_A and role_B cannot be treated as persons. They are abstract positions in a boundary configuration. Second, there is no enacted behavior to score. Third, no temporal sequence can be reconstructed. Fourth, no asymmetry direction can be assigned. Fifth, no responsibility band, maturity band, dignity profile, sexual drift, or legal interpretation can be concluded. A conceptual scene can show where formal categories might attach; it cannot do the evidentiary work of a documented case.

The empirical boundary is equally strict. The case is not empirical. It does not establish that anything happened. It does not identify a factual crossing, violation, misunderstanding, negotiation, refusal, repair, or harm. It does not supply observational indicators. It does not contain evidence about motive, injury, consent, institutional context, communication, or consequence. The operationalization requirements therefore remain unfulfilled. Any empirical or applied use would require concrete episodes, actor roles, boundary type, frame evidence, recurrence evidence, asymmetry direction, temporal sequence, and external standards where legal, clinical, institutional, or applied claims are sought.

The metaphysical boundary is also explicit. PMS does not use this case to make claims about consciousness, qualia, inner experience, essence, selfhood, personhood, moral worth, or ontological dignity. Even Dignity-in-Practice is not treated as metaphysical worth. It is a praxeological constraint on how asymmetry is handled in analysis and action. The dignity clause protects against humiliation, ranking, and person-worth inference; it does not authorize a metaphysics of the person.

The diagnostic boundary prohibits psychological and clinical conversion. The case may discuss boundary confusion as a praxeological structure, but it may not infer traits, pathology, attachment style, competence, disorder, risk, maturity, or inner motive. PMS drift patterns, where considered later, are structural risks only. They are not clinical categories and do not identify person-types.

The legal and forensic boundary prohibits rights or culpability conclusions. Boundary language often invites legal interpretation: trespass, consent, role authority, jurisdiction, custody, ownership, mandate, privacy, or liability. This analysis does not decide any of those. A legal or forensic reading may be a rival reading, but PMS does not replace legal evidence, procedural standards, statutory categories, or institutional authority.

The implementation boundary is likewise closed. YAML representation is a formal encoding of a PMS reading, not a direct implementation of praxis, agency, consciousness, moral reasoning, clinical validity, or public accountability. Computational representation may support machine-readable loading, dependency reasoning, operator-chain validation, and simulation scaffolding, but it does not guarantee empirical adequacy, interoperability, or explanatory sufficiency.

Finally, the PMS Base validation boundary prevents a common formalist error. A case cannot validate PMS Base. The analysis is subordinate to PMS.yaml. It does not redefine PMS operators, alter the repository dependency order, introduce new meta-axioms, treat derived axes as primitives, or prove that PMS is complete. It applies the repository reference; it does not revise or certify it.

The operationalization boundary follows from all of these limits. The completed Core YAML includes requirements for empirical or applied use, but those requirements are not fulfilled in this conceptual projection. Stronger claims would require observable indicators for each operator: a specific boundary distinction for Δ, observable pressures for ∇, governing frames for □, an expected event whose absence matters for Λ, repeated structurally similar episodes for Α, calibrated imbalance for Ω, temporal sequence for Θ, source and target frames for Φ, explicit restraint and reversibility for Χ, concrete coordination for Σ, and durable self-binding for Ψ. None of this is supplied at the concrete scene level.

The scope discipline therefore has two sides. Positively, the case may be read as a framed distinction problem with possible but unproven extensions into recurrence, asymmetry, temporality, rival framing, distance, integration, and self-binding. Negatively, it may not decide whether a boundary is morally legitimate, legally valid, therapeutically salient, culturally appropriate, institutionally enforceable, or subjectively experienced as harm, care, intrusion, abandonment, autonomy, or obligation.

### Scene and frame structure

The scene is not an enacted scene. It is a conceptual scene-boundary: *Boundary Confusion* as an abstract domain of situations in which boundaries between persons, roles, responsibilities, spaces, or decisions become unclear, contested, or repeatedly crossed. The absence of a concrete scene is not a minor evidentiary gap; it is the primary structural condition of the reading. The analysis is case-type-bound rather than scene-final.

The frame operator □ is central. A boundary does not become meaningful merely because a difference exists. It becomes meaningful inside a frame. A role boundary is not the same as a spatial boundary. A responsibility boundary is not the same as a decision-authority boundary. A privacy boundary is not the same as an institutional mandate. The governing frame determines what counts as crossing, ambiguity, refusal, repair, overreach, care, control, abandonment, shared responsibility, or legitimate exception. Therefore, the case is not simply “about boundaries”; it is about how a frame makes a distinction count as a boundary problem.

Inside the frame are the abstract positions and structural possibilities supplied by the Core case. There is a role_A position: an abstract boundary-position that may claim, cross, defend, blur, or contest a role, responsibility, space, or decision boundary. There is a role_B position: an abstract counter-position that may hold, receive, resist, clarify, contest, or be affected by that boundary. There is also a field_or_medium: the conceptual boundary frame itself, later recognized in MIP as part of the scientific-model context. None of these are person-types. None are psychological profiles. None are sexual identities. None are legal subjects in the strict forensic sense.

Outside the frame are precisely the claims the YAML refuses. The frame does not contain empirical evidence, legal rights, clinical significance, person essence, sexuality-specific cost, embodied boundary data, public accusation, or maturity ranking. It also does not contain a settled distinction between unclear, contested, crossed, violated, shared, negotiated, absent, or legally defined boundaries. Those distinctions are required for stronger analysis, but they are not supplied by the abstract case-name itself.

The protected constraints are Distance, reversibility, and Dignity-in-Practice. Χ requires the analysis not to fuse with urgency, enforcement, or verdict. Reversibility requires the reading to remain revisable and non-totalizing. Dignity-in-Practice requires the analysis not to shame, rank, humiliate, diagnose, or ontologically evaluate persons or groups. These constraints are not external afterthoughts. They are entry conditions for valid PMS application in this stack.

Temporality Θ is present only as a weak and conditional possibility. The case language includes “repeatedly crossed,” and repetition suggests duration, sequence, persistence, accumulation, or pattern. But no concrete chronology is supplied. There are no dates, no order of events, no escalation, no repair attempt, no delay, no after-effect, and no persistence data. Therefore, temporality cannot be used as if a trajectory had been observed. At most, Θ marks a condition for later calibration: if repeated boundary crossing is documented, temporal structure becomes relevant.

The reversibility window is the whole analysis itself. Because the case is abstract, the reading must remain open to revision if a concrete scene is added. A later case could supply actors, time windows, episode sequence, institutional frames, affected-party perspectives, publicness, or evidence. Such material could strengthen some operators and weaken others. It could also show that PMS is not the most discriminating framework. The current reading is therefore structurally valid only as a reversible conceptual projection.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are similarly conditional. The Core case allows possible asymmetry in role, responsibility, authority, vulnerability, exposure, access, obligation, or interpretive control. The MIP layer later names possible asymmetry in interpretive authority, boundary-setting power, responsibility allocation, access to clarification, and burden of correction. But direction and magnitude are unspecified. No role can be assigned the position of violator, victim, authority-holder, burden-carrier, or repair-obligated agent. The case may become asymmetry-relevant; it is not yet asymmetry-determined.

The scene and frame structure therefore yields a disciplined paradox. The case is readable enough to require formal treatment, but not specified enough to authorize closure. It has a boundary-frame structure, but no empirical boundary event. It has abstract roles, but no persons. It has possible recurrence, but no trajectory. It has possible asymmetry, but no calibrated imbalance. It has strong guardrails, but no decision.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading proceeds through the fixed operator inventory in repository order: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. The order matters because later operators depend on earlier ones. The case cannot cheaply jump from “boundary” to “responsibility,” “maturity,” “drift,” or “self-binding.” It has to earn each step.

#### Δ — Difference

Δ is the strongest Core entry point. *Boundary Confusion* is first legible as a problem of differentiation: one role is not another role, one responsibility is not another responsibility, one space is not another space, one decision is not another decision. The case becomes PMS-readable because it concerns distinctions that may be unclear, contested, or crossed.

Δ is therefore active and major. But the calibration problem is immediate. The case does not specify which boundary is at stake. A role boundary differs from a responsibility boundary; a responsibility boundary differs from a spatial boundary; a spatial boundary differs from a decision boundary; a decision boundary differs from access, authority, privacy, obligation, or institutional mandate. If these are conflated under the general term “boundary,” Δ becomes too broad. The analysis must therefore ask: what is being differentiated, and are several differences being collapsed into one term?

The overclaim would be to treat every difference as already a boundary violation. Difference enables boundary formation, but it does not decide legitimacy, ownership, harm, or breach. Nor does Δ prove that PMS captures the case. It only provides the minimal entry point for structural differentiation.

#### ∇ — Impulse

∇ is weak. A boundary problem may involve directed pressure: pressure to cross, defend, clarify, contest, withdraw, repair, reassign, or maintain a boundary. But the case does not supply an enacted orientation. There is no concrete movement, desire, motive, urgency, refusal, takeover, or repair attempt. Therefore ∇ can be named only as a possible directional tension arising from the differentiated field.

The calibration issue is to distinguish structural directedness from psychological inference. If one party crosses a boundary, that may be a directed action; but the abstract case does not say anyone crossed anything in a specific way. If one party contests a boundary, that may be argumentative or institutional rather than affective. PMS must therefore not infer desire, hostility, dependency, pathology, or motive from the mere fact that a boundary is unclear.

The dependency risk is that later operators often require some directed activation. If ∇ remains weak, later claims about Non-Event, Attractor, Integration, or Action must remain weak as well. The overclaim would be to treat boundary confusion as already showing a drive, impulse, or intentional pattern.

#### □ — Frame

□ is active and major. The frame determines what kind of boundary is at stake and how the distinction becomes relevant. A family frame, legal frame, institutional frame, therapeutic frame, spatial frame, role frame, care frame, autonomy frame, decision-authority frame, or responsibility-distribution frame can make the same surface event mean different things. A crossing in one frame might be care in another, intrusion in a third, mandate in a fourth, and legal violation in a fifth.

The case therefore centrally requires frame discipline. PMS Core does not solve the boundary by naming it. It asks what frame makes the boundary intelligible. It also asks whether parties or rival formalisms are using different frames: care versus control, shared responsibility versus overreach, autonomy versus abandonment, flexibility versus violation.

The calibration risk is that a frame can clarify without exhausting. A legal frame may be decisive for legal questions but not for subjective experience. A relational frame may illuminate care and dependence but not institutional mandate. A PMS frame may organize praxis but not replace evidence. The overclaim would be to treat □ as totalizing: once the frame is named, the case is settled. The YAML explicitly refuses that. A frame is a relevance structure, not closure.

#### Λ — Non-Event

Λ is conditional and weak. Non-Event becomes relevant when an expected occurrence fails or is delayed within a frame. In boundary cases, this could be missing clarification, missing refusal, missing permission, missing restraint, missing repair, missing acknowledgment, missing decision, or missing non-interference. Such absences can matter intensely because boundaries often depend not only on actions but also on what should have happened and did not.

In the present case, however, no such absence is specified. The Core material does not say that clarification was expected and absent, that repair was delayed, that permission failed to appear, or that refusal was suppressed. Therefore Λ cannot be asserted as active. It remains a possible calibration point for later concrete material.

The dependency risk is subtle. Boundary confusion often tempts the analyst to assume that a missing clarification must be the problem. But absence may also reflect missing evidence, rival framing, non-capture, or the fact that no shared expectation existed. The overclaim would be to convert every unclear boundary into a meaningful Non-Event. Λ requires a frame-specific expectation; without that expectation, absence cannot carry the structure.

#### Α — Attractor

Α is weak-to-moderate as a candidate, not as a finding. The case says boundaries may be repeatedly crossed. Repetition can indicate stabilization: a role script, pattern, routine, or path-dependent structure. If boundary crossing recurs in structurally similar ways, then an Attractor may be forming. The case’s language therefore gives Α more plausibility than some other later operators.

But the evidence remains abstract. There are no repeated episodes. There is no sequence. There is no comparison across instances. There is no indication that repetition is stabilized rather than accidental aggregation, temporary emergency, mutually negotiated exception, semantic looseness, or merely an abstract example. Therefore Α cannot be treated as established.

The dependency risk is that Ω and Θ depend partly on Α. If recurrence is not shown, asymmetry and temporality cannot be strongly derived from a stabilized pattern. The overclaim would be to treat the word “repeatedly” as if it were documented recurrence. PMS requires structural repetition, not lexical repetition.

#### Ω — Asymmetry

Ω is weak and conditional. Boundary confusion may involve asymmetry in power, exposure, capacity, vulnerability, obligation, authority, access, interpretive control, or responsibility. One party may define the boundary while another bears its cost. One role may have authority while another carries correction burden. One position may have easier exit, clearer language, or greater frame-setting power. These are legitimate Ω questions.

Yet none of them is specified. There is no direction of imbalance. There is no magnitude. No role_A or role_B can be assigned superiority, vulnerability, authority, dependence, or burden. The case may become asymmetry-relevant if concrete material is supplied, but at the Core level Ω remains a calibrated possibility.

The overclaim would be moralization. Asymmetry is not moral fault by default. The party who crosses, resists, refuses, contests, or defines a boundary is not thereby wrong. Nor is the exposed party automatically right. Ω names structural imbalance; it does not decide guilt, legitimacy, dignity, or harm.

#### Θ — Temporality

Θ is weak and conditional. Repeated boundary crossing suggests temporal extension: duration, recurrence, accumulation, anticipation, escalation, delay, failed repair, or changing expectation. Temporality would become central if the case documented a trajectory: repeated crossings over time, increasing burden, sedimented expectation, or a pattern of non-repair.

The current case does not. There are no dates, sequences, intervals, before/after relations, accumulated consequences, or repair attempts. Therefore Θ may be visible as a condition implied by the case-type, but it is not established as an observed trajectory.

The overclaim would be developmental narration. The analyst must not turn conceptual repetition into a history. This matters especially because later MIP trajectory language can easily sound as if a development has been observed. In this case, the only safe temporal claim is that stronger readings would require temporal calibration.

#### Φ — Recontextualization

Φ is conditional and important as a risk-control operator. Boundary confusion often arises because different frames recontextualize the same distinction. One frame may call an act care; another may call it intrusion. One may call it shared responsibility; another may call it overreach. One may call it autonomy; another abandonment. One may call it flexible coordination; another violation.

Φ therefore helps identify rival framing. But it must not absorb every disagreement. Recontextualization requires a source frame and a target frame. It must show how placing a structure into a new frame transforms its meaning. Without that, Φ becomes a way of redescribing non-fit as PMS sophistication.

The overclaim would be over-assimilation. If every refusal, rupture, rival reading, legal category, or subjective experience is folded into Φ, PMS becomes a closure machine. The YAML explicitly guards against this. Recontextualization must improve discrimination; it must not erase non-capture.

#### Χ — Distance

Χ is active as application discipline. It is one of the strongest elements in the whole reading. Distance is not simply a case-internal operator here; it is a condition of valid analysis. The analyst must pause before verdict, preserve reversibility, inhibit person-ranking, and avoid converting boundary confusion into diagnosis, blame, legal conclusion, maturity failure, or sexual pathology.

In this case, Χ functions as restraint under formal temptation. Because the PMS grammar can map the case smoothly, Distance prevents the mapping from becoming authority. It blocks premature closure. It requires the reading to remain revisable, bounded, and non-totalizing.

The calibration issue is that Distance must not become false neutrality. If later concrete material establishes harm, coercion, legal obligation, or real asymmetry, Χ cannot be used to avoid naming it. Distance is not denial. It is the reflective discipline that prevents the analysis from exceeding the evidence.

The overclaim would be to suspend Χ in order to force clarity. A PMS application that drops Distance to secure a moral, diagnostic, legal, or person-level verdict would be invalid within this usage convention.

#### Σ — Integration

Σ is weak. Boundary cases often invite integration: roles, responsibilities, frames, expectations, obligations, and refusals may need coordination. A concrete case might show an integrated solution or a failure of integration. It might show that competing frames were coordinated without erasing difference.

The present case does not show that. No integrated praxis is supplied. No contradiction is resolved. No failure of integration is documented. Therefore Σ can only be a possible need or analytic horizon. It cannot become an achieved structure.

The overclaim would be forced coherence. Integration must not mean smoothing over conflict, suppressing refusal, erasing separation, or treating PMS coherence as sufficient. In boundary cases, preserving difference may be more mature than integrating it. The YAML therefore keeps Σ weak and conditional.

#### Ψ — Self-Binding

Ψ is weak and mostly analytic. Self-Binding would require durable commitment, role ownership, promise, stable normativity, accountability across time, or responsibility-as-self-commitment. Boundary problems can certainly involve such structures: promises, caregiving roles, institutional mandates, long-term obligations, or self-binding to restraint. But none is supplied here.

At the Core level, Ψ is relevant mainly because the analysis itself must be self-bound. The PMS application must not suspend its own guardrails in order to obtain closure. But as a case-level structure, Ψ is not warranted.

The dependency risk is high. Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. Since Σ and Θ remain weak in the concrete case, Ψ cannot be applied strongly. The overclaim would be to infer selfhood, maturity, identity failure, or person-level responsibility from abstract boundary confusion.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The dependency discipline is one of the central achievements of the Core YAML. It prevents the case from becoming a free association around the word “boundary.” PMS supplies a full operator chain, but the presence of the chain in the repository does not mean every operator is active in every case. Later operators cannot be used cheaply.

The minimal active Core signature is best understood as □ ∘ Δ, with Χ as application discipline. Δ supplies the distinction; □ supplies the frame in which the distinction becomes a boundary issue; Χ prevents the analysis from turning the framed distinction into verdict. Everything beyond this minimal structure remains calibration-dependent. ∇ requires directed pressure. Λ requires a meaningful expected absence. Α requires recurrence or stabilization. Ω requires identifiable imbalance. Θ requires temporal sequence or trajectory. Φ requires specified source and target frames. Σ requires coordination of conflicting elements without forced coherence. Ψ requires durable self-binding or commitment across time.

Σ and Ψ therefore remain conditional because the case supplies neither integrated enactment nor durable self-relation. A boundary problem can involve integration, but it does not automatically do so. A responsibility boundary can involve self-binding, but the word “responsibility” does not establish Ψ. The YAML’s restraint is especially important here because Integration and Self-Binding are attractive closure operators. They promise coherence and maturity. But in this case, such promise would exceed the material.

Coherence is not proof. The YAML names coherence risk as high because *Boundary Confusion* maps easily onto many operators: Δ, □, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, Ψ. This easy mapping could become too smooth and under-discriminating if unclear, contested, crossed, violated, negotiated, legal, relational, and institutional boundaries are not separated. A formally coherent PMS reading could still be wrong, too broad, or less discriminating than a rival framework. PMS coherence is therefore a feature of the analysis, not evidence of capture.

Non-capture is not failure. The analysis preserves the possibility that some dimensions of the case require legal, ethical, institutional, relational, cultural, semantic, clinical, phenomenological, or non-PMS frameworks. Non-capture may mark the integrity of the phenomenon rather than a weakness of the model. In this case, non-capture is not a residue to be eliminated. It is part of the discipline that prevents the model from converting all resistance into drift or incomplete integration.

PMS must not become a closure machine. A closure machine would take every rival frame as Φ, every absence as Λ, every repetition as Α, every imbalance as Ω, every unresolved conflict as failed Σ, and every responsibility term as Ψ. That would make PMS powerful but not precise. The completed Core analysis refuses this. It marks statuses as active, weak, conditional, under-specified, analytic-only, or not applicable. It uses falsifier conditions, calibration questions, rival readings, and operationalization requirements to prevent smooth capture.

Invalid dependency shortcuts include using Ω without established Α, using Θ without recurrence or sequence, using Φ without a source and target frame, using Σ as mere desire for coherence, using Ψ without Σ, Θ, and Χ calibration, and using drift patterns without evidence of configuration trajectory. Another invalid shortcut would be to treat derived structures such as Awareness, Coherence, Responsibility, Action, or Dignity-in-Practice as primitives. The YAML explicitly forbids that. They must remain formula-derived PMS constructs.

The discipline is therefore not merely technical. It is ethical and epistemic. It protects the case from overinterpretation, protects persons from ranking or diagnosis, protects rival frameworks from premature dismissal, and protects PMS itself from being misused as a totalizing authority.

### Derived structures

The derived structures are formula-derived PMS constructs. They are not primitive axioms. They do not replace the eleven operators, and their consideration does not imply activation. In this case, they are especially important because terms such as awareness, coherence, responsibility, action, dignity, and selfhood are familiar enough to invite overreading. The YAML prevents that by deriving each structure from operator compositions and assigning bounded statuses.

#### Awareness_A

Awareness_A is weak-to-moderate. Its formula is Θ, □, Δ: sustained, framed differentiation across time. The Core case contains the relevant ingredients only partially. Δ is strong because the case concerns boundary distinctions. □ is strong because those distinctions require a frame. Θ is weak because the case does not supply concrete temporal sequence, although repeated boundary crossing suggests possible temporal relevance.

The case-specific reading is that *Boundary Confusion* involves structural awareness when a distinction between one person’s role, responsibility, space, or decision and another’s remains relevant across time. But this is not phenomenological consciousness. It does not claim that any person is aware, unaware, reflective, defensive, confused, or mature. It is a structural projection: the case-type requires sustained framed differentiation to be intelligible.

The calibration requirement is to identify what is differentiated, which frame makes it relevant, and whether the distinction persists across repeated episodes rather than appearing only as an abstract contrast. Without concrete temporal evidence, Awareness_A cannot become a full enactment score. This is why the later MIP layer marks A-score as not assigned.

#### Coherence_C

Coherence_C is weak. Its formula is Θ, Λ, □, ∇: temporally stabilized structuring of impulse and expectation within a frame. The current case does not supply enough of this chain. ∇ is weak because directed pressure is unspecified. Λ is weak because no expected clarification, refusal, repair, permission, restraint, or decision is concretely absent. Θ is weak because no sequence or trajectory is supplied. □ is active, but Frame alone does not yield Coherence.

The case-specific reading is that Coherence_C may become relevant if boundary confusion stabilizes around expected clarifications, omissions, delays, or repeated unresolved boundary-crossings within a frame. For example, a later concrete case might show that a repeated failure to clarify responsibility creates a coherent but problematic role script. The present case does not show that.

The overclaim would be to treat narrative coherence as truth or adequacy. A coherent story about boundary confusion may still be false, incomplete, imposed, or less discriminating than another framework. ### Example 1 — The shared document that became a role boundary

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “PMS Core structural reading,” before “PMS dependency and calibration discipline.”

A research team maintains a shared draft for a conference paper. One member, A, regularly rewrites sections written by another member, B. At first, this is interpreted as ordinary collaboration: the file is shared, the deadline is close, and everyone has permission to improve the argument. After several rounds, B says that the edits no longer feel like collaboration but like displacement. A replies that the document is collective, that no one “owns” a paragraph, and that the project requires decisive revision. The team lead has not specified whether contributors retain section authority, whether edits require prior discussion, or whether the final argument belongs to the group as a whole. No one has yet accused anyone of misconduct. No formal policy is invoked. The case remains a bounded academic collaboration vignette about role, authorship, and decision boundaries.

This example demonstrates why *Boundary Confusion* cannot be solved by the word “boundary” alone. At the PMS Core level, Δ is immediately visible: there is a distinction between contribution and control, editing and replacing, collaboration and takeover, shared authorship and local authorship. But Δ does not yet decide whether A crossed a boundary. That depends on □, the frame. In a collective drafting frame, A’s conduct may be ordinary revision. In a section-authorship frame, the same conduct may be overreach. In a deadline-management frame, it may be emergency coordination. In a mentorship frame, it may be guidance. In a peer-equality frame, it may become unilateral authority.

The example also shows why ∇ remains cautious. There is directed action: A edits, B objects, the draft changes. But the vignette does not establish motive. It does not show domination, insecurity, hostility, carelessness, rescue, or ambition. ∇ can mark directional pressure toward revision and control of the text, but it cannot become a psychological explanation. Λ may become relevant if an expected clarification is absent: perhaps the team lead should have defined edit authority; perhaps A should have asked before replacing B’s prose; perhaps B should have stated earlier what level of revision was acceptable. Yet the example does not automatically decide which expectation governs. Λ requires a frame-specific expectation, not merely disappointment.

Α and Θ are plausible but still bounded. The repeated edits suggest possible pattern formation, and the sequence across several rounds gives minimal temporality. Still, this is not enough to call the pattern stabilized drift. The example would require more detail: how many revisions, over what period, whether only B’s sections were rewritten, whether A responds differently to different collaborators, whether objections were heard, and whether the process changed after clarification. Ω is also possible but not settled. A may have more technical expertise, institutional standing, confidence, or access to the team lead; B may carry more exposure because their visible contribution is being erased. But asymmetry cannot be presumed simply because B feels displaced.

Φ is central because the dispute is partly a clash of frames. A recontextualizes the file as a shared product. B recontextualizes the same file as a field of contribution boundaries. The team lead may recontextualize it again as a deadline problem. PMS can make these frame shifts visible, but it must not treat every recontextualization as progress. If A uses “collective work” to erase local authorship, Φ may conceal rather than clarify. If B uses “my section” to block all integration, Φ may preserve a boundary that the project frame does not support. The point is not to decide but to show what must be discriminated.

Χ is the main guardrail. The analysis must not jump to “A violated B,” “B is territorial,” “the team lead failed,” or “the group is immature.” Σ remains conditional: the group may need to integrate contribution boundaries and collective authorship, but no integration has yet occurred. Ψ remains weak: durable commitments, authorship obligations, and responsibility-as-self-binding would require explicit agreement or repeated accountability over time.

The PMS-SEX overlay is not substantively relevant. The case contains no sexual scene, no sexual access threshold, no body-boundary or genital meaning, no sexual cost topology, and no after-scene sexual carryover. PMS-SEX would function only as a scope guard if someone tried to import sexuality or embodied intimacy into the word “boundary” by analogy. That would be an overreach. The add-on hook is therefore negative: the example confirms why the SEX overlay must not activate merely because boundary language is present.

MIP would also remain cautious. Awareness and coherence can be discussed at the documentation level: the team may become more aware of role boundaries, and a coherent process may need to be created. But no A-score or M-score should be assigned to A, B, or the team lead from this vignette. The case lacks enough information about knowability, alternatives, power, predictability, and serious integration attempts. AHP would flag an analysis risk if the example were written as though repeated editing already proved responsibility, domination, or immaturity. The precision task is to keep “possible pattern” separate from “finding.”

Forbidden claims are therefore clear. The example does not prove plagiarism, harassment, legal authorship violation, clinical control dynamics, narcissism, immaturity, exploitation, or moral fault. It does not rank A and B. It does not decide the correct policy. It does not validate PMS as sufficient for academic governance. It only demonstrates how a boundary case requires frame discipline before responsibility language can become justified.

PMS helps sort the structure; it does not replace those frameworks.

The article takeaway is that *Boundary Confusion* begins with Δ and □, not with blame. The example shows how PMS can slow down closure: distinguish the boundary, name the frame, test recurrence and asymmetry, preserve rival readings, and refuse Σ or Ψ until the evidence warrants them.

### Example 2 — The family appointment and the responsibility boundary

In a medical-efficiency frame, it may appear pragmatic. In a legal-authority frame, it may be impermissible unless authorized. In a family-history frame, it may repeat an old pattern of overmanagement or rescue.

### Non-capture and rival readings

Non-capture is not an afterthought in this case. It is one of the most important outputs of the completed stack. PMS Core preserves non-capture for legal, ethical, institutional, relational, cultural, semantic, clinical, phenomenological, and non-PMS frameworks. PMS-SEX produces strong non-capture as its main substantive result. MIP preserves non-capture by refusing A/M scoring and full D activation. AHP preserves non-capture by marking formal completeness as an attack surface rather than evidence.

An **epistemological rival reading** would ask what is known, by whom, through what evidence, and under what uncertainty. It would focus less on PMS operator structure and more on warrant. Such a reading preserves the difference between conceptual availability and evidentiary entitlement. PMS can say that a boundary distinction is structurally legible; epistemology asks whether anyone has enough evidence to identify the boundary, the crossing, the frame, the expectation, the harm, or the responsibility. This rival reading is especially important because the case is abstract. It protects against treating formal schema completion as knowledge.

A **phenomenological rival reading** would ask how boundary confusion is lived or experienced. It might examine intrusion, care, anxiety, abandonment, obligation, shame, autonomy, belonging, fear, or recognition as subjective phenomena. PMS explicitly does not claim to capture qualia or inner experience. Its operator grammar may model structures of praxis, but it does not describe what it is like to experience a boundary as violated, uncertain, protective, oppressive, intimate, or necessary. Phenomenology therefore preserves first-person and intersubjective dimensions that PMS does not exhaust.

A **language-practice rival reading** would focus on how the word “boundary” functions in discourse. It might ask whether the case conflates several concepts: limit, right, role, preference, privacy, consent, jurisdiction, norm, expectation, or spatial separation. It might treat *Boundary Confusion* as a semantic or pragmatic problem before it becomes a praxeological one. This reading preserves the possibility that the apparent PMS richness partly arises from ambiguity in the term “boundary.” If the term is doing too much work, conceptual clarification may be required before operator mapping.

An **ethical rival reading** would focus on obligations, care, autonomy, harm, respect, dependence, vulnerability, and justification. It might ask whether a boundary crossing is supportive, intrusive, negligent, coercive, protective, abandoning, or required. PMS can structure asymmetry and responsibility gradients, but it does not by itself supply a normative ethics. Care ethics, relational autonomy, deontological rights, virtue ethics, or harm-based frameworks may discriminate issues that PMS leaves open. Ethics preserves the normative question without reducing it to operator composition.

A **legal or forensic rival reading** would define boundaries through rights, contracts, policies, jurisdiction, liability, consent standards, evidence, custody, ownership, office, procedure, or enforceable mandate. PMS cannot replace that. A boundary may be legally decisive even if PMS reads it as structurally ambiguous. Conversely, a boundary may be praxeologically meaningful without being legally actionable. Legal reading preserves procedural and institutional authority where the case demands it.

A **relational autonomy reading** would examine negotiated agency, dependence, mutual recognition, and the conditions under which boundaries enable rather than block relation. It may preserve the insight that boundaries are not always separations; they may be conditions for non-dominating relation. PMS can model distinction and frame, but relational autonomy can give a richer account of dependence and agency.

A **care-ethics reading** would ask whether crossing a boundary is care, intrusion, rescue, abandonment, obligation, burden, or overreach. It preserves the possibility that boundary crossing is not always a violation and boundary maintenance is not always respect. Without concrete context, PMS cannot decide these. Care ethics keeps context, vulnerability, and relational responsibility visible.

An **institutional role-theory reading** would treat the case as a problem of mandate, office, policy, governance, or role-design. Boundary confusion may arise not from persons but from poorly designed institutions or ambiguous role allocations. PMS can identify role frames and asymmetry, but role theory may better specify how mandates produce boundary conflicts.

A **privacy and disclosure reading** would be especially relevant in examples like the group-chat vignette. It would ask who controls disclosure, what counts as public enough to matter, how social legibility is formed, and whether private information becomes exposed through ordinary conversation. PMS can name frame, publicness risk, and asymmetry, but privacy ethics may better specify the social meaning of disclosure.

A **publication and research-ethics reading** becomes relevant because the present article itself is designed for scientific use. The abstract case may be anonymized and non-decisional, but publication can still stabilize terminology and make a framework appear authoritative. A research-ethics reading preserves the question of transmission: how should formal model analyses be presented so they remain reversible, non-shaming, non-diagnostic, and non-decisional?

An **anti-capture reading** would hold that the case’s abstraction itself defeats strong formalization. On this view, PMS, PMS-SEX, MIP, and AHP may be useful as guardrails, but the case should remain unclosed until a concrete scene exists. This reading preserves humility. It does not reject PMS; it prevents PMS from overclaiming.

None of these rival readings are defects. They are part of the case’s methodological openness. PMS translation does not defeat them unless comparison criteria are explicitly specified and applied. In this stack, rival readings remain epistemically open, and non-capture remains a protected result.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The YAML gives extensive weakening and falsifier logic. This is necessary because *Boundary Confusion* is structurally attractive. Without falsifiers, the model could always find a way to read the case through PMS. The falsifier conditions prevent that.

The PMS reading would be weakened first if no relevant distinction can be identified. If a concrete case shows no meaningful boundary between roles, responsibilities, spaces, decisions, access, authority, privacy, or obligations, then Δ loses its entry point. If the relevant boundary is already explicit, mutually understood, and neither contested nor crossed, then the case may not be *Boundary Confusion* at all.

The PMS reading would also be weakened if it cannot discriminate among different boundary types. Ordinary ambiguity, negotiated flexibility, mutually accepted role-sharing, legal violation, relational disagreement, institutional role conflict, accidental misunderstanding, and recurrent boundary-crossing pattern are not the same. If PMS treats all of them as one boundary configuration, the reading becomes under-discriminating.

The reading becomes over-assimilative if resistance, rupture, refusal, disagreement, missing evidence, unresolved absence, or rival framing is automatically redescribed as drift, failed integration, incomplete calibration, recontextualization, or maturity failure. This is one of the most important falsifiers. PMS must not absorb every non-fit as proof of its own deeper relevance. Sometimes resistance is a legitimate rival frame. Sometimes missing evidence is simply missing evidence. Sometimes refusal is not failed integration but a boundary.

Ω would be weakened if no directional imbalance can be identified once a concrete scene is specified. If role_A and role_B are symmetrical positions in a semantic disagreement, then asymmetry should be removed or marked weak. Α and Θ would be weakened if alleged repetition is not supported by structurally similar episodes, temporal sequence, persistence, accumulation, anticipation, escalation, or failed repair. Φ would be weakened if alleged recontextualization merely renames the case in PMS terms without identifying a source frame, target frame, and discriminative gain. Σ would be weakened if integration functions as forced coherence, consensus pressure, erasure of separation, suppression of refusal, or premature resolution. Ψ or SELF_FIXPOINT would be invalid if the case lacks durable self-binding, promise, role ownership, stable normativity, or responsibility-as-self-commitment and nevertheless produces person-level maturity, identity, or responsibility verdicts.

The PMS-SEX reading has its own weakening logic, although in this case it already resolves as non-capture. A substantive PMS-SEX reading would be weakened if the case remains an abstract boundary-domain projection without sexual praxis, embodied sexual cost, access logic, direct-otherness problem, sexual publicness, body-boundary coding, genital meaning, withdrawal/binding material, or after-scene residue. It would be defeated if no concrete sexual scene, sexualized access threshold, sexual cost topology, direct-otherness problem, embodiment/genital meaning, or withdrawal/binding material is introduced. The MIP reading would be weakened if its not-assigned statuses were converted into hidden scores. The IA-box would require stronger evidence before it could become more determinate. Transparency would require knowing who knew what boundary, in which frame, and under what communicative conditions. It would also need a reason to believe that D-language brings more clarity than shame. In the current case, D remains guardrail-only because no identifiable person-level D-enactment is supplied.

The AHP layer identifies further hardening conditions. The most serious attack surface is that formal completeness may be mistaken for evidentiary completeness.
