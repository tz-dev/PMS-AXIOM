### Why this case matters

Boundary confusion matters because it represents one of the most common and most difficult closure-demands in practical life: the demand to say where one person’s role, responsibility, space, decision, authority, privacy, obligation, or permissible involvement ends and another’s begins. The case is philosophically important because such boundaries are rarely merely factual. They are not only lines in space, rules in a policy, feelings of discomfort, legal entitlements, or private preferences. They are frame-bound structures that become meaningful only within some context of role, relation, expectation, power, time, and consequence.

The closure-demand is therefore strong. When a boundary is unclear, contested, or crossed, the pressure is often to settle the matter quickly: whose role was it, whose responsibility, whose decision, whose space, whose authority, whose burden, whose right to speak, whose right to refuse, whose duty to repair? The problem becomes structurally difficult because different frames may produce different answers. A familial frame may read involvement as care. An institutional frame may read the same involvement as overreach. A legal frame may read a decision boundary differently from an ethical frame. A relational frame may treat flexibility as trust, while a procedural frame may treat the same flexibility as ambiguity that requires correction. The case is therefore not merely about “where the boundary is,” but about what kind of frame makes a boundary visible at all.

This is why the case belongs in AXIOM. AXIOM is not trying to solve the boundary problem by authority. It is not trying to decide metaphysically what persons “really” are, where the self “really” ends, or what a role “essentially” contains. It is also not trying to diagnose boundary confusion as psychological pathology, legal fault, immaturity, sexual dysfunction, manipulation, coercion, or moral failure. Instead, the case belongs in AXIOM because it is a clean example of a classical closure-demand that can be mapped structurally without pretending to settle every possible domain question. The point is cartographic: to show what a disciplined PMS reading can make legible, where add-ons may or may not help, where downstream MIP governance remains under-specified, and where Λ-residue must remain visible.

Boundary confusion is also difficult because it sits close to misuse. If the analysis is too weak, it may falsely neutralize real harm, coercion, intrusion, or responsibility dumping in a later concrete scene. If it is too strong, it may convert mere disagreement, flexibility, cultural difference, non-fit, emergency exception, refusal, silence, or missing evidence into drift, immaturity, pathology, violation, or blame. The completed YAML analyses therefore treat the case as reversible, bounded, and non-tribunal. They preserve the possibility that a concrete scene could later reveal serious asymmetry or harm, while refusing to infer that from the abstract case description alone.

The philosophical difficulty is not only that boundaries are contested. It is that the demand for closure often arrives before the relevant frame has been clarified. The case asks for the distinction between one person’s role, responsibility, space, or decision and another’s. But each of those terms belongs to a different family of closure-demands. A role boundary may be institutional or relational. A responsibility boundary may be ethical, procedural, legal, or practical. A space boundary may be bodily, domestic, symbolic, professional, or digital. A decision boundary may concern authority, consent, consultation, governance, authorship, or accountability. The phrase “boundary confusion” compresses all of these into one label. That compression is precisely why the case is useful for AXIOM: it tests whether a structured reading can keep distinctions open without collapsing them into a single verdict.

The case is not being solved empirically because no empirical material is supplied. There are no interviews, observations, records, messages, policies, timelines, or comparative data. It is not being solved legally because no jurisdiction, statute, contract, right, duty, evidentiary threshold, or due-process context is given. It is not being solved clinically because no clinical presentation, diagnosis, therapeutic context, or safety assessment is provided. It is not being solved metaphysically because PMS does not require an ontology of persons or final boundary substance. It is not being solved diagnostically because no person is the object of assessment. The analysis instead asks: under what structural conditions does “boundary confusion” become a frame-bound difference problem, and what must remain conditional?

The final reason the case matters is methodological. It shows that a successful AXIOM case analysis need not intensify toward stronger and stronger claims. Sometimes the most disciplined result is not closure but guarded non-closure. PMS Core can identify the minimal structure. PMS-SEX can show that an overlay does not activate. MIP can show that scoring is not responsible. AHP can show where the analysis artefact can be hardened without changing the results. The case therefore demonstrates a central AXIOM commitment: mapping is not verdict-making.

The claim is not that any particular person crossed a boundary, failed to respect another, manipulated responsibility, behaved immaturely, or caused harm. The primary case formula remains conservative: `□ ∘ Δ`.

The case type matters. It is not a report of an event. It is not a testimony, not a legal complaint, not a clinical vignette, not a governance incident, not a sexual scene, not an empirical dataset, and not a validation case for PMS. It is a philosophical structural case-type. This permits a high-density conceptual mapping, but it does not permit conclusions that require concrete evidence. The correct analysis therefore works with possibility, dependency, calibration, and falsifier logic rather than verdict.

The concrete-scene status is explicitly negative. The case is not a concrete praxis scene. The empirical boundary is strict. The metaphysical boundary is equally strict. The analysis does not claim to locate the metaphysical boundary between persons, selves, bodies, minds, rights, spaces, or agency. The diagnostic boundary is non-negotiable. The claim boundary can therefore be stated positively and negatively. Positively, the case shows how boundary confusion becomes structurally legible as a framed distinction problem. Negatively, the case does not establish any person-level, legal, clinical, metaphysical, empirical, sexual, maturity, or implementation conclusion. The analysis remains reversible, case-type-bound, non-diagnostic, non-tribunal, and open to rival formalizations.

### Scene and frame structure

The case has no concrete scene boundary. Instead, it has a conceptual scene boundary: the analysis covers situations where the distinction between one person’s role, responsibility, space, or decision and another’s becomes unclear, contested, or repeatedly crossed. This abstract boundary is wide enough to include relational, institutional, familial, ethical, procedural, spatial, privacy-related, access-related, decision-authority, and responsibility-distribution configurations. But it is also too wide to justify any concrete finding.

The frame operator `□` is therefore central. Boundary confusion is not readable without a frame that defines what kind of boundary is at issue. The same action can shift meaning across frames. Helping may be care in one frame and intrusion in another. Shared decision-making may be trust in one frame and overreach in another. Taking responsibility may be repair in one frame and control in another. Refusal may be autonomy in one frame and abandonment in another. The frame decides what counts as a boundary, what counts as crossing, what counts as permission, what counts as role fidelity, and what counts as repair.

Inside the frame are the structural elements named by the case: roles, responsibilities, spaces, decisions, unclear distinctions, contestation, and repeated crossing. Also inside the PMS-readable frame are possible questions of directed pressure, missing clarification, recurrence, asymmetry, temporal accumulation, recontextualization, distance, integration, and binding. But these are not all equally active. The frame licenses their consideration only as possible structures requiring calibration.

Outside the frame are all claims that require evidence not supplied: empirical occurrence, motive, harm, legal status, clinical meaning, metaphysical boundary fact, sexual scene, public exposure, dignity violation, maturity deficit, adultness verdict, or person-level responsibility judgment. Also outside the frame is any attempt to treat PMS coherence as proof that PMS has captured the whole case.

The protected constraints are reversibility, distance, Dignity-in-Practice as praxeological guardrail, non-ranking, non-diagnosis, non-tribunal posture, rival openness, and non-capture. These constraints are not ornamental. They are what prevent the analysis from becoming a closure machine. Boundary confusion is a high-risk topic precisely because people often want a final verdict: who crossed, who failed, who owns the problem, who is immature, who must stop, who must repair. PMS can structure the demand, but it must not satisfy the demand by unearned authority.

Roles are present only abstractly. The MIP analysis names possible abstract role positions: a role-holder whose boundary may be confused with another’s; another role-holder whose role, responsibility, space, or decision may be implicated; a frame-holder or context-defining role; and an analyst role. These are not persons. They are placeholders for possible structural positions. A future concrete case would have to replace them with actual role descriptions, mandates, dependencies, and constraints.

Temporality `Θ` is weak but relevant. The case wording includes “repeatedly crossed,” which suggests possible recurrence, accumulation, pattern formation, or failed repair over time. Yet this remains under-specified. Repetition is not established by the abstract phrase alone. A real Θ analysis would require episodes, sequence, duration, recurrence, anticipation, escalation, repair attempts, repair failures, delayed effects, or lock-in.

The reversibility window is analytic rather than scene-based. Because no concrete scene is supplied, the analysis remains reversible. No conclusion is bound into a verdict. If later material shows explicit boundaries, mutual understanding, negotiated flexibility, emergency exception, legal violation, coercive pattern, or serious harm, the reading could change. This reversibility is a strength, not a lack of commitment.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are possible but not established. `Ω` may become highly relevant in boundary confusion: one party may have greater authority, access, interpretive power, vulnerability, dependency, exposure, or responsibility. But direction and magnitude are unknown. The case therefore cannot identify who bears more cost, who controls the frame, who can contest, who can exit, or who can impose closure. Any stronger asymmetry claim would be overreach.

What follows from the absence of a concrete scene is not that the case is empty. It means the case must be read as a calibration problem. The analysis can specify what would need to be known: the boundary type, active frame, role mandates, expected events, non-events, recurrence evidence, asymmetry direction, temporal sequence, repair possibilities, publicness, and domain-specific standards. This is a substantial result. It shows what a responsible case analysis must not skip.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading begins with `Δ`, Difference. Boundary confusion first becomes legible because a distinction is at stake. One role is not another role; one responsibility is not another responsibility; one space is not another space; one decision is not another decision. The case-specific role of `Δ` is therefore to make separateness visible. But `Δ` is only partially calibrated. The analysis does not yet know which boundary type is primary. Role, responsibility, space, decision, access, privacy, authority, and obligation may all be involved, and multiple types may be conflated under the single phrase “Boundary Confusion.” Pushing `Δ` too far would turn an abstract distinction into a determinate boundary finding without knowing the frame.

`∇`, Impulse or directional pressure, is weak. Boundary confusion often involves pressure: someone pushes, crosses, defends, refuses, yields, clarifies, withdraws, repairs, takes over, or contests. But the case material does not specify the direction of pressure. There is no concrete act. We do not know whether one role intrudes, another resists, both negotiate, an institution fails to define roles, or a frame shifts under pressure. Therefore `∇` can be named only as possible directional structure. Overclaim would occur if the analysis inferred motive, coercion, manipulation, resistance, or repair from the abstract case label.

`□`, Frame, is active and primary. The strongest PMS Core reading is `□ ∘ Δ`: boundary confusion is a framed distinction problem. A boundary only becomes meaningful inside a frame that says what kind of distinction matters and why. The calibration issue is that the relevant frame is not given. It may be relational, institutional, familial, spatial, legal, ethical, procedural, decision-authority-based, or responsibility-distribution-based. Overclaim would occur if PMS selected one frame as primary without evidence, or if it treated PMS framing itself as replacing domain frames such as law or clinical safety.

`Λ`, non-event or residue, is weak but important. Boundary confusion often turns on something expected but absent: missing clarification, missing refusal, missing permission, missing repair, missing restraint, missing acknowledgment, missing answer, missing decision, or missing non-interference. In this case, however, no specific absence is supplied. The calibration question is: what expected event failed to occur, and who or what frame made that absence meaningful? It would be overclaim to treat every silence, delay, ambiguity, disagreement, or unresolved absence as drift, evasion, immaturity, or failure. `Λ` must remain visible as possible non-closure, not be converted into proof.

`Α`, Attractor, is weak. The phrase “repeatedly crossed” suggests possible patterning, but no stable pattern is established. `Α` would become stronger if structurally similar episodes showed recurring boundary scripts: repeated takeover, repeated responsibility dumping, repeated intrusion, repeated ambiguity, repeated refusal to clarify, or repeated collapse of roles. But a single abstract phrase does not establish attractor formation. Overclaim would occur if the analysis treated a named case-type as evidence of compulsive repetition, habit, drift, or stable role script.

`Ω`, Asymmetry, is weak and requires careful calibration. Boundary confusion may be shaped by power, access, vulnerability, obligation, interpretive authority, institutional authority, dependency, exposure, or responsibility gradients. One role may be able to define the boundary while another bears consequences. One may be able to cross without cost while another cannot refuse. But none of this is specified. `Ω` becomes analytically important because boundary confusion often becomes practically serious under asymmetry, but in this case its direction is unknown. Overclaim would occur if possible asymmetry were moralized, personified, or treated as proof of domination, vulnerability, guilt, or entitlement.

`Θ`, Temporality, is also weak. Boundary confusion may unfold across time through repetition, accumulation, delay, anticipation, repair attempts, repair failures, escalation, or lock-in. The YAML especially marks `Θ` because repeated crossing would matter if later established. But the present case has no timeline. `Θ` cannot be used to claim persistent harm, chronic delay, accumulated responsibility, or pattern lock-in. Overclaim would occur if “repeatedly crossed” were treated as sufficient evidence of temporal overload or irreversible trajectory.

`Φ`, Recontextualization, is weak but structurally promising. Boundary confusion often involves frame shifts: help becomes intrusion; shared responsibility becomes overreach; autonomy becomes abandonment; flexibility becomes violation; role support becomes role capture. `Φ` would make visible how the same enactment changes meaning when moved from one frame to another. But it requires source frame, target frame, and discriminative gain. Overclaim would occur if recontextualization merely renamed the case in PMS terms, absorbed disagreement into PMS vocabulary, or treated rival framing as incomplete PMS calibration.

`Χ`, Distance, is satisfied at the level of analysis. The YAML marks `Χ` as operative because the analysis preserves reflective distance, reversibility, non-verdict status, and restraint. `Χ` prevents the case from becoming a tribunal. It blocks premature sexualization, diagnosis, legal conclusion, moral condemnation, maturity ranking, or drift labeling. The calibration issue is that `Χ` must not become false neutrality. If a later concrete scene establishes harm, coercion, legal violation, or enforceable obligation, distance must not erase that. But at the abstract level, `Χ` is a major validity condition.

`Σ`, Integration, is weak. Boundary confusion may require integration of conflicting elements: role distinction and shared responsibility, autonomy and care, privacy and access, authority and participation, refusal and repair, flexibility and rule clarity. But no concrete elements are supplied. Integration would become illegitimate if it forced coherence by smoothing over conflict, suppressing refusal, erasing separation, or treating PMS coherence as proof. In this case, `Σ` remains conditional. The analysis must not integrate what has not yet been specified.

`Ψ`, Self-binding, is weak. Responsibility-as-self-commitment, durable role ownership, promise, accountability relation, stable normativity, or binding decision could become relevant in a concrete boundary case. But the abstract material does not establish durable self-binding. It names responsibility as one possible boundary domain, not as a calibrated PMS `Responsibility_R` or `SELF_FIXPOINT`. Overclaim would occur if `Ψ` were used to rank maturity, identify role failure, assign moral responsibility, or bind a person to a boundary verdict without evidence.

The final PMS Core formula is therefore deliberately conservative: `□ ∘ Δ`. Boundary Confusion is minimally a framed distinction problem. Conditional extensions into `∇`, `Λ`, `Α`, `Ω`, `Θ`, `Φ`, `Χ`, `Σ`, and `Ψ` become legitimate only where concrete calibration shows directed pressure, meaningful absence, recurrence, asymmetry, temporal trajectory, frame transformation, reflective distance, integration, or self-binding.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The completed PMS YAML insists that later operators cannot be used cheaply. This is not merely a formal rule; it is the central safeguard of the case. Boundary confusion invites premature closure. Once a distinction feels unclear or crossed, the temptation is to jump directly to responsibility, blame, repair demand, legal conclusion, diagnosis, or identity judgment. PMS dependency discipline blocks that jump.

`Σ` cannot be used merely because the analysis can tell a coherent story. Integration requires identified elements to integrate, a frame in which their relation matters, and preservation of difference. In this case, the elements are mostly placeholders. We do not yet know whether the relevant conflict is between care and autonomy, role and authority, privacy and access, duty and capacity, decision and participation, or responsibility and control. Therefore `Σ` remains conditional. A smooth PMS story would be dangerous if it integrated abstractions while erasing the unresolved material that should remain Λ.

`Ψ` is even more expensive. Self-binding requires prior integration, temporality, and distance. It cannot be inferred from the word “responsibility” alone. A boundary case may eventually involve promise, commitment, durable role duty, institutional accountability, or self-binding to repair. But in the current case, no such structure is established. Using `Ψ` prematurely would transform a conceptual distinction into a binding responsibility claim. That would violate both PMS discipline and the MIP score-hygiene logic.

Coherence is not proof. This is one of the central lessons of the completed YAMLs. PMS can produce a coherent structural reading of Boundary Confusion, but coherence does not establish empirical truth, legal relevance, diagnostic validity, moral authority, or full capture. A coherent PMS derivation must still show discriminative force. It must distinguish ordinary ambiguity from negotiated flexibility, mutual role-sharing, accidental misunderstanding, legal violation, relational disagreement, institutional role conflict, and recurrent boundary-crossing pattern. If it cannot make those distinctions, it becomes under-discriminating.

Non-capture is not failure. The PMS reading deliberately leaves rival readings open. Some concrete boundary cases may be better handled by law, safety protocols, clinical care, institutional policy, relational ethics, cultural interpretation, or phenomenological description. PMS is allowed to map structural closure-demands; it is not allowed to absorb every remainder into its own grammar. Refusal, rupture, disagreement, absence, or non-fit may be meaningful without being PMS drift.

Invalid dependency shortcuts include: treating `Δ` plus discomfort as evidence of crossing; treating `Λ` as evasion; treating repetition language as calibrated `Α` or `Θ`; treating possible asymmetry as domination; treating a frame shift as proof that one frame is correct; treating `Χ` as neutrality that ignores harm; treating `Σ` as consensus; treating `Ψ` as person-level maturity or responsibility verdict; and treating PMS coherence as closure by authority.

The calibration discipline is therefore the heart of the case. It keeps the analysis from doing what boundary-confusion discourse often does: collapsing uncertainty into a verdict before the relevant frame, roles, asymmetry, evidence, and time structure have been made explicit.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core YAML treats drift-pattern language cautiously. Boundary confusion can certainly become a drift-prone configuration in concrete life, but the abstract case does not itself establish drift. This distinction is essential. In PMS and AXIOM usage, a drift pattern is not a diagnosis, not a character type, not a moral verdict, and not a hidden person label. It is a conditional structural risk: a way a configuration may become unstable, over-assimilative, under-discriminating, coercive, or self-sealing if certain operator conditions are actually present.

The first relevant drift-pattern status is `AD_A>>E`, the asymmetry between awareness and enactment. In a concrete boundary-confusion case, one might imagine a configuration in which awareness expands while action remains suppressed or chronically delayed: parties know that a boundary is unclear, know that clarification is needed, know that responsibility is misallocated, or know that space and decision authority are being blurred, but no effective action follows. Such a configuration could generate a recognizable awareness-action split. In the completed YAML, however, this remains under-specified. There is no concrete actor whose awareness can be assessed and no action sequence whose delay can be measured. Therefore the awareness axis cannot be treated as over-expanded in a participant-level sense, and the action axis cannot be treated as suppressed or chronically delayed as a finding. At most, the risk is conceptually visible: boundary confusion can become structurally costly when recognition of ambiguity does not translate into clarification, refusal, repair, review, or reallocation. But this is not yet a case finding.

The second relevant drift-pattern status is `AD_Sigma_low`, the low-integration or unstable-coherence pattern. Boundary confusion can easily create volatile coherence. Competing frames may each make local sense while failing to integrate: one role reads an act as help, another as intrusion; one frame reads responsibility as care, another as overreach; one party reads flexibility as trust, another as lack of protection; an institution reads ambiguity as discretion, while an affected role reads it as abandonment. If no integration occurs, the configuration may become unstable. Responsibility may become inconsistent because the frame never clarifies who is authorized, who is exposed, who may refuse, who may decide, or who must repair.

Yet here again, the YAML does not convert risk into finding. No concrete conflicting elements are supplied. `Σ` is weak and cannot be forced. `AD_Sigma_low` therefore remains a possible drift risk if a later scene shows volatility, inconsistent responsibility, failed coordination, or repeated non-integration. It is not an established pattern in the abstract case. This preserves an important AXIOM boundary: under-specification is not failure, and non-assignment is not a low score. A case can be structurally relevant precisely because it shows what cannot responsibly be claimed yet.

The main coherence risk is that PMS may fit the case too smoothly. Boundary confusion maps elegantly onto `□ ∘ Δ`, with possible extensions into `Λ`, `Ω`, `Θ`, `Φ`, `Σ`, and `Ψ`. That elegance is dangerous if it turns into automatic assimilation. The YAML explicitly guards against this. PMS coherence must show discriminative force. It must distinguish ordinary ambiguity from negotiated flexibility, mutual role-sharing, accidental misunderstanding, legal violation, relational disagreement, institutional role conflict, and recurrent boundary-crossing pattern. If PMS cannot discriminate these, the reading becomes too smooth and under-discriminating.

The main under-discrimination risk is especially acute around `Λ`, `Α`, `Ω`, and `Σ`. Missing clarification must not automatically become evasion. Repetition language must not automatically become attractor formation. Possible asymmetry must not automatically become domination. Integration must not automatically become consensus. Rival framing must not automatically become incomplete PMS calibration. Resistance, refusal, rupture, non-fit, absence, or disagreement may be legitimate, externally governed, unresolved, or non-capturable. PMS drift language is admissible only where a concrete configuration shows drift conditions. It is never a substitute for evidence.

This is why the PMS drift-pattern result is structurally modest but analytically important. The case identifies where drift could emerge, and what would have to be checked, without calling drift present. The drift catalogue remains a map of risks, not a verdict engine.

### Layer interaction

The layer interaction is deliberately non-mixing. PMS Core remains the base structural reading. PMS Core says: Boundary Confusion is minimally a framed distinction problem, `□ ∘ Δ`, with later operators conditional. PMS-SEX does not alter that. AHP finally hardens the previous MIP analysis as an artefact, not as a case verdict.

What remains PMS Core is the operator grammar: `Δ`, `∇`, `□`, `Λ`, `Α`, `Ω`, `Θ`, `Φ`, `Χ`, `Σ`, `Ψ`; the dependency structure; the derived-structure discipline; the claim boundary; the non-capture rule; and the conservative formula `□ ∘ Δ`. PMS Core is the only layer that can define the PMS operator reading. All later layers are subordinate.

What PMS-SEX sharpens is the overlay boundary: it asks whether a boundary case activates sexuality-specific scene grammar. In this case, it does not. The overlay makes visible the absence of sexual scene conditions and protects the analysis from accidental sexual expansion. None of these are active now.

What MIP sharpens is governance and responsibility discipline. MIP asks whether the analysis can responsibly produce awareness, coherence, responsibility, power-to-act, IA-box, D-profile, or maturity-in-practice results. The answer is mostly no at the participant level. MIP therefore contributes a disciplined non-score. A-score is not assigned. M-score is not assigned. IA-box is not assigned. D-module is not activated. Transmission status remains working note. This is not evasion; it is the correct result under missing scene evidence.

MIP also makes explicit why non-assignment is itself a substantive boundary result. Awareness cannot be assigned to participants because no participant knowledge, role-perception, or information access is given. Responsibility cannot be assigned because knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and integration attempts are unknown. Power-to-act cannot be assessed because no real levers, exit paths, dependencies, or constraints are supplied. The IA-box cannot be classified because no concrete asymmetry has been tested for transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. The D-module remains off because there is no concrete D-enactment, no safe communication space, and no reason to risk a dignity-reading where the case is only conceptual. MIP therefore does not weaken the analysis by refusing scores; it protects the analysis from pretending to know what the case material does not contain.

What AHP sharpens is the analysis artifact itself. It identifies attack points and hardening actions: prior-analysis dependency, risk of confusing high precision with case certainty, IA non-assignment qualification, non-score status hygiene, D-language notation, role-label ambiguity, and polished YAML over-authority. AHP does not change the MIP results. It does not make the analysis transmissible as a verdict. It improves auditability and misuse resistance.

AHP’s most important contribution is to prevent the article’s polish from becoming false authority. A high Precision Heuristic concerns the discipline of the analysis artefact, not the certainty of the case. It means that the article has made its boundaries, non-scores, non-activation decisions, and transmission limits easier to audit. It does not mean that the boundary dispute is empirically clearer, that an IA verdict is available, or that maturity can now be assessed. In this stack, AHP hardens language; it does not harden claims.

Overlay language could overreach in several ways. PMS-SEX could overreach if it sexualized the case from the words “space,” “crossed,” or “responsibility.” MIP could overreach if it turned non-assignment into hidden low maturity, or if it used abstract boundary confusion to issue responsibility findings. AHP could overreach if high Precision Heuristic were read as high case certainty. PMS itself could overreach if the smoothness of `□ ∘ Δ` were treated as final capture. The completed YAMLs prevent these by keeping layer boundaries explicit.

The central interaction across the stack is therefore not additive escalation but controlled differentiation. PMS Core establishes the minimal reading. PMS-SEX tests and rejects a sexuality-specific activation. MIP tests and rejects scoring. AHP tests and hardens the analysis artifact. Each layer adds something, but none is allowed to overwrite the previous layer. The add-on remains an overlay. MIP remains downstream. AHP remains second-order. PMS Base remains unchanged.

Non-closure remains visible across all layers. PMS Core leaves `Λ` and rival formalizations open. PMS-SEX leaves sexual activation absent. MIP leaves scores unassigned. AHP leaves transmission status unchanged. The layered result is therefore not “more closure” at each stage. It is more disciplined non-closure.

### Example 1 — Shared Help, Unclear Decision

A small team is preparing a public report. One member, Mara, is formally responsible for the final wording. Another member, Jonas, has more technical expertise and has helped shape the analysis throughout the project. During the final week, Jonas begins editing the report directly, rewriting several passages and rearranging the order of recommendations. Mara does not immediately object, because some of the edits improve accuracy. But after two days, she notices that the document no longer reflects the decision structure she had agreed to manage. Jonas says he was “only helping” and that the report is “shared work anyway.” Mara says the distinction between help and taking over has become unclear.

This example demonstrates the basic PMS Core structure of Boundary Confusion without turning it into a verdict. The relevant distinction is not whether Jonas is a good or bad collaborator, or whether Mara is too protective of her role. The first structural question is simpler: which boundary is at stake? The example contains several possible boundaries at once: role boundary, decision-authority boundary, authorship boundary, responsibility boundary, and perhaps institutional mandate. PMS begins with `Δ`, because a distinction between Mara’s responsibility and Jonas’s contribution has become salient. But `Δ` alone does not settle the matter. The distinction becomes meaningful only within a frame: project governance, authorship norms, team collaboration, accuracy responsibility, deadline pressure, or final accountability. This is why the minimal PMS formula remains `□ ∘ Δ`.

The case also shows why later operators cannot be used cheaply. There is possible `∇`, because Jonas acts into the document and Mara experiences pressure around her role, but the example does not yet establish motive, aggression, evasion, or manipulation. There may be `Λ` if an expected clarification never occurs: perhaps nobody said who had final edit authority, or Mara did not explicitly mark the boundary when Jonas began editing. Yet this absence remains ambiguous. It could be a failure of communication, a normal collaborative gap, an institutional omission, or a reasonable assumption under deadline pressure. It should not automatically be translated into drift or maturity failure.

`Α` and `Θ` would become stronger only if the pattern repeated: if Jonas repeatedly takes over final decisions in similar projects, or if the team repeatedly leaves formal responsibility unclear until conflict emerges. In this bounded vignette, there is only a short sequence. `Ω` is also possible but not established. Jonas may have technical authority; Mara may have formal responsibility; the institution may make Mara accountable for final output; Jonas may bear epistemic burden for accuracy. The direction of asymmetry is therefore not obvious. One role may carry expertise; another may carry accountability. Treating either person as simply dominant or weak would overread the scene.

`Φ` is visible as a rival-frame possibility. Jonas frames his action as help; Mara frames it as role displacement. The analysis must not decide too quickly which frame is correct. `Χ` is required from the analyst: pause before assigning fault, preserve reversibility, and ask what frame the team actually uses. `Σ` remains conditional: integration would require a concrete coordination of expertise and final responsibility, not a forced consensus that “both were partly right.” `Ψ` is also conditional. If Mara formally owns final wording, there may be self-binding to the role; if Jonas is accountable for technical accuracy, he may have another kind of responsibility. But neither can be converted into a person-level maturity verdict from the vignette alone.

The PMS-SEX overlay is not activated. The words “boundary,” “space,” “taking over,” and “responsibility” may appear in many domains, but nothing here supplies sexual scene material, body remainder, sexual cost topology, public sexual legibility, adult sexual reciprocity, or sexuality-specific drift. The overlay would therefore serve only as a guardrail: do not sexualize the example by inference.

MIP remains under-specified. No A-score or M-score should be assigned. Mara’s awareness, Jonas’s awareness, and the team’s responsibility cannot be scored without knowing the explicit role mandate, previous agreements, real alternatives, time pressure, predictability, and repair options. The IA-box would also remain unassigned unless a concrete asymmetry is specified and tested for transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility. AHP would treat the example as a useful analysis-artifact test: does the prose preserve non-score status, avoid person labels, and prevent “help” or “overreach” from becoming verdict terms?

Forbidden claims are therefore clear. The example does not show that Jonas violated Mara’s role. It does not show that Mara failed to communicate. It does not establish manipulation, incompetence, immaturity, legal responsibility, workplace misconduct, or psychological pattern. It does not validate PMS. It only shows how boundary confusion can become PMS-readable as a frame-dependent distinction problem.

The non-capture note is that in a real organization, the decisive framework might be a contract, editorial policy, workplace hierarchy, authorship norm, or project-management rule. PMS can map the structural ambiguity, but it cannot replace those domain standards.

The article takeaway is that boundary confusion often looks like a dispute over behavior, but the first analytic task is to identify the frame that makes the behavior count as help, overreach, shared work, or role displacement. Until that frame is calibrated, the mature PMS reading remains conservative.

### Example 2 — The Open Door That Was Not a Standing Invitation

In a shared apartment, one resident, Eli, often leaves his door open while working. Another resident, Sam, sometimes steps in to ask short questions or continue household conversations. At first, Eli does not object. Over time, Sam begins entering more frequently, sometimes while Eli is on calls or preparing for deadlines. Eli finally says, “Leaving the door open does not mean I am always available.” Sam replies, “I thought the open door meant it was fine. You never said it was a problem.”

This vignette is deliberately ordinary. Its usefulness lies in showing how easily a boundary can be interpreted as obvious by one participant and ambiguous by another. The relevant boundary is partly spatial, partly temporal, partly attentional, and partly relational. The open door is not simply a physical fact. It becomes meaningful only in a frame: household friendliness, privacy, availability, interruption norms, shared space, or individual work time. PMS Core therefore begins again with `Δ` and `□`: a distinction between shared access and personal availability becomes contested within a frame that had not been explicitly stabilized.

The example demonstrates the importance of `Λ`. The conflict turns on non-events: Eli did not explicitly say, “Please knock before entering”; Sam did not explicitly ask, “Does an open door mean I can come in?” The absence of clarification becomes meaningful only after repeated entries accumulate. But PMS must not overread this absence. Eli’s silence is not necessarily consent, avoidance, passivity, or immaturity. Sam’s assumption is not necessarily entitlement, intrusion, or disregard. The non-event is structurally relevant, but it does not yet decide responsibility.

`Α` and `Θ` are stronger here than in a one-off case, because the pattern repeats. The repeated entries may begin to form an attractor: open door → entry → interruption → no explicit correction → continued assumption. Temporality matters because the meaning of the open door changes over time. A single entry might be harmless; repeated entries during calls or deadlines may create a different structure. Still, even here, `Α` and `Θ` are not verdicts. Repetition shows pattern potential, not blame by itself.

`Ω` is possible but must be calibrated. If Eli has less ability to refuse because he wants household peace, Sam may have an access advantage. If Sam reasonably relies on visible signals and Eli never clarifies them, Sam may lack relevant knowledge. If one resident owns the apartment or controls shared resources, asymmetry may run differently. The example therefore blocks a cheap asymmetry reading. PMS must ask: who can define the access rule, who bears interruption cost, who can exit the interaction, who can repair, and who has realistic alternatives?

`Φ` appears when the same open door is recontextualized. For Sam, it was an availability signal. For Eli, it was ventilation, habit, or non-isolation, not a standing invitation. Recontextualization can clarify the conflict, but it can also become a way to erase one frame with another. The analysis should preserve both frames until the relevant household norm is negotiated or specified.

`Χ` is central. A mature analysis must not rush to say “Sam violated privacy” or “Eli failed to communicate.” Either may become true in a fuller case, but not from the vignette alone. `Σ` would require a new coordination: perhaps an open door means “visible but not interruptible,” or a knock rule is introduced, or deadline hours are marked. `Ψ` would become relevant only if the residents bind themselves to a new shared rule over time. Without such binding, PMS cannot claim stable responsibility-as-self-commitment.

The PMS-SEX overlay remains non-activating. The example includes a room, entry, privacy, and personal space, but no sexual scene, bodily exposure, sexual role-position, sexual cost topology, public sexual legibility, or adult sexual reciprocity. This is precisely the kind of example where the overlay’s restraint matters. “Space” and “entry” are not sufficient to infer sexuality-specific structure. PMS-SEX would add only the guardrail that body remainder, publicness, and sexual cost topology must not be invented.

MIP status remains mostly non-scoring. One might be tempted to score Eli’s awareness or Sam’s responsibility, but that would be premature. Eli’s A-awareness of Sam’s interpretation is unclear. Sam’s A-awareness of Eli’s work boundary is unclear. M-responsibility cannot be assigned without knowledge, predictability, power to act, alternatives, and integration attempts. The IA-box could become useful if a concrete asymmetry appears: for example, if one resident has authority over the housing arrangement, or if repeated entry creates a real inability to protect work time. But as written, IA remains under-specified. AHP would flag a likely attack point: the example is readable enough to tempt verdicts. The article must therefore keep its language disciplined.

Forbidden claims include: Sam is intrusive; Eli is conflict-avoidant; an open door legally or morally means permission; repeated entry proves disrespect; silence proves consent; discomfort proves violation; household ambiguity proves immaturity. None of these follows from the vignette alone.

The non-capture note is that real household boundaries may be governed by culture, disability, work conditions, tenancy rules, trauma history, family norms, or material constraints. PMS can map the boundary ambiguity, but it cannot replace the lived or institutional context that determines what repair is appropriate.

The article takeaway is that boundary confusion often arises not from one dramatic crossing but from repeated low-level non-events. The open door becomes a boundary object because it carries more meaning than either participant explicitly gave it. PMS helps preserve that structure without deciding the case prematurely.

### Example 3 — When Responsibility Becomes Frame Ownership

A university department asks a senior coordinator, Dr. Vale, to “support” a new lecturer, Rowan, during the first semester. The instruction is informal. Dr. Vale begins by answering questions and explaining procedures. Soon, however, she starts reviewing Rowan’s course emails before they are sent, advising students to copy her on requests, and making small changes to Rowan’s seminar schedule “to prevent problems.” Rowan feels that support has become supervision, but is unsure whether objecting would seem ungrateful or unprofessional. Dr. Vale says she is simply carrying responsibility for a new colleague’s success.

This example is more structurally loaded than the first two because it shows how boundary confusion can arise through responsibility language. The vignette is still bounded. It does not establish misconduct, harassment, incompetence, legal breach, or institutional liability. But it makes visible a recurring AXIOM problem: responsibility may clarify a frame, or it may become a route into another person’s role-space.

PMS Core begins with `Δ`: there is a distinction between support and supervision, advice and control, responsibility for one’s own teaching and responsibility for another’s integration, institutional care and role capture. The frame `□` is unstable because the department’s instruction is informal. “Support” is not defined. It could mean availability for questions, mentorship, oversight, quality assurance, pastoral care, risk management, or temporary delegated authority. Without frame clarification, each action can be read differently.

`∇` appears as directional pressure: Dr. Vale moves from answering questions into reviewing communications and adjusting schedules; Rowan feels pressure around objection, gratitude, and professional standing. But PMS must not infer motive. Dr. Vale may be conscientious, controlling, anxious, institutionally pressured, or acting under unclear expectations. Rowan may be reasonably concerned, uncertain, conflict-avoidant, strategically silent, or still learning the frame. The vignette does not decide.

`Λ` is significant. The missing event is formal clarification: what kind of support was authorized, what remains Rowan’s decision, whether students should copy Dr. Vale, and whether schedule changes require consent. This non-event structures the situation. It is not merely absence; it becomes meaningful because actions accumulate under the undefined support frame.

`Α` and `Θ` are plausible but still conditional. The shift from answering questions to reviewing emails and changing schedules may suggest pattern formation over time. If this continues, an attractor could form: support → preventive intervention → reduced autonomy → more dependency or oversight → further intervention. But the example remains a vignette, not a full timeline. `Α` and `Θ` are visible as risk conditions, not established drift findings.

`Ω` is central but must be handled carefully. Dr. Vale has seniority, institutional knowledge, and perhaps informal authority. Rowan may have less status, less security, and less ability to refuse. But Dr. Vale may also bear institutional responsibility if new lecturers are unsupported. The asymmetry is therefore not morally simple. It must be mapped by channels: status, access to students, control over procedures, accountability for course quality, reputational exposure, and capacity to object. PMS does not say asymmetry equals abuse. It says asymmetry must be named before responsibility can be understood.

`Φ` appears in the rival framing. Dr. Vale frames her actions as responsibility and protection; Rowan experiences them as supervision or role capture. The same practice is recontextualized across support, governance, care, control, and institutional risk. PMS can make this visible, but it must not decide by vocabulary. Calling an act “support” does not settle its outer structure. Calling it “control” does not prove bad intent.

`Χ` is required to keep the analysis from becoming a tribunal. `Σ` would become relevant if the department creates a clearer arrangement: what Dr. Vale may advise on, what Rowan decides, how students communicate, when review ends, and how disagreement is handled. `Ψ` would become relevant only if roles bind themselves to clarified commitments over time: Dr. Vale to restraint and transparent support, Rowan to participation or refusal pathways, and the department to formal role definition.

This is the strongest of the three examples for showing where MIP may become relevant, while still refusing scores. MIP would ask about awareness, coherence, responsibility, and power-to-act. Did Dr. Vale know that support had become directive? Did Rowan have a real option to object? Did the department create ambiguity and then leave both parties to absorb it? Was the asymmetry transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible? These are maturity-in-practice questions. But no A-score or M-score should be assigned from the vignette alone. MIP would remain a working note unless more evidence is provided.

The IA-box is tempting here, because asymmetry is more visible. But even then, it should not become a verdict. Transparency is unclear: the support arrangement was informal. Justification is unclear: support may serve a legitimate good, but the scope is not defined. Time-boundedness is unclear: no end condition is specified. Reversibility is unclear: Rowan’s ability to object safely is unknown. Therefore the IA-box may identify questions but not produce a result.

PMS-SEX remains non-activating. The example contains role support, authority, responsibility, and possible control, but no sexual scene, no body remainder, no sexual publicness, no sexual cost topology, no adult sexual reciprocity, and no SEX drift finding. However, the PMS-SEX overlay is still useful as a negative guardrail. It reminds the article that `responsibility-to-authority` is a PMS-SEX drift handle only in sexual-praxis contexts where sexual cost topology, Ω/Θ distribution, and re-binding conditions are actually present. In this vignette, that handle is not activated. The article may speak in ordinary prose about responsibility language becoming frame ownership, but it must not import the PMS-SEX drift category as a sexuality-specific finding.

AHP/QC status is especially relevant to how this example is written. It is vivid enough to invite judgment. The article must therefore harden the language: Dr. Vale is not labeled controlling; Rowan is not labeled passive; the department is not labeled negligent; the vignette is not treated as legal or HR evidence. AHP would likely flag any sentence that converts unclear support into established domination, or unclear discomfort into established harm.

Forbidden claims include: Dr. Vale abused authority; Rowan lacked maturity; the department violated policy; support is really control; seniority is inherently coercive; responsibility always becomes authority; Rowan’s discomfort proves boundary violation. These may be possible claims in a fuller case, but the vignette does not supply the evidence.

The non-capture note is that a real case might require institutional governance, employment law, mentoring policy, union rules, professional ethics, or safeguarding procedures. PMS and MIP can map the structural ambiguity, but cannot replace those frameworks.

The article takeaway is that boundary confusion becomes most difficult when responsibility language is itself the vehicle of crossing. A role can enter another role-space in the name of care, prevention, or support. PMS makes that structure visible, MIP asks what mature handling would require, and the add-on/QC layers prevent the example from becoming a verdict before the frame is specified.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The YAMLs preserve multiple rival readings. These rival readings are not defects. They indicate what PMS, PMS-SEX, MIP, or AHP may not fully capture.

An epistemological rival reading would treat boundary confusion as a problem of knowledge, evidence, and uncertainty. The central question would be: who knows what boundary applies, who has evidence of crossing, what counts as clarification, and what remains unknown? This reading preserves the fact that boundary disputes often involve incomplete information and contested evidence. PMS can map this as `□`, `Λ`, and `Χ`, but it does not replace epistemic standards or evidence practices.

A phenomenological rival reading would focus on lived experience: how boundary confusion feels from inside the situation. It might attend to discomfort, intrusion, relief, care, pressure, ambiguity, shame, safety, or loss of agency. PMS does not deny such experience, but the current case does not provide phenomenological material. A phenomenological reading preserves the first-person texture that PMS structural mapping may bracket.

A language-practice reading would treat “boundary,” “role,” “responsibility,” “space,” and “decision” as terms whose meaning depends on use, convention, and practical language games. It would ask how these words function in a community, institution, family, or relationship. This preserves semantic and pragmatic variability. PMS can map frame-bound usage, but it does not provide a full philosophy of language for the case.

An ethical reading would focus on care, autonomy, respect, obligation, vulnerability, and responsibility. It might ask whether boundary crossing is wrong, when care becomes control, when refusal is owed respect, and what forms of repair are required. PMS can structure ethical closure-demands, but it does not itself issue the ethical verdict. The ethical reading preserves substantive normative questions that PMS leaves outside its claim boundary.

A legal reading would ask whether a boundary is recognized by law, policy, contract, consent standards, privacy rules, professional duty, property rights, workplace procedure, or due process. This reading preserves enforceable categories and evidentiary thresholds that PMS must not simulate. In a concrete case, legal analysis could become primary.

A clinical or therapeutic reading would ask whether boundary confusion relates to safety, trauma, attachment, family systems, coercion, capacity, or psychological distress. PMS does not diagnose and cannot replace clinical judgment. Such a reading preserves care, treatment, and risk-assessment contexts that are out of scope here.

An institutional reading would focus on role design, unclear mandates, accountability gaps, escalation channels, and review procedures. It may be more appropriate if the boundary confusion arises from organizational ambiguity rather than interpersonal failure. PMS can map responsibility and frame ambiguity, but institutional analysis may better specify levers.

A cultural or relational reading would preserve variation in norms around space, obligation, family roles, care, authority, privacy, and decision participation. What one frame calls overreach another may call solidarity. PMS must not erase this by assuming a universal boundary norm.

An anti-capture reading would insist that some boundary experiences resist formalization. A refusal may not need to justify itself in PMS terms. A person may reject the frame. A rupture may remain unresolved. Non-fit may be the point. This reading preserves the possibility that PMS mapping is useful but not exhaustive.

The PMS-SEX overlay also leaves rival readings open. The absence of SEX activation is not a claim that sexuality could never matter in a future boundary case. It only says that this case material does not warrant sexual overlay activation. If a later scene includes sexual bodies, consent, public sexual exposure, sexual cost topology, or sexual role-positions, PMS-SEX may become more active. Until then, non-activation preserves non-capture.

MIP and AHP also preserve rival readings by refusing to score. MIP does not say the case lacks maturity relevance; it says maturity cannot be responsibly assessed from this material. AHP does not say the analysis is final; it identifies how to harden it and where reuse risks remain.

The three examples sharpen this non-capture discipline. The report example may ultimately be governed by authorship and project authority. The apartment example may require tenancy norms, household culture, work constraints, or disability-related context. The university support example may require institutional governance or employment rules. In each case, PMS maps structural ambiguity, but does not absorb the decisive domain framework. Rival readings preserve information PMS does not own.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would be weakened if a concrete scene showed no relevant distinction between roles, responsibilities, spaces, decisions, access, authority, privacy, or obligations. If the supposed boundary is already explicit, mutually understood, non-contested, non-crossed, and low-consequence, then the boundary-confusion reading would lose force. It would also be weakened if the issue is not boundary confusion at all but a different primary structure: factual misunderstanding, semantic ambiguity, legal breach, safety risk, clinical crisis, institutional malfunction, or simple preference conflict.

The PMS reading would be under-discriminating if it could not distinguish ordinary ambiguity from negotiated flexibility, mutually accepted role-sharing, legal violation, relational disagreement, institutional role conflict, accidental misunderstanding, and recurrent boundary-crossing pattern. This is the central falsifier for PMS elegance. If every such situation maps to the same `□ ∘ Δ` without further discrimination, the analysis becomes too general.

The reading becomes over-assimilative if resistance, rupture, refusal, disagreement, missing evidence, unresolved absence, or rival framing is automatically redescribed as drift, failed integration, incomplete calibration, recontextualization failure, or maturity failure. A valid PMS reading must allow refusal to remain refusal, absence to remain unresolved, and rival frames to remain live.

The `Ω` reading is weakened if no directional imbalance of power, exposure, capacity, vulnerability, obligation, responsibility, access, or authority can be identified once a concrete scene is specified. Since `Ω` is weak here, future scene data could either strengthen or dissolve it.

The `Α` and `Θ` readings are weakened if alleged repeated crossing is not supported by structurally similar episodes, temporal sequence, persistence, accumulation, anticipation, escalation, or failed repair. The word “repeatedly” is not enough. Recurrence must be shown.

The `Φ` reading is weakened if alleged recontextualization merely renames the case in PMS terms without identifying source frame, target frame, and discriminative gain. A frame shift must do analytic work; it cannot be decorative vocabulary.

The `Σ` reading is weakened if integration functions as forced coherence, consensus pressure, erasure of separation, suppression of refusal, or premature resolution. Integration is legitimate only if it preserves difference and non-capture.

The `Ψ` or SELF_FIXPOINT reading is invalid if the case lacks durable self-binding, promise, role ownership, stable normativity, or responsibility-as-self-commitment and nevertheless produces person-level maturity, identity, or responsibility verdicts.

The PMS-SEX overlay would be weakened or invalidated if it smuggled in sexual content without evidence. It would become under-discriminating if it treated any boundary language as sexuality-relevant. It would become over-assimilative if it read space, crossing, ambiguity, or responsibility as sexual cost topology without sexual role-positions, body remainder, publicness, consent frame, or sexual consequence channels. Conversely, PMS-SEX would become stronger if future scene data supplied sexual embodiment, sexual publicness, sexual cost distribution, asymmetry activation, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, or adult sexual reciprocity conditions.

The MIP analysis would be weakened if it assigned A or M bands without concrete roles, knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, or integration attempts. It would also be weakened if “not assigned” were read as low maturity. The MIP result depends on score hygiene: non-assignment is a protective non-score, not a hidden judgment. The IA-box would become assessable only if a concrete asymmetry, purpose, time-boundary, and reversible exit or review structure were supplied. D-profile would become assessable only if a safe communication space, concrete D-enactments, role/time boundaries, and D-protective purpose were present.

The AHP result would be weakened if its high Precision Heuristic were interpreted as case certainty. AHP hardens the analysis artifact; it does not make the case empirically stronger. It identifies attack points and hardening actions, but does not upgrade transmission status. If a polished YAML form caused readers to treat the analysis as a verdict, AHP would have identified exactly the risk but not solved it by itself.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. A stronger applied analysis would require at minimum: concrete actors or role positions; boundary type; governing frame; sequence of events; communication record; expected and absent events; recurrence evidence; asymmetry map; publicness status; consequences; available alternatives; repair attempts; domain routing; and safe use conditions. For legal use, legal standards would be required. For clinical use, clinical assessment would be required. For institutional use, policy and mandate analysis would be required. For sexual-praxis use, explicit sexual scene evidence and cost-topology reconstruction would be required. For MIP scoring, knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and integration attempts would be required.

The examples illustrate these falsifier conditions without replacing them. In the report example, a written authorship policy could settle the frame differently. In the apartment example, a household agreement could make the open door convention explicit. In the university example, formal mentoring policy could define support and supervision. Each example could become a different kind of case under further evidence. This is why examples demonstrate the analysis but do not serve as proof of it.

Until operational conditions are supplied, the correct result is structural working analysis, not applied decision.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It does not identify boundary pathology, personality patterns, psychological deficits, immaturity, coercive tendencies, trauma responses, attachment style, narcissism, dependency, or any clinical category.

It must not be used for person ranking. No person is more or less mature, dignified, responsible, coherent, aware, adult, healthy, or worthy because of this analysis. The case has no concrete persons and no person-level findings.

It must not be used as a legal or forensic conclusion. It does not establish violation, consent failure, liability, culpability, negligence, harassment, trespass, abuse, breach, or evidentiary sufficiency. Legal categories require legal frames.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. It does not prove where persons begin or end, what selfhood is, what responsibility essentially is, or what a boundary ultimately is. It maps frame-bound distinction.

It must not be used as empirical proof. It does not show that boundary confusion occurred in any actual case. It does not show frequency, harm, recurrence, intent, effect, or causality.

It must not be used as implementation validation. It does not supply policy, protocol, therapy plan, governance intervention, safety measure, or enforcement guide.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. A coherent PMS application is not a proof of PMS. PMS.yaml remains the reference, not the object validated by the case.

It must not treat add-ons as operators. PMS-SEX handles such as sexual cost topology, publicness overlay, body remainder, asymmetry-switch drift, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, and adult sexual reciprocity are overlay concepts only. They do not redefine `Δ–Ψ`.

It must not turn drift into verdict. PMS drift patterns, PMS-SEX drift markers, MIP risks, and AHP attack points are not findings about persons. Drift risk is not drift finding. Attack surface is not person defect. Non-assignment is not low score.

It must not use closure by authority. AXIOM maps where closure is possible, where add-ons are justified, and where Λ-residue must remain visible. It does not solve boundary confusion by imposing a final interpretive authority.

It must not be used publicly against identifiable persons. The MIP and AHP layers explicitly preserve working-note status and block public D readings, shame, humiliation, maturity verdicts, or dignity hierarchy.

It must not erase harm if later concrete evidence appears. The misuse boundary cuts both ways. Restraint is not denial. If a future case shows coercion, legal violation, safety risk, abuse, serious harm, public degradation, or blocked exit, then the analysis must route to the appropriate domain rather than hide behind abstraction.

It must not treat the examples as evidence. The examples are article-ready illustrations of structural logic. They are not data, precedents, proof cases, or tribunal scenes. They are bounded vignettes designed to make the analysis readable while preserving the claim boundaries. Any reuse of the examples must keep that status intact.

It must not treat AHP hardening as a truth upgrade. AHP improves the analysis artifact by identifying attack points and hardening actions. It does not rescore A, M, IA, or D. It does not activate D. It does not make the case transmissible as a verdict. It does not change the underlying claim status.

The misuse boundary is therefore not an appendix to the analysis. It is part of the analysis itself. Boundary Confusion is exactly the kind of case where the demand for closure can become misuse. The discipline of the article lies in keeping structural visibility separate from adjudicative authority.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is deliberately conservative and layered. PMS Core identifies `Boundary Confusion` as a framed distinction problem: `□ ∘ Δ`. This is the strongest safe formula because the case is not a concrete praxis scene. Later PMS operators remain relevant only as calibrated possibilities. `∇` may appear as directed pressure, `Λ` as missing clarification or expected non-event, `Α` as recurrence, `Ω` as asymmetry, `Θ` as temporal accumulation, `Φ` as rival reframing, `Χ` as analytic distance, `Σ` as possible integration, and `Ψ` as possible self-binding. But none of these later structures may be pushed into findings without scene evidence.

The PMS-SEX overlay contributes by refusing improper activation. It shows that an add-on can add value by marking what is absent. Boundary Confusion contains no sexual scene, no body remainder, no sexual cost topology, no public sexual legibility, no adult sexual reciprocity, and no sexuality-specific drift evidence. The overlay therefore does not enrich the case by adding sexual content. It enriches the analysis by protecting the case from being sexualized by inference. Its active contribution is boundary discipline.

MIP contributes a downstream maturity-in-practice guardrail. It does not score persons. It does not assign A-band, M-band, IA-box result, or D-profile. It treats non-assignment as a protective non-score, not as low maturity. This is essential to the case. Boundary confusion may become maturity-relevant in a concrete scene, especially where knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, asymmetry, and repair attempts can be assessed. But this case does not yet supply those conditions. MIP therefore confirms that responsible maturity analysis may require withholding maturity judgment.

AHP contributes second-order hardening. It identifies where the analysis artifact could be misread: high precision could be mistaken for case certainty; non-assignment could be mistaken for low score; IA non-assignment could be mistaken for weak analysis rather than disciplined refusal; D-language could appear activated if repeated too strongly; polished YAML or polished prose could over-signal readiness for transmission. AHP hardens the article against those risks, but it does not rescore or upgrade the case.

The examples contribute readability without becoming evidence. The report example shows how help and role displacement can become frame-dependent. The open-door example shows how a low-level non-event can accumulate into boundary ambiguity without proving blame. The university support example shows how responsibility language can become structurally difficult when support begins to resemble supervision, while still refusing to label persons or assign institutional fault. Together, the examples demonstrate the case’s central logic: boundary confusion often becomes visible where a distinction is real enough to matter but insufficiently framed to support a verdict. Their function is demonstrative, not evidentiary: they make the operator discipline, add-on non-activation, MIP non-scoring, and AHP hardening readable, but they do not strengthen the case claim beyond what the YAML artefacts already warrant.

What remains unclaimed is extensive and important. The article does not claim that any boundary was violated. It does not claim that boundary confusion is always harmful. It does not claim that ambiguity is always immature or that refusal is always mature. It does not claim that asymmetry is present in every boundary case. It does not claim that responsibility language is always suspicious. It does not claim that PMS captures all boundary phenomena. It does not claim that PMS-SEX is relevant beyond its non-activation guard. It does not claim that MIP scoring is possible. It does not claim empirical, legal, clinical, diagnostic, metaphysical, implementation, or PMS Base validation.

The boundary discipline is the methodological core. PMS must not become a closure machine. PMS-SEX must not become a sexualization engine. MIP must not become a maturity tribunal. AHP must not become a truth upgrade. Drift must not become verdict. Risk must not become finding. Coherence must not become proof. Non-capture must not be treated as failure. Resistance, refusal, non-fit, disagreement, rupture, or absence must not be automatically redescribed as drift, incomplete integration, or maturity failure.

The final methodological takeaway is that Boundary Confusion shows the value of AXIOM as cartography rather than adjudication. The case maps where closure pressure arises, which operators become available, which dependencies block premature escalation, which overlay handles remain inactive, which downstream governance questions are under-specified, and where rival readings must remain open. The proper result is not a final answer to every boundary dispute. The proper result is a disciplined map of what would have to be known before stronger claims could responsibly begin.
