### Why this case matters

Responsibility is one of the strongest closure-demand terms in philosophical, legal, moral, institutional, and everyday discourse. When something has happened, when someone is exposed, when a duty appears unmet, or when a vulnerable party bears a cost, the demand for responsibility often arrives as a demand for closure: someone must answer, something must be owned, repair must be carried, or a commitment must hold across time.

This makes responsibility structurally difficult. It sits close to several neighboring concepts that are often collapsed into one another: blame, guilt, liability, causality, agency, voluntariness, autonomy, control, intention, consequence, obligation, accountability, and moral worth. The case material explicitly requires that these distinctions remain visible. PMS therefore cannot simply say “responsibility means self-binding” and stop. It must show how the responsibility structure is differentiated from these adjacent formations and why a PMS reading does not become a tribunal.

The philosophical closure-demand is especially strong because responsibility is often asked to do too much. In metaphysical contexts, it is asked to ground freedom or answer the problem of ultimate agency. In moral contexts, it is asked to justify blame or desert. In legal contexts, it is asked to support liability or sanction. In clinical or psychological contexts, it may be incorrectly treated as a trait, capacity, pathology, or maturity status. In institutional contexts, it may be used to allocate costs, preserve control, or assign repair burdens. In everyday relations, it may become a demand for acknowledgement, care, or continuity.

AXIOM includes this case because it is a central example of the paper’s method: a classical closure-demand is not solved by metaphysical assertion, moral authority, legal substitution, empirical measurement, diagnosis, or enforcement. Instead, the demand is mapped structurally. The point is not to discover who is “really responsible” in an absolute sense. The point is to show what has to be structurally present before responsibility can be modeled at all: a frame of answerability, asymmetry, temporal extension, recontextualization under changed conditions, integration, distance, and self-binding.

This case is therefore important for AXIOM because it demonstrates both the strength and the limits of PMS Core. PMS has a derived structure for Responsibility_R, and the case material fits it strongly. That strength is useful, but also risky. If the fit is too smooth, PMS may become under-discriminating: it might absorb responsibility too quickly into its own formula and fail to preserve rival readings, non-capture zones, and weakening conditions. For that reason, the analysis must explicitly mark coherence risk. A coherent PMS derivation is not proof of exhaustive capture.

Responsibility is not solved here metaphysically because the case explicitly excludes metaphysical free will as the grounding condition. It is not solved empirically because no dataset, behavior record, testimony, experiment, or observation is supplied. It is not solved clinically or diagnostically because PMS does not evaluate mental states, traits, pathology, maturity, or competence. It is not solved legally or forensically because liability, culpability, negligence, evidentiary standards, and sanction remain outside the PMS claim. It is not solved morally because the analysis does not assign blame, guilt, desert, worth, or rank. It is not implemented because no system, policy mechanism, architecture, or operational protocol is supplied.

The case matters precisely because responsibility remains indispensable even when all those stronger closure forms are withheld. PMS asks: what remains structurally legible when responsibility is stripped of metaphysical freedom, moral desert, legal verdict, diagnosis, and enforcement? The answer is not “nothing.” What remains is a structural pattern: self-binding across time under asymmetry, held through recontextualization and constrained by distance and dignity-in-practice.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading proceeds through the eleven operators in order. The order matters because later operators cannot be used cheaply. Responsibility_R depends on Ψ, but Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. Σ depends on Χ and Φ. Φ depends on Θ, Ω, and □. If these prior structures are absent, responsibility cannot simply be asserted.

**Δ — Difference** is active and foundational. The first task is differentiation. Responsibility must be distinguished from blame, guilt, liability, causality, agency, voluntariness, autonomy, obligation, accountability, coercion, control, intention, consequence, and moral worth. Without Δ, the case would collapse into an undifferentiated moral-legal-psychological field. Δ also distinguishes structural responsibility from metaphysical freedom. Its calibration risk is under-discrimination: if responsibility is not clearly separated from these neighboring concepts, PMS loses its analytic value. Pushed too far, Δ could generate artificial separations where real-world practices overlap. But in this conceptual case, strong differentiation is necessary.

**∇ — Impulse** is weak and conditional. Responsibility often involves pressure toward answerability: someone should respond, own, repair, continue, acknowledge, or carry a commitment. But the case provides no concrete actor and no actual directional drive. Therefore ∇ cannot be used to infer motive, intention, desire, voluntariness, or agency. Its role is limited to structural closure pressure: the field of responsibility tends to generate a demand that something be answered for. Pushed too far, ∇ would psychologize the case.

**□ — Frame** is active. Responsibility is always frame-bound. Legal responsibility, moral responsibility, relational responsibility, professional responsibility, care responsibility, and operational responsibility do not share identical criteria. The PMS frame here is structural-praxeological: responsibility as answerability and self-binding under temporal extension and asymmetry. The calibration issue is frame discipline. If legal, moral, causal, psychological, and PMS-structural frames slide into one another, the analysis becomes misleading. Pushed too far, □ could become relativistic frame control; underused, responsibility becomes unscoped authority.

**Λ — Non-Event** is weak and conditional. Responsibility often concerns missing events: an expected response does not arrive, a handover fails, repair is delayed, care is absent, acknowledgement is withheld, or a duty is not fulfilled. But this case does not specify any such expected event. Therefore Λ cannot be assigned as a finding. It remains relevant as a possible responsibility structure: absence matters only when a frame establishes what was expected. The calibration point is crucial: absence, silence, delay, rupture, disagreement, or non-fit must not automatically become irresponsibility. Pushed too far, Λ would turn every non-event into failure.

**Α — Attractor** is weak and conditional. Responsibility can stabilize into recurrent scripts: the same role is always blamed, the same institution always assigns downward, the same person is always expected to repair, or the same duty becomes routine. But no recurrence is supplied. Α therefore remains under-specified. It matters because Ω formally depends on Α in the PMS dependency table, yet in this conceptual case asymmetry is explicitly named without a concrete recurrence history. The responsible treatment is to mark Ω as conceptually central but dependency-sensitive. Pushed too far, Α would invent patterns not present in the case.

**Ω — Asymmetry** is active and central. The case explicitly names unequal power, capacity, exposure, vulnerability, dependency, and obligation. Responsibility is not neutral across these gradients. A party with greater capacity may carry a different responsibility structure than one with high vulnerability and low control. A role with authority may impose responsibility but not bear the same costs. A dependent party may generate responsibility without being blameworthy. Ω makes these gradients visible. But Ω must not be moralized. Asymmetry is not guilt. It is not inferiority or superiority. It is not a person-ranking. Pushed too far, Ω would convert structural imbalance into automatic blame.

**Θ — Temporality** is active and central. Responsibility exists across time: promises persist, obligations unfold, consequences lag, repair takes time, vulnerability changes, capacities shift, and commitments may need revision. Without Θ, responsibility collapses into a momentary attribution or causal mark. The calibration issue is temporal openness. Persistence matters, but so do delay, interruption, tragedy, changed capacity, and changed conditions. Pushed too far, Θ could rigidify responsibility into permanent obligation; underused, it would reduce responsibility to a point-event.

**Φ — Recontextualization** is active. Responsibility under changed conditions requires recontextualization. A duty may change when capacity changes; answerability may shift when authority shifts; obligation may need reframing when exposure or dependency changes. Φ allows responsibility to remain structurally live without becoming either rigid continuity or pure excuse. The calibration challenge is sharp: recontextualization must not be used to launder evasion, but neither may every revision be treated as irresponsibility. Pushed too far, Φ absorbs all rupture into maturity language; underused, responsibility becomes brittle.

**Χ — Distance** is active and necessary. Responsibility is one of the concepts most likely to collapse into verdict. Χ prevents this collapse. It holds distance from blame, guilt, legal liability, diagnosis, metaphysical proof, moral worth, and person-ranking. It also protects against urgency: the pressure to assign responsibility quickly may be understandable, but PMS requires reflective distance before binding. Pushed too far, Χ could become detachment or avoidance. Underused, responsibility becomes tribunal language.

**Σ — Integration** is active but calibration-dependent. Responsibility requires integration of distinctions, frames, asymmetries, temporal horizons, changed conditions, and reflective distance. Without Σ, there is no coherent responsibility map. But Σ is not proof. Integration does not mean truth, maturity, compliance, closure, or moral correctness. In this case, Σ must coordinate without smoothing away conflict, tragedy, ambiguity, or rival readings. Pushed too far, Σ becomes forced coherence.

**Ψ — Self-Binding** is active and primary. The case title explicitly places responsibility under Ψ, and the PMS derived structure Responsibility_R begins with Ψ. Responsibility is modeled as commitment to integrated structures across time. This does not mean metaphysical free will. It does not mean inner moral purity. It does not mean full autonomy. It means structural self-binding: the formation of a responsibility relation in which a commitment, role, duty, or answerability structure is held across time under asymmetry. Pushed too far, Ψ becomes moral essence or irreversible identity claim. Properly calibrated, it is the core of the PMS reading.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The case is a strong PMS fit, but that strength makes dependency discipline more important, not less. Later operators cannot be used cheaply. It is tempting to begin with Ψ because responsibility is so obviously linked to self-binding. But PMS does not permit Ψ to float free. Ψ requires Σ, Θ, and Χ. If responsibility is asserted without integration, temporal extension, and reflective distance, the reading is under-specified.

Σ is also not cheap. It depends on Χ and Φ. A responsibility map that integrates duties, capacities, exposures, and obligations without distance from verdicts and without recontextualization under changed conditions is not a valid PMS integration. It is only closure pressure pretending to be coherence.

Φ cannot be used cheaply either. Recontextualization depends on Θ, Ω, and □. A changed-condition reading must specify what changed, under which frame, across what temporal horizon, and in relation to which asymmetry. Otherwise Φ becomes rhetorical flexibility.

Χ depends on Φ, Θ, and □. Reflective distance is not empty neutrality. It is distance inside a framed and temporalized structure. Χ must remain accountable to the case’s asymmetries and obligations. It cannot simply withdraw from responsibility.

Ω is central, but it is also dependency-sensitive. In PMS, Ω depends on Α. In a concrete case, asymmetry often becomes visible through repeated patterns, role scripts, or stabilized positions. Here, however, the case material names asymmetry conceptually without giving a recurring pattern. The responsible interpretation is to treat Ω as active at the conceptual level while refusing to infer concrete attractors.

Λ also requires discipline. A non-event exists only where the frame generates an expected event. In responsibility contexts, missing handovers, absent repair, silence, delay, or non-acknowledgement may matter deeply. But without a specified frame, absence cannot be assigned as failure. Non-capture must remain possible.

Coherence is not proof. The PMS formula Responsibility_R fits the case well, but a strong fit can hide under-discrimination. PMS must not become a closure machine that converts every responsibility question into Ψ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω and then treats the matter as settled. The analysis must preserve the possibility that legal, phenomenological, moral, causal, care-ethical, institutional, or distributed-system readings capture something PMS does not fully capture.

Dependency shortcuts would be invalid if they moved directly from Δ and ∇ — “something happened; someone must answer” — to Ψ — “this party is responsible” — without □, Λ, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, and Σ. It would also be invalid to move from Ω to blame, from Θ to permanent obligation, from Φ to excuse, from Σ to maturity, or from Ψ to moral worth.

Non-capture is not failure. If PMS cannot settle metaphysical free will, legal liability, lived guilt, or distributed institutional responsibility, that does not mean the case analysis fails. It means AXIOM has done its work: it has shown where structural closure is possible and where Λ-residue or rival framing must remain visible.

### Example 1 — The missed handover in a care rotation

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** After “Scene and frame structure,” before the full PMS Core operator reading.

A small care team rotates responsibility for an elderly person who depends on daily medication, food preparation, and help with appointments. One evening, the outgoing caregiver believes the next caregiver has already received the updated medication note. The incoming caregiver believes no change has been made. The note exists, but it sits in a messaging thread that the incoming caregiver was not told to check. The medication is delayed. The dependent person is not harmed, but the delay exposes a fragile handover structure.

This vignette is intentionally bounded. It does not ask who is morally guilty, legally liable, negligent, clinically impaired, or “an irresponsible person.” It asks how responsibility becomes structurally legible when care, dependency, time, missing handover, and unequal exposure intersect.

What the example demonstrates is the difference between **responsibility as structural answerability** and **responsibility as blame**. The scene produces a strong closure pressure: someone wants to know who “was responsible.” But PMS does not allow an immediate jump from a visible gap to a verdict. The frame must first be declared. Is the operative frame a care-practice frame, an institutional documentation frame, a family trust frame, a legal liability frame, or a process-design frame? These frames may overlap in ordinary language, but they are not the same PMS frame. In the article’s terms, the example shows why responsibility must remain frame-bound before it can be bound to a role or agent.

The PMS Core hooks are direct. **Δ** appears in the contrast between expected handover and actual handover. Something differs from the expected continuity of care. **∇** appears as the pressure to close the gap quickly: someone must answer, explain, repair, or own the delay. **□** is the care-rotation frame: medication updates must be transferred in a reliable way. **Λ** is present more concretely than in the abstract case analysis, because an expected event is missing: a handover should have occurred in a recognizable channel, but it did not. **Α** may become relevant only if this is not a one-time gap but a repeated handover pattern. The example therefore must not claim an attractor unless recurrence is actually shown.

**Ω** is central because the dependent person carries high exposure and low control. The caregivers and organizers have more capacity to design, check, or repair the handover system. **Θ** matters because responsibility extends through time: the prior medication decision, the note, the evening rotation, the delayed response, and the later repair conversation form a temporal chain. **Φ** becomes relevant if the team learns that “handover” cannot simply mean “a note exists somewhere”; the responsibility frame must be recontextualized around accessible transfer, confirmation, and capacity. **Χ** prevents the scene from collapsing into accusation. **Σ** would be the integration of several factors: who knew what, which channel was expected, what dependency was involved, what repair is possible, and whether the system made the duty performable. **Ψ** would appear only where a role, person, or team legitimately binds itself to a revised handover commitment across time.

There are no add-on hooks activated here. PMS Core is sufficient to demonstrate the responsibility structure. A LOGIC overlay would be unnecessary unless the example were later used to analyze justification claims, such as whether a documentation rule was being laundered into certainty. A CONFLICT overlay would be unnecessary unless the scene escalated into a conflict pattern. A QC or MIP layer would be unnecessary unless this vignette became part of an applied institutional artefact.

MIP, AHP, and QC are therefore not active. If the care team later turned the analysis into a policy, audit trace, or institutional accountability procedure, MIP might be relevant as downstream artefact governance. AHP might be relevant if the written analysis needed claim-hardening: explicit failure conditions, test hooks, and counterfactual handles. QC might be relevant for reviewing whether the artefact overclaims. None of these are warranted by the vignette alone.

The forbidden claims are important. The example does not show that the outgoing caregiver is guilty. It does not show that the incoming caregiver is negligent. It does not show that the team is morally deficient. It does not prove legal liability. It does not diagnose avoidance, irresponsibility, incompetence, or personality. It does not rank the dependent person, caregivers, or institution. It does not establish an empirical generalization about care work.

The non-capture note is equally important. PMS can map the responsibility structure, but it does not fully capture the lived anxiety of the dependent person, the emotional burden of the caregivers, the legal standards that might apply in a real jurisdiction, or the ethical thickness of care. It also does not settle whether the correct repair is apology, redesign, documentation, compensation, training, or some combination. Those require additional frames.

The article takeaway is that responsibility becomes structurally visible where a temporal commitment under asymmetry is interrupted by a meaningful non-event. PMS can hold that structure without converting it into blame. The example prepares the reader to see why **Λ must be specified**, why **Ω and Θ are central**, and why **Ψ cannot be asserted until frame, distance, and integration discipline have done their work**.

### Example 2 — The inherited maintenance ticket

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** Inside “PMS dependency and calibration discipline,” after the paragraph warning against the invalid shortcut from Δ/∇ directly to Ψ.

A software operations team inherits an old maintenance ticket for a failing internal tool. The original developer has left the organization. The manager who approved the tool has moved to another department. The current operator receives user complaints but lacks permission to change the architecture. Several teams rely on the tool, but no one clearly owns it. The ticket is repeatedly reassigned: from operations to engineering, from engineering to product, from product back to operations. After several months, the tool fails during a routine process and causes a temporary service disruption.

The closure pressure is immediate: who is responsible for the failure? The example is concrete enough to show the structure of responsibility, but bounded enough not to decide a real case. No legal liability is assigned. No professional incompetence is inferred. No person is ranked as irresponsible. The vignette is not evidence of negligence, bad management, or institutional guilt. It is a structural demonstration of how responsibility can become distributed, delayed, and misaligned with authority.

The example demonstrates why **responsibility cannot be reduced to causality**. The original developer may have caused parts of the tool’s architecture. The former manager may have approved the deployment. The current operator may have seen the ticket most recently. The users may have generated the complaints. But causal contribution and responsibility are not identical. PMS requires the frame to specify what kind of responsibility is at stake: maintenance ownership, architectural authority, user-support duty, incident response, risk governance, or post-failure repair. Without that frame, the demand for a “responsible party” is structurally premature.

The PMS Core hooks show why later operators cannot be used cheaply. **Δ** appears as a visible divergence: a tool expected to function instead fails, and ownership expected to be clear is diffuse. **∇** appears as pressure to assign ownership quickly after the disruption. **□** must define the responsibility frame. Is the ticket a support object, a risk object, a product object, or an infrastructure object? Each frame distributes responsibility differently. **Λ** appears in the missing handover, missing ownership declaration, and absent authority-capacity alignment. A responsibility-relevant event should have occurred: explicit transfer, closure, escalation, or resource assignment. It did not.

**Α** may be relevant if reassignment is repeated. In that case, “pass the ticket elsewhere” becomes a responsibility attractor. But PMS must still be careful: recurrence must be shown, not assumed. **Ω** is central because authority and exposure are misaligned. The current operator bears user pressure but lacks architectural authority. The teams relying on the tool bear disruption costs but may lack control over maintenance. Managers may have formal authority but lower immediate exposure. **Θ** is also central because the problem unfolds across months: earlier decisions, missing handovers, repeated deferrals, and eventual failure produce a temporal responsibility field.

**Φ** becomes necessary when the responsibility frame shifts. Before the failure, the ticket may be treated as maintenance backlog. After the failure, the same ticket may be recontextualized as operational risk. That shift can be legitimate, but it must not be used to rewrite the past as if every later risk had been equally visible earlier. **Χ** is required to prevent the incident from becoming a blame search. **Σ** would integrate distributed duties: who had knowledge, who had authority, who had capacity, who bore exposure, and which handovers were missing. **Ψ** would be valid only if a role, team, or institution binds itself to a clarified ownership structure going forward, or if a prior binding duty can be shown within the frame.

No add-on is activated. PMS Core is enough to show the invalid shortcut: “something failed; someone must be responsible; therefore the last visible operator owns it.” A LOGIC overlay would only be relevant if the case centered on justificatory closure, such as treating one metric or report as proof of ownership. A CONFLICT overlay would be relevant only if role dispute, escalation, or adversarial positioning became the object of analysis. A QC layer might later inspect an incident report, but it is not active in the example itself.

MIP and AHP are not active. If the vignette became an institutional incident-review artefact, MIP could evaluate whether the artefact preserves scope, reversibility, dignity, and governance discipline. AHP could harden the incident analysis by requiring explicit claims, evidentiary limits, counterfactual handles, and failure conditions. But the example itself does not supply governance validation.

The forbidden claims are numerous. The example must not say the current operator is responsible merely because they touched the ticket last. It must not say the original developer remains responsible indefinitely. It must not say management is guilty because authority existed somewhere. It must not say distributed responsibility means no responsibility. It must not convert institutional ambiguity into moral defect. It must not infer intention, negligence, incompetence, or liability.

The non-capture note is that PMS maps the structural responsibility field but does not determine the correct organizational remedy. It also does not replace engineering root-cause analysis, labor policy, contract law, incident management, or ethics of organizational care. The emotional and professional stress of participants is not captured by the operator chain. Nor does PMS decide whether the fairest response is ownership redesign, tooling migration, compensation, apology, resource allocation, or governance change.

The article takeaway is that responsibility under Θ/Ω often appears where authority, capacity, exposure, and temporal continuity fail to align. PMS helps prevent a premature jump from incident to blame by requiring □, Λ, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, and only then possible Ψ. The example prepares the later conclusion by showing responsibility as a **map of binding conditions**, not a searchlight for blame.

### Example 3 — The promise that becomes unsafe to keep

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** In the “Derived structures” section, after the discussion of Responsibility_R and before Dignity_in_Practice_D.

A person promises a close friend that they will provide temporary housing during a difficult transition. At the time of the promise, the arrangement appears workable: the stay is expected to last two weeks, both parties understand the limits, and the host has enough financial and emotional capacity. After several days, the situation changes. The guest’s crisis deepens, the stay becomes indefinite, the host’s child begins to feel unsafe, and the host’s own health deteriorates. The host still feels bound by the promise, but keeping it exactly as first stated would now expose others to serious strain.

The example is designed to show responsibility under changed conditions. It is not a story about betrayal, selfishness, moral failure, or heroic duty. It is a bounded vignette about how self-binding must be recontextualized when temporal extension and asymmetry change the responsibility field.

What the example demonstrates is that **responsibility is not rigid sameness of obligation**. The original promise matters. PMS does not erase it. But responsibility also includes temporality, exposure, vulnerability, and changed capacity. A promise that was responsible in one frame may require recontextualization in another. This is why Responsibility_R includes Φ. Without Φ, responsibility would become brittle. With unrestrained Φ, responsibility could become evasion. The case requires calibration.

The PMS Core hooks begin with **Δ**: the situation now differs from the situation in which the promise was made. The expected two-week stay differs from the emerging indefinite dependency. The host’s original capacity differs from current capacity. The guest’s need differs from the child’s exposure. **∇** appears as pressure from both directions: pressure to honor the promise, pressure to protect the household, pressure to avoid abandonment, and pressure to avoid harm. **□** must specify the frame. Is the promise a friendship commitment, a caregiving arrangement, a household safety decision, an emergency support structure, or a moral duty? The answer may not be singular.

**Λ** may be present in several ways, but only where expectations are specified. An expected exit date does not arrive. An expected stabilization of the guest’s situation does not occur. An expected capacity to host safely may be absent. These non-events matter because they alter the responsibility structure. They do not automatically make the guest blameworthy or the host irresponsible. **Α** would require recurrence: perhaps a pattern of indefinite rescue, boundary collapse, or crisis escalation. But the vignette alone does not prove such a pattern.

**Ω** is central and multi-directional. The guest is vulnerable and dependent. The host has housing power and decision authority. The child has high exposure and low control. The host has obligations in more than one direction. PMS prevents the analysis from flattening this into one moral line. **Θ** matters because the promise persists through time, but the time horizon has changed. Temporary care and indefinite care are structurally different. **Φ** is the key operator: the original commitment must be placed into a new frame that includes changed duration, changed exposure, changed capacity, and multiple dependencies.

**Χ** prevents the analysis from forcing an immediate verdict: “the host must keep the promise” or “the host is free to withdraw.” **Σ** is required to integrate the competing structures: the original promise, the guest’s vulnerability, the child’s safety, the host’s capacity, the time horizon, and possible alternatives. **Ψ** appears not as blind adherence to the first wording of the promise, but as self-binding to an integrated responsibility structure. That might mean renegotiating the arrangement, setting a transition plan, involving other support, or maintaining care in a changed form. PMS does not prescribe which action is correct; it marks what a responsible re-binding would have to consider.

There are no active add-on hooks. PMS Core is sufficient to demonstrate why Φ and Dignity-in-Practice matter in responsibility. A LOGIC overlay would be unnecessary unless the article wanted to analyze how the promise is justified by a rule. A CONFLICT overlay would become relevant only if the vignette focused on escalation between host and guest. A MIP layer could become relevant if a housing organization, care institution, or public policy adopted this as a governance case. That is not the current use.

MIP, AHP, and QC are therefore inactive. If the example were later used in a training artefact for social care, tenancy support, or institutional duty allocation, MIP might evaluate the artefact’s governance boundaries. AHP might harden claims by requiring explicit conditions for revision, transition, and support alternatives. QC might check whether the artefact smuggles in blame or unsafe simplification. But no such validation is supplied here.

The forbidden claims must remain explicit. The example does not claim the host is morally wrong for changing the arrangement. It does not claim the guest is at fault for needing more support. It does not claim the child’s exposure automatically overrides the guest’s vulnerability. It does not prove what the law would require. It does not diagnose dependency, manipulation, avoidance, trauma, or incapacity. It does not rank the moral worth of the persons involved.

The non-capture note is substantial. PMS maps the responsibility structure, but it does not capture the lived pain of renegotiating a promise, the phenomenology of guilt, the ethical depth of friendship, the legal status of housing arrangements, or the clinical realities that may shape crisis and capacity. It also does not decide the correct practical outcome. The structural analysis leaves room for care ethics, legal advice, social support, mediation, and concrete risk assessment.

The article takeaway is that responsibility under Ψ is not mere persistence of an initial commitment. It is self-binding under Θ and Ω, recontextualized through Φ and disciplined by Χ and Σ. The example shows why changed conditions do not automatically erase responsibility, but also why responsibility cannot demand unchanged obligation when the frame, exposure, and capacity structure have materially changed.

Across the three examples, the function is demonstrative rather than evidentiary. The care handover, maintenance ticket, and changed promise do not prove the PMS reading; they show how the PMS reading must be calibrated if a concrete scene is later supplied. Each example keeps a different boundary visible: Λ must be specified before absence becomes responsibility-relevant; Ω and Θ must be mapped before responsibility is bound; Φ must remain available under changed conditions without becoming evasion; and Ψ must not be asserted as blame, liability, moral worth, diagnosis, or person-essence. The examples therefore prepare the conclusion without replacing the structural analysis.

### Non-capture and rival readings

Several rival readings remain open. They are not defects. They preserve dimensions that PMS Core may not fully capture.

A **legal-liability reading** treats responsibility as liability, culpability, negligence, evidentiary attribution, or sanctionable standing before an institution. PMS does not provide that. It may map structural answerability, but it does not establish legal criteria or forensic sufficiency.

A **moral-desert reading** treats responsibility as blameworthiness, praiseworthiness, guilt, desert, or moral worth. PMS does not provide that either. Responsibility_R is structural self-binding under asymmetry and time; it does not determine who deserves blame or praise.

A **metaphysical free-will reading** treats responsibility as grounded in ultimate agency, libertarian freedom, compatibilist control, or self-causation. PMS does not solve that debate. It deliberately allows responsibility without metaphysical free will.

A **causal-responsibility reading** treats responsibility as causal contribution to an outcome. PMS distinguishes structural responsibility from causality. A causal account may be necessary in many real cases, but causality alone does not establish Responsibility_R.

A **role-obligation reading** treats responsibility primarily as duty inside a frame. This may emphasize □, Ω, Θ, and Λ more than Ψ. It preserves the importance of institutional, professional, familial, or relational roles that may not be reducible to self-binding.

A **care/dependency reading** treats responsibility as arising from vulnerability, dependency, and care relations. This may preserve ethical and relational dimensions that PMS renders only structurally through Ω, Θ, Χ, and D.

A **distributed-system reading** treats responsibility as spread across roles, institutions, infrastructures, policies, and technical systems. This may weaken individual Ψ and strengthen □, Α, Ω, and Θ. PMS can model distributed structures, but the current case does not provide enough material to do so.

A **phenomenological reading** might focus on lived guilt, remorse, burden, obligation, or answerability as experienced from within. PMS does not capture qualia or subjective experience.

An **anti-capture reading** may argue that responsibility cannot be adequately rendered through operator grammar without losing its ethical force. PMS does not refute this by formalizing the case. It only offers a structural projection.

These rival readings remain available because PMS translation is not a conquest. AXIOM maps closure-demands; it does not solve by authority.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would weaken if no situated frame of answerability could be identified. Responsibility cannot float without □. If there is no relevant frame defining duty, answerability, care, repair, commitment, or obligation, the structural reading loses its anchor.

It would weaken if no relevant asymmetry were present. Responsibility_R includes Ω. If power, capacity, exposure, vulnerability, dependency, or obligation asymmetry is absent or irrelevant, the formula no longer fits strongly.

It would weaken if no temporal extension were involved. Responsibility_R depends on Θ. If the case were only a point-event attribution without persistence, commitment, delay, consequence, or changed condition, the Θ component would be overextended.

It would weaken if no self-binding were present. If only external enforcement, causal contribution, or imposed liability exists, Ψ cannot be assumed. A duty may be assigned without being self-bound.

It would weaken if Φ were not relevant. The case material explicitly includes changed conditions, so Φ is active here. But in a narrower responsibility case with no recontextualization, the formula would need adjustment or qualification.

The reading would become under-discriminating if it could not distinguish responsibility from blame, guilt, liability, causality, obligation, accountability, control, voluntariness, intention, consequence, agency, moral worth, and metaphysical freedom. That is the central discrimination test.

The reading would become over-assimilative if rupture, resistance, disagreement, absence, delay, or rival framing were automatically redescribed as PMS drift, incomplete integration, failed maturity, or recontextualization. PMS must not treat non-fit as a defect by default.

The reading would become invalid as PMS application if Χ were suspended to force a verdict. Responsibility-talk is especially prone to urgency and enforcement. If the analysis becomes a tool for shaming, ranking, blaming, diagnosing, or imposing legal-like conclusions, it violates the PMS application discipline.

The reading would also be weakened if a rival formalization explained the responsibility field with sharper discrimination and less overreach. PMS must remain open to that possibility.

Operationalization would require more than the conceptual case supplies. A stronger applied analysis would need concrete scenes, role descriptions, asymmetry gradients, temporal horizons, evidence of duty or commitment, handover records, repair windows, omission criteria, recurrence evidence, frame-change criteria, integration criteria, and external accountability. If empirical claims were made, observable indicators and validation procedures would be needed. If legal claims were made, legal standards and forum-specific evidence rules would be needed. If clinical claims were made, clinical legitimacy would be needed — but PMS is not a clinical tool. If computational implementation were claimed, architecture-specific calibration and evaluation would be required.

None of these operationalization requirements are fulfilled in the current case. That is not a defect. It is a boundary.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It does not identify irresponsible persons, responsibility deficits, maturity failures, avoidance patterns, or psychological traits.

It must not be used for person ranking. PMS does not rank persons as more or less responsible in worth, dignity, maturity, moral status, or human standing.

It must not be used legally or forensically. Responsibility_R is not liability, culpability, negligence, guilt, legal accountability, or sanction justification.

It must not be used metaphysically. The analysis does not prove free will, selfhood, agency, moral desert, ontological dignity, or ultimate authorship.

It must not be used empirically unless evidence is supplied. The case contains no observations, records, measurements, experiments, or behavioral data.

It must not be used as implementation validation. A YAML or Markdown rendering does not implement responsibility, governance, accountability, agency, or safety.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. A strong case fit does not prove PMS_1.3 is complete, final, uniquely ordered, or superior.

It must not treat add-ons as operators. No add-on is activated here, and even if one were, it could not redefine PMS operators.

It must not treat drift as verdict. AD_A>>E and AD_Sigma_low are conditional structural risks only. No drift is assigned.

It must not close by authority. PMS terms do not license final settlement. The analysis remains reversible, bounded, non-diagnostic, non-tribunal, and open to rival readings.

The specific misuse most likely in this case is responsibility-to-blame fusion: using the structural language of self-binding and asymmetry to smuggle in guilt, shame, liability, or punishment. The second likely misuse is responsibility-to-person-essence fusion: treating a scene-bound responsibility structure as evidence that someone is an irresponsible kind of person. The third likely misuse is coherence-to-validity fusion: treating a smooth PMS formula as proof that all responsibility has been captured.

All three are invalid uses.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of the case is that responsibility can be rendered within PMS Core as **self-binding commitment across time under asymmetry, recontextualizable under changed conditions and constrained by distance, reversibility, and dignity-in-practice**. The primary formula, **Responsibility_R = Ψ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω**, is a strong structural fit for the case material because responsibility is explicitly framed as answerability, temporal extension, self-binding, unequal exposure, vulnerability, dependency, obligation, and changed conditions. That fit, however, remains a PMS-internal structural projection. It does not settle metaphysical freedom, moral desert, legal liability, empirical causality, psychological diagnosis, or institutional enforcement.

The three examples contribute by making the abstract calibration visible without turning the examples into evidence or verdicts. The missed care handover shows how Λ becomes relevant only when a frame establishes an expected event, and how responsibility can be mapped without collapsing into blame. The inherited maintenance ticket shows why responsibility cannot be reduced to causality, last contact, or visible ownership, and why authority, exposure, time, and integration must be mapped before Ψ is asserted. The promise that becomes unsafe to keep shows why responsibility requires Φ: changed conditions do not automatically erase responsibility, but they may require a commitment to be re-bound under a new frame rather than repeated in its first wording.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is claimed. The article does not decide who is responsible in any concrete case. It does not provide a legal standard, clinical category, moral ranking, empirical finding, implementation specification, or proof of PMS Base. It does not diagnose irresponsibility, assign guilt, or authorize enforcement. It does not treat resistance, rupture, disagreement, absence, refusal, delay, or non-fit as drift by default. It does not treat drift risks as findings. It does not treat non-assignment as a low score. It does not treat Dignity-in-Practice as ontological dignity. It does not treat PMS coherence as proof.

The boundary discipline of the case is therefore double. On one side, PMS must not underclaim so much that responsibility disappears whenever metaphysical free will, legal liability, or moral desert are withheld. Structural responsibility remains legible: there can be answerability, temporal commitment, asymmetry-sensitive obligation, and self-binding without metaphysical closure. On the other side, PMS must not overclaim so much that every responsibility question becomes automatically captured by Ψ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω. The formula is useful only under dependency discipline, calibration, reversibility, non-capture, and openness to rival readings.

The final methodological takeaway is that responsibility is not treated here as a verdict but as a **closure-demand map**. PMS Core can show where responsibility becomes structurally possible, where it remains under-specified, where Λ-residue must stay visible, where asymmetry creates exposure gradients, where temporality creates lock-in and revision costs, where recontextualization is required, and where self-binding may become legitimate. AXIOM’s contribution is not to solve responsibility by authority, but to make explicit the conditions under which responsibility-talk can remain structurally disciplined rather than becoming blame, diagnosis, liability, metaphysics, or coercive closure.
