### Why this case matters

Invariance matters because it is one of the hidden closure-demands underneath many philosophical, scientific, formal, normative, interpretive, and institutional questions. Whenever a claim says that something remains the same despite change, that a structure is preserved across transformation, that a rule holds across contexts, that a pattern repeats, that an identity persists, or that a system remains equivalent under variation, an invariance problem has appeared.

The difficulty is that the word “invariance” can name very different structures. Strict identity is not the same as functional invariance. Formal invariance is not the same as empirical regularity. Structural equivalence is not the same as interpretive stability. Normative rule-constancy is not the same as resilience under disturbance. Self-consistency is not the same as mathematical symmetry. These readings can overlap, but they cannot be collapsed without loss. The case therefore belongs in AXIOM because it exposes a classical closure-demand in its most general form: the demand to say what remains stable when conditions change.

The closure-demand is philosophically powerful because invariance appears to promise a route out of contingency. If something is invariant, it seems not merely repeated, but preserved. It seems to provide reliability, identity, lawfulness, rule-stability, or form. That promise easily becomes inflated. A local invariant may be treated as an essence. A functional equivalence may be treated as strict sameness. A measurement regularity may be treated as metaphysical necessity. A standard may be treated as an unquestionable rule. A repeated pattern may be treated as proof of identity. PMS-AXIOM does not resolve these inflation risks by replacing them with a new metaphysical doctrine. It maps where the closure-demand arises and where its residues remain.

This case is structurally difficult because it is not a concrete scene. There is no actor, no institution, no event, no harm, no legal dispute, no diagnostic object, no implementation architecture, and no empirical dataset. The case material is conceptual: it names a field of possible invariance forms and asks for structural discrimination. That means PMS operators can be used only as a bounded analytic grammar, not as findings about an observed situation.

The case is also difficult because PMS itself can generate a smooth reading too easily. Invariance naturally fits a Δ–□–Α–Θ–Φ–Χ–Σ pattern: distinguish change from preservation, frame the relevant sameness, stabilize it, test it across time or transformation, move it across contexts, hold reflective distance, and integrate competing readings. But that coherence is precisely dangerous. PMS coherence is not proof. A polished PMS formula may under-discriminate among rival invariance types unless it preserves the differences between strict identity, formal invariance, functional preservation, structural continuity, empirical regularity, normative rule-stability, interpretive stability, resilience, and self-consistency.

The value of the case is therefore not that PMS “solves” invariance. Its value is that PMS can show what must be declared before an invariance claim becomes structurally responsible: the frame, the transformation set, the invariant feature, the equivalence criterion, the temporal scope, the possible failure conditions, the role of non-events or edge cases, the status of integration, and the limits of binding.

### Scene and frame structure

Because the case is not a concrete scene, the “scene” is a conceptual scene: an invariance claim is demanded. Across transformations, contexts, repetitions, rule applications, interpretations, or temporal intervals, something is asserted to remain the same. PMS treats that assertion not as self-explanatory, but as a structure requiring frame discipline.

The scene boundary is the problem of sameness-under-change. Inside the frame are claims about preservation, repeatability, identity, equivalence, stability, conservation, symmetry, rule-continuity, pattern-continuity, resilience, and self-consistency. Outside the frame are final metaphysical verdicts, empirical proof, diagnostic inference, legal adjudication, implementation validation, and PMS Base validation.

The key frame operator is □. Invariance is never simply “sameness” in the abstract. A frame must declare what counts as change, what counts as preservation, which transformations are admissible, which features matter, what tolerance applies, and what purpose the invariance claim serves. A mathematical frame may define invariance through formal transformation. An empirical frame may define it through repeated measurement under controlled variation. A functional frame may define it through preserved role or operation. A normative frame may define it through rule-stability. An interpretive frame may define it through stable meaning across contexts. These are not interchangeable without recontextualization.

Protected constraints follow from the non-concrete status. First, no invariance claim may be treated as frame-independent unless the case supplies grounds for such a claim, which it does not. Second, no actor or group may be evaluated. Third, no rival reading may be dismissed merely because PMS can translate it. Fourth, non-fit must remain possible. Fifth, any integration of multiple invariance types must be provisional and discriminative, not totalizing.

Temporality Θ is relevant but not uniform. Some invariance claims are diachronic: persistence, continuity, resilience, and identity-preservation over time. Others are synchronic or formal: equivalence under transformation may not require temporal development. PMS must therefore avoid treating all invariance as temporal. Where time matters, the temporal grain must be specified: single transformation, repeated iteration, long-horizon stability, drift-prone regime, or self-consistency across changing conditions.

Asymmetry Ω is conditional. Invariance itself is not automatically an asymmetry problem. Ω becomes relevant only when some role can impose an invariance criterion, when misfit costs are unevenly distributed, when standards harden into obligations, when interpretive authority suppresses alternatives, or when normative invariance becomes responsibility-bearing. In the abstract case, Ω remains weak.

The reversibility window is open. Since no concrete binding, implementation, standard, or legal decision is supplied, invariance readings remain revisable. Revision may occur through Φ when the claim moves into a new frame, through Λ when edge cases or missing proof remain unresolved, through Χ when reflective distance blocks overclaim, and through comparison with rival formalizations.

### PMS Core structural reading

Δ, Difference, is the entry point. Invariance requires a distinction between what changes and what is claimed to remain stable. Without Δ, there is no invariance problem. The case activates Δ strongly because it asks how sameness, persistence, conservation, repeatability, structural equivalence, rule-stability, and self-consistency can be distinguished from mere difference, variation, drift, rupture, or loss. Calibration requires specifying which difference matters. A formal difference is not necessarily a functional difference; a contextual difference is not necessarily a structural loss; a change in expression may not be a change in rule. Overclaim occurs if all kinds of change and all kinds of preservation are treated as a single contrast.

∇, Impulse, is weak. The case contains directional pressure toward preservation, explanation, recognition, and closure: the desire to say what remains the same. But there is no concrete actor, urgency, affective drive, institutional demand, or practical decision. ∇ must therefore remain a conceptual pressure, not a psychological impulse. It would be overclaim to infer desire, intention, anxiety, or agency from the topic of invariance alone.

□, Frame, is active and central. Invariance depends on relevance conditions. A claim of sameness always occurs within a frame that determines which transformations matter and what counts as preservation. In a formal frame, invariance may mean preservation under a transformation group. In an institutional frame, it may mean stable rule application. In an empirical frame, it may mean regularity under repeated measurement. In an interpretive frame, it may mean meaning-stability across contexts. Calibration requires naming the frame before deciding what is invariant. Overclaim occurs when a claim valid in one frame is transported into another without Φ.

Λ, Non-Event, is weak and conditional. Invariance may involve an expected loss that does not occur: a transformation happens, but the form remains; a context shifts, but the rule still applies; a disturbance occurs, but the pattern persists. In those cases, Λ marks the absent expected breakdown. But no concrete expected event is supplied. Therefore Λ must not be globally activated. It is relevant only where the analysis specifies an expected disruption, proof, failure, edge case, or loss that does not arrive. It would be overclaim to convert every missing criterion or unresolved rival reading into Λ.

Α, Attractor, is active but dependency-sensitive. Invariance often involves stabilization: repeated patterns, recurrent rules, regular measurements, preserved forms, stable interfaces, or continuity across reuse. Α captures the formation of such recurrence or pattern-continuity. Yet in PMS, Α depends on Δ, ∇, □, and Λ. Since ∇ and Λ are weak here, Α is conceptually active but not fully scene-grounded. Calibration requires distinguishing stable recurrence from strict identity, accidental repetition, and purely formal equivalence. Overclaim occurs if a recurrent pattern is treated as proof of essence.

Ω, Asymmetry, is weak and conditional. An invariance claim can be purely formal or conceptual without asymmetry. Ω enters only when invariance is imposed, enforced, contested, or made responsibility-bearing. A standard-setter may define what remains invariant. A regulator may require compliance. A modeler may select the equivalence relation while implementers bear misfit costs. A dominant interpretive frame may suppress alternative readings. In the abstract case, none of these are supplied as a concrete scene. Ω must therefore remain conditional. It would be overclaim to assume that every invariance claim conceals power.

Θ, Temporality, is thematically active and dependency-sensitive. Persistence, repeatability, continuity, resilience, and identity-preservation all involve time. But not every invariance claim is temporal. Formal invariance may be transformation-based without diachronic persistence. PMS must distinguish invariance across time from invariance across formal operations, contexts, roles, or interpretations. Calibration requires specifying temporal grain: moment, sequence, recurrence, long-horizon stability, drift, or self-consistency over changing conditions. Overclaim occurs if every invariance problem is forced into a temporal trajectory.

Φ, Recontextualization, is active but bounded. The case explicitly includes context-shift without loss of form. Φ tracks what happens when an existing structure is placed into a new frame: a rule moves from theory to institution, a formal invariant becomes a measurement standard, a functional equivalence becomes a policy criterion, or an interpretive pattern moves across contexts. Calibration requires naming the source frame and target frame. Overclaim occurs if every rupture, disagreement, or rival reading is redescribed as recontextualization. Φ must preserve the possibility that the claim simply fails to travel.

Χ, Distance, carries major analytic weight. Invariance is broad enough to invite false unity. Χ prevents the analysis from declaring closure too quickly. It keeps strict identity, formal invariance, functional preservation, empirical regularity, normative stability, interpretive continuity, resilience, and self-consistency apart unless a justified integration is supplied. Χ also prevents PMS from becoming a closure machine. It requires reversibility, non-totalization, non-diagnostic use, and openness to rival formalisms. In this case, Χ is fully valid as an application-gate discipline, while its case-internal dependency remains weak because Φ and Θ are themselves dependency-sensitive.

Σ, Integration, is active but risky. The case invites integration because invariance types appear related. But integration can erase the differences the case exists to protect. Σ is legitimate only when it improves discrimination: for example, by coordinating transformation set, invariant feature, tolerance, temporal scope, and failure condition. It is illegitimate when it turns many invariance concepts into one smooth master structure. PMS coherence is insufficient by itself. In this case, Σ remains weak as a case-operator dependency and provisional as an analytic function.

Ψ, Self-Binding, is weak and conditional. Ψ applies where invariance becomes a stable commitment, rule, standard, contract, proof obligation, policy, or self-consistency claim. It does not apply to invariance as such. The abstract concept of invariance is not a subject, agent, or self. Ψ must not be used to smuggle in metaphysical identity, consciousness, or personhood. It becomes relevant only when a structure binds itself or is bound across time: a rule remains in force, a standard is maintained, a commitment is preserved, or a system must remain self-consistent under changing conditions.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

Later PMS operators cannot be used cheaply. Invariance tempts shortcutting because it seems already to contain stability, persistence, and identity. But PMS requires dependency hygiene. Δ and □ can be activated early because the case clearly distinguishes change from preservation within possible frames. ∇ and Λ are weaker because there is no concrete impulse or specified expected non-event. Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, and Ψ must therefore be calibrated carefully.

A central invalid shortcut would be Δ/∇ → Ψ: something changes, closure pressure appears, and the analysis immediately declares a binding invariant. This would skip the frame, the transformation set, the residue, the temporal scope, the recontextualization risk, the distance requirement, and the integration criterion. In practice, that shortcut produces slogans such as “this is invariant,” “this is the essence,” “this always holds,” or “this standard is final” without the structural work required to support the claim.

Σ remains conditional because integration is not the same as adequacy. A PMS reading can integrate many invariance types, but if the integration does not preserve their differences, it is under-discriminating. Strict identity, formal equivalence, empirical regularity, and normative rule-stability may all be called invariance, but they do not share the same warrant conditions. A good Σ must specify what it integrates and what it leaves distinct.

Ψ remains conditional because binding is expensive. A rule, standard, theorem, contract, commitment, or self-consistency requirement may bind an invariance criterion, but binding does not make the criterion metaphysically final. Binding only means that an integrated structure is stabilized as a commitment within a frame. Without Θ, Χ, and Σ, Ψ would be premature.

Non-capture is not failure. Some aspects of invariance may be better handled by mathematics, formal logic, metaphysics, physics, semantics, phenomenology, or language-practice theory. PMS does not need to absorb those rival formalisms in order to be useful. Its role is to map the praxeological structure of invariance-claims: how they are framed, stabilized, transported, integrated, bound, and misused. Where PMS does not capture a rival account, that may mark a real boundary rather than a defect in the rival account.

PMS must not become a closure machine. AXIOM maps closure-demands; it does not solve by authority. The Invariance case makes that especially clear. If PMS were used to declare that every invariance problem is ultimately Δ–□–Α–Θ–Φ–Χ–Σ–Ψ, it would reproduce the very totalization it is meant to discipline.

### Example 1 — Rotating the square: formal invariance without metaphysical identity

A square is drawn on a page. It is then rotated ninety degrees. In one sense, something has changed: the relation between the drawn figure and the page has shifted. The top side is no longer the same physical side in the same page-orientation. The visual presentation has changed, and a viewer may describe the square as “turned.” In another sense, something has not changed: the figure remains a square. Its side-lengths remain equal, its internal angles remain right angles, and its structural relation among sides is preserved.

This vignette demonstrates the difference between strict identity and formal invariance. The rotated figure is not “unchanged” in every respect. It is invariant only relative to a frame that declares rotation an admissible transformation and defines the relevant preserved feature as geometric structure. The example therefore shows why invariance cannot be read as simple sameness. The invariant is not the whole situation. It is a selected property or relation preserved under a declared transformation.

The PMS Core hooks are straightforward. Δ marks the difference between changed orientation and preserved form. □ defines the frame: geometry, not page-position, perceptual familiarity, legal identity, or historical continuity. Λ remains weak but visible if one expects orientation to matter and then finds that, inside this frame, orientation-loss does not destroy squareness. Α appears only minimally as a stabilized rule of classification: rotations of a square are routinely still counted as square. Θ is not central unless repeated rotations or temporal persistence are introduced. Φ becomes important when the same example is moved into another frame. In a geometry lesson, the square remains formally invariant. In a manufacturing alignment task, the rotation may matter. In a user-interface icon, the rotated shape may preserve geometry while changing meaning. Χ prevents the formal reading from becoming a global claim. Σ may integrate the distinctions, but only provisionally: changed orientation, preserved geometry, and altered practical meaning must remain separate. Ψ is not active unless the frame binds a rule such as “all rotations preserving side equality and angle structure count as equivalent.”

There are no add-on hooks here. PMS Core is sufficient. The example does not require LOGIC, ANTICIPATION, CONFLICT, CRITIQUE, EDEN, SEX, QC, MIP, or AHP. MIP / AHP / QC status remains not activated because the vignette is not governance-relevant, not an analysis-artifact hardening task, and not a quality-control or implementation claim.

The forbidden claims are important. The example must not be used to claim that formal invariance proves metaphysical identity. It must not imply that every preserved structure is an essence. It must not claim empirical invariance, since no measurement protocol is supplied. It must not claim implementation validity, since no system is being tested. It must not validate PMS Base; it only demonstrates how PMS can organize the distinction.

The non-capture note is equally important. Mathematics can specify formal invariance more precisely than PMS by defining transformations, groups, mappings, equivalence classes, and preserved properties. PMS does not replace that formal machinery. Its contribution is cartographic: it shows which frame is doing the work, which difference is being ignored or preserved, and where the invariance claim would overreach if transported into another context without re-declaration.

The article takeaway is that invariance is not bare sameness. It is sameness-under-a-frame, across a declared transformation, with a selected preserved feature. PMS helps prevent the slide from “this property is invariant under rotation” to “the whole object is unchanged in every relevant sense.”

### Example 2 — The stable API contract: functional invariance under version change

A software service exposes an interface. Over time, the internal implementation changes: a database is replaced, error-handling is rewritten, latency improves, and internal modules are reorganized. For ordinary users of the interface, however, the same request still returns the same kind of response in the same documented format. From their position, the service is “the same.” From the maintainer’s position, much has changed. From the perspective of the interface contract, the relevant invariant is not the internal structure but the preserved external behavior promised by the contract.

This vignette demonstrates functional invariance, not strict identity and not metaphysical persistence. The system is not unchanged. It is invariant only relative to a declared interface frame: certain inputs, outputs, formats, error codes, and compatibility promises are preserved across internal transformation. The example is useful because it makes visible why the phrase “same system” is too coarse. The system may be materially, architecturally, and operationally different while remaining functionally invariant for a particular class of users.

PMS Core maps the structure without claiming implementation validation. Δ marks the difference between internal change and externally preserved behavior. ∇ appears as a practical pressure for continuity: users, maintainers, and dependent systems need the interface to keep working. □ is the interface contract frame. It declares what counts as sameness: not identical code, not identical storage, not identical runtime behavior in all respects, but preserved public behavior within specified tolerances. Λ appears through edge cases: undocumented behavior, missing compatibility tests, untested inputs, or a response that was expected to remain stable but changes under a boundary condition. Α appears as reuse stabilization. The more the interface is used, documented, and relied upon, the more its behavioral pattern becomes a practical attractor. Ω is conditional but visible if maintainers define the invariant while downstream users bear breakage costs. Θ is central because compatibility is a time-structured claim: the promise must persist across versions, updates, migrations, and drift. Φ appears when the interface is transported into a new environment, user community, regulatory context, or integration scenario. Χ prevents the contract from being treated as total sameness. Σ integrates inputs, outputs, exceptions, tolerances, versioning rules, and migration notes into a usable invariance package. Ψ becomes active only if the contract is explicitly bound: for example, when maintainers commit to backward compatibility or publish a versioning guarantee.

There are no add-on hooks required. A QC or implementation layer could become relevant in a different task if the example were used to validate an actual system, test a migration, or audit compatibility. That is not happening here. MIP / AHP / QC status remains not activated. The example remains conceptual and structural.

The forbidden claims are strict. This example does not prove that the interface is actually compatible. It does not validate a software system. It does not show that all users experience continuity. It does not provide a benchmark, test suite, liability finding, or governance recommendation. It also does not establish that functional invariance is the same as identity. Internal changes may matter in security, performance, maintainability, or compliance frames even when the public interface remains stable.

The non-capture note preserves the limits of PMS. Software engineering can specify compatibility more precisely through tests, semantic versioning, formal interface definitions, type contracts, migration rules, and runtime monitoring. PMS does not replace those tools. It clarifies what kind of invariance is being claimed and where the claim depends on frame, tolerance, temporality, and binding.

The article takeaway is that functional invariance depends on declared relevance. A system can change substantially while preserving a contractually relevant behavior. PMS helps keep that claim bounded: invariant for whom, under which interface, across which changes, for how long, and with which edge cases still carried as Λ.

### Example 3 — Equal rule, unequal setting: normative invariance and asymmetry risk

An institution adopts a rule: the same deadline applies to every participant. The rule is presented as invariant. It does not change by status, preference, history, or individual appeal. In one sense, this is a clear normative invariant: the deadline is stable across persons and cases. But the practical setting is not invariant. Some participants have stable internet access, flexible schedules, and institutional familiarity. Others have unreliable access, caregiving burdens, translation needs, or limited ability to contest the rule. The rule remains formally constant while its burden is unevenly distributed.

This vignette demonstrates why normative invariance is structurally delicate. A rule can be invariant at the level of wording and still generate unequal exposure under application. The example does not show that the rule is wrong. It does not show that exceptions are required. It does not prescribe policy. It only shows that “same rule for all” is not the same as “same practical situation for all.” PMS is useful here because it can keep the invariant rule visible while also tracking asymmetry, time-cost, residue, and misuse risk.

The PMS Core hooks are layered. Δ marks the difference between formal sameness of the rule and practical difference in conditions of compliance. ∇ appears as closure pressure: the institution wants a stable standard that can be applied without endless case-by-case renegotiation. □ defines the normative frame: the deadline counts as invariant because the institution has declared a rule-stability frame. Λ appears where expected fairness, access, or contestability does not arrive. A deadline may be stable, but the missing event may be equal practical capacity to meet it, or an available revision channel. Α appears when the rule becomes routine and the institution repeatedly treats the deadline as the default. Ω is active as a risk condition because some roles impose the invariant while others bear the cost of misfit. Θ matters because deadlines produce lock-in: missed dates, records, penalties, and delayed remedies accumulate. Φ appears if the rule shifts frames, for example from coordination tool to disciplinary marker. Χ is required to prevent invariant-rule language from becoming moral verdict. Σ may integrate rule-stability, access conditions, exception criteria, and administrative feasibility, but only if those distinctions remain explicit. Ψ becomes active if the institution binds itself to the rule or to a revision policy. That binding does not make the rule metaphysically fair; it only stabilizes a commitment inside a frame.

No add-on is used in the current article layer. A governance case might later justify MIP, and a quality-control or AHP layer might be relevant if an actual policy artefact were being audited. Here, MIP / AHP / QC status remains not activated. The example is guardrail-oriented and does not evaluate the institution or participants.

The forbidden claims are essential. The example must not be used to diagnose participants as disadvantaged, irresponsible, privileged, or noncompliant types. It must not rank persons. It must not make a legal claim about discrimination, a clinical claim about capacity, or an empirical claim about actual access rates. It must not prescribe enforcement or non-enforcement. It also must not claim that normative invariance is invalid. The point is narrower: formal constancy and practical equivalence are distinct.

The non-capture note preserves rival readings. Legal theory, political philosophy, ethics, administrative design, and empirical policy evaluation may each offer stronger tools for deciding whether such a rule is justified, fair, lawful, or effective. PMS does not decide those questions. It maps the structure: invariant rule, non-invariant conditions, asymmetrical exposure, temporal lock-in, possible Λ residue, and the need for Χ before binding or enforcement.

The article takeaway is that normative invariance can be structurally real and still incomplete. A stable rule may support coordination, but PMS prevents the rule’s invariance from being mistaken for total fairness, moral authority, or person-level judgment.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The strict identity reading treats invariance as literal sameness. This preserves the strongest possible sense of identity: the thing is not merely equivalent, similar, functional, or continuous; it is the same. PMS can map how such a claim functions within frames, but it does not prove or disprove strict identity. This reading may resist PMS capture where the issue is metaphysical identity rather than praxeological use.

The formal reading treats invariance as preservation under specified transformations. This is often sharper than PMS for mathematical or logical contexts because it can define transformation groups, equivalence classes, mappings, and preserved properties directly. PMS can help identify frame and closure discipline, but it should not replace formal specification.

The functional reading treats invariance as preservation of role, operation, or use. A system may change materially while preserving function. PMS captures much of this through □, Α, Θ, Φ, and Σ, but functional theories may offer more detailed criteria for performance equivalence.

The structural reading treats invariance as persistence of relations or pattern. This aligns strongly with PMS attractor and integration language, but PMS still must not collapse structural continuity into identity. A preserved relation is not automatically a preserved entity.

The normative reading treats invariance as rule-stability, standard continuity, or obligation across changing conditions. This is where Ω, Θ, Φ, Σ, and Ψ can become more active. But normative invariance requires explicit rule, authority, and obligation structures. Without them, PMS must not infer responsibility.

The empirical regularity reading treats invariance as observed stability across cases. PMS can frame empirical regularity as Α/Θ stabilization under □, but empirical validation requires data. The present case supplies none.

The interpretive stability reading treats invariance as continuity of meaning across contexts. PMS can map this through □, Φ, Χ, and Σ, but semantic and hermeneutic accounts may preserve dimensions PMS does not exhaust.

The resilience reading treats invariance as maintained organization under disturbance. PMS can model disturbance, continuity, and path dependence, but resilience may require system-specific criteria and empirical or computational testing.

The self-consistency reading treats invariance as non-contradiction or stable self-relation across changing conditions. Ψ may become relevant here, but only if a self-binding structure is specified. PMS must not turn abstract consistency into literal selfhood.

An anti-capture reading remains possible: invariance may be too heterogeneous to be unified under any single structural grammar. This is not a defect. It may be one of the central results of the case: PMS can organize the closure-demand while preserving the possibility that invariance remains a family of concepts rather than one structure.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading is weakened if no relevant Δ can be specified. If there is no distinction between transformation and preservation, there is no invariance problem for PMS to map.

It is weakened if no frame □ can be identified. Without a frame, “same” has no admissible criterion. The analysis would then risk unscoped sameness-talk.

It is weakened if no transformation, context-shift, temporal interval, recurrence, or comparison condition is involved. Invariance requires something against which preservation is tested.

It is weakened if the invariant feature cannot be named. A claim that something “remains the same” without specifying what remains the same is under-specified.

It is under-discriminating if it cannot distinguish strict identity, formal invariance, functional invariance, structural invariance, normative invariance, empirical regularity, interpretive stability, resilience, and self-consistency.

It is over-assimilative if resistance, rival framing, rupture, absence, disagreement, or non-fit is automatically redescribed as PMS drift, incomplete integration, recontextualization, or maturity failure.

It is invalid if Ω is claimed where no power, exposure, vulnerability, capacity, authority, or obligation gradient exists.

It is invalid if Ψ is claimed where no self-binding, rule-binding, standard-binding, commitment, or self-relation exists.

It is invalid if Σ is treated as proof of adequacy. Coherence is not enough. A coherent reading can still be too smooth.

It is invalid if PMS translation is treated as PMS Base validation.

Operationalization would require a concrete object or structure; declared frame; transformation set; equivalence relation; tolerance rules; temporal grain if time is involved; edge-case and failure conditions; rival formalizations; and, where empirical or computational claims are made, actual testing protocols. None of these are fulfilled by the case material. The present output remains a boundary and taxonomy article, not an implementation artefact.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It says nothing about cognitive style, personality, pathology, rigidity, maturity, or psychological capacity.

It must not be used for person ranking. No one is more or less valuable, rational, mature, coherent, or dignified because of how an invariance claim is structurally mapped.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusion. It provides no evidentiary standard, liability rule, admissibility test, or culpability finding.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. PMS does not decide whether invariance is real, primitive, constructed, formal, empirical, semantic, or ontological.

It must not be used as empirical proof. No dataset or measurement protocol is present.

It must not be used as implementation validation. YAML form is not an algorithm or operational invariance detector.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. A successful case projection does not prove the PMS operator inventory, order, minimality, or completeness.

It must not turn an add-on into an operator, though no add-on is used here. Future overlays would remain subordinate lenses, not PMS Base revisions.

It must not treat drift as verdict. Drift risks are conditional structural risks, not diagnoses, scores, or moral findings.

It must not close by authority. AXIOM maps where closure is possible and where Λ-residue remains visible. It does not erase residue by declaring a final answer.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of this case is that invariance becomes structurally legible as a framed claim of preservation under difference. PMS Core can map the closure-demand by beginning with Δ, locating sameness inside □, treating Α and Θ as stabilization and persistence structures where the case warrants them, tracking Φ when invariance travels across contexts, using Χ to preserve reversibility and non-capture, allowing Σ only as provisional discriminative integration, and keeping Λ, Ω, and Ψ conditional rather than fully claimed.

The three examples contribute by separating major invariance families without turning them into evidence for PMS. The rotated square shows formal invariance: preservation of geometric structure under a declared transformation, without metaphysical identity. The stable API contract shows functional invariance: preserved public behavior across internal change, without implementation validation. The equal-deadline rule shows normative invariance: stable rule wording under unequal conditions, without legal, moral, or diagnostic verdict. Together, the examples demonstrate why invariance must always ask: invariant in which frame, under which transformation, with respect to which feature, for whom, across which time horizon, and with what residue still visible?

What remains unclaimed is just as important as what is claimed. The article does not prove what invariance ultimately is. It does not reduce mathematical invariance to PMS, replace formal tools, establish empirical regularity, decide metaphysical identity, validate software, assess lawfulness, diagnose persons, rank groups, or authorize enforcement. It does not claim that PMS exhausts rival readings. Strict identity, formal transformation theory, functional accounts, structural equivalence, empirical regularity, normative rule-stability, interpretive continuity, resilience, and self-consistency may each preserve dimensions PMS only partially maps.

The boundary discipline of the case is therefore central. Coherence is not proof. Smooth integration is not adequacy. Non-capture is not failure. A weak operator is not a defect. Non-assignment of drift is not a low score. Resistance, refusal, rupture, absence, disagreement, or rival framing must not be translated automatically into PMS drift, incomplete integration, recontextualization, or maturity failure. Dignity-in-Practice remains a praxeological constraint on use, not an ontological ranking. Ψ remains conditional binding, not metaphysical selfhood. Ω remains structural asymmetry, not moral accusation.

The final methodological takeaway is that invariance is not a single closure to be solved, but a family of closure-demands to be discriminated. PMS-AXIOM’s contribution is cartographic: it shows where an invariance claim can be structurally stabilized, where it requires further specification, where binding would be premature, and where Λ-residue must remain visible. Invariance becomes usable only when its frame, transformation, preserved feature, tolerance, temporal scope, and limits are declared. Where those conditions are absent, the responsible PMS result is not forced closure, but disciplined non-closure.
