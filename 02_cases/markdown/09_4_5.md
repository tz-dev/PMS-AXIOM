### Why this case matters

*Repair Relationships* matters because it concentrates one of the strongest closure-demands in practical life: the demand to know whether trust has been broken, whether repair is owed, whether refusal is justified, whether silence means avoidance, whether apology is enough, whether renewed reliance is possible, and whether non-repair is failure, protection, incapacity, resistance, or something that no available grammar can fully capture. Trust-repair situations tend to pull interpretation toward conclusion. The field asks for an answer: who failed whom, what should now happen, whether the relation can be restored, and whether the unresolved remainder is immaturity, betrayal, trauma, principled withdrawal, institutional failure, or irreparable difference.

The completed PMS Core case deliberately resists that closure-demand. It treats *Repair Relationships* not as an adjudicated event but as a conceptual domain projection. This matters because trust-repair language is especially prone to over-assimilation. Once one says “trust,” “betrayal,” “repair,” or “responsibility,” it becomes tempting to complete the missing scene: someone must have trusted, someone must have failed, someone must have withheld repair, someone must now carry the burden of restoration. The YAML refuses that move. It recognizes that the trust-repair field is structurally rich enough to activate much of PMS, while also being under-specified enough that nearly every strong application claim must remain suspended.

This makes the case suitable for AXIOM because AXIOM is not merely a repository of examples. It is a stress-test environment for whether a formal grammar can hold pressure without becoming a closure machine. *Repair Relationships* tests whether PMS can describe a field with high intuitive and normative charge while preserving distance, reversibility, dignity-in-practice, rival openness, and non-capture. It also tests whether an add-on overlay such as PMS-EDEN can sharpen the analysis without colonizing the base grammar. EDEN introduces questions of real asymmetry, responsibility/action-power distribution, false coordination, cost shifting, non-action, non-binding, correctability, and the description-to-action firewall. Those additions are analytically powerful, but they are dangerous if allowed to become verdicts.

The case is structurally difficult because trust repair is not a simple event. It is a configuration of distinction, expectation, absence, temporality, asymmetry, recontextualization, distance, integration, and possible self-binding. Trust may be damaged not only by an action but also by silence, delay, non-response, withheld acknowledgment, failed explanation, or a missing future commitment. Yet absence is not automatically culpable. Silence may be refusal, boundary, incapacity, strategic withholding, lack of obligation, procedural caution, or non-capture. Repair may involve apology, restitution, clarification, renewed conduct, institutional reform, or mutual recontextualization. Yet coherent repair-language is not repair. A promise is not self-binding merely because it sounds like commitment. Reconciliation is not maturity merely because it resolves tension.

This is also why the case is not solved metaphysically. PMS does not define the essence of trust, forgiveness, betrayal, or personhood. It does not claim that trust has a metaphysical core that can be identified independently of praxis. Nor is the case solved empirically. There is no data set, interview record, observable sequence, event log, or behavioral evidence. It is not solved clinically or diagnostically. Doubt, withdrawal, refusal, silence, or failed repair are not read as symptoms, traits, attachment patterns, pathology, or risk indicators. It is not solved legally or forensically. Reliance, breach, liability, evidence, duty, remedy, or procedural accountability remain outside the authority of the PMS reading unless an appropriate legal frame and evidence are supplied.

The case belongs in AXIOM precisely because it shows that a mature formal analysis need not end in stronger assertion. The layered YAML stack demonstrates that “full-depth” does not mean “full verdict.” In this case, analytical depth means preserving the structure of possible repair while refusing to invent the scene that would justify application. It means keeping the operator chain visible without allowing the chain to produce false closure. It means treating maturity not as the ability to decide, but as the ability to remain structurally exact under conditions where decision would be premature.

The philosophical closure-demand is especially intense because trust repair often appears to require resolution for praxis to continue. Unlike abstract disagreement, a damaged trust relation may interrupt cooperation, speech, reliance, institutional legitimacy, or shared future orientation. The unresolved state itself can become costly. Waiting for acknowledgment may expose one role. Refusing to return may expose another. Naming betrayal may stabilize a conflict. Refusing to name betrayal may erase harm. Accepting apology may restore movement. Accepting apology too quickly may collapse responsibility. Declining repair may protect dignity. Declining repair may also become a new non-event in another frame. The field is therefore structurally saturated with reasons to decide and equally saturated with reasons why deciding too soon would be analytically irresponsible.

This is why the case cannot be treated as a compact moral example. It does not ask, “What is the right thing to do after trust is broken?” It asks what must be structurally present before one may even say that trust was broken, that repair is available, that refusal is justified, that responsibility attaches, or that renewed self-binding has occurred. The case matters because it demonstrates how a grammar of praxis can remain useful precisely where moral, psychological, legal, and metaphysical shortcuts are most tempting.

The PMS Core case classifies *Repair Relationships* as a conceptual domain projection. The case asks how trust-repair phenomena can be structurally rendered through PMS operators, not whether any particular betrayal, apology, failure, refusal, or reconciliation has occurred.

It may also say that some derived structures become active, weak, under-specified, or analytic-only. It may map possible drift risks. The diagnostic boundary prevents any inference from the trust-repair field to mental states, traits, personality patterns, clinical conditions, attachment styles, pathology, competence, or risk. It can help describe structural relations among expectation, absence, asymmetry, temporality, and responsibility, but it cannot decide legal validity, forensic sufficiency, procedural fairness, or institutional legitimacy.

The implementation boundary is also explicit. YAML representation does not implement praxis, agency, morality, consciousness, repair, responsibility, dignity, or legal judgment. ### Scene and frame structure

The case has no concrete scene. Its scene boundary is the conceptual field in which trust may be tested, damaged, withdrawn, restored, refused, or reconfigured through reliance, vulnerability, expectation, confidence, doubt, repair, or betrayal between persons, roles, claims, or institutions. This boundary matters because it prevents the analysis from inventing facts. The case is not “someone betrayed someone.” It is “the trust-repair domain structurally involves the possibility of betrayal, repair, refusal, absence, and renewed reliance.”

The frame operator, □, is therefore central. Trust and repair are frame-dependent. Interpersonal trust, institutional trust, epistemic trust, contractual reliance, familial expectation, political legitimacy, philosophical recognition, and therapeutic repair are not the same frame. The PMS Core YAML repeatedly insists that the active frame must be specified before deciding what counts as reliance, expectation, failure, repair, betrayal, or refusal. In this case, no single frame is selected. The domain remains mixed: interpersonal, role-based, epistemic, institutional, contractual, familial, political, and philosophical frames are all possible, but none is concretely dominant.

Inside the frame are structural possibilities: trust/distrust, reliance/withdrawal, confidence/doubt, expectation/non-event, repair/non-repair, betrayal/non-betrayal, silence, delay, missing acknowledgment, withheld repair, refusal, renewed confidence, and future commitment. These are not findings; they are possible trust-field structures. What is outside the frame are conclusions requiring evidence: that betrayal occurred, that trust was owed, that repair was refused, that reconciliation should happen, that a person is responsible, that an institution is legitimate or illegitimate, that legal breach occurred, or that a clinical pattern is present.

The protected constraints are distance, reversibility, and dignity-in-practice. Distance, Χ, prevents immediate accusation, forced reconciliation, diagnosis, and closure. Reversibility keeps the reading revisable and non-final. Dignity-in-Practice protects against shaming, ranking, humiliation, person-level labeling, and coercive application under asymmetry. These constraints are not decorative. They are entry conditions for the application. If the analysis suspends them to force truth, clarity, authority, resolution, or action, it becomes formally invalid as a PMS application within the present usage convention.

Roles are present only as placeholders. In the EDEN overlay, role_A is a placeholder for a relying, affected, withdrawing, or repair-seeking position. Role_B is a placeholder for a trusted, responding, repairing, refusing, or institutionally positioned role. A field_or_medium may represent the relational, epistemic, institutional, contractual, familial, political, or philosophical frame. None of these placeholders is an identity. None is a person. None carries responsibility, guilt, maturity, dignity status, or action-power by default.

Temporality, Θ, is indispensable but under-specified. Trust repair is inherently temporal: reliance develops, expectations persist, silence delays, rupture changes the past’s meaning, apology or refusal occurs later, repair may require repeated conduct, and renewed trust depends on future enactment. Yet the case supplies no concrete chronology. The YAML therefore gives only a conceptual sequence: possible reliance or trust expectation; possible rupture, non-event, doubt, refusal, or failed acknowledgment; possible repair, withdrawal, recontextualization, integration, or non-capture. This is a structural trajectory field, not a timeline.

The reversibility window remains open. Because no concrete scene is supplied, the analysis must remain reversible at every strong point: betrayal may become misunderstanding; absence may become justified refusal; repair-language may become institutional reassurance rather than integration; withdrawal may become protection; non-repair may become legitimate boundary; apparent coherence may become narrative smoothing; apparent drift may become rival framing. Reversibility is the condition under which the conceptual richness of the case remains safe.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are possible but uncalibrated. Trust often involves vulnerability: one relies, waits, expects, discloses, depends, or remains exposed to another’s response. But the direction and type of asymmetry cannot be inferred from the concept alone. Exposure may be emotional, institutional, epistemic, material, procedural, reputational, temporal, linguistic, or interpretive. EDEN later sharpens this by asking for real Ω mapping: who has capacity, exposure, dependency, protection, obligation, access, timing control, frame control, recognition control, binding control, language access, explanation burden, vulnerability, action-power, risk, cost, and exit or waiting capacity? In the completed overlay, every such dimension remains indeterminate because no scene packet exists.

The absence of a concrete scene has decisive consequences. It allows operator relevance, calibration logic, and non-capture zones to be mapped. It prevents responsibility assignment, repair judgment, diagnosis, legal conclusion, implementation claim, dignity reading, EDEN finding, M-score for roles, and AHP transmission upgrade. It also means that the case is not weak because it refuses these conclusions. Its discipline consists in refusing them.

The scene boundary also determines how examples must later function. Examples may be introduced only as illustrative calibration scenes, not as hidden evidence for the completed case. They can show how the architecture works when a small amount of scene structure is added, but they cannot retroactively make the original YAML case concrete. This distinction will remain important when the article later introduces three worked examples.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading applies the eleven PMS operators in order: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, and Ψ. The completed YAML treats them not as a checklist to force total capture, but as a dependency-ordered grammar for distinguishing what can and cannot be said about the trust-repair domain.

**Δ, Difference**, is the entry point. In this case it marks trust versus distrust, reliance versus withdrawal, confidence versus doubt, expectation versus failure, repair versus non-repair, betrayal versus non-betrayal, and person, role, claim, or institution as distinct relational sites. Δ is active and major because without such distinctions the trust-repair field cannot even be structured. Yet Δ does not prove that any distinction is concretely instantiated. Naming trust/distrust does not show that trust existed; naming betrayal/non-betrayal does not show that betrayal is the correct contrast. The calibration task is to specify which distinction is active in a future scene and to preserve the possibility that the distinction itself is contested, misunderstood, frame-dependent, or non-captured.

**∇, Impulse**, appears as structural directedness. In trust repair, there may be movement toward reliance, withdrawal, repair, doubt, renewed confidence, protection, accusation, apology, or refusal. ∇ is active as a case-relevant operator, but it is not psychological desire. It does not infer motive, affect, intention, pathology, resentment, guilt, fear, or hope. Its calibration question is: what direction of praxis is structurally present, and in which frame? If no directional orientation arises from Δ, ∇ is under-specified. If ∇ is read as inner state, it is overclaimed.

**□, Frame**, is central. It constrains and shapes what trust, reliance, absence, and repair mean. A missed reply in a friendship, a delayed institutional response, a legal breach, a theological confession, a clinical repair process, and an epistemic credibility dispute have different frames. □ is active because the case explicitly spans persons, roles, claims, and institutions. But its activation is partial in the current case because no concrete dominant frame is selected. The main overclaim would be to slide between frames: to treat interpersonal hurt as legal breach, institutional legitimacy as emotional repair, legal remedy as forgiveness, or philosophical recognition as concrete reconciliation.

**Λ, Non-Event**, is one of the strongest operators in the case. Trust may be tested through absence: silence where response is expected, delay where confirmation is needed, a missing acknowledgment, an unmet promise, a withheld apology, an absent repair action, or unresolved expectation. Λ is active at the conceptual level because trust-repair phenomena often hinge on what does not happen. But Λ requires □. Absence matters only when a frame establishes an expectation. If no structured expectation exists, absence is not yet a PMS non-event. The YAML therefore preserves alternative readings: absence may be justified refusal, unavailable evidence, procedural delay, incapacity, boundary, rival framing, irreparability, or non-capture. The major overclaim would be to treat every silence as avoidance, every delay as betrayal, or every missing repair as failed maturity.

**Α, Attractor**, becomes relevant when trust, doubt, withdrawal, avoidance, repair attempts, or betrayal narratives stabilize into recurrent patterns or role scripts. The YAML marks Α as weak or moderate. It is conceptually relevant because trust relations often become patterned over time: repeated silence, repeated reassurance, repeated failure, repeated repair, repeated suspicion. But no recurrence evidence is supplied. A single conceptual mention of trust repair does not establish attractor formation. The calibration demand is to show recurrence, stabilization, path-dependence, routine, or role-script formation. Without that, Α remains weak. Overclaiming Α would turn the domain into a pattern diagnosis.

**Ω, Asymmetry**, is salient but weakly calibrated. Trust and reliance imply possible vulnerability, exposure, dependence, credibility asymmetry, role authority, institutional power, obligation, or capacity difference. Ω is therefore major in analytic weight. But PMS dependency makes it depend on Α, and the current case has only weak attractor support. EDEN later sharpens the same issue by requiring real Ω mapping, but the PMS Core already warns that asymmetry is structural, not a moral verdict. The calibration task is to specify who or what is exposed, obligated, empowered, dependent, or positioned. The overclaim would be to moralize asymmetry by default, to assign victim/perpetrator roles without evidence, or to infer responsibility from vulnerability alone.

**Θ, Temporality**, is central and also under-specified. Trust repair requires time: expectation persists, absence delays, damage accumulates, repair unfolds, renewed reliance must be maintained, and self-binding must endure. Θ is active at the domain level because trust repair cannot be reduced to a static contrast. Yet the actual sequence is absent. There is no before, rupture, delay, attempted repair, refusal, repetition, or restoration trace. The calibration task is to distinguish diachronic trajectory from conceptual ordering. Overclaiming Θ would infer development, deterioration, healing, or maturity trajectory where only domain logic exists.

**Φ, Recontextualization**, appears where rupture, doubt, failure, or absence is placed into a new frame: misunderstanding, accountability, changed obligation, renewed trust, justified withdrawal, institutional reform, or irreparable rupture. Φ is important because repair often involves reframing. But it remains weak unless source frame and target frame are named. Recontextualization can clarify, but it can also erase. If every rupture is redescribed as misunderstanding, every refusal as healing, every absence as structured non-event, or every disagreement as incomplete integration, Φ becomes over-assimilative. The YAML therefore requires that recontextualization preserve possible non-fit.

**Χ, Distance**, is the operator that protects the case from becoming a tribunal. Χ suspends verdict, inhibits immediate accusation or reconciliation, preserves reversibility, and prevents PMS from becoming enforcement, person-ranking, diagnosis, or forced closure. In the completed Core case, Χ is active and satisfied. It is not mere neutrality, indifference, avoidance, or strategic delay. It is reflective withdrawal from premature closure. If Χ is suspended to force blame, absolution, reconciliation, diagnosis, legal conclusion, or maturity judgment, the PMS application becomes invalid. In this case, Χ is not optional; it is the hinge that keeps the analysis usable.

**Σ, Integration**, is relevant to repair but weak. Restoration of trust would require integrating reliance, vulnerability, expectation, rupture, recontextualization, responsibility, and future enactment. But the case supplies no enacted integration. The YAML therefore treats Σ as a conditional repair-threshold rather than an achieved result. A coherent explanation of repair is not yet repair. Recontextualization is not yet integration. Institutional reassurance is not yet integration. A smooth narrative is not yet integration. The calibration task is to test whether tensions are actually coordinated in practice without being flattened. The overclaim would be to treat coherence as proof of repair, truth, responsibility, adequacy, or maturity.

**Ψ, Self-Binding**, is analytic-only and conditional. Restored trust may require accountability, promise-keeping, renewed role ownership, institutional constraint, or stable commitment across time. Yet no concrete actor, role, or institution is shown to bind itself to integrated commitments. A promise, apology, declaration, policy, or renewed confidence is not self-binding merely by being stated. Ψ depends on Σ, Θ, and Χ. Since Σ is weak and the concrete temporal path is absent, Ψ cannot be strongly activated. SELF_FIXPOINT remains analytic-only. The overclaim would be to infer subjectivity, identity, accountability, or institutional selfhood from repair-language alone.

The Core reading therefore uses the full PMS operator sequence without pretending that all later operators are achieved. The early operators—Δ, ∇, □, and Λ—are strongest because the domain itself supplies distinctions, directed tensions, frames, and possible meaningful absences. Α, Ω, Θ, and Φ are structurally important but calibration-dependent. Χ is active as a governing restraint. Σ and Ψ remain conditional or analytic-only. This asymmetry within the operator chain is not a weakness; it is what prevents the PMS reading from manufacturing a concrete case out of conceptual material.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The PMS dependency discipline is one of the most important parts of the completed Core YAML. Later operators cannot be used cheaply. Ψ cannot be asserted merely because the case involves trust. Σ cannot be asserted merely because the analysis is coherent. Φ cannot be asserted merely because a new interpretation is available. Ω cannot be asserted in a morally loaded direction merely because vulnerability is mentioned. Α cannot be asserted merely because trust relations often repeat. Λ cannot be asserted merely because something is absent.

This matters because trust repair tempts the interpreter to leap forward in the chain. One sees absence and infers failure. One sees vulnerability and infers asymmetry. One sees asymmetry and infers responsibility. One sees responsibility and infers apology. One sees apology and infers repair. One sees repair-language and infers self-binding. PMS forbids that sequence unless the dependencies are satisfied. The chain must remain calibrated.

Σ remains conditional because integration requires more than narrative coherence. A repair account may integrate terms conceptually while failing to integrate practice. It may smooth over conflict, pressure reconciliation, erase refusal, convert institutional messaging into accountability, or treat disagreement as under-integration. The YAML therefore requires that Σ discriminate rather than smooth. Integration must coordinate rupture, expectation, vulnerability, responsibility, and future practice in a scene. None is supplied.

Ψ remains conditional because self-binding requires integrated commitments across time under reflective distance. It is not enough for someone to say “I will repair,” for an institution to issue a policy, or for a relationship to resume. Self-binding requires that a concrete actor, role, or institution remain bound to integrated structures across time. Without concrete Σ, Θ, and Χ evidence, Ψ remains analytic-only.

Coherence is not proof. This warning runs through the PMS Core, EDEN overlay, MIP analysis, and AHP hardening layer. The case fits PMS vocabulary well. Trust repair naturally involves difference, expectation, non-event, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and self-binding. That fit creates coherence risk. A strong formal match may feel like explanatory success, but PMS translation does not prove exhaustive capture, empirical adequacy, moral truth, legal validity, or practical maturity.

Non-capture is not failure. The case explicitly preserves the possibility that some aspects of trust, vulnerability, forgiveness, hurt, fear, hope, confidence, refusal, and irreparable rupture exceed PMS representation. Non-capture may also appear where legal proof, institutional legitimacy, cultural meaning, religious interpretation, phenomenological experience, or strategic interaction requires a rival grammar. The analysis must not redescribe all resistance, refusal, disagreement, absence, or rival framing as drift, failed integration, incomplete calibration, or immaturity.

PMS must not become a closure machine. This is the central discipline of the Core reading. The operators generate intelligibility conditions, not verdicts. They help ask better questions: What distinction is active? What frame makes absence meaningful? What asymmetry exists? What temporal sequence matters? What recontextualization is being proposed? Is distance maintained? Is integration enacted? Is self-binding demonstrated? But they do not decide the case by themselves.

Invalid dependency shortcuts would include treating trust vocabulary as proof of Λ; treating meaningful absence as proof of betrayal; treating vulnerability as proof of Ω in a fixed direction; treating Ω as proof of responsibility; treating repair-language as proof of Σ; treating apology or promise as proof of Ψ; treating coherence as proof of truth; treating refusal as failed integration; treating non-fit as drift; treating PMS rendering as Base validation. The completed YAML repeatedly blocks these shortcuts.

The dependency discipline also matters for the later overlays. EDEN’s reduced signatures are explicitly overlay shorthand only. They do not replace PMS formulas. MIP’s A-band, M-band, IA-box, and D-module do not revise PMS operators. AHP’s Precision Heuristic and attack points do not rescore the MIP result. Each layer inherits the same principle: later compressions may help render the analysis, but they must not become shortcuts around the underlying dependency structure.

### Derived structures

The completed PMS Core case treats derived structures as formula-derived constructs, not primitive axes, diagnoses, metaphysical entities, or independent proofs. Awareness_A, Coherence_C, Responsibility_R, Action_E, Dignity_in_Practice_D, and SELF_FIXPOINT are generated through operator compositions. Their presence, weakness, or suspension depends on those formulas and on the case material.

**Awareness_A** is active as a conceptual projection. Its formula is Θ, □, and Δ: sustained, framed differentiation across time. In the trust-repair domain, Awareness_A appears as the ability to distinguish trust, distrust, reliance, withdrawal, confidence, doubt, repair, betrayal, and non-repair within a frame and across a temporal field. It is structural awareness, not phenomenological consciousness. The case can say that the domain supports such differentiated awareness. It cannot say that any person is aware, unaware, reflective, avoidant, or mature. Calibration would require a concrete frame, temporal sequence, and role-specific distinctions.

**Coherence_C** is also active as a conceptual projection. Its formula includes Θ, Λ, □, and ∇: temporally stabilized structuring of impulse and expectation within a frame. Trust repair depends heavily on coherence because reliance and withdrawal become meaningful only when directional movement is framed by expectations and non-events over time. A delayed response, an unmet promise, a repeated repair attempt, or renewed reliance may form a coherent trajectory. But coherence does not prove adequacy. A coherent betrayal narrative may be false. A coherent repair narrative may be premature. A coherent institutional account may be defensive messaging. Coherence_C is active, but not decisive.

**Responsibility_R** is weak and conditional. Its formula involves Ψ, Φ, Θ, and Ω: self-binding orientation toward asymmetry extended across time and recontextualization. The case makes responsibility relevant because trust repair often concerns obligation, vulnerability, accountability, and future conduct. But the required elements are not established. There is no calibrated asymmetry, no concrete temporal obligation, no demonstrated recontextualization, and no self-binding actor or institution. Responsibility_R therefore cannot be assigned. It remains a future calibration field, not a moral verdict, legal liability, diagnosis, or blame attribution.

**Action_E** is under-specified. Its formula is Σ, Θ, and ∇: integrated realization of directedness across time. The case provides no concrete withdrawal, apology, restitution, repair attempt, refusal, renewed reliance, institutional change, or future practice. It contains action-possibilities, but no enactment. This distinction is crucial. A structural analysis of repair is not repair. A conceptual mapping of refusal is not refusal. A promise mentioned as a possibility is not enacted self-binding. Action_E remains under-specified because the case lacks scene data.

**Dignity_in_Practice_D** is active as an application constraint, not as a dignity score or metaphysical claim. Its formula is Ψ, Χ, and Ω: self-bound reflective restraint and protection in asymmetrical relations. In this case, D functions by constraining the analysis itself. It prevents shaming, humiliation, person-ranking, diagnosis, forced reconciliation, and irreversible attribution. It requires that possible asymmetry be handled with restraint. It also prevents the analysis from ranking human beings or converting structural maturity into moral superiority. Because Ψ is not concretely established, D is not a finding about a subject’s dignity performance. It is a guardrail on the analytic posture.

**SELF_FIXPOINT** is analytic-only. The formula sequence is Ψ ∘ Σ ∘ Χ ∘ Φ ∘ Θ ∘ Ω ∘ Α ∘ Λ ∘ □ ∘ ∇ ∘ Δ. In PMS, this represents self-binding as the result of integrated, reflexively modulated, temporally extended, asymmetry-aware patterns of praxis being bound into a relatively stable self-relation. In this case it is not primary. It becomes relevant only if a concrete actor, role, or institution binds itself to integrated repair commitments across time. No such binding is supplied. The YAML therefore refuses to infer literal selfhood, consciousness, metaphysical subjectivity, or institutional personhood. SELF_FIXPOINT remains a possible analytic structure for future calibrated repair, not a result.

The derived-structure analysis is a major safeguard against premature application. It shows that even when Awareness_A and Coherence_C are active at the conceptual level, Responsibility_R, Action_E, and SELF_FIXPOINT remain weak, under-specified, or analytic-only. Dignity_in_Practice_D is active only as a constraint on handling the analysis. This prevents the article from moving from “the field is intelligible” to “someone is responsible,” from “the narrative is coherent” to “repair occurred,” or from “commitment is conceptually relevant” to “self-binding exists.”

### PMS drift-pattern status

The PMS Core case treats drift patterns as possible structural risks, not as findings. This distinction is crucial. A drift pattern in PMS is not a diagnosis, not a person type, not a clinical category, not a moral verdict, and not evidence that anyone has failed. It is a derived structural distortion possibility that may become relevant only if the required operator conditions are concretely supported. In *Repair Relationships*, the completed YAML marks drift-pattern status as `possible_but_not_entailed`.

The first drift risk is **AD_A>>E**, excessive distance between awareness and enactment. In PMS terms, this risk concerns a configuration in which evaluative or positional asymmetry becomes stabilized through attractor formation while recontextualization reinforces evaluation rather than supporting balanced enactment. For the trust-repair domain, AD_A>>E could appear if a relation becomes trapped in interpretation, evaluation, suspicion, accusation, or reflective distance while concrete repair, accountability, withdrawal, renewed practice, or protected refusal remains blocked or indefinitely delayed.

This is a plausible trust-repair risk because repair fields often contain strong awareness without adequate enactment. Persons, roles, or institutions may know what happened, speak fluently about rupture, identify possible asymmetries, and generate sophisticated narratives of repair or non-repair, while nothing actually changes in practice. A relationship may become rich in explanation and poor in action. An institution may produce language of accountability that substitutes for structural change. A party may repeatedly reinterpret the rupture without entering the practical cost of repair. A system may become reflective enough to describe the asymmetry while remaining unable or unwilling to alter the conditions that reproduce it.

Yet the completed YAML is careful: this is only a risk. It is not established in the case. There is no concrete recurrence, no actor map, no stabilized evaluative pattern, no observable gap between awareness and enactment, no evidence that action is suppressed, and no proof that recontextualization reinforces drift. The awareness axis and action axis remain under-specified for concrete roles. Treating AD_A>>E as active would be an overclaim.

The second drift risk is **AD_Sigma_low**, fragmented integration. This risk concerns a pattern in which recontextualization outpaces integration. Frames may be shifted repeatedly, explanations may be updated, meanings of rupture or repair may be revised, and obligations may be recoded, while no stable integration of expectations, responsibility, vulnerability, and future practice occurs. In trust-repair terms, this could look like repeated reframing of apology, betrayal, refusal, obligation, or restoration without actual integration.

Again, the case makes this risk intelligible but does not establish it. There is no evidence of volatile reframing. There is no concrete sequence of changing explanations. There is no demonstrated failure to consolidate responsibility or repair practice. There is no role-specific instability. The YAML therefore treats AD_Sigma_low as `possible_but_not_entailed`. The coherence axis and responsibility axis are under-specified.

The broader coherence risk is high because the case fits PMS vocabulary unusually well. Trust repair naturally calls up difference, expectation, absence, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and self-binding. This means PMS can produce a smooth and convincing rendering. But smoothness is precisely the risk. A coherent PMS rendering may become under-discriminating if it cannot distinguish betrayal from disappointment, justified refusal from avoidance, legal breach from relational hurt, institutional reassurance from repair, apology from self-binding, silence from meaningful non-event, and non-fit from failed maturity.

The completed YAML therefore treats under-specification as a restraint condition, not as a defect. The absence of concrete data does not mean the case has a “low score.” It means strong drift attribution is not permitted. Non-assignment is not failure. Weak status is not immaturity. Possible drift is not finding. The analysis preserves drift-pattern language only as a future calibration field.

This also means that drift language must not be used rhetorically. It would be a misuse to say that a person who talks about repair but does not act is necessarily AD_A>>E, or that a relation with shifting explanations is necessarily AD_Sigma_low. The patterns require recurrence, stabilization, asymmetry, recontextualization, and integration conditions that the current case does not supply. The correct use of drift analysis here is preparatory: it tells future analysts what to watch for, not what has already occurred.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant PMS add-on in this case is **PMS-EDEN**. It is followed by the later **MIP** and **MIP-AHP** analysis layers, which are not PMS add-ons in the same sense but maturity, transmission, precision, and hardening overlays over the completed case analysis. None of these layers replaces PMS Core. Together, they add visibility to aspects that PMS Core alone does not isolate as explicitly: real asymmetry, responsibility/action-power distribution, false coordination, cost distribution, non-action, non-binding, correctability, maturity-in-practice, transmission risk, attack surfaces, and hardening requirements.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer here because the trust-repair domain raises questions that the Core PMS operator grammar can make visible but not fully differentiate. Core PMS can say that Ω is possible, Θ is central, Λ is important, Χ must be maintained, Σ is conditional, and Ψ is analytic-only. PMS-EDEN asks a sharper overlay question: if a future concrete trust-repair scene is supplied, does it truthfully coordinate real Ω, or does it falsely coordinate real Ω through displaced responsibility, shifted costs, blocked or externalized Σ/Ψ, and weakened shared correctability?

This EDEN question is not a PMS operator. It is an overlay handle. EDEN’s constructs—Adult Reciprocity, False-Coordinated Real Asymmetry, Action-Power, AEP, PFO, Reciprocity Loss, Correctability, Minimal Recovery Signature, and the description-to-action firewall—remain subordinate to PMS Base. They do not redefine Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, or Ψ. They do not change PMS dependency order. They do not add new PMS derived axes. They do not validate PMS Base. They organize already available PMS structures through a more specific application profile.

The EDEN overlay treats **Adult Reciprocity** as a normative benchmark for truthful coordination of real Ω. In this case it remains indeterminate. Adult Reciprocity would require real asymmetry to be named, responsibility to follow actual structuring effect, Σ and Ψ to remain open rather than blocked or simulated, Χ and reversibility to be preserved, and Dignity-in-Practice to be protected. The conceptual Core case does not supply concrete Ω, responsibility distribution, integration, self-binding, or cost mapping. Adult Reciprocity therefore functions only as a future test, not as a finding.

**False-Coordinated Real Asymmetry** is likewise not established. EDEN defines the relevant mechanism as real Ω plus a false coordination frame, displaced responsibility, and blocked, weakened, simulated, avoided, or externalized Σ/Ψ. The completed overlay explicitly says that none of these components is concretely shown. No real Ω is mapped. No displaced responsibility is established. No false coordination frame is identified. No blocked or externalized Σ/Ψ is evidenced. The overlay therefore cannot affirm a false-coordination finding.

The **Minimal Recovery Signature** is present only as a viability threshold. Its reduced shorthand—Ω named, responsibility reattached to real effect, Λ made speakable, Χ restored, Σ reopened, Ψ self-bound, and D protected—is not a sequence, duty, intervention protocol, repair guarantee, or PMS-derived axis. It describes what a future recovery-oriented scene would need to make visible. The current case cannot show that the signature is fulfilled.

The EDEN overlay also sharpens the status of **non-action** and **non-binding**. PMS Core already treats Λ as meaningful absence only when a frame establishes expectation. EDEN adds that non-action becomes praxis only when it structures a scene under □/Λ/Ω/Θ. Silence, delay, refusal, non-response, and absence are not praxis by default. They do not prove motive, manipulation, drift, responsibility, avoidance, or failure. Similarly, non-binding is not automatically evasion. It may be legitimate where uncertainty, cost, alternatives, or exit conditions remain live. This is one of the most important EDEN contributions: it blocks the temptation to treat lack of repair or lack of commitment as immature by default.

The EDEN overlay’s **responsibility/action-power distribution** remains indeterminate. It asks who structures conditions, who bears costs, who has legibility, who has alternatives, who can act, who controls timing or frame, and who remains exposed. But no such distribution is supplied. Role_A and role_B remain placeholders. The overlay refuses guilt, hidden motive, moral rank, person type, or total liability. Action-Power is capacity to structure praxis conditions; it is not blame, motive, person type, moral rank, diagnosis, or a PMS operator.

The overlay also treats **immunization**, **overexposure**, and **AEP** as not supported. Immunization would require a pattern of effect without responsibility. Overexposure would require responsibility without sufficient legibility or protection. AEP would require high exposure, low legitimate language, critique-to-threat switch, and responsibility overload. None of these components is present in the conceptual case. The overlay therefore records them as possible future profiles only if a concrete scene supplies evidence. They are not sex truths, person identities, diagnoses, or verdicts.

The EDEN overlay remains governed by the **description-to-action firewall**. Structural description supplies no independent application authority. Even if a future scene made EDEN-relevant patterns visible, the analysis itself would not authorize confrontation, punishment, public exposure, coercion, forced repair, institutional sanction, therapy planning, or legal decision. Application would require independent authority, affected-role contestation, contextual responsibility, evidentiary answerability, reversibility, and dignity protection.

The later **MIP layer** adds a different kind of overlay. It does not reinterpret PMS operators or EDEN constructs. It evaluates the maturity-in-practice of the completed case-analysis posture. It asks how the layered analysis handles awareness, coherence, responsibility, power-to-act, dignity protection, inadult asymmetry risk, trajectory, bias, open questions, possible next steps, and transmission. MIP’s object is not a person or real relationship. Its object is the completed conceptual YAML stack itself.

MIP finds relatively strong A-awareness and C-coherence at the analysis-artifact level. The prior layers clearly know what they are and what they are not. They distinguish conceptual projection from concrete scene, source material from inference, guardrail from finding, and overlay from base grammar. MIP therefore allows a provisional A-band for the analysis posture, not for persons or the concrete case. The completed MIP analysis assigns no concrete M-score to role_A, role_B, or any institution. Responsibility remains located in the analysis procedure: do not overclaim, do not transmit as a decision basis, do not treat placeholders as persons, and do not convert D-language into a dignity reading.

MIP’s IA-box identifies the central asymmetry as methodological: the model stack has high structuring power, while the case material has low concrete evidence. This asymmetry is functional only as an internal working note because it is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. It becomes IA-risk if the same material is transmitted as authority, used to pressure persons, or treated as a decision basis.

MIP also activates the D-module only as a protection signal. D0 remains untouched. D1 and D2 are not scored. No D-Quick-Grid level is assigned. There is no dignity profile of any person. The D-module exists to protect against public shaming, dignity labeling, person-ranking, forced reconciliation, and misuse of maturity language. In other words, MIP does not make the case more verdictive. It makes the conditions of non-verdictive analysis more explicit.

The final **MIP-AHP layer** is a second-order quality overlay. It does not rescore A, M, IA, or D. It does not activate D. It does not create a D-profile. It does not change transmission status. It hardens the previous MIP output by identifying precision strength, attack surfaces, and hardening actions. Its Precision Heuristic is high because the previous MIP analysis defines terms, marks scope, states thresholds, links evidence, preserves reversibility, and maintains language hygiene.

AHP’s attack points are not criticisms of persons or of the trust-repair domain. They are vulnerabilities in the analysis artifact. It flags that formal coherence may be mistaken for empirical grounding; that the numeric-looking A-band may be extracted as a person or case score; that D-module fields may be misread as a dignity profile; that the IA-box result may be misread as application clearance; that “full-depth” may be mistaken for exhaustive capture; that counter-perspectives are constructed rather than voice-grounded; and that plain-language portability remains underdeveloped. These are hardening targets for future iterations, not failures of the present analysis.

The add-on stack therefore increases analytical specificity without increasing first-order claim strength. EDEN names what a concrete trust-repair scene would need to show before false coordination, reciprocity loss, or recovery could be assessed. MIP evaluates the maturity of the analysis posture, not the maturity of persons. AHP tests the quality and attack surface of that MIP artifact, not the truth of a concrete case. Each add-on contributes a distinct type of restraint.

### Layer interaction

The layer interaction is disciplined by subordination. PMS Core remains the base structural grammar. PMS-EDEN remains an overlay/application profile. MIP remains a maturity-in-practice review of the completed layered analysis. AHP remains a second-order quality overlay over the MIP artifact. None of the later layers may redefine earlier ones.

What remains PMS Core is the operator architecture: Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Α, Ω, Θ, Φ, Χ, Σ, and Ψ; the derived structures A, C, R, E, D, and SELF_FIXPOINT; the dependency table; the guardrails of distance, reversibility, dignity-in-practice, non-diagnosis, rival openness, and non-capture. PMS Core also retains the central claim that the case is a conceptual domain projection, not a concrete scene.

What EDEN sharpens is the asymmetry and repair-responsibility field. PMS Core can say that Ω is possible and responsibility is weak. EDEN asks whether real Ω is truthfully coordinated, whether responsibility follows real structuring effect, whether action-power is distributed or hidden, whether costs are shifted, whether non-action structures the scene, whether non-binding externalizes load, whether correctability is preserved, and whether description is firewalled from action. These are sharper questions, but they are not new PMS operators.

What MIP sharpens is the maturity of the analysis itself. PMS Core and EDEN already refuse strong findings. MIP asks whether that refusal is mature, coherent, reversible, dignified, and safe to transmit. It distinguishes analysis posture from person scoring. It treats the main responsibility as procedural: keeping the artifact within its legitimate use. It therefore shifts the maturity question away from “who in the case is mature?” toward “is the analysis mature in how it handles an under-specified, high-pressure trust-repair field?”

What AHP sharpens is the artifact’s precision risk. It recognizes that even a guardrail-heavy YAML can be misused. AHP does not say the previous output is invalid. It says that the output’s formal completeness creates predictable attack surfaces. A careful reader might preserve the boundaries; a careless reader might extract the A-band, over-read D-language, treat the IA-box as clearance, or confuse structured prior analysis with independent evidence. AHP therefore adds hardening without expanding claim strength.

Overlay language could overreach in several ways. EDEN language could make the case sound as if false coordination has been found. MIP language could make the case sound as if maturity has been scored. D-language could make the case sound as if dignity has been assessed. AHP language could make the case sound as if hardening upgrades the artifact to external use. The completed analyses block all of these overreaches. EDEN remains suspended for application. MIP remains working-note only. AHP does not upgrade transmission.

Non-closure remains visible at every layer. PMS Core preserves non-capture and rival readings. EDEN requires counter-scenes and falsifiers. MIP requires open questions, blind spots, and review before transmission. AHP identifies attack surfaces and hardening actions. The stack therefore becomes more precise without becoming more closed. This is the central layered achievement of the case.

The layers also interact through a shared refusal to treat non-assignment as deficit. PMS Core does not assign Responsibility_R because the conditions are absent. EDEN does not assign false coordination because the scene packet is absent. MIP does not assign concrete M-scores because no role evidence is supplied. AHP does not treat attack points as failures of persons or of the case. In each layer, withholding is not weakness. Withholding is the correct response to missing conditions.

This is why the integrated stack is not simply additive. Each layer both expands and restricts. PMS Core expands intelligibility while restricting verdict. EDEN expands asymmetry analysis while restricting action authority. MIP expands maturity reflection while restricting score use. AHP expands precision review while restricting transmission upgrade. The resulting article is therefore not a staircase toward certainty. It is a set of increasingly explicit boundary disciplines around a closure-sensitive field.

### Example 1 — The unanswered acknowledgement

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions,” before “Misuse boundary.”

**Bounded vignette.**
A small research group has an informal practice: when one member sends a draft containing substantial work, the recipient normally acknowledges receipt within a few days, even if substantive feedback will take longer. One member sends a draft that includes sensitive revisions and an explicit note: “No rush on comments, but please confirm that this reached you.” No acknowledgement comes. After ten days, the sender sends a brief follow-up. Again, no reply. Several weeks later, the recipient reappears in a group meeting and speaks as if the work stream is proceeding normally. The sender feels unsure whether the silence was accidental, dismissive, strategic, overloaded, or a sign that the working relation is no longer reliable.

This example is deliberately bounded. It does not say that betrayal occurred. It does not say that the recipient acted wrongly. It does not say that the sender is entitled to repair. It supplies only enough concrete structure to show why trust-repair analysis becomes tempting: there is a frame of expected acknowledgement, a non-event, a temporal delay, possible asymmetry in visibility and waiting, and a later scene in which the unresolved absence becomes relevant.

**What the example demonstrates.**
The example demonstrates why Λ, the structured non-event, is central to trust repair. The important feature is not mere silence. Silence becomes analytically relevant only because the frame already contains an expectation: a confirmation of receipt is normally given, and the sender explicitly requested it. The non-event therefore has a possible structuring effect. It may affect whether the sender continues to rely on the recipient, whether future work is shared, whether explanation is requested, or whether the collaboration is recontextualized.

At the same time, the example demonstrates why Λ must not be overclaimed. The absence could have many explanations: technical failure, overload, illness, misunderstanding, different communication norms, avoidance, disagreement, or lack of perceived obligation. The PMS reading may mark the absence as meaningful within a frame, but it cannot decide what the absence means without further scene data.

**PMS Core hooks.**
Δ appears in the distinctions between acknowledgement and non-acknowledgement, reliance and withdrawal, normal workflow and disrupted expectation, repair and non-repair. ∇ appears as directional tension: the sender may move toward renewed inquiry, withdrawal, accusation, protection of future work, or suspension of reliance. □ is the research-group communication frame, including the informal norm of acknowledging receipt. Λ is the missing confirmation. Α remains weak unless the silence repeats or stabilizes into a pattern. Ω is possible but not yet calibrated: perhaps the recipient has more institutional standing, perhaps the sender is more exposed, perhaps neither asymmetry is significant. Θ is active because the delay changes the meaning of the scene over time. Φ may appear if the silence is later reframed as misunderstanding, overload, avoidance, or boundary. Χ is necessary because the analysis must suspend verdict. Σ is not achieved merely because someone later explains the delay. Ψ is not achieved merely because someone says, “I’ll do better next time.” Integration and self-binding would require changed practice over time.

**Add-on hooks.**
The EDEN overlay would ask whether real Ω is present and whether responsibility follows actual structuring effect. Did the silence shift waiting costs to the sender? Did the recipient control timing, frame, or recognition? Was non-action structuring the relation, or merely incidental? Was there false coordination, or simply an under-specified communication failure? None of these can be answered from the vignette alone.

The Minimal Recovery Signature would not be treated as fulfilled. A future repair scene might name the missing acknowledgement, reattach responsibility to actual effect, make the non-event speakable, restore distance, reopen integration, and establish a practice of acknowledgement. But the example does not show that this has occurred. It only shows where such a test would begin.

**MIP / AHP / QC status.**
MIP would treat the example as a useful calibration scene, not a maturity assessment of either party. It may increase awareness of frame, non-event, and temporality, but it does not authorize an A-score or M-score for the sender or recipient. The main MIP responsibility is procedural: distinguish what is known from what is inferred, preserve alternatives, and avoid turning silence into a person label.

AHP would flag likely attack surfaces. The phrase “unanswered acknowledgement” could be over-read as evidence of culpability. The group norm could be treated as more fixed than it is. The sender’s uncertainty could be mistaken for proof of harm. A hardening note would require the example to keep the norm, expectation, and cost distribution explicit.

**Forbidden claims.**
This example does not diagnose avoidance, passive aggression, narcissism, anxiety, bad faith, incompetence, or immaturity. It does not establish breach, liability, harassment, exploitation, betrayal, or institutional misconduct. It does not prove that repair is owed. It does not validate PMS, EDEN, MIP, or AHP. It does not rank the parties.

**Non-capture note.**
The lived uncertainty of waiting for a reply may exceed structural mapping. The silence may feel humiliating, trivial, threatening, or insignificant depending on histories the example does not supply. PMS can mark the non-event; it cannot exhaust the affective or relational meaning of being left without acknowledgement.

**Article takeaway.**
This example is useful because it shows the case’s central discipline: absence can matter without yet deciding what it means. PMS makes the absence legible; EDEN asks whether it structures asymmetry and cost; MIP protects against premature scoring; AHP hardens the example against over-reading. The example prepares the reader to see why trust repair requires both structural precision and restraint.

### Example 2 — The apology that explains but does not yet bind

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “PMS Core structural reading” or after “Derived structures.”

**Bounded vignette.**
Two collaborators have worked together for several months on a public-facing essay. One collaborator, A, expects that major changes will be discussed before submission. The other collaborator, B, submits a version with substantial revisions and only informs A afterwards. A says that the submission has damaged trust because the shared authorship frame was altered without prior consultation. B apologizes and explains that the deadline was close, that the changes seemed minor in the moment, and that there was no intention to bypass A. B also says, “I understand why this landed badly, and I’ll be more careful next time.”

The apology changes the scene, but it does not close it. A may accept the explanation, remain uncertain, request a future process, withdraw from the collaboration, or treat the relation as repairable but not yet repaired. B’s apology may be sincere and still insufficient as self-binding. A’s continued hesitation may be reasonable and still not a verdict against B.

**What the example demonstrates.**
This example demonstrates the difference between Φ, Σ, and Ψ. B’s explanation recontextualizes the event: what looked like disregard may be reframed as deadline pressure, misjudgment, or frame mismatch. That is Φ. But recontextualization is not yet integration. For Σ to be active, the conflicting elements would need to be coordinated in practice: shared authorship expectations, deadline pressure, future decision rules, communication procedures, and the relational effect of the prior submission. For Ψ to be active, B or the collaboration would need to become bound across time to the integrated structure, not merely express regret.

The example is useful because many repair scenes stall precisely here. An apology may be received as explanation, but explanation is not yet repair. A future-oriented statement may sound like commitment, but commitment is not yet self-binding. A coherent account of what happened may reduce confusion without restoring reliance.

**PMS Core hooks.**
Δ distinguishes consultation from non-consultation, shared authorship from unilateral submission, explanation from repair, apology from self-binding. ∇ appears in the movement toward repair, withdrawal, clarification, or renewed collaboration. □ is the co-authorship frame, including expectations about major revisions and submission authority. Λ may appear if prior consultation was expected and did not occur. Α remains weak unless unilateral changes or late explanations recur. Ω is possible: perhaps B controlled the submission channel, deadline timing, or public exposure; perhaps A bears reputational risk; perhaps both had different constraints. Θ is central because the event, apology, and future collaboration unfold over time.

Φ is active as recontextualization. B’s explanation changes how the event may be interpreted. Yet Χ must remain active: the analysis must not decide whether the explanation is adequate, manipulative, sincere, or evasive. Σ remains conditional. The parties would need to integrate authorship expectations, deadline procedures, and future practice. Ψ remains analytic-only unless the future commitment is enacted and maintained. “I’ll be more careful” is not yet self-binding in the PMS sense.

**Add-on hooks.**
EDEN would sharpen the question of real Ω and Action-Power. Did B have unilateral submission power? Did A bear public or reputational cost? Was responsibility reattached to the actual structuring effect, or displaced into intention alone? Did the apology make Λ speakable, or did it close the matter prematurely? Did B’s explanation reopen Σ, or did it simulate repair by making the event narratively coherent without changing future conditions?

These are not findings. The example does not establish false coordination. It only shows why EDEN asks useful questions. If B’s apology acknowledges effect, clarifies the process failure, accepts future constraints, and leaves A free to contest or withdraw, EDEN may find no false coordination. If the apology instead converts the event into a misunderstanding while leaving the same unilateral power intact, EDEN may later have more traction. The vignette itself does not decide.

The Minimal Recovery Signature would require more than apology. Ω would need to be named: who had submission power, who bore risk, who controlled timing? Responsibility would need to attach to effect, not merely intention. The non-event—lack of consultation—would need to remain speakable. Χ would need to be restored so neither party is forced into immediate accusation or reconciliation. Σ would need to reopen through a future process. Ψ would require durable commitment.

**MIP / AHP / QC status.**
MIP would treat this as a case where A-awareness may be relatively high if the parties can distinguish event, explanation, effect, responsibility, and future process. But MIP would not score either collaborator. B’s apology is not automatically high maturity. A’s hesitation is not automatically low maturity. The maturity-relevant question would be how roles, knowledge, power, alternatives, predictability, and integration attempts are handled over time.

MIP would also keep D active only as protection. A must not be shamed for not immediately accepting repair. B must not be reduced to a person label because of one unilateral submission. Public dignity risk is present if the essay is public-facing, but no public D-profile follows.

AHP would flag the danger that readers may treat the apology as closure. It would also flag the danger that “unilateral submission” sounds like a verdict. A hardened version of the example must preserve the distinction between effect and intent, explanation and integration, apology and self-binding.

**Forbidden claims.**
The example does not claim that B betrayed A, that A is entitled to reconciliation, that B is manipulative, that A is unforgiving, or that the collaboration must continue. It does not establish legal authorship violation, professional misconduct, diagnosis, moral rank, or repair success. It does not infer inner states from apology language.

**Non-capture note.**
An apology’s meaning cannot be fully captured structurally. Tone, history, vulnerability, prior disappointments, cultural norms of authorship, and the lived experience of being bypassed may matter. PMS can distinguish recontextualization, integration, and self-binding; it cannot exhaust what apology or forgiveness means to the parties.

**Article takeaway.**
This example demonstrates why the PMS Core reading insists that later operators cannot be used cheaply. Φ may happen without Σ. Σ may be discussed without being enacted. Ψ may be promised without becoming self-binding. The example prepares the article’s later conclusion by showing that repair is not a word, a feeling, or an explanation, but a temporally testable structure of practice.

### Example 3 — The justified refusal that must not be redescribed as failed repair

**Type:** worked
**Recommended insertion point:** after “Non-capture and rival readings,” before “Misuse boundary.”

**Bounded vignette.**
A community organizer, C, previously relied on a local committee to provide meeting space for a recurring public discussion series. After a conflict about moderation rules, the committee asks C to return and “repair trust” by resuming the series under revised conditions. C declines. The committee describes the refusal as disappointing and says the community needs closure. C replies that the revised conditions still leave moderation authority unclear, that previous concerns were never acknowledged, and that restarting the series would expose C to the same unresolved pressures. C does not accuse the committee of bad faith. C simply refuses renewed participation.

This vignette is structured to be high-risk. It contains a repair demand, a refusal, possible asymmetry, possible publicness, possible institutional responsibility, and possible non-capture. It is therefore exactly the kind of scene in which a model could become coercive if it equates repair with maturity. The example’s purpose is to show why refusal must remain available as a legitimate structural outcome.

**What the example demonstrates.**
The example demonstrates that non-repair is not automatically failed maturity, drift, avoidance, or under-integration. The committee’s language of repair may be sincere. C’s refusal may be justified. The relation may be repairable under different conditions, or not repairable at all. The important point is that PMS and EDEN must not transform resistance into pathology or immaturity simply because a repair frame has been proposed.

It also demonstrates why rival readings matter. The committee may read the situation as a community repair problem. C may read it as a boundary, governance, or exposure problem. A legal or institutional reading may ask about mandate and procedure. A phenomenological reading may attend to the burden of returning to a space that felt unsafe or illegible. A conflict reading may say the expectations are incompatible. PMS can structure these differences, but it cannot erase them.

**PMS Core hooks.**
Δ distinguishes repair from refusal, renewed participation from withdrawal, closure from non-closure, community need from role exposure. ∇ appears in competing directions: the committee moves toward renewed series and closure; C moves toward withdrawal or protection. □ is contested. The committee frames the scene as community repair. C frames it as unresolved governance and exposure. Λ may appear if prior concerns were expected to be acknowledged and were not. But the non-event must be calibrated: was acknowledgement owed in the relevant frame, or merely desired?

Α remains uncertain. If the committee has repeatedly invited return without addressing conditions, a pattern may emerge. If this is a single conflict, attractor claims remain weak. Ω is possible and important: the committee may control space, moderation rules, access, public framing, and legitimacy; C may bear exposure to participants and reputational risk; the community may also bear loss from the discontinued series. The asymmetry may be multidirectional rather than simple.

Θ is active because trust, refusal, and repair are temporal. The prior conflict, the unresolved concerns, the revised conditions, and the invitation to return form a sequence. Φ appears in competing recontextualizations: the committee reframes return as repair; C reframes non-return as boundary protection. Χ is essential. The analysis must suspend the urge to decide whether C is avoidant or the committee is coercive. Σ is not achieved merely by naming “community closure.” Integration would require coordination of moderation authority, prior concerns, exposure, responsibility, and future practice. Ψ is not established because no actor or institution has yet demonstrated stable self-binding to a corrected structure.

**Add-on hooks.**
EDEN is especially relevant here, but still cannot produce a finding without more data. It asks whether the committee’s repair frame truthfully coordinates real Ω or falsely coordinates it. Does the committee control the meeting space and public narrative while asking C to carry the burden of return? Does the appeal to community closure shift costs onto C? Are C’s prior concerns made speakable, or are they absorbed into a generalized need for repair? Is non-binding by C a legitimate refusal, or is it being framed as failure?

The example shows why EDEN’s non-action and non-binding rules matter. C’s refusal is non-action relative to the committee’s desired repair sequence. But it is not avoidance by default. It may be a structurally mature refusal if the revised conditions do not change the exposure pattern. Non-binding may preserve dignity and reversibility where self-binding would prematurely re-enter an unresolved asymmetry.

The Minimal Recovery Signature would require the committee to name Ω, reattach responsibility to actual structuring effects, make the prior non-event speakable, restore Χ, reopen Σ, and demonstrate Ψ through stable moderation commitments. But the example does not say the committee failed to do this. It says only that C’s refusal cannot be interpreted as failure without that calibration.

**MIP / AHP / QC status.**
MIP would treat this as a case where publicness and D-protection become especially important. A public committee can unintentionally shame a refusing role by describing non-return as blocking community repair. MIP would not score C or the committee. It would ask whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible; whether C has real alternatives; whether the committee has acknowledged its own structuring effects; and whether public language preserves dignity.

The D-module, if activated, would remain protective. It would guard against framing C as immature, selfish, obstructive, or anti-community merely because C refuses. It would also guard against treating the committee as morally degraded merely because it seeks repair. D protects both sides from person-reduction.

AHP would flag strong misuse risk. The example could easily be weaponized: one side might cite PMS to demand integration; the other might cite EDEN to accuse false coordination. AHP would require hardening: state that the vignette is illustrative, that no finding is made, that counter-readings remain open, and that any public use would require independent authority and affected-role contestation.

**Forbidden claims.**
The example does not claim that C is right, that the committee is wrong, that the committee is manipulative, that C is mature, that refusal is always protective, or that repair demands are always coercive. It does not establish legal liability, institutional misconduct, clinical harm, trauma, bad faith, or dignity violation. It does not treat public disappointment as public shaming by default.

**Non-capture note.**
The meaning of refusal may exceed the PMS/EDEN/MIP stack. C’s refusal might involve embodied fatigue, prior exposure, political disagreement, loss of hope, ethical boundary, strategic choice, or simple lack of capacity. The committee’s request might involve sincere concern, institutional pressure, community grief, or inadequate language. A formal reading must leave these possibilities open.

**Article takeaway.**
This example shows why the case must not equate repair with maturity. A mature trust-repair grammar must protect the possibility that non-repair, refusal, withdrawal, or non-binding is the structurally appropriate outcome. PMS makes the competing frames visible; EDEN tests whether repair language coordinates or distorts real asymmetry; MIP protects dignity and transmission boundaries; AHP flags the risk of weaponizing the analysis.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The completed YAML stack preserves rival readings as epistemically open. Rival readings are not defects in PMS, EDEN, MIP, or AHP. They are part of the case’s maturity discipline. A model that cannot allow rival readings to remain genuinely capable of defeating or narrowing its interpretation would become over-assimilative.

An **epistemological reading** would treat trust primarily as credibility, testimony, evidence, confidence in claims, and conditions of justified belief. From this perspective, the central issue may not be relational repair but whether a claim, person, institution, or testimony remains credible under uncertainty. Such a reading preserves standards of evidence and justification that PMS may structurally describe but cannot itself decide. It may ask whether doubt is rational, whether confidence is warranted, whether silence affects epistemic standing, or whether renewed trust requires proof rather than reconciliation.

A **relational-ethical reading** would focus on fidelity, betrayal, apology, forgiveness, care, recognition, and responsibility within relation. It may preserve the lived moral texture of trust: the pain of being let down, the vulnerability of apology, the ambiguity of forgiveness, the risk of renewing reliance, and the legitimacy of refusing repair. PMS can map structural conditions, but it does not exhaust the ethical meaning of forgiveness, fidelity, or care.

A **legal-contractual reading** would treat reliance, duty, breach, liability, remedy, evidence, and procedural standards as primary. This reading preserves the distinction between moral hurt and legal wrong, between apology and remedy, between institutional reassurance and procedural accountability. PMS cannot replace legal standards. EDEN cannot decide liability. MIP cannot assign legal responsibility. If the case were legal, the appropriate framework would need evidentiary rules, jurisdictional standards, burdens of proof, and remedy structures outside the PMS stack.

An **institutional reading** would understand trust repair as legitimacy, transparency, governance, procedural reform, accountability architecture, or public confidence. In such a frame, the relevant actor may not be a person but an institution or role system. Repair may require policy, audit, oversight, explanation, structural change, or public procedure. PMS can describe institutional self-binding analytically, but it must not infer literal subjectivity or moral personhood for institutions.

A **phenomenological or affective reading** would preserve lived vulnerability, hurt, fear, hope, shame, grief, relief, or renewed confidence. PMS explicitly does not analyze qualia, inner experience, or consciousness. Its awareness is structural, not phenomenological. Therefore, the lived texture of trust or betrayal may remain only partially captured by PMS operators. EDEN’s real Ω mapping may sharpen exposure, but it cannot exhaust what it feels like to remain exposed. MIP’s D-protection may prevent humiliation, but it does not describe the inner experience of dignity violation or restoration.

A **language-practice reading** would focus on how words such as “trust,” “repair,” “betrayal,” “accountability,” “closure,” “refusal,” and “forgiveness” function in a concrete practice. It might ask who is allowed to name repair, whose vocabulary is recognized, whether “trust” is used descriptively or coercively, whether “closure” is demanded as compliance, and whether “forgiveness” functions as ethical release or social pressure. PMS and EDEN both attend to frame and language access, but a language-practice reading may preserve local pragmatic nuance that the operator grammar abstracts.

A **strategic or game-theoretic reading** would treat trust as repeated interaction, incentive structure, reputation, cooperation, defection, risk, signaling, and cost. It may explain repair not as moral reconciliation but as recalibration of expected behavior under uncertainty. This reading preserves incentive logic that PMS may describe through Α, Ω, Θ, and Σ but does not compute or validate.

A **conflict reading** would treat rupture as evidence of incompatible expectations, non-fit, disagreement, or contested frame, not necessarily betrayal. This is crucial for the present case. Non-repair may not be immaturity. It may be the correct outcome of incompatible goods. Reconciliation may be inappropriate. Refusal may be structurally mature. PMS must not absorb conflict into failed integration.

A **refusal reading** is especially important. It treats non-repair or withdrawal as potentially justified rather than immature, avoidant, or under-integrated. A person, role, or institution may refuse repair because the conditions are unsafe, the demand is illegitimate, the asymmetry is uncorrected, the frame is wrong, or the relation should not be restored. EDEN reinforces this by refusing to treat non-action or non-binding as failure by default.

An **anti-capture reading** preserves the possibility that the trust-repair field resists formalization. Some ruptures may not be repairable. Some losses may not become coherent. Some refusals may not need explanation. Some vulnerability may not be representable without distortion. Some dignity harms may be worsened by analysis. PMS explicitly allows this. Non-capture is not residue to be absorbed later; it is an epistemic and praxeological boundary.

The three examples above are designed to keep these rival readings alive. The unanswered acknowledgement can be epistemic, relational, practical, technical, or affective. The apology that explains but does not yet bind can be ethical, procedural, collaborative, or strategic. The justified refusal can be governance-related, phenomenological, institutional, conflict-theoretic, or anti-capture. None of the examples functions as evidence for the completed conceptual case. They demonstrate how the stack behaves when a small bounded scene is introduced.

This is also why rival readings must not be treated as mere “alternative interpretations” to be defeated by better PMS calibration. They may preserve goods that PMS or EDEN cannot fully capture: lived vulnerability, legal proof, institutional legitimacy, procedural authority, cultural meanings of repair, language-practice nuance, strategic rationality, or the dignity of refusal. A mature PMS application does not require the defeat of rivals. It requires knowing where its own grammar has traction, where it must remain tentative, and where it must release the scene to another framework.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The completed YAML gives detailed weakening and falsifier logic. The PMS reading would be weakened if no framed expectation, meaningful non-event, temporal trajectory, or asymmetry of reliance and vulnerability can be specified once a concrete case is supplied. If a future scene shows no relevant distinction between trust and non-trust, no expectation within a frame, no meaningful absence, no temporal extension, and no asymmetry of exposure or obligation, then PMS would have little traction beyond abstract vocabulary.

The PMS reading would become under-discriminating if it cannot distinguish betrayal from disappointment, justified refusal from avoidance, disagreement from rupture, misunderstanding from breach, irreparable non-fit from failed integration, legal breach from relational hurt, clinical repair from structural repair, institutional legitimacy from interpersonal trust, and coherent repair-language from enacted repair. Under-discrimination is a serious risk because trust-repair language is broad and emotionally charged. A model that makes everything legible in the same way loses analytic force.

The PMS reading would become over-assimilative if it redescribes all resistance, rupture, non-fit, absence, refusal, or rival framing as drift, failed integration, incomplete calibration, or maturity failure. This is one of the strongest falsifiers. If PMS cannot let refusal remain refusal, cannot let non-repair be justified, cannot let rival frameworks defeat the PMS reading, or cannot preserve non-capture, then PMS becomes a closure machine rather than a structural grammar.

Specific operator-level weakening conditions also remain active. Δ weakens if stable trust-relevant distinctions cannot be preserved. ∇ weakens if no directional orientation toward reliance, withdrawal, repair, doubt, or protection arises. □ weakens if no trust-relevant frame can be specified. Λ weakens if no structured expectation exists within a frame. Α remains weak unless recurrence or stabilization is shown. Ω must be removed or marked weak if no directional imbalance of exposure, power, capacity, obligation, or role differentiation is identifiable. Θ is overextended if no temporal extension, delay, persistence, or trajectory matters. Φ is over-assimilative if every rupture or absence is absorbed as recontextualization. Χ fails if distance is suspended to force blame, absolution, reconciliation, diagnosis, or legal conclusion. Σ is overclaimed if coherence is treated as proof of repair. Ψ is overclaimed if self-binding is inferred without integrated, temporally maintained, reflectively constrained commitment.

The EDEN overlay would be weakened if no real Ω is present, if responsibility remains properly attached to actual structuring effect, if critique and correction remain possible, if Σ/Ψ are robustly open, if protection is bounded and responsibility-compatible, if vulnerability remains criticizable without degradation, if no cost distribution is shifted, if no erosion of shared correctability occurs, or if a rival explanation is stronger. It would also be weakened if the alleged pattern is not repeated, consequential, or scene-structuring.

The strongest EDEN falsifier is the absence of a bounded scene packet. Without role positions, temporal limits, principal effects, evidence, costs, and application standing, EDEN cannot affirm false coordination, Reciprocity Loss, AEP, PFO, immunization, overexposure, or Minimal Recovery fulfillment. A plausible counter-scene must be allowed to matter. The overlay must release or narrow its reading if a concrete scene does not satisfy its conditions.

MIP would be weakened if the analysis posture stopped distinguishing artifact from case, source material from evidence, score language from person assessment, D-protection from dignity scoring, and working-note status from transmission clearance. The prior MIP analysis is strong because it maintains those distinctions. If a future version quoted the A-band without its boundary, treated the IA-box as application clearance, or used D-language toward identifiable persons, MIP conformity would weaken. If it scored role_A or role_B without concrete role evidence, it would fail its own score hygiene.

AHP would be weakened if its attack points were read as case findings or if its hardening backlog were treated as an upgrade of claim authority. AHP is second-order. It hardens the analysis artifact; it does not validate the underlying case. Its own falsifier would be misuse: if AHP precision language made the article sound more empirically grounded, more transmissible, or more decisive, it would fail its purpose.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled across the stack. For PMS, stronger application would require observable indicators for each operator: explicit distinctions, directional movement, active frame, expected occurrence, recurrence evidence, asymmetry mapping, temporal sequence, source and target frames, distance procedures, enacted integration, and self-binding commitment. For EDEN, operationalization would require real Ω mapping, responsibility/action-power distribution, cost distribution, Σ/Ψ status, non-action/non-binding traceability, counter-scene exposure, affected-role contestation, and independent application authority. For MIP, concrete use would require role-, context-, and time-window-bound evidence for A, C, R, P, D, IA, trajectory, bias, and transmission. For AHP, future hardening would require clearer source-status language, score-hygiene boundaries, D-profile boundaries, IA transmission phrasing, full-depth scope qualification, constructed-counter-perspective labeling, and a plain-language glossary if shared beyond model-literate readers.

Stronger claims would require actual scene data: who relied on whom, what was expected, what was absent, what frame governed the expectation, what asymmetry existed, who had what alternatives, what actions or non-actions occurred, what costs were borne, what repair attempts were made, what refusals were justified, what commitments endured, and which rival readings better discriminate the case. Without this, the completed analysis remains a full-depth conceptual rendering, not a concrete application.

The examples clarify these operationalization requirements without satisfying them for the original case. The unanswered acknowledgement shows what a concrete Λ might look like but does not establish betrayal. The apology example shows the difference between Φ, Σ, and Ψ but does not establish repair. The justified refusal shows why non-repair may remain legitimate but does not establish that any particular refusal is justified. The examples therefore prepare application logic while preserving non-application status.

### Misuse boundary

The misuse boundary is not ancillary. It is part of the case’s formal validity. Because trust repair invites closure, the analysis must actively prevent misuse.

There is **no diagnosis**. The case does not diagnose individuals, relationships, institutions, or roles. It does not infer personality, pathology, attachment pattern, trauma state, risk profile, competence, sincerity, bad faith, immaturity, avoidance, manipulation, or clinical condition. Doubt, withdrawal, silence, refusal, and non-repair remain structurally ambiguous.

There is **no person ranking**. PMS maturity, EDEN reciprocity, MIP A/M language, and D-protection do not rank human beings. No person is more or less dignified. No person is assigned maturity as essence. No person is labeled mature, immature, adult, inadult, reciprocal, non-reciprocal, avoidant, overexposed, immunized, or failed. Any future scoring would have to be role-, context-, and time-window-bound, and even then not a person score.

There is **no legal or forensic conclusion**. The analysis cannot establish breach, liability, culpability, remedy, evidence sufficiency, institutional fault, procedural legitimacy, or forensic truth. Legal and forensic contexts require their own standards and authorities. PMS/EDEN/MIP may help structure questions, but they cannot replace adjudication.

There is **no metaphysical proof**. PMS does not prove the essence of trust, forgiveness, dignity, consciousness, selfhood, or repair. Dignity-in-Practice is a constraint on handling asymmetry, not a metaphysical statement about worth. SELF_FIXPOINT is a derived structural representation, not proof of subjectivity.

There is **no empirical proof**. The case is based on completed YAML analyses, not independent scene evidence. The AHP overlay explicitly flags the risk that formal coherence could be mistaken for empirical grounding. That must not happen. The YAML stack is a structured artifact, not a data set.

There is **no implementation validation**. YAML form does not implement praxis or agency. It does not make the analysis deployable in AI systems, organizational processes, clinical tools, legal workflows, or policy contexts. Any implementation would require separate calibration, testing, accountability, and domain validation.

There is **no PMS Base validation**. Applying PMS successfully to a conceptually fitting field does not prove PMS complete, optimal, uniquely ordered, empirically superior, or exhaustive. The case remains one application with limits.

There is **no add-on-as-operator**. PMS-EDEN constructs are overlay handles, not PMS operators. Adult Reciprocity, False-Coordinated Real Asymmetry, Action-Power, AEP, PFO, Correctability, Reciprocity Loss, and Minimal Recovery Signature do not enter the PMS operator inventory. MIP A/M/IA/D and AHP PH/AP/HB do not revise PMS Base or EDEN.

There is **no drift-as-verdict**. AD_A>>E and AD_Sigma_low are possible risks, not findings. EDEN drift profiles are not established. MIP IA-risk is not a person verdict. AHP attack points describe analysis vulnerabilities, not case participants.

There is **no closure-by-authority**. The layered stack may be formal, detailed, and internally coherent, but it has no authority to close the case. It cannot demand repair, compel apology, authorize confrontation, prescribe reconciliation, enforce silence, justify public exposure, or bind any role. The description-to-action firewall remains active.

The misuse boundary also applies to transmission. The completed MIP analysis remains a working note. The AHP overlay does not upgrade it. If the artifact is transmitted without its caveats, quoted selectively, attached to identifiable persons, or used in a live conflict, the risk profile changes. The safe status depends on maintaining internal conceptual use, explicit source boundaries, non-person scoring, D-protection, and review conditions.

The examples require the same misuse discipline. The unanswered acknowledgement must not become evidence that silence is betrayal. The apology example must not become evidence that apology is insufficient by default. The refusal example must not become evidence that refusal is always mature or that repair demands are always coercive. Examples demonstrate calibration; they do not decide cases.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result of *Repair Relationships* is a layered structural analysis of a closure-sensitive trust-repair domain that deliberately refuses to become a verdict. PMS Core shows why the domain is structurally rich: it activates distinctions, directional tensions, frames, possible non-events, possible patterns, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, conditional integration, and analytic self-binding. But it also shows why that richness must remain calibrated. Early operators are strongly relevant; later operators remain conditional, weak, under-specified, or analytic-only. Coherence is not proof. Non-capture is not failure. Drift risk is not finding.

PMS-EDEN adds a sharper asymmetry and reciprocity lens without replacing the Core grammar. It asks whether a future concrete trust-repair scene truthfully coordinates real Ω or falsely coordinates it through displaced responsibility, shifted costs, blocked or externalized Σ/Ψ, and weakened correctability. Yet the EDEN overlay remains suspended for application because the case supplies no scene packet. Adult Reciprocity is indeterminate. False-Coordinated Real Asymmetry is not established. Minimal Recovery is a threshold, not a fulfilled signature. Non-action and non-binding are not failure by default. The description-to-action firewall remains active.

MIP then evaluates the maturity-in-practice of the analysis posture rather than the maturity of persons. It finds that the layered artifact is relatively strong in awareness and coherence because it knows its limits, preserves role placeholders, separates source material from inference, and maintains transmission boundaries. But MIP assigns no concrete M-score and no person score. The IA-box identifies methodological asymmetry: high formal structuring power under low concrete evidence. D is active only as protection, not as dignity assessment. The report remains a working note.

AHP finally hardens the MIP artifact. It marks high precision for the internal working-note artifact while naming predictable attack surfaces: formal coherence could be mistaken for empirical grounding; A-band language could be extracted as scoring; D-module fields could be misread as dignity profile; IA language could be misread as clearance; “full-depth” could be confused with exhaustive capture; constructed counter-perspectives could be mistaken for affected-role voice; and dense model language may need plain-language translation. AHP therefore strengthens the artifact’s internal safety and readability, but it does not rescore, validate, authorize, or make the analysis externally transmissible.

The three examples contribute calibration without becoming evidence. **The unanswered acknowledgement** shows how a non-event can become structurally meaningful only within a frame of expectation, while still remaining undecided in meaning. **The apology that explains but does not yet bind** shows why Φ, Σ, and Ψ must remain distinct: explanation is not integration, and future-oriented language is not self-binding. **The justified refusal** shows why non-repair, withdrawal, and non-binding must remain available as legitimate outcomes rather than being automatically redescribed as immaturity or drift. Together, the examples demonstrate how the analysis would behave if bounded scene elements were added, but they do not retroactively concretize the original case.

What remains unclaimed is as important as what is claimed. The article does not claim that betrayal occurred. It does not claim that repair succeeded or failed. It does not assign responsibility. It does not diagnose. It does not rank persons. It does not make legal, forensic, clinical, metaphysical, empirical, implementation, or PMS Base validation claims. It does not treat EDEN constructs as PMS operators. It does not turn drift risks into findings. It does not score dignity. It does not authorize action. It does not transform resistance, refusal, non-fit, disagreement, rupture, or absence into drift by default.

The boundary discipline of the case is therefore its main methodological achievement. The analysis demonstrates that a formal system can be most useful not when it resolves a charged field, but when it prevents premature resolution. PMS provides a grammar of structural intelligibility. EDEN adds asymmetry and responsibility precision. MIP adds maturity and transmission discipline. AHP adds artifact hardening. Each layer increases visibility while preserving restraint. None is allowed to become a closure machine.

The final methodological takeaway is that trust repair requires a grammar capable of holding absence, vulnerability, time, explanation, refusal, and possible self-binding without forcing them into verdict. A mature analysis does not treat every silence as betrayal, every apology as repair, every refusal as immaturity, every coherence as truth, every asymmetry as guilt, or every non-capture as failure. It asks what would have to be shown, what remains open, what rival readings preserve, and what must not be done with the analysis. In *Repair Relationships*, the most disciplined conclusion is not closure. It is the preservation of the conditions under which any future closure would have to earn its authority.
