### Why this case matters

The case matters because privacy and publicness generate one of the most persistent closure-demands in moral, political, institutional, interpersonal, and technological life. At first glance, the opposition looks familiar and almost self-explanatory: privacy protects what should not be exposed; publicness opens what should be accountable. But the case becomes difficult precisely because neither term has a stable moral meaning in isolation.

Privacy may protect a vulnerable person from exposure. It may preserve trust, intimacy, lawful confidentiality, discretion, bodily or informational safety, or legitimate opacity. It may create a boundary without which relation, repair, or deliberation becomes impossible. Yet privacy may also conceal power, block accountability, prevent affected parties from knowing what matters to them, or stabilize a structure in which one party controls what others are allowed to see.

Publicness may create legitimate accountability and shared intelligibility. It may allow hidden consequences to become contestable. It may open a frame in which those affected can speak, respond, and demand reasons. Yet publicness may also become spectacle, humiliation, surveillance, public pillory, reputational lock-in, coercive exposure, or irreversible degradation. It may produce the appearance of correction while bypassing actual repair.

This means that the philosophical closure-demand is not merely: “Which value wins, privacy or publicness?” The deeper demand is: “Can the relation between concealment, exposure, access, and accountability be made structurally legible without deciding too soon, without moralizing the parties, and without converting publicness into authority or privacy into evasion by default?”

That is why the case belongs in AXIOM. AXIOM is not trying to solve privacy or publicness by declaring one primary. It maps closure-demands. This case is exemplary because the closure-demand remains unstable across frames. The same non-disclosure can be protection or evasion. The same disclosure can be accountability or harm. The same access request can be legitimate review or violation. The same refusal can be dignity-preserving boundary or accountability failure.

The case is structurally difficult because it carries several overlapping tensions at once.

First, there is a **frame tension**. Privacy and publicness mean different things in interpersonal, institutional, legal, technological, familial, professional, political, and public-discursive settings. A confidential internal review, a public-interest disclosure, a private relationship, an academic archive, and a platform-mediated accusation cannot be handled under one undifferentiated meaning of “privacy” or “publicness.”

Second, there is an **asymmetry tension**. The party who controls access may not be the party who bears exposure. The party demanding accountability may also create vulnerability. The party protected by privacy may also shield a structure from scrutiny. The party made visible may not control the frame in which visibility is interpreted.

Third, there is a **temporal tension**. Concealment and exposure are not neutral events. They accumulate. Delay may protect a process, but it may also obstruct accountability. Public exposure can become archived, reputationally sticky, and practically irreversible. The same act may be reversible while private and irreversible once public.

Fourth, there is a **non-event tension**. Silence, non-disclosure, missing explanation, withheld access, or delayed accountability may be meaningful absences, but they are not automatically evidence of wrongdoing. A missing public response may be evasion, but it may also be protected process, legal caution, safety, confidentiality, or non-capture.

Fifth, there is a **closure tension**. Readers, institutions, publics, and analysts often want the case to decide whether privacy or publicness is more legitimate. But the abstract material does not warrant such a decision. The case must be held at the level of structural pre-application unless a concrete scene supplies actors, frames, information objects, expectations, temporal sequence, and external standards.

For that reason, the case is not being solved metaphysically. Nothing in the material tells us what privacy “really is” as an essence, or what publicness is in ontology. It is not being solved empirically because no actual event, data, institution, platform, audience, person, or harm sequence is supplied. It is not being solved clinically or diagnostically because no person, pathology, maturity deficit, motive, shame structure, or psychological trait is at issue. It is not being solved legally or forensically because there is no jurisdiction, right, evidence standard, duty, liability structure, or public-interest test. It is not being solved through implementation because no access-control system, disclosure protocol, moderation rule, audit process, or governance mechanism is specified.

The case matters precisely because it shows how much must be held open before stronger claims are legitimate. PMS Core can map the operator grammar of the tension. PMS-CRITIQUE can show why publicness, critique, drift, and correction thresholds matter. MIP can show what would be required before maturity, responsibility, asymmetry, or dignity-related governance claims could be made. AHP can show where the analysis artefact remains attackable despite high structural precision.

Together, the layers show not closure by authority, but disciplined non-closure with explicit conditions for possible future strengthening.

### Scene and frame structure

The case does not contain a concrete scene. Its “scene” is a conceptual scene: a structural field in which privacy and publicness become contested through concealment, exposure, access, limitation, and accountability. This has several consequences.

First, the **scene boundary** is abstract. Inside the boundary are the concepts and tensions explicitly supplied by the case material: privacy, publicness, concealment, exposure, access, accountability, limitation, contestation, and moral difficulty. Also inside the boundary are the structural relations made visible by the completed YAML analyses: frame-dependence, possible asymmetry, possible non-events, rival reframings, reflective distance, unresolved integration, and guardrail-only self-binding.

Outside the boundary are all concrete determinations not supplied: specific actors, institutions, legal regimes, technologies, platforms, publics, audiences, access systems, data types, information objects, consent rules, harms, motives, sanctions, outcomes, and timelines. The case cannot say whether an exposure was justified, whether a non-disclosure was evasive, whether a public has a right to know, whether privacy has been violated, or whether accountability has failed. Those questions require a concrete frame.

The PMS operator **□ Frame** is therefore central. In this case, □ does not simply “provide context.” It marks the fact that privacy and publicness are not self-interpreting. What counts as private in a friendship may not count as private in public office. What counts as legitimate public accountability in an institution may count as harmful exposure in an intimate relation. What counts as lawful confidentiality may become illegitimate concealment in a different setting. What counts as transparency in governance may become surveillance in another frame.

The frame contains not only the topic but also the admissibility rules. A legal frame would ask about rights, duties, procedure, evidence, jurisdiction, and remedies. An institutional frame would ask about role obligations, reporting duties, review mechanisms, and accountability channels. An interpersonal frame would ask about trust, consent, intimacy, boundary, repair, and vulnerability. A technological frame would ask about access permissions, audit logs, data retention, visibility, platform persistence, and reversibility. A public-discursive frame would ask about audience, circulation, reputational residue, public reason, and spectacle. The abstract case contains none of these as determinate; it only shows why they would matter.

The protected constraints are correspondingly strict. The analysis must preserve reversibility, distance, non-ranking, and non-diagnostic language. It must not force privacy into evasion or publicness into accountability. It must not treat silence as guilt, refusal as immaturity, exposure as truth, or public attention as correction. It must preserve the possibility that privacy is legitimate opacity and the possibility that publicness is legitimate accountability. It must also preserve the possibility that either term can be misused.

The MIP role mapping helps articulate possible roles without turning them into actors. It names an abstract privacy claimant or privacy-protecting role, an abstract publicness or accountability claimant, an abstract exposed or potentially exposed party, an abstract access-controlling role, an abstract public or institutional audience, and the analyst as a structural interpreter. These are placeholders. They are not persons, parties, or empirical subjects. Their purpose is to make visible what would need to be specified in a future scene.

Temporality **Θ** is relevant but weak. Concealment, exposure, access, and accountability often unfold over time. Delay may protect, obstruct, accumulate, or harden. Publicness may create temporal residue through archive, reputation, repetition, or institutional memory. But no actual sequence is supplied. There is no before/after, no disclosure moment, no delayed reply, no repeated refusal, no accumulated harm, no review window, no expiry, no appeal, and no follow-up trajectory. Therefore Θ cannot be treated as an established case finding. It is a calibration requirement.

The **reversibility window** is likewise conceptual. The analysis can say that reversibility must be protected, especially where publicness may become irreversible exposure. It can say that any stronger application would need to identify exit options, contest channels, review points, correction routes, withdrawal possibilities, or contextualization mechanisms. But it cannot say whether reversibility was actually available or lost.

Exposure and asymmetry conditions are highly salient. Publicness may expose one party while empowering another. Privacy may protect one party while withholding access from another. Access control may create dependency. Accountability demand may shift vulnerability. Public attention may increase status loss or pressure. But because no scene exists, **Ω Asymmetry** remains dependency-sensitive: structurally important, but not assignable as a proven gradient among actual roles.

The conceptual scene therefore functions as a disciplined pre-scene. It tells us what must be tracked before judgment: frame, roles, information object, expected event, temporal sequence, exposure gradient, access control, protected goods, reversibility, rival readings, and non-capture zones. Its value lies in preventing the case from becoming too quickly “about” one side’s moral victory.

The examples later in the article must be read under the same constraint. They are illustrative test-scenes for calibration, not evidence for the abstract case and not substitutions for concrete case material. Their function is to show how frame, non-event, publicness, and reversibility would have to be handled if a concrete scene were supplied. They do not establish that such scenes have occurred, that any party is responsible, or that any legal, empirical, diagnostic, or implementation conclusion follows.

### PMS Core structural reading

The PMS Core reading begins with **Δ Difference**. The case is activated by distinctions: privacy/publicness, concealment/exposure, access/limitation, accountability/non-accountability, visibility/opacity, disclosure/non-disclosure. Δ is active because without these distinctions there is no case. However, the calibration problem is immediate. Privacy and publicness must not be reduced to a simple binary. There may be layered access, partial disclosure, confidentiality, protected reporting, anonymized accountability, private review, public explanation, staged exposure, or limited transparency. Pushing Δ too far would create false opposition: privacy as secrecy, publicness as truth. The completed YAML treats Δ as a structural entry point, not a verdict.

From Δ follows **∇ Impulse**, the directional pressure generated by difference. Once privacy and publicness are distinguished, pressures appear: to conceal, expose, access, withhold, justify, limit, disclose, respond, or demand accountability. ∇ is active, but only abstractly. It does not license motive inference. The analysis cannot say that someone wanted to hide, punish, protect, reveal, dominate, shame, or repair. ∇ marks directional tension, not psychology. Overclaim would occur if the case treated a privacy claim as evasive intent, or a publicness claim as righteous accountability motive, without evidence.

**□ Frame** is the first major stabilizing operator. The YAML marks it as active and central. Privacy and publicness become meaningful only within frames. A disclosure may be required in one frame and harmful in another. A silence may be dignified restraint in one setting and accountability failure in another. A public statement may be necessary explanation in one context and performative humiliation in another. The calibration problem is that the frame is not supplied. Therefore □ is active as a requirement and under-specified as a concrete finding. Overclaim would occur if the analysis silently selected a legal, political, interpersonal, or technological frame and treated it as given.

**Λ Non-Event** is active but fragile. The case material directly invokes concealment, limited access, contested accountability, and exposure. Each can involve meaningful absence: a missing disclosure, absent reply, withheld explanation, non-access, delayed accountability, refusal, silence, or missing public reason. But Λ requires an expected event. A non-event is meaningful only if the frame establishes that something was due: disclosure, access, explanation, refusal, protection, review, response, or accountability. In this case no expected event is specified. Calibration therefore requires naming the expected event before treating absence as structurally significant. Overclaim would occur if concealment or silence were automatically treated as evasion, failure, guilt, or immaturity.

**Α Attractor** remains weak. Attractor formation would require recurrence or stabilization: a repeated secrecy script, routine public shaming, institutional non-response, habitual exposure, stable surveillance pattern, default confidentiality regime, or repeated accountability deferral. The case material does not show recurrence. Therefore Α is only a possible patterning operator. It becomes relevant if later material shows that privacy/publicness handling has stabilized into a script. Overclaim would occur if the analysis treated a single abstract tension as an established drift pattern.

**Ω Asymmetry** is materially salient but dependency-sensitive. The YAML rightly gives it major analytic weight while marking the dependency problem. Privacy/publicness conflicts often distribute exposure, access, vulnerability, authority, and obligation unevenly. One role may be made visible; another may control the frame. One role may demand accountability; another may bear reputational cost. One role may hold information; another may be affected by its concealment. But PMS dependency places Ω after Α: asymmetry is fully established in the chain through stabilized patterns. Because Α is weak here, Ω cannot be treated as a proven role-gradient. It remains a central calibration need. Overclaim would occur if the analysis assigned power, blame, vulnerability, or responsibility to abstract roles without scene evidence.

**Θ Temporality** is weak but important. Privacy and publicness are rarely instantaneous. They involve timing: delayed disclosure, accumulated secrecy, archived publicity, repeated access denial, review windows, reputational residue, and the closing of reversibility. Yet the case gives no sequence. It does not tell whether exposure happened before harm, whether silence persisted, whether access was requested, whether accountability was delayed, or whether publicness became irreversible. Therefore Θ cannot ground a trajectory claim. It marks what a stronger analysis would need: sequence, duration, recurrence, accumulation, and horizon. Overclaim would occur if the case were read as showing long-term lock-in or delayed responsibility without evidence.

**Φ Recontextualization** is strongly active. The same structure can be reframed in multiple ways. Privacy may be protection, intimacy, lawful confidentiality, safety, refusal, privilege, secrecy, or evasion. Publicness may be accountability, public reason, truth-telling, transparency, surveillance, spectacle, coercion, humiliation, or structural harm. Concealment may be care or domination. Exposure may be repair or degradation. Access may be right or violation. Φ is therefore central to the case’s philosophical difficulty. But Φ also creates over-assimilation risk. PMS must not redescribe every rival reading as mere recontextualization within its own grammar. Some rival readings may genuinely exceed PMS or show that another formalization discriminates better.

**Χ Distance** is a major operator and an application gate. In this case, Χ means reflective withdrawal from premature verdict. It inhibits forced exposure, forced secrecy, forced accountability, forced neutrality, forced integration, and person-level attribution. It makes the analysis reversible. It prevents the reader from turning privacy into innocence, publicness into guilt, concealment into wrongdoing, or exposure into correction. But Χ must not become avoidance. It cannot be used to suspend urgent protection or evade accountability where a concrete scene would warrant action. In this abstract case, Χ is satisfied as analytic discipline, not as an observed actor capacity.

**Σ Integration** is weak and unresolved. The case asks how privacy/publicness tensions should be understood, but it does not supply an integrated resolution. A valid Σ would have to coordinate privacy protection, accountability, access, exposure risk, consent, public reason, and reversibility in a discriminating way. The YAML refuses to claim this. PMS coherence itself is not enough. A smooth operator chain does not prove moral adequacy, legal sufficiency, empirical truth, or practical resolution. Overclaim would occur if the analysis treated its own coherence as a solution.

**Ψ Self-Binding** is weak and guardrail-only. Ψ would require an identified agent, institution, role, commitment, promise, policy, audit loop, or long-term self-relation. The abstract case supplies none. Therefore Ψ cannot be used to assign responsibility-as-self-commitment or stable identity binding. It applies only as analytic self-binding: the analysis binds itself to PMS discipline, claim boundaries, reversibility, and non-person-ranking. Overclaim would occur if the case inferred a SELF_FIXPOINT, institutional commitment, or obligation trajectory without material support.

The resulting PMS Core reading is therefore not a full chain activation in the strong sense. It is a calibrated structural projection. Δ, ∇, □, Λ, Ω, Φ, and Χ carry the main case visibility. Α, Θ, Σ, and Ψ remain weak, conditional, unresolved, or analytic-only. This is not a failure. It is the correct result of respecting the case material.

### PMS dependency and calibration discipline

The most important discipline in the PMS Core reading is that later operators cannot be used cheaply. The case strongly invites later-operator language: responsibility, integration, public accountability, self-binding, maturity, drift, and dignity. But the YAML repeatedly blocks premature escalation.

The first dependency risk is **Ω without Α**. The case is obviously asymmetry-relevant: exposure, access, vulnerability, obligation, and accountability are uneven by nature. Yet PMS dependency requires attention to stabilized patterns before strong Ω conclusions. Without recurrence or patterning, Ω remains salient but not fully evidenced. This matters because asymmetry language can quickly become moral accusation. To say “there may be an exposure gradient” is not to say “one party is abusing power.”

The second risk is **Φ without Θ**. Recontextualization is central, but without sequence it is unclear whether reframing is clarification, evasion, escalation, repair, reinterpretation, or drift. Privacy-as-protection and privacy-as-evasion may be rival frames, but the case does not show how one becomes the other over time. Publicness-as-accountability and publicness-as-harm may both be possible, but the case does not show a trajectory of exposure. Therefore Φ must preserve rivalry rather than absorb it.

The third risk is **Χ as over-restraint**. Distance is required to prevent premature judgment, but distance cannot become paralysis or false neutrality. In a concrete case, delaying disclosure might intensify harm; public accountability might be urgently required; privacy might be necessary to protect someone from irreversible exposure. Because this case is abstract, Χ functions as analytic restraint. A future concrete case would have to test whether restraint protects dignity or merely defers responsibility.

The fourth risk is **Λ over-reading**. PMS makes non-events visible, but that visibility is dangerous if absence is treated as evidence. Silence may be refusal, protection, fear, confidentiality, legal caution, non-capture, or evasion. Non-disclosure may be legitimate privacy or concealment. Missing accountability may be procedural delay or structural avoidance. Λ is meaningful only in a frame of expectation. The analysis must never treat absence itself as proof.

The fifth risk is **Σ premature-resolution**. A common temptation is to synthesize: privacy and publicness can be integrated through balanced disclosure, accountable confidentiality, procedural review, or staged access. Such designs may be useful in concrete cases, but none is warranted here. Σ remains unresolved because the case supplies no conditions for choosing among possible integrations. PMS coherence is therefore explicitly insufficient.

The sixth risk is **Ψ over-binding**. Privacy or publicness can become identity commitments, institutional doctrines, public obligations, or moral absolutes. But Ψ requires temporal commitment and integration. In the abstract case, no such commitment is supplied. The YAML therefore uses Ψ only as a guardrail: the analysis binds itself not to overclaim. It does not bind any party.

The seventh risk is **Δ binary collapse**. Privacy/publicness looks like a binary, but real cases may be layered: private-to-some, public-to-others, confidential-but-reviewable, disclosed-with-redaction, accessible-under-procedure, public-in-result-but-private-in-process. Treating Δ as a simple opposition would under-discriminate the field.

These dependency risks explain why PMS must not become a closure machine. PMS is powerful enough to map the case smoothly. That is exactly the danger. A polished PMS chain could create the impression that the tension is resolved, when the actual output is more modest: the conditions for responsible analysis are visible, and many stronger claims are blocked.

Non-capture is not failure here. If privacy as intimacy resists public accountability grammar, that may be a genuine boundary. If public reason resists being reduced to exposure, that may be a genuine rival structure. If legal procedure discriminates better than PMS in a concrete case, PMS should yield. If silence or refusal is not legible as drift, that is not a defect. PMS remains valid only as a bounded structural grammar, not as a totalizing absorber of all meanings.

Invalid dependency shortcuts would include: treating public exposure as accountability without Φ/Σ; treating privacy as concealment without □/Λ calibration; treating repeated public discourse as established Α without recurrence evidence; assigning Ω blame without a scene; treating coherent PMS interpretation as Σ resolution; using Ψ to impose commitment on an unspecified other; treating Dignity-in-Practice as ontological dignity; or reading drift patterns as verdicts.

The dependency discipline is therefore the heart of the Core pass. It shows that the case is highly legible but not resolved.

### PMS drift-pattern status

The case is tempting because privacy/publicness conflicts often look like drift: silence may look like evasion, exposure may look like escalation, publicness may look like pillory, and repeated framing may look like integration failure. But the case material does not provide recurrence, concrete enactment, temporal sequence, or actor-level data. Therefore drift cannot be asserted.

#### AD_A>>E — Excessive Distance between Awareness and Enactment

The drift pattern **AD_A>>E** is only a weak risk. It would become relevant if awareness, framing, or evaluative visibility around privacy/publicness expanded while enactment remained suppressed, chronically delayed, or structurally blocked. In a concrete case, this might look like endless analysis of whether something should be public, endless discussion of accountability, repeated recognition that disclosure is needed, or repeated acknowledgement of exposure risk without any integrated action path. The YAML therefore marks the awareness axis as under-specified and the action axis as under-specified. That is important. Under-specification does not mean failure. It means that the available material does not support the claim that awareness has over-expanded or that action has been suppressed. The drift remains useful only as a warning. A privacy/publicness debate can become highly reflective while avoiding action. It can also become action-heavy while bypassing reflection. AD_A>>E would name the first risk only if the case later showed a stabilized gap between interpretive awareness and enacted responsibility. In the present case, to classify the case itself as AD_A>>E would be an overclaim.

#### AD_Sigma_low — Fragmented integration

The second PMS drift pattern, **AD_Sigma_low**, is also only a weak risk. It would become relevant if recontextualization outpaced integration: privacy is repeatedly reframed as protection, then evasion, then intimacy, then privilege; publicness is repeatedly reframed as accountability, then exposure, then surveillance, then public reason; but no coherent integration emerges. In a concrete case, this might produce volatile coherence and inconsistent responsibility.

Again, the current material does not establish that pattern. The YAML marks the coherence axis as under-specified and the responsibility axis as under-specified. That is not a low score. It is not a maturity judgment. It is not a diagnosis of fragmentation. It simply means that the case does not supply the temporal and enacted structure required to say that integration is failing.

The risk remains analytically relevant because the case strongly invites frame shifts. Φ is central, while Σ is unresolved. A reader could mistake repeated recontextualization for mature pluralism, or mistake unresolved integration for failure. The YAML blocks both shortcuts. Recontextualization may be legitimate. Non-integration may be appropriate. Some privacy/publicness conflicts may remain tragic, plural, or structurally unresolved. Therefore AD_Sigma_low is a risk condition, not a verdict.

#### Coherence and under-discrimination risks

The broader PMS coherence risk is high. Privacy/publicness maps very smoothly onto PMS operators: Δ distinguishes private/public; ∇ supplies pressure; □ supplies frame; Λ supplies non-event; Ω supplies asymmetry; Θ supplies delay and residue; Φ supplies rival reframing; Χ supplies restraint; Σ supplies possible integration; Ψ supplies possible self-binding. This smoothness is analytically powerful, but it can also under-discriminate.

A coherent PMS rendering could wrongly make the case appear more resolved than it is. It could merge intimacy, secrecy, confidentiality, surveillance, accountability, public reason, protected disclosure, exposure harm, consent, and access into a single privacy/publicness grammar. The YAML explicitly treats that as a risk. PMS coherence is not sufficient by itself. The analysis must show discriminative force: it must distinguish protective privacy from evasive concealment, public accountability from harmful exposure, confidentiality from secrecy, legitimate opacity from blocked answerability, and public reason from spectacle.

The drift-pattern conclusion is therefore cautious: the case has drift risks because the thematic field is drift-prone, but no drift finding is made. Risk is not finding. Under-specification is not failure. Non-assignment is not a low score. The correct PMS output is a guarded map of possible drift pathways, not a classification of the case as drifted.

### Add-on / overlay reading

The relevant add-on is **PMS-CRITIQUE**. It is treated strictly as an overlay/application profile. It does not redefine PMS operators. It does not change PMS dependencies. It does not rewrite the PMS Core pass. Its reduced signatures are overlay shorthand only. Its constructs, including publicness overlay **P**, critique thresholds, reach levels, drift classes, and modulators, are non-operator handles. They sharpen visibility without becoming PMS Base.

PMS Core alone is not the final layer here because the case is not only about privacy and publicness as abstract values. It also concerns exposure, accountability, interruption, critique, correction, publicness amplification, and drift under critique pressure. PMS Core makes the structural tension legible, but PMS-CRITIQUE isolates a more specific question: when does a privacy/publicness mismatch become critique, and when does critique mutate into reaction, judgment, narrative repair, commentary, public pillory, or silence?

This matters because privacy/publicness cases easily borrow the language of critique. A demand for disclosure is called accountability. A refusal is called evasion. A public statement is called correction. A silence is called complicity. But PMS-CRITIQUE requires a stricter threshold grammar. Interruption is not yet correction. Publicness is not yet productive critique. Exposure is not yet repair. A demand that another actor bind itself is not stable critique. These distinctions are exactly what the overlay adds beyond the PMS Core reading.

#### CRITIQUE threshold status

The overlay identifies **CRITIQUE_MIN** as weakly applicable. Minimal critique requires, in reduced overlay shorthand, Δ, □, and Χ. The case has Δ because privacy/publicness mismatch is explicit. It has □ because privacy, publicness, access, exposure, and accountability are frame-dependent. It has Χ because the completed PMS Core analysis maintains stop-capability: it suspends verdict, refuses enforcement, and preserves reversibility.

But this is only weak minimal critique. The case material does not supply a concrete critique act. There is no critic, addressee, target, public, accusation, response, correction, or follow-up. Therefore the case can be read as a possible interruption of too-smooth framing, but not as a concrete critique scene. CRITIQUE_MIN here means that the case can interrupt a simplistic privacy/publicness opposition; it does not mean that anyone has successfully criticized anyone else.

**CRITIQUE_PROD**, productive critique, is not applicable. Productive critique would require not only Δ, □, and Χ, but also Φ and Σ. Φ is strongly present because rival reframings are central. Privacy can be reframed as protection or evasion; publicness as accountability or exposure; concealment as care or domination; access as right or violation. But Φ alone does not produce critique. Productive critique requires Σ: a consolidated corrective step, action unit, repair, accountability channel, procedural move, or implementation path. The case supplies none. Therefore productive critique is not reached.

This distinction matters because the case is highly recontextualizable. It is easy to produce many readings: privacy as dignity, privacy as secrecy, publicness as answerability, publicness as spectacle. But multiplying frames is not yet a corrective act. Without Σ, recontextualization can clarify, complicate, or destabilize, but it cannot be counted as productive critique.

**CRITIQUE_STABLE**, stable critique or follow-up binding, is also not applicable. Stable critique would require Ψ: a self-bound follow-up trajectory, audit loop, role commitment, institutional promise, or review obligation. The case supplies no self-binding agent or institution. The overlay explicitly rejects any move in which Ψ becomes a demand imposed on the other. Ψ must bind self or one’s own follow-up; it must not become Ψ→Other coercion. Therefore stable critique is absent.

This is especially important in privacy/publicness cases. Public demand often wants the other party to bind itself: disclose, answer, accept the frame, undergo exposure, or submit to public review. CRITIQUE does not reject such demands in general, but it does block calling them stable critique unless the relevant self-binding structure is present. Stable critique requires follow-up responsibility, not merely pressure.

### Example 1 — Confidential review without public exposure

A staff member reports a concern about a colleague’s conduct to an internal review role. The concern involves a pattern that may affect others, but the reporter asks that their identity and some contextual details remain private. The review role now faces a privacy/publicness tension. If the report remains entirely private, accountability may become impossible or appear impossible. If the report is made public too quickly, the reporter may be exposed, the colleague may be reputationally marked before the frame is clarified, and the situation may become harder to revise.

The bounded vignette is deliberately limited. It does not say whether the concern is true, whether the colleague acted wrongly, whether the reporter is justified, or whether the institution has fulfilled any legal duty. It only creates a structural scene in which privacy, access, exposure, and accountability become mutually entangled.

What the example demonstrates is the difference between **privacy as protection** and **privacy as accountability failure**. A PMS reading must not decide between these too early. The privacy request may protect the reporter from retaliation or unwanted exposure. It may also limit the ability of others to respond, contest, or understand what is being claimed. The same non-disclosure can therefore carry different meanings depending on the frame.

The PMS Core hooks are clear. **Δ** appears in the distinction between private report, limited review, and public disclosure. **∇** appears in the pressure to act: someone wants protection, someone may need accountability, and the reviewing role may feel pressure to decide. **□** is decisive: the active frame might be confidential review, workplace procedure, relational trust, risk assessment, or public accountability. Each frame changes what counts as legitimate access. **Λ** appears where an expected event is absent: perhaps no public explanation is given, no full access is granted, or no response is yet possible. But the example shows why Λ must be calibrated. The absence of public disclosure is not automatically concealment; it may be a protected review condition.

**Ω** is present as an exposure gradient. The reporter may bear a high cost if identified. The colleague may bear a high cost if publicly named. The review role may control access and timing. **Θ** appears in the review window: delay may protect process integrity, but prolonged delay may also create accountability residue. **Φ** is active because the same act can be reframed: confidentiality as care, secrecy as evasion, publicness as accountability, publicness as harm. **Χ** is required to prevent immediate collapse into either “publish everything” or “keep everything hidden.” **Σ** remains conditional: a limited-access review, staged disclosure, or accountable confidentiality process could integrate competing constraints, but only if a concrete procedure exists. **Ψ** would require a self-bound follow-up commitment, such as a review deadline or documented responsibility for next steps. The vignette does not itself prove that such Ψ exists.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay is relevant but limited. The case reaches only weak minimal critique if the report interrupts a too-smooth institutional frame. It does not automatically become productive critique. Productive critique would require a consolidated corrective step, not merely a complaint, exposure, or silence. Stable critique would require follow-up binding: for example, a review role binding itself to a time-bounded response. The publicness overlay **P** remains conditional because the case is not yet public. If publicness later appears, it may amplify Ω and Θ by making exposure more durable.

MIP status remains guardrail-only. No A-score, M-score, or D-score is assigned. The IA-box would require transparency, justification, time-boundedness, and reversibility before any applied asymmetry reading could be made. Dignity-in-Practice is relevant as a protection signal: the analysis must avoid humiliating exposure and avoid using confidentiality to erase affected parties.

AHP is relevant because even this example can become too smooth. It is easy to make the internal review scenario look like a balanced design problem. But the example is not evidence that confidential review works, that it is legally sufficient, or that it protects all parties. It is only an illustration of the calibration problem.

Forbidden claims are central. This example does not show that confidentiality is good, secrecy is bad, publicness is required, or exposure is harmful. It does not diagnose the reporter, the colleague, or the review role. It does not establish legal duties or institutional adequacy. It does not validate PMS.

The non-capture note is that a legal, institutional, or trauma-informed framework may discriminate this scene better than PMS if real facts are supplied. PMS maps the structural tension, but it does not decide the procedure.

The article takeaway is that privacy/publicness analysis must begin by declaring the frame and the expected event. Without that, absence becomes too easy to moralize: non-disclosure becomes evasion, disclosure becomes accountability, and the case loses the very distinctions the analysis is meant to preserve.

### Example 2 — Public accusation and the risk of exposure becoming “correction”

A public post claims that an unnamed organization has hidden information that affects a wider community. The post does not identify individuals, but it uses a strong moral vocabulary: people deserve to know, silence is complicity, and privacy is being used as a shield. The post receives attention. Some readers demand full disclosure. Others warn that premature publicness could expose vulnerable participants, distort the record, or make later review impossible.

This vignette is bounded. It does not say whether the public post is accurate, whether the organization has done anything wrong, whether the wider community has a right to know, or whether public disclosure is legally or morally required. It simply stages a publicness-heavy situation in which accountability pressure and exposure risk grow together.

What the example demonstrates is why PMS Core alone is not the final layer for this case. PMS Core can describe the privacy/publicness tension, but PMS-CRITIQUE makes a more specific danger visible: public interruption can be mistaken for productive critique. A public post may interrupt silence, but interruption is not yet correction. A demand for disclosure may be structurally intelligible, but it is not automatically accountable, proportionate, reversible, or dignified.

The PMS Core hooks begin with **Δ**: hidden/public, silence/disclosure, community access/organizational privacy. **∇** appears as pressure toward exposure: readers want the matter clarified, answered, or made visible. **□** is unstable because several frames compete. In one frame, this is public accountability. In another, it is reputational exposure. In another, it is incomplete information circulating without review. In another, it is a protective warning. **Λ** appears in the missing response: no full explanation has yet arrived. But that absence is ambiguous. It may be evasion, process protection, legal caution, or an unavailable event.

**Ω** intensifies once attention grows. The organization may have resources and control over information, but the public post may control narrative momentum. Unnamed participants may bear exposure risk without having voice. **Θ** appears as public residue: once the post circulates, screenshots, commentary, and reputational effects may outlast any later correction. **Φ** is central because every side can reframe the same move: publicness as accountability, publicness as pressure, privacy as protection, privacy as concealment. **Χ** is needed to stop the analysis from siding with whichever frame feels urgent. **Σ** is absent unless a corrective pathway appears: for example, a time-bounded public statement, protected review, or accountable disclosure channel. **Ψ** is absent unless someone binds themselves to follow-up rather than only demanding that others respond.

The CRITIQUE overlay carries the example. **CRITIQUE_MIN** is plausible because there is mismatch, frame contestation, and interruption. But **CRITIQUE_PROD** is not yet reached. The post may make a problem visible, but it does not by itself consolidate a corrective step. **CRITIQUE_STABLE** is also not reached unless the critic, organization, or process binds itself to follow-up. The overlay’s publicness modulator **P** is active: publicness amplifies Ω, Θ, and Α. Attention can create an attractor. Readers repeat simplified claims. Silence becomes interpreted as guilt. Response becomes interpreted as damage control. The case can drift before facts are clarified.

The drift risks are not findings, but this example shows why they matter. **Judgment masquerading as critique** is a risk if “privacy” becomes a label for guilt or “publicness” becomes a label for virtue. **Public pillory** is a risk if exposure becomes degradation rather than correction. **Commentary drift** is a risk if the public discussion expands while no corrective path emerges. **Integration bypass** is a risk if moral clarity substitutes for Σ.

MIP remains non-scoring. No maturity or responsibility band is assigned. The IA-box would ask whether the exposure process is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. Those conditions are not answered by the vignette. Dignity-in-Practice is active only as protection discipline: publicness must not become humiliation, and privacy must not become a tool for erasing affected parties.

AHP is relevant at the article level because this example can be easily overread. The example itself must be presented as an analysis artefact with attack surfaces: publicness is semantically loaded; critique clarity is not critique decidability; exposure risk is not proof of harm; non-response is not proof of guilt.

Forbidden claims must be explicit. The example does not claim that public accusation is wrong. It does not claim that public accountability is illegitimate. It does not claim that the organization is guilty, that the critic is reckless, or that public attention is inherently harmful. It also does not claim that privacy defeats accountability.

The non-capture note is that a public-reason, legal, media-ethics, or surveillance-critical framework may be needed in a concrete case. PMS-CRITIQUE makes drift risk visible; it does not settle public legitimacy.

The article takeaway is that publicness can interrupt, but interruption is not correction. CRITIQUE helps preserve this distinction: a public act may open a frame, but productive critique requires integration, and stable critique requires self-bound follow-up.

### Example 3 — Access request, refusal, and the ambiguity of “nothing happened”

A researcher asks an institution for access to archived material relevant to a past decision. The institution refuses full access and offers only a short explanation: the material contains private information and cannot be released. The researcher argues that the refusal blocks accountability. The institution argues that disclosure would violate privacy and expose people who did not consent to public review. Observers split: some see a privacy boundary; others see institutional opacity.

This vignette is bounded. It does not say whether the archive should be opened, whether the refusal is lawful, whether accountability is being obstructed, whether the researcher has a right to access, or whether the private material would harm anyone if released. It stages a structurally difficult non-event: requested access does not occur.

What the example demonstrates is the calibration of **Λ Non-Event**. A request is made; access is not granted. It is tempting to treat the missing access as evidence that accountability has failed. It is equally tempting to treat the refusal as obviously justified by privacy. PMS allows neither shortcut. The non-event becomes meaningful only if the frame establishes what kind of access was expected, why it was expected, what protected good limits it, and what alternative review path exists.

The PMS Core hooks unfold in order. **Δ** appears in the distinctions between access and refusal, privacy and accountability, archive and public record, full disclosure and partial explanation. **∇** appears as pressure to resolve: the researcher seeks access; the institution seeks limitation; observers seek an interpretation. **□** is the decisive missing element. Is the frame scholarly inquiry, institutional accountability, legal access, archival ethics, privacy protection, public-interest review, or internal governance? The refusal changes meaning depending on which frame applies.

**Λ** is the center of the example. The expected event might be access, explanation, review, redaction, appeal, or protected third-party audit. If the expected event is full public release, the refusal is one kind of non-event. If the expected event is accountable review without public disclosure, the refusal may be different. If no access right exists in the operative frame, the absence of access may not be a failure at all. The example therefore shows why Λ must not be moralized.

**Α** remains conditional. A single refusal does not prove an institutional secrecy pattern. If many similar requests were refused without review, an attractor might become visible. But this vignette does not establish that. **Ω** is salient because the institution controls the archive, the researcher lacks access, and the persons represented in the archive may bear exposure costs. Yet Ω remains a structural gradient, not a blame finding. **Θ** is relevant because archives preserve past decisions and public access can reopen old effects; but the example gives no timeline for review, delay, or appeal. **Φ** is active: the same refusal can be recontextualized as protection, opacity, bureaucratic caution, confidentiality, or accountability failure. **Χ** is required to prevent the analysis from converting refusal into guilt or access into justice. **Σ** would require a concrete integration, such as redacted access, independent review, staged release, consent process, or public summary. **Ψ** would require a self-bound follow-up commitment by the institution or reviewer. Neither is supplied by the vignette.

PMS-CRITIQUE is relevant if the researcher’s access request functions as critique. At most, the request may reach weak minimal critique: it identifies a mismatch, invokes a frame, and can be interruptible under Χ. It does not become productive critique unless a correction or review pathway is consolidated. It does not become stable critique unless someone binds themselves to follow-up. The refusal itself is not automatically silence attractor; a pattern would need recurrence.

MIP is relevant as a downstream guardrail. The IA-box would ask whether the asymmetry is transparent, justified, time-bound, and reversible. Is the refusal explained? Is the privacy good specified? Is there a review window? Is there an appeal or redacted alternative? Are affected parties protected? In the vignette, those questions are not answered. Therefore IA remains a risk structure, not a finding. No M-score can be assigned to the institution, researcher, or observers because knowledge, power, predictability, alternatives, and serious integration attempts are not established.

AHP is relevant because this example can look cleaner than it is. The analysis can become formally precise while the evidence remains thin. AHP would flag the attack surface: public interest, privacy, access, archive, and accountability are all under-defined unless the frame is specified.

Forbidden claims include: the institution is hiding wrongdoing; the researcher is entitled to the archive; privacy is merely an excuse; accountability requires full public access; refusal proves guilt; access would necessarily harm private persons. None follows from the vignette.

The non-capture note is that archival ethics, privacy law, institutional procedure, research ethics, or public-records doctrine may be necessary in a real case. PMS clarifies structural dependencies; it does not supply the access standard.

The article takeaway is that “nothing happened” is often the most dangerous part of a privacy/publicness case. A missing disclosure, missing access, or missing reply can be structurally significant, but only after the expected event and frame are declared. Without that discipline, Λ becomes accusation by absence.

### Non-capture and rival readings

The YAML analyses preserve non-capture as a positive methodological requirement. Non-capture does not mean failure. It means that PMS and overlays do not claim to exhaust the case.

A **privacy-protective reading** treats opacity, boundary, intimacy, confidentiality, safety, and non-access as primary. This reading preserves the possibility that privacy is not absence, evasion, or blocked accountability, but a positive condition of relation, protection, or dignity. PMS can map privacy as frame and non-event, but a privacy theory may better distinguish intimacy, secrecy, confidentiality, data minimization, consent, and lawful protection.

An **accountability-publicness reading** treats public reason, oversight, answerability, and shared consequence as primary. This reading preserves the possibility that publicness is not exposure or spectacle, but the necessary condition under which hidden consequences become contestable. PMS can map publicness as Ω/Θ amplification and CRITIQUE can mark P, but a public-reason or accountability theory may better discriminate legitimate answerability from mere visibility.

A **consent/access reading** centers authorization, control, revocability, differentiated access, and the right to decide who may know what. This reading preserves a dimension not fully captured by the privacy/publicness binary. Access may be neither fully private nor fully public; it may be layered, conditional, revocable, procedural, role-based, or context-bound.

A **legal-procedural reading** would require jurisdiction, rights, evidentiary standards, due process, confidentiality obligations, public-interest tests, liability rules, and remedies. This reading preserves the fact that some privacy/publicness conflicts cannot be responsibly analyzed without law or institutional procedure. PMS cannot supply those standards.

A **surveillance-critical reading** treats publicness and exposure as possible instruments of domination. It preserves the asymmetry of visibility: who watches, who is watched, who stores, who interprets, who circulates, and who cannot exit. CRITIQUE’s publicness overlay gestures toward this, but a surveillance-specific framework may discriminate the technological and political conditions more sharply.

A **phenomenological or intimacy reading** may preserve the lived meaning of privacy, shame, exposure, vulnerability, or being-seen. PMS does not claim phenomenology. It can map structural exposure, but not subjective experience.

A **language-practice reading** may focus on how “privacy,” “publicness,” “accountability,” and “access” function in discourse. It may preserve ordinary-language plurality, speech-act conditions, or institutional vocabularies that PMS only abstracts.

An **anti-capture reading** may argue that the case should remain partially unresolved because any structural grammar risks absorbing refusal, opacity, or dissent into its own terms. The completed YAML explicitly keeps this open. Resistance, rupture, silence, non-fit, and rival framing must not automatically become PMS drift or incomplete integration.

A **pluralist non-integrative reading** treats the conflict as irreducibly tragic, politically contested, or structurally unresolved. This reading preserves the possibility that no Σ integration is appropriate. Sometimes privacy and accountability may both have legitimate claims that cannot be harmonized without loss.

Each rival reading preserves something important: specificity, legal authority, lived experience, institutional procedure, consent nuance, surveillance structure, public reason, or tragedy. The layered PMS analysis does not defeat these rivals. It only provides a disciplined structural map. A rival reading may be superior if it discriminates privacy, secrecy, confidentiality, access, publicity, accountability, exposure, and harm more sharply than the current PMS projection.

The examples reinforce this non-capture discipline. The confidential review example may require institutional procedure or trauma-informed process. The public accusation example may require media ethics, public-reason theory, or surveillance critique. The archive-access example may require law, archival ethics, or research governance. PMS and its overlays help map structural dependencies, but they do not replace those domain grammars.

### Weakening, falsifier, and operationalization conditions

The PMS reading would be weakened if later material showed that no privacy/publicness tension is actually present. It would also be weakened if concealment, exposure, access, or accountability are not contested in the relevant case, or if the issue is purely technical, definitional, legal, or procedural without praxeological tension.

It would be under-discriminating if it cannot distinguish privacy-as-protection from privacy-as-evasion, accountability from spectacle, confidentiality from secrecy, access from violation, publicness from surveillance, or refusal from drift. This is the most important analytic falsifier. PMS must earn its usefulness by discriminating, not by translating everything into its own terms.

It would be over-assimilative if it treats resistance, refusal, silence, disagreement, rival framing, unresolved absence, or non-fit as PMS drift, failed integration, incomplete calibration, or maturity failure by default. That would violate both PMS non-capture discipline and CRITIQUE guardrails.

The CRITIQUE overlay would be weakened if later material showed that no critique threshold is relevant: no interruption, no frame contestation, no publicness amplification, no correction demand, no accountability pressure. It would also be weakened if another overlay, such as LOGIC, CONFLICT, or QC, captured the relevant structure more directly. In this case CRITIQUE is justified by publicness and accountability language, but that does not make it inevitable.

CRITIQUE would overreach if it treated weak CRITIQUE_MIN as a concrete critique act, if it treated P as a PMS operator, if it treated publicness as correction, if it classified drift without scene evidence, or if it turned Ψ into a demand that the other bind itself.

MIP would be weakened if the case remains abstract and no applied use is intended. The completed MIP layer already accepts this by making the result a structural working note only. MIP would overreach if it assigned A-score, M-score, IA finding, D-score, or person-level maturity. It would also overreach if it treated low evidence as low responsibility. Non-assignment is not low score.

AHP would be weakened if its precision analysis were mistaken for case validation. Its purpose is not to increase confidence in the factual case, but to harden the analysis artefact. AHP itself names the major attack point: high formal precision may mask lack of concrete scene evidence.

For stronger claims, the operationalization requirements are extensive. A concrete analysis would need:

* a concrete scene;
* identified roles;
* the information object at issue;
* who claims privacy;
* who claims publicness;
* who seeks access;
* who is exposed;
* who controls access;
* who is accountable or asked to account;
* the expected event under Λ;
* the active frame under □;
* the temporal sequence under Θ;
* recurrence or stabilization under Α;
* exposure, vulnerability, control, and obligation gradients under Ω;
* source and target frames for Φ;
* distance and reversibility conditions under Χ;
* whether any Σ integration is possible, inappropriate, or already attempted;
* whether any Ψ self-binding belongs to an actor, institution, role, policy, or follow-up loop;
* legal, empirical, technical, clinical, or institutional standards where relevant;
* affected-party voice and review channels;
* external evaluation if any applied claim is made.

Operationalization remains unfulfilled. The current article cannot be used as a policy, moderation rule, legal analysis, institutional procedure, or access-control design. It can only serve as a structural map and preparation layer.

The three examples show how these conditions would become relevant without satisfying them. The confidential-review example would require actual procedure, mandate, review limits, and affected-party protections. The public-accusation example would require audience, platform, evidence, response path, and reversibility data. The archive-access example would require access standard, legal or institutional frame, redaction possibility, and review process. They demonstrate requirements; they do not fulfill them.

### Misuse boundary

This case must not be used diagnostically. It does not infer motives, traits, pathology, shame, secrecy needs, maturity, avoidance, domination, sincerity, or competence.

It must not be used for person ranking. No actor, group, public, institution, claimant, exposed party, or access controller is morally ranked by association with privacy or publicness.

It must not be used for legal or forensic conclusion. It does not determine rights, liability, evidence, culpability, public interest, procedural validity, or lawful disclosure.

It must not be used as metaphysical proof. It does not say what privacy, publicness, dignity, personhood, accountability, or truth are in themselves.

It must not be used as empirical proof. It does not establish facts about actual privacy harms, public exposure, surveillance, institutional conduct, disclosure patterns, or accountability outcomes.

It must not be used for implementation validation. It does not define a system, protocol, model, workflow, access-control mechanism, audit design, governance rule, or moderation policy.

It must not be used as PMS Base validation. The case does not prove PMS, PMS-CRITIQUE, MIP, or AHP. It only applies them under strict boundaries.

It must not use add-ons as operators. CRITIQUE thresholds, drift classes, publicness overlay P, MIP A-C-R-P, IA-box, D-module, and AHP Precision Heuristic are not PMS operators and do not revise PMS dependencies.

It must not treat drift as verdict. PMS drift patterns and CRITIQUE drift risks are conditional structural risks, not findings, diagnoses, accusations, sanctions, or moral judgments.

It must not treat publicness as correction. Exposure may be accountability, but may also be harm, spectacle, surveillance, or humiliation. Productive critique requires more than publicness.

It must not treat privacy as evasion. Privacy may be secrecy, but may also be intimacy, safety, confidentiality, discretion, legitimate opacity, or Dignity-in-Practice protection.

It must not treat absence as proof. Missing access, missing disclosure, missing explanation, or delayed public response may be structurally meaningful only after the expected event and frame are declared. Λ is not accusation by absence.

It must not treat non-assignment as low score. The absence of A-score, M-score, D-score, IA finding, responsibility assignment, or drift finding does not mean low maturity, low responsibility, low dignity, or low risk. It means the case does not supply the conditions for assignment.

It must not produce closure by authority. The layered analysis is not stronger because more layers are present. Each layer adds visibility under constraints. None authorizes final closure.

It must not let polished analysis become overclaim. AHP explicitly warns that high precision is not evidence. A complete article is still only a structural rendering of an abstract case unless concrete scene material is supplied.

### Case conclusion

The integrative result is that **Privacy vs. Publicness** is best understood as a structurally rich but non-concrete AXIOM case. PMS Core makes the tension legible: privacy/publicness distinctions activate pressures around concealment, exposure, access, limitation, and accountability within frames that are not supplied. Λ marks possible meaningful absence; Ω marks possible exposure and access asymmetry; Θ remains weak because no sequence is given; Φ is central because rival reframings are unavoidable; Χ is required as analytic restraint; Σ remains unresolved; Ψ remains guardrail-only. The case is therefore highly legible but not resolved.

The PMS-CRITIQUE overlay adds that the case can reach only weak formal critique-interruptibility. It can interrupt simplistic framings of privacy and publicness, but it does not produce correction or stable follow-up. CRITIQUE_PROD is not reached because no Σ-consolidated corrective step exists. CRITIQUE_STABLE is not reached because no Ψ-bound follow-up trajectory exists. Publicness overlay P is active conceptually because publicness can amplify exposure gradients, temporal residue, and attractor formation, but P remains an overlay handle, not a PMS operator and not a finding of public accountability.

MIP adds a downstream caution: the case can support maturity-in-practice questions, but not maturity scoring. No A-score, M-score, D-score, or person-level reading is assigned. IA remains conceptual IA-risk, not an asymmetry verdict. Dignity-in-Practice remains a praxeological protection constraint, not ontological dignity or moral superiority. AHP adds a final methodological hardening: the analysis is precise as an artefact, but precision is not scene evidence. The main risk is that a complete structure may appear more decisive than the case material warrants.

The examples contribute by showing how the abstract structure would behave in bounded scenes without turning those scenes into evidence. The confidential-review example shows why privacy can be protection without defeating accountability and why non-disclosure must be framed before it is judged. The public-accusation example shows why publicness can interrupt without yet becoming correction and why exposure can drift toward pillory. The archive-access example shows why “nothing happened” can become structurally significant only after the expected event and access frame are specified. Together, the examples demonstrate the analysis; they do not replace it.

What remains unclaimed is substantial. The article does not decide whether privacy or publicness should prevail. It does not identify any actor, assign responsibility, diagnose motive, classify maturity, establish harm, validate public exposure, condemn concealment, create a legal standard, prescribe a procedure, or implement a system. It does not prove PMS, PMS-CRITIQUE, MIP, or AHP. It does not claim exhaustive capture. It leaves open that privacy theory, public-reason theory, consent/access theory, legal procedure, surveillance critique, phenomenology, language-practice analysis, or pluralist non-integrative accounts may better capture specific concrete cases.

The boundary discipline is therefore the case’s central result. Risk is not finding. Coherence is not proof. Non-assignment is not low score. Absence is not guilt. Resistance is not drift. Refusal is not immaturity. Publicness is not correction. Privacy is not evasion. Dignity-in-Practice is not ontological dignity. Add-on language is not PMS Base. AHP hardening is not rescoring.

The final methodological takeaway is that privacy/publicness cases require a layered but restrained cartography. The question is not how to force closure between privacy and publicness, but how to keep visible the frames, absences, asymmetries, temporal residues, recontextualizations, critique thresholds, and dignity-protection constraints that make premature closure dangerous. AXIOM’s value here is not that it solves the tension. It shows where a solution would require more material, where integration must remain conditional, where publicness must not be confused with correction, where privacy must not be confused with evasion, and where Λ-residue must remain visible rather than being converted into verdict.
